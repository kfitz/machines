The Semantic Web

By campagnolo - Posted on 21 September 2007 - 7:15pm.
Tagged:
  • hakia
  • hierarchy
  • search engine
  • semantic
  • spock
  • Tim Berners-Lee

With our talks of hierarchies and how the current drive on the internet is to improve ways to index and search content, this seemed particularly interesting. Read. this article. All together too much jargon, but I am sure some of you will understand more than me. The gist seems to be that the future is in organizing the internet in a semantic hierarchy. Or how I dumb things down for myself: them computers are learnin' human.
The series of articles is certainly interesting, but what is even cooler is exploring the links they provide. Namely the two I found endless entertainment from were hakia.com and spock.com.

Hakia is a semantic search engine. I just spent maybe 10 minutes thinking up searches google couldn't handle and trying them out. Interesting results came in for "What are bottle caps made of?" and a few others. In the end its results were not much better than google's but give it time.

Spock was way cooler. It's a hierarchical search engine of people. Search your parents, search your professors. It's frightening.

I think we do need a narrative so to speak. And it seems the computers do too. At least to search effectively. In my mind this almost like taching computers to speak. Frustratingly unfulfilling questions of AI arise, but isn't this sort of an orality to literacy move?

reliability of "web facts"
Submitted by jughead on 22 September 2007 - 5:58pm.

It was a little difficult for me to figure out what that article was saying in tech talk, so what I got out of it came mostly from what was summed up in the ensuing comments. I'm assuming that the "semantic web" then refers to a web that would have the ability to make connections between existing "facts" and take those facts a step further by connecting them through inferences, or "web facts," based on those existing facts.

The way the internet and search engines are set up now, there is a hierarchal system based on the most popular pages people go to when they search for something (right?). The "semantic web" would then actually create new facts the way the human mind does when it logically connects two related facts. Jean-Michel Decombe comments on the article and the potential problems that arise from computers suddenly doing our work for us:

The major problem that the Semantic Web will face in the future is not even scalability, anyway, but trustability. Indeed, what makes a fact on the Web true? And if you cannot guarantee that all Web facts that were evaluated to answer a person's question are true, then what is the value of the Semantic Web's answer? And then, the facts that embody the truth for one person do not necessary embody it for another (e.g. "abortion is amoral", "the Catholic Church is the only true church", "Paris Hilton is cool", etc.). Every Web fact will have to be authenticated and certified by the issuing "authority", and people will be able to choose which authority they wish to rely on.

Get ready for RDF spam, the worst and most insidious spam form of all.

I understand his fears, but isn't "trustaility" os web facts already a major issue today? Search engines can already be affected by forms of spam (google bombing, etc). I agree with campagnolo that people need to impose narratives on a group of non-hierarchal facts in order to understand them. But by relinquishing the actual construction of the narrative to a machine, we lose the aspect of control over which facts to paste together, so to speak. But I suppose that's already happened when the first thing you see on your Google search is the most popular link to your keyphrase.

So does this potential new form of automated hierarchy on the Semantic Web make a difference?

Machines that think on their own
Submitted by stumpy on 24 September 2007 - 2:25am.

In the last pages of Armstrong's article she wrote, "Mass man is distinguished by the fact that its psychic energy exceeds the sum total off individual desires composing such a group, were that group indeed composed of individuals." I think that one of the defining characteristics of new media is its ability to work on algorithms in a way that makes it more capable than the person who wrote the algorithm. I was reminded of Tivo and it's ability to predict and schedule shows on its own that it believes (based on previous choices) you will enjoy. It also reminded me of websites that update themselves. The computer may not be able to think on its own, but it does perform many actions without them being prescribed.
