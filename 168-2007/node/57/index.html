The Sims
Submitted by stumpy on 27 October 2007 - 11:05am.

I completely forgot about my seventh grade obsession with the Sims. All my friends had it, so of course I had to have it too. I just played a little Sims to try and remember what it was like (because it reminded me of facade... or the other way around) and there are some interesting features I had totally forgotten about. For example, in addition to making sure that your Sims pay their bills and go to the bathroom, you have to make sure their "fun level" is at a certain level. If the fun level drops too low, the Sims become depressed (although I read somewhere that they can never commit suicide) and then they make the other Sims depressed. This was the part that reminded me of facade. The funny thing is, this is the bigggest danger in the game. The Sims can't really get physically hurt. Most video games deal with actions to evade physical danger, which is easier for the player to estimate. How do I not get shot by my opponent? a) shoot him back or b) dodge/hide. Because emotions and reactions are so complicated, we are much more prone to be critical of games like facade and the Sims, which fail in their attempt to recreate such complex concepts like personality and emotions.

more sims
Submitted by campagnolo on 28 October 2007 - 10:19pm.

I played the sims a little obsessively for about a week before it got old for me. I do have a pretty good memory of it however. What's interesting to me is that the sims was an obvious quantification of human life, and much more addicting than facade which I think is more of a qualification of human life (or attempted at leas).
What I mean is the sims you had obvious measuring sticks, ratings if you will. Your job, your material possessions, your attempts at love. ("tell joke, give compliment, tell joke, give compliment, tell joke, give compliment" all the while your relationship rating going up--if i remember the game properly) This at least for me was much more addicting than facade which is a muddle. In facade there is a very apparent disconnect between what you do and what happens. A lack of clarity of what you have power over. I saw this as a clear shortcoming of the AI to properly interact with you. But in comparison to the sims its a much more accurate representation of real life, where we have no idea what result our actions have as opposed to a sound effect and +3 above someones head or some garbage like that.

Now there is something very appealing about talking to real people and having pleasing/irritating sound effects play when they're digging you. This aside however no one really wants this, at least I don't think so. Thats why the sims gets old. no complexity. There is some sort of balance here where the is no clear answer to what is preferred. Look at in terms of face book. What are friend counts and pokes on facebook but the sim-ulification (to drop some lexia to perplexia neologisms on this post) of life.
