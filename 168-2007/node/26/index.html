subjectivity

By gnugnu - Posted on 24 September 2007 - 10:10am.
Tagged:
  • object
  • subject

I want to comment on the discussion we had at the end of class, when we were talking about how Don Quixote (or Emma Bovary, if you prefer) sees objects in the "real" world as extensions of the fantastic narrative he imposes on the world. I'm not quite sure what to do with this. On the one hand, we all impose our own narratives on the world and thus do not see things as they are, but as we are. But we (some of us anyway) can at least acknowledge that fact and recognize: that other people might see things differently; that objects might mean different things to different people; that other people might think it strange that we use a wonderful helmet as a shaving bowl.

I think what's really interesting to me is the two different notions of seeing the world as an extension of the self. There's the one described above, which is pretty common even in adulthood. There's also the way babies see the world, which I find fascinating. They (I'm pretty sure) can't distinguish between the subject and object (here I mean "object" not in the sense of a physical object). In fact, they can't imagine that distinction at all. For them, there is no subject; there is no object; everything is one. That might be why we can't remember anything from when we were babies. We structured the world in such a radically different way that even remembering it would foul up the way we structure it now.

Submitted by jughead on 24 September 2007 - 9:32pm.

Since we're thinking about weird scenarios like the mental processes (or lack of?) of babies, I thought I'd go ahead and just throw some fun questions out there:

If you somehow grew up without any interaction (and sadly enough I know we have cases of this) with another person, would you still have a notion of yourself? Do you have thoughts if you don't have language, and what would they be like if you did? Would they be pure images? Would you have to adapt to some way of thinking to process the images without using language?

Is language therefore crucial to developing our identities? If it is, doesn't that mean it has to be "natural" to us even though it is constructed?

The concept of "natural" really bothers me, and I think Ong deals with it best: "artificiality is natural to human beings."

I've seen more than one special on the Discovery Channel about children who grow up without human interaction. It's possible that the issues raised by that are interesting, but so are the reasons we find these situations so fascinating.

Thinking of this always reminds me of the degree to which we're socially constructed. People talk so much about the "natural" role of a man, woman, or child, but when you encounter cases like these...

I like that Ong quotation a lot. Artifice does come pretty naturally to us.

Wildchild
Submitted by stumpy on 26 September 2007 - 1:46am.

If any of you guys have not read about Genie you definitely need to. I spent an entire night researching her on the internet because her story is so interesting. The basic premise is that her father locked her in a room from the age of 20 months to 13 years. After she was rescued by California authorities, she was helped for several years by Children's Hospital in LA, but was never able to learn how to speak. As to what Jughead said, she was definitely able to express herself better through pictures and motions than she was through speech, so perhaps she did think in pictures. However, maybe thought is socially constructed/learned as well. I definitely wondered what Genie thought about for 12 years while tied to a chair. I read about 10 different articles and my question was never answered. Obviously, I thought, there must have been something going on in her head. But, thinking is a combination of reflecting on past actions and planning ahead for future actions. It is not necessary to do these, but it is socially acceptable.
Without thought, a person is a combination of action and reaction. It's ppossible that people without speech or much visual stimulation have very few thoughts at all.

Submitted by jughead on 26 September 2007 - 8:41am.

I just don't see how that could be... the human mind is so complex I just can't image it not doing much of anything during those 12 years. And doesn't it usually seem that if somebody is mentally "impared" (by our constructed definition) in one way, the brain sort of overemphasizes a different process? I've met a boy with autism, for example who has to wear special noise-blocking earphones everywhere because he is much more sensitive to ambiente noise than most people. Everyday noises like doors opening and closing and people talking on the street would damage his ears. But I guess we can't really know what or how Genie "thinks" as that attempts to grasp what Burnett says our brain imaging technology inherently leaves out.
