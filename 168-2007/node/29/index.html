blooks for sale

By jughead - Posted on 27 September 2007 - 1:52am.
Tagged:
  • blooks

So earlier I was reading a book called Good City Form for one of my other classes, and, although its author Kevin Lynch was not referring to media forms when he wrote it, I was struck in particular by one of his observations: "There is an inherent tension as well as a circularity between continuity and development--between the stabilities and connections needed for coherence and the ability to change and grow."

I think this is an accurate description for what we noted in class today about Bush's awkward limitations in describing the future of technology by using the language of his present understanding of it (as the language of newer media does not yet exist for him). It seems to me that some of this tension manifests itself in the desire of new media forms--blogs, youtube, you name it--to be seen as separate from their precursors in old media. And yet all forms of new media are remediated forms of older media, hence the circularity within the process of development.

While thinking about this I happened to stumble upon an apparently very well-known phenomenon from which I have obviously been hiding in a dark cave the past few years: "blooks."

blook (bluk), n. A printed and bound book, based on a blog (cf. web log) or web site; a new stage in the life-cycle of content, if not a new category of content and a new dawn for the book itself. cf.

The Lulu Blooker Prize ("The Blooker"), a literary prize founded in 2005 for blooks. [der. Eng. book, a bound collection of sheets of paper; blog (abbrev. web log, an internet journal, diary or personal web site)]

I guess I realize now that I think about it that I do know of blogs that have been turned into books. I just haven't really considered what that means on the McLuhan level (the media IS the message!) before now. Part of what makes a blog a blog is its existence in the digital world alone. I think that remediating a blog "backwards" in the technological progression to printed form only takes away from its power as a source of user-generated content and user interactivity. And although we pointed out problems with the idea (I can't remember whose) that a text is completely non-refutable, a physical text is undeniably less immediately contestable than the medium of online blogging.

I suppose I don't take issue with "blooks" that are published as mere records of ongoing blogs (e.g. the post secret books in which having a hard copy of the images is sort of nice in a nostalgic way), but I don't know how I feel about the rest.
