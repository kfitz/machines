Bloggers quoted on the Senate Floor

Submitted by Natwwal on 8 September 2006 - 10:48am.
  • blog credibility
  • famous blogs

I just ran across a bit of information that I found quite fascinating. According to the congressional record, Senator Rick Santorum quoted two bloggers in support of his argument regarding a judicial nomination.

Both bloggers have reasonable legal credentials. Eugene Volokh, founder of The Volokh Conspiracy, is a constitutional law professor at UCLA. Howard Bashman, author of How Appealing, is respected appellate lawyer. What I find particularly interesting is that Senator Santorum cited Bashman's blog as a credential despite the fact that Bashman's comments about the case were published not on his blog, but in the New York Times. There were many people in class who questioned the reliability of blogs. This story provides an interesting contrast to that perspective. It indicates that in the eyes of some individuals, running a successful blog enhances rather than damages a writer's credibility.

It's funny--I wonder how

Submitted by marmalade on 8 September 2006 - 12:02pm.

It's funny--I wonder how responsible someonle like Volokh can (or wants to) be for his blog's content. We were talking about trying to protect our identities by posting here on our blog using pseudonyms--protecting ourselves from future employers or really anyone out there who could label us or hold us accountable for these posts. As a university professor, Volokh has a job which is (in some ways) contingent upon a certain persona; just like Bashman must mantain a similar public face for his firm and clients. Publishing a blog seems like quite treacherous area to enter--it could affect public policy (like it appears to have in this case), but maybe it could also make one too famous (or infamous) to be employable. Is this a risky gamble? A brilliant strategy for affecting law policy? Or a mixture? I can't tell--does anyone else have any ideas?

I think that for people like

Submitted by Natwwal on 11 September 2006 - 10:25am.

I think that for people like Professor Volokh, a blog can be an extension of the identity its author has already cultivated in academic or journalistic writing. Someone in class made the claim that a blog becomes the blogger's identity to his or her online readership. For people like Volokh and Bashman, this isn't necessarily true. I think Volokh and Bashman are well aware of the personas that they present in their blogs. They may write in part for their own enjoyment, but I think they are prepared to use their blogs for professional advantage as well.
