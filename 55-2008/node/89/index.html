And as for your intuition that they come into play in the Last Supper fresco, they do. In it, Jesus and Mary are wearing red and blue, mirrors of each other, with Mary wearing a blue robe/dress-thingy and a red shawl, and jesus wearing a red robe/dress thingy and blue shawl. Which makes sense for having the wives in mostly blue, as Mary is the alleged Wife of Jesus and therefore the Wives are the prominent part of her, where the light touch of red can signify how the Handmaids are supposed to be a minimal part of the equation. Submitted by LeoniaTavira on 13 February 2008 - 1:45am.

Yeah, there's definitely the hypothesis of it being Mary Magdalene in Leonardo's Last Supper. As much as I'd love to believe it, even writers 50 years after the painting was completed commented on its disrepair and virtually unidentifiable figures. What we're familiar with today was recreated from a copy done at the time, copy of a copy type of thing, filtered through a different (lesser) artist. But that's just me being picky because I'm an Art History major. Anyway, I found this interesting too, because the colors are obviously loaded with meaning, especially in conjunction with such a biblically laced culture/government.

The Virgin Mary is definitely traditionally imagined in blue, a sign of her elevation above the rest of man, also her sinless body that gave birth to a sinless man (that whole Jesus time traveling to forgive her sins so she could be sinless to give birth to him, it's an amazing rationalization). As for the red, it's definitely a color that signifies in no uncertain terms the blood which is so essential a part of the handmaids' purpose. Blood, womb, that which gives life, and also sex and passion, even the anger that is the reaction of the wives against them. Another interesting thing about the Wives being dressed in blue is that it puts them, in some ways, in the same category as Mary, choosing to highlight a virginal childlessness, rather than a barren one.

This is probably totally irrelevant, but the Virgin of Guadalupe is depicted wearing both red and blue. Her dress is red with a black sash across her belly to signify her pregnancy and the blue mantle covered in stars to signify the divine importance of the child. There is also a moon beneath her and Offred mentions telling time by the moon, but that's probably such a stretch as to be worth ignoring.

Hope some of that helps.
