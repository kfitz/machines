More Web 2.0 Stuff

By Bumpkins - Posted on 13 February 2007 - 6:54pm.
Tagged:
  • web 2.0

I think the Google Search format is about to take over the world. Ever wanted to call 411 and just say, "Ralph's Pizza", and have them call if for you? Well, soon you will be able to. Check this out.

Web 2.0 is becoming World 2.0, where every everything we touch has felt the effects of computer technology. McDonalds outsources its drive through to Missouri, why can't 411 figure out where I want to call base on my voice's commands? While a computer might still talk like a computer, it can now understand English better than ever could before.

Step backwards
Submitted by Crazy Enough on 13 February 2007 - 8:13pm.

"World 2.0" is cute, Bumpkins. Maybe a little scary. But cute.

The content of that Forbes article actually seems like a step backwards to me. Applying lessons learned from the web and applying them to telephony...telephony? When reading about the idea of a 411 that is as smart as Google, I had to think, "why not just use Google?!?" It's much easier to browse search results when you can see them in front of you than listening to them one by one.

The only advantage is that phone access these days is everywhere, and internet access is still limited. But not for long. In the same way that cellular access is universal, it is only a matter of time before wi-fi (wireless internet) is also universal. Imagine being able to connect to the internet while cruising down the freeway (only if you're in the passenger seat, of course). I would bet that by 2020 we will take this for granted. Anyone have earlier predictions? It's been in the works for a couple years in San Francisco and was just approved last month in downtown LA. I expected this movement to be more like cell service now -- a number of service providers that profit from subscribers. Instead it's all being paid for by local governments. Again, my predictions is that by 2020 we will demand free wifi from our local government in the same way that we demand trash disposal service.

Japan is there
Submitted by Bumpkins on 13 February 2007 - 10:31pm.

I'm not positive, but I think I read, in The World Is Flat, that Japan is already completely wireless. I also know that there are new products coming out that will allow wireless to be paid for cheaply by local governments, i.e. towns.

And yes I think you are right when you say "Telephony" is backwards from web 2.0. The world, or maybe physical reality, has to catch up to the web, and cyberspace.

I browsed the web looking
Submitted by Crazy Enough on 14 February 2007 - 9:43am.

I browsed the web looking for some indication of a public wireless network throughout Japan. I haven't found it, but I'm not done with my search yet. (Does anyone know more about it?) While looking, however, I ran across something else on the subject. This article draws my attention to the political hurdles of blanket wifi coverage. The whole article is interesting, but if you want to most bang for your buck just read the case study under the subheading "The Philadelphia Story."

These public wifi movements aren't getting as much press as they deserve. The future will be in broadband and we must acknowledge it. As the authors of the linked article say, "In the near future, telephone, television, radio and the web all will be delivered to your home via a single broadband connection."

Computers understanding english
Submitted by BenManTheEvil on 14 February 2007 - 2:37pm.

One of the things that Google is not leading the industry in right now is video search. Yes, they own youtube and they get a significant number of hits on google video, but their search technology for rich media is behind those of others.

As far as I know, Google video only searches meta data and tags. Its results are limited by the information users write about it in the title and in the tag section. Other video search engines have incorporated a voice recognition software that allows them to pick out key words and sentences in a video so "I" can find that video even if i don't know what it's called.

This just seems to be one of the newest and most important uses for programs that can interpret and understand the human voice.
