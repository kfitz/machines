Effects of Misogyny

By allnighter - Posted on 2 April 2007 - 4:36pm.
Tagged:
  • class discussion
  • comments
  • misogyny
  • opinion

I realized something during our class discussion today about the effects of misogyny on the web. Specifically, it was when Prof. Fitzpatrick used the article from Salon.com to illustrate her point about how offensive flamings can affect female writers. I'd read that article before class, because I wanted to see how it would address the same problems that you find in the comments on salon.com against female columnists there. That site features just as much anti-female vitriol as any other one, and I definitely agree that comments like this:

"Is there any reason why this is acceptable from liberal Salon, but not Ann Coulter?
I just can't believe that such a terrible writer could get paid for writing this. This isn't journalism, it's just narcissistic navelgazing. I don't give a flying fuck if you, Debra Dickerson, don't care for Dan Savage's work. Nor do I care that Rebecca Traister got acne and cramps from smoking cessation. This isn't journalism. Get a fucking blog!"

are pretty heinous and innapropriate (why did the commentor - who I'm assuming is a man - even bother to bring up Rebecca Traister?) But what I realized in class today, I guess, is that these sort of comments also serve another purpose. The one that I quoted was aimed at Salon.com's Debra Dickerson. Now, if you've ever read any of her stuff there, you'll realize she sometimes says some pretty out-there stuff. She was one of the main people who was saying that Obama "wasn't black". Anyway, my point is that I rarely agreed with what she wrote, until I started reading some of the comments disagreeing with her. Now, while I've yet to write a comment defending her, I at least find myself defending her in my mind, trying to see things from her point of view and more often than not agreeing with her. So I guess what I'm trying to say is that sometimes when people are victimized on the web, it can actually give their points of view more credence. Looking at the example of Kathy Sierra, the orginal post that started all the hate-spewing was sort of a mushy call for more kindness in the technology industry. Not something that should elicit such a negative response, but it's really not that substantive or outrageous of a statement. Yet we (or at least I) found myself in accord with what she was saying. Would I have agreed so easily if she hadn't faced all the backlash she did?
PS: Please don't read this as some sort of rationalization of death threats etc, because I'd rather have the whole world disagreeing with Dickerson than for her to have to deal with one or two death threats that cause many more people to see things her way.
