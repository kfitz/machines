Life without structure?

By BasilikonTron - Posted on 17 February 2007 - 11:21am.
Tagged:
  • multi-touch

The readings this past week characterize hypertext as non-linear, devoid of a structure, lacking centrality, and linking between anything and everything.

Much of their work seems to be made in opposition to the idea of an author and his complete, linear, and structured work. However, aren't these characteristics more a part of life than just a literary tradition?

Take Pomona College for example. You could argue that everyone's schedule is like a hypertext since there really is no set path to graduation. However, the institution is such that all science courses are generally in Seaver North/South, math courses are in Milikan, theater classes are in Seaver Theater, and everyone registers at Alexander. People need organization in their lives.

I just watched the multi touch interface video, and I thought it was pretty neat, but if I had it, I would eventually revert to the old files and folders approach and start organizing my stuff. If I wanted to look at my photos, I was not going to just plop it on my computer in the manner that the speaker did.

I feel that the multi touch interface is all flair and no substance. I compare the multi-touch interface to the Nintendo Wii. The controller allows me to interact with the game in a new way, but in the end, its all about the games. The multi touch interface may be fun to play around with and touch, but in the end, I just need to get that essay done...if I can find it.

graphics and life
Submitted by twosandwichesshort on 19 February 2007 - 2:46pm.

I think the multi-touch interface is generating a lot more buzz in the graphics and audio professions than others; while the benefits of such an interface to most word processing work are slim, they are enormous in fields like video postproduction, still image manupulation, node-based programming (see Max/MSP/Jitter), and probably 3d modeling and animation. Workflows can be very personal things, granted, but I hardly think I'm alone in wanting to see this developed and implemented for some of the software packages I use.

My impression is that most hypertext theorists would assert that hypertext does have a structure, but that its structure is a nodal one, probably without any kind of "center" as this would imply privilege and thus heirarchy. I don't entirely follow your analogy to classes, but I'm pretty tired, so that could be why. I think most would agree with you in some respect that people need organization in their lives, although I might ask about the structure of that organization. Both my experience of memory and the present are largely fragmented, held together with bizarrely intertwined threads I seem to give attention to in turn and follow along something like a time axis. Linear, heirarchical organization plays a critical role in making my life run (see the always-open ICal and Omnioutliner on my desktop), but I find both of these, despite modulating a great deal of my interactions with the outside world, to be a mode of thinking unrelated to most of my experience of life. It seems to me that order isn't so much an inherent quality of life as one that we find useful to collate our experiences; because of the high degree of abstraction and the necessity of prediction in our world, you can't really survive without this ordering process but that's just because of the way our external world has been set up. If food was super easy to get, and there wasn't really any danger to avoid, then we could just drift through the fragments of our experiences without having to formalize them into constructs of an outside reality. Note also that this total retreat into subjectivity flies in the face of everything western civilization is built on which is why it may ring strange and off key at first.
