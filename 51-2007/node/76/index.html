Quick on one the reading

By twosandwichesshort - Posted on 13 February 2007 - 10:49pm.
Tagged:
  • class reading

A few thoughts on the Landow: wow; always nice to see someone take on hypertext and dig deeper than "this is really convenient" as to why it's important. On a personal note, it's also nice to get an indirect introduction to some thoughts and movements in criticism that I have been itching to learn about but haven't been exposed to really. Great point that people have been doing this in slower ways (activation energy too high for hypertext's emergent properties to show up) with footnoted scholarly articles and criticism, and then in a more centered way with postmodern fragmentation & allusion (see ulysses). What I really like was that I was able to see some bigger ideas here though; I finally understood in some fundamental way that's hard to put into words the "what you are creating" of a hypertext, or hyperfilm, or other more ergodic experience. It's like an architect creating a building, or an interior designer crafting a space; the phenomenological experience of both is a decentralized, holistic, thing; you wander through and feel the uses of space, the transitions, etc; same way with hypertext. A million possible experiences are layered upon each other, a million worlds and states of consciousness, and the art is comprehending somehow this totality without destroying it by trying to center it.. or to "heirarchize" it maybe is a better way to put it. And of course, what on earth is this medium good for; why inherent support for representing the postmodern experience of fragmentation within the viewing experience in a more total way, recognizing and refuting the constructedness of meanings by adopting a node instead of heirarchical structure. Another way to look at it is to take a book and just dump all the important contents, characters, moments of emotion, plot devices, things that make you go "hmm...", etc out onto some metaphorical drawing board and sort through them that way, instead of setting them in a particular, priveledged order. These are my thoughts on the matter at least, and now I want to make a hyperfilm, and figure out why Derrida says film is not literature because it's an "assemblage". Who's in?

Postmodernism
Submitted by BasilikonTron on 14 February 2007 - 12:36am.

It is interesting that you bring up the idea of postmodernism since it appears that the majority of these hypertext theories utilize many of its concepts like fragmentation, intertextuality, nonlinearity, etc. The "metaphorical drawing board" is a great example.

Postmodernism is somewhat of a loaded term and has some baggage like anti-morality and anti-progress. However, most of what we have been discussing involves technological progress, and I am assuming that the majority of us do not consider things like the internet immoral.

I do not want to jump to conclusions, but I think postmodernism may explain why people are skeptical towards new media. (e.g. We cite books, not websites). In adapting some aspects of postmodernism, do you think new media has also taken in some of the negative connotations? (guilt by association?)

Submitted by twosandwichesshort on 14 February 2007 - 5:12pm.

eeeh, I'm a little fuzzy on what the 'negative' connotations of postmodernism you're referring to, but that may be beacuse postmodernism is so scattered and ill-defined; everything I've ever read that attempts to deal with "postmodernism" as a unit acknowledges first and foremost that it is much more of a grouping of ideas than a cohesive and easily defined unit, and that even the groupings of ideas get scattered and diffused at the edges. So your postmodernsim may not be my postmodernism, which is the point.. eh. Anyhow, I can see how in a totally relativist, subjectivist, view of things morality, yes, is irrelevant; but it's not so much a negative I wouldn't say as an inevitable conclusion from semi-unrefutable premises; the only thing I can think about here is the whole nobrow idea? The eradication of "high" and "low" cultural distinctions, which could relate to a sort of very traditionalist puritain kind of morality maybe and scare some of those people off (Gravity's Rainbow did get turned down by the Nobel comittee I think it was for obscenity)?

Also lemme say really fast that a lot of what's new in new media that's actually 'new' is these ideas that originate in recent times (rhizomes, etc), and so tends to be postmodern in their tendencies.

I guess postmodernism, yes, is pretty scary sufff to the lay public; that I can see, but I don't think most people see new media and go "ah ha! Postmodernism! I'm hep to your nefarious games!"... I guess an interesting point you raise here is whether people understand just how subversive the hyper part of hypertext can be, the total decentraliztion and "de-heirarchization" (hence, with no objective reality, no morality, I guess, again, ok), without actually being able to articulate it. This is something similar to the Intelligent Design fiasco; part of the issue is that people too simple to hold more than one system of values are hitting the deterministic part of scientific naturalism and going "whaaaaaaat?"; you'll hear rhetoric of "hope" and some other stuff if you dig deep enough in conservative christian people's responses to all of it. They see it all as you and I might emotionally see nihilism, and not in the lebowski way either.

Good point; the world has been crazy telecommunicated and fragmented for a long time now so I would think people would have internalized these ideas by now but maybe not.
