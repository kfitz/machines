materialism and marxism

3NT, you mentioned in class that for a revolution to be considered marxist it had to be more than just class conflict; the revolution had to incorporate some sort of material basis, usually the workers against the bourgeoise. I was hoping you, or someone else, could expand on that point here.

I can see why my phrasing in class was ambiguous. I meant to argue that the putative erasure of class difference in postmodernity (no more false divides between "high" and "low" culture; equal access to the means of production of knowledge for all in the digital age; etc.) is not necessarily on par with the orthodox-Marxian promise of "class-less" society. In fact - this was what I was trying to articulate in class - I would suggest that such narratives are deeply ideological, in the pejorative, Althusserian sense of constructing an imaginary relationship of our relationship to material conditions that ends up re-inforcing the harmful, exploitative elements of those conditions. Put simply, get people to believe that, for instance, television is an outlet of class-less expression (because everyone can understand it, view it for free, etc.) and you can get away with leaving more important questions unasked: who really controls the means of production of content? How is class implicated in the creation of the actual television sitting in front of you?

In general, Marxist theory posits that the social field arising out of an inherently class-based economic system will reflect the constitutive antagonisms of that system (i.e. two or more parties whose interests fundamentally and irreconciliably diverge). In order to smoothly maintain inherently unequal relations, various institutions (again, in the Althusserian sense) arise to present to the social field as illusorily non-antagonistic or de-antagonized. Zizek argues that this is basically the role of political philosophy in modernity. Don't worry, we all get to come to the public arena to vote / make informed consumer decisions / figure out the best way to live! But who really gets to participate? And, moreover, even if everyone does participate, are the structural inequalities of bourgeois society overcome whatsoever? My point, in short, was that conceptions of "class-lessness" in postmodernity fit along this trajectory of de-antagonized visions of social life and thus bolster the operation of capital.
