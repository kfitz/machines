Liberal Arts Contradiction

By blankman - Posted on 16 September 2007 - 10:30pm.
Tagged:
  • bourgeois
  • liberal arts
  • personal cultivation
  • skills

I have often thought about the conundrum presented by the liberal arts idea of a well-rounded education. In his analysis of social structures, Habermas notes, "occupational requirements also contradicted the idea of a personal cultivation as its own end...The old contradiction continues on today in the conflict between a cultivation of the person, on the one hand, and a training that provides mere skills, on the other." (48) I believe this contradiction questions the core of the liberal arts ideal.

I certainly support the liberal arts ideology, both theoretically and financially (40k plus/year). However, I sometimes find it hard not to wonder about the rationale of spending such exorbitant amounts to educate oneself as a critical being. Ignoring the question of whether critical reasoning can be taught, the concept of non-professional education smacks of some bourgeois ideology. I still can not quite align the idea that every person would/should benefit from balanced learning with the practicality that only the wealthy (barring the influence of financial aid) are able to afford it. Within the college's student body a wide spectrum of opinion exists about how practical or apractical the optimal education should be.

I think I made my point. Opinions?

Oil and water
Submitted by BasilikonTron on 17 September 2007 - 11:01am.

Liberal arts schools do appear to have an oppositional mentality toward the state schools. One is "learning for the sake of learning" and the other is "Learning skills for a prosperous career." They are like oil and water.

Lyotard notes something similar to the Habermas quote you posted. Another post has remarked on it as well. "Knowledge is and will be produced in order to be sold, it is and will be consumed in order to be valorized in a new production: in both cases, the goal is exchange. Knowledge ceases to be an end in itself, it loses its 'use-value'".

This idea basically notes the practicality of knowledge and perhaps the "bougeoise ideology" you mention. I will coin it the "state school knowledge" as opposed to the "liberal arts knowledge." I am not saying that one cannot be cultivated at a state school. I am merely referring to some of the job-tailored majors at these schools. Examples include communications, marketing, journalism, etc.

Are they separate though? I do not think the answer is as black and white. I know a lot of people here who want to get a good job after graduating from Pomona. At the same time, I know a lot of people who are following their passions first and worrying about money later. Some of these people are already financially stable to have the freedom to do that and some people are not as well-off (the contradiction you mention). I do not think it is very clear.

Lyotard appears to bridge a relationship between the two as well. When he speaks of the legitimation of knowledge, he posits two types: narrative and scienctific. The first does not necessarily have to be true while the second must be true in order to be legitimate. He is entirely skeptical of the second and eventually approves of going beyond science's established rules. "The postmodern artist or writer is in the position of a philosopher: the text he writes, the work he produces are not in principle governed by preestablished rules, and they cannot be judged according to a determined judgment, by applying familiar categories to the text or to the work."

So perhaps the postmodern, whatever it is, is transforming the nature of knowledge so that liberal arts schools and state schools do not form a binary. Considering Lyotard believes people will fight wars over it, knowledge is definitely becoming more than just an abstract concept.
