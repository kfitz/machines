Anderson: what'd he mean by "prodrome?"

By es23 - Posted on 24 September 2007 - 9:16pm.
Tagged:
  • anderson
  • pre-conditions of postmodernism
  • prodrome
  • symtoms of a disease

In beginning to read Anderson's "The Origins of Postmodernity," I noticed something curious in how he begins to unpack the conditions (political, spatial, and intellectual) for the emergence of the Postmodern . . . he names the chapter on the preconditions "Prodromes." A prodrome, according to the Oxford, can be defined as: "A symptom or sign, or a set of symptoms or signs, that is characteristic or premonitory of the onset of a disease." (**Note: this is but one of three definitions proposed, and it may very well be that Anderson didn't mean it in this way.)

space and the dialectic

By morefuntocompute - Posted on 24 September 2007 - 1:12pm.
Tagged:
  • harvey
  • Jameson
  • Soja

First of all, Jameson's identification of two related but different working definitions for postmodernism made sense of a lot of things for me. Lyotard's use of "postmodern" as "the condition of knowledge in the most highly developed societies" is all encompassing: things in current society are postmodern because everything in this age is postmodern. The state of knowledge for all is different because of changes in our world. But as Jameson says, "I am far from feeling that all cultural production today is 'postmodern' in the broad sense I will be conferring to the term".

Jameson on Postmodernism

By CA92075 - Posted on 23 September 2007 - 11:01pm.
Tagged:
  • aesthetics
  • Postmodernism features
  • schizophrenia

I find Jameson's discussion of schizophrenia very interesting. He highlights the notions of Lacan on his emphasis of the relationship of schizophrenia as one of postmodernism's privative features. Lacan's view is that meaning is not based on a "one-to-one relationship between signifier and signified," but rather, "is generated by the movement from signifier to signifier" (26). It is when this breaks down that schizophrenia arises, however, Jameson brings up examples of how this disjointedness can function as the prime aesthetic aspect of certain works.
