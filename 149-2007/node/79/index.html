Baudrillard: Balance and Neutrality

By Bumpkins13 - Posted on 14 October 2007 - 2:02pm.
Tagged:
  • atomic bombs and such
  • Balance and Neutrality
  • Baudrillard

In "The Orbital and the Nuclear", Baudrillard introduces the concept of balance and neutralization. However, "balance and neutrality" are not balanced and neutral; they seem to be more of a conscious effort to negate the eminent threat of something more. In the context of the atomic bombs potential threat as a deterrent to its use, Baudrillard claims "deterrence itself is the neutral, implosive violence of metastable systems or systems in involution." (32) Neutrality masks the inherent instability of wielding an unbelievable amount of physical power. We are blinded into thinking we have peace when all we is a lack of action. In the words of Baudrillard, "The balance of terror is the terror of balance." (33) This statement reinforces the idea that something is on the brink of happening. Balance petrifies us into inaction which continues the unstable neutrality we live with. This sense of pacifism ultimately leads to the "neutralization and homogenization of everyone else on earth."(35) The fear becomes global; nobody lives in complete fear, yet nobody lives in complete peace.

How can one come to live sensibly in a situation such as this? Is neutrality a necessary simulacrum for the ordinary person? Does it "denature" reality?

terror
Submitted by morefuntocompute on 14 October 2007 - 11:23pm.

Reading this passage made some sense to me of offhand allusions to 'terror' in previous writers, especially in regards to totalization. I find Baudrillard really interesting because he deals so much with real-world (i guess that's kind of loaded, but whatever) examples of what he discusses (even if some of them I still don't get- like where he goes with the whole Kennedy and assassination situation). It's interesting that Baudrillard almost comes out and ironically (since it seems to be a two wrongs make a right sort of logic) suggests terrorism as a means of bringing society face to face with the actual catastrophe whose virtual impossibility the system of control thrives on. This doesn't immediately strike me as maybe the best way of doing things, but I'm not clear on at what level this intervention should occur. It seems to me to be sort of despairing and inspired by feelings of powerlesness, and I wonder if this isn't because of its outwardly focused nature. Just sort of off the cuff I would say in response to "how does one live sensibly in a situation such as this" that one should focus inward, should focus not on attempts to shatter the system of control from the outside, but to form groups of passive resistance which are bad enough for information flow and a commodified communication space that they ultimately affect a positive and dramatic reconfiguration of the overall system.

To address the question of whether "neutrality is a necessary simulacrum for the ordinary person", and if it "denatures reality", I would first offer the disclaimer that my grasp on the concept of simulacra is less than perfect. Let me try to answer in two parts. First, treating "neutrality" as a sort of world-political state of control through deterrence policy, I think the answer is not exactly. Material security makes a part of this "neutrality": in total "neutrality", which I am reading as a lack of physical conflict due to effective, centralized, control, no one really worries about the immediate threat of their property rights being violated. Instead conflict becomes more abstract: neurotic political posturing and 'cold' wars of production that function only to make the deterrent to actually engaging in warfare greater. In order to be sane, people (including myself) need some sense of security, so this makes neutrality better than open, constant, material conflict. However, addressing the importance of "neutrality" as a simulacrum, I get tripped up. For historical objects, we've read about how they can be ascribed new attributes as they are commodified and exchanged independent of their "real" identity (impact of simulacra in Harvey around p289), and thus their identity can be altered in this process. So if "neutrality" is a floating signifier, how does this matter except in that it makes it difficult to qualify what, exactly, we're talking about? It seems that it is simulacra as a category and not any specific one that makes questions of "reality" not applicable. Thoughts?

Kennedy ... !
Submitted by capt haddock on 15 October 2007 - 12:51am.

"The Kennedys died because they incarnated something: the political, political substance, whereas the new presidents are nothing but caricatures and fake film--curiously, Johnson, Nixon, Ford, all have this simian mug, the monkeys of power" (24).

Uhhh?

One of the archetypal, nearly cliche examples of media's impact on politics is the famous poll conducted after one of Nixon/Kennedy debates in the 1960 election, where TV viewers claimed Kennedy won, and radio listeners claimed Nixon won. Because Kennedy had make-up, better posture, and more confident body language. And Nixon, though simian, had more "substance." Respectively.

I'd like to hear Baudrillard sound off on that. I guess his ideas make sense in a theoretical sense based on the death-narratives-as-justification that he develops, and that I could not follow, but sometimes I think he gets his head stuck in the clouds, too distracted by how pleasurable it must feel to be able to parse semantic distinctions more minutely than everyone else, ever. Did I just say that out loud? Oops...
