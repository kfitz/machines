Digital media: Chaos in the social order?

By es23 - Posted on 14 October 2007 - 10:48pm.
Tagged:
  • Baudrillard
  • file sharing
  • music piracy

I know the leap between copying/downloading digital media content illegally and nuclear warfare is gargantuan. BUT! Suspend for a moment the difference, and consider Baudrillard's quote from page 39:

"Responsibility, control, censure, self-deterrence always grow more rapidly than the forces or the weapons at our disposal: this is the secret of the social order."

I think that in the digital media era--in which I, you, anyone, can easily partake in illegal activity regarding music piracy, file sharing, DVD/CD burning, etc.--there exists a correlation between how Baudrillard attributes the lag in development of "force or weapons" to the growth of "responsibility, control, censure, and self-deterrence."

It seems to me there is indeed a crisis in the very fact that there is virtually NO time lapse between new technologies and their dispersal in which "authorities" can develop and diffuse "control" on this topic. We--the media consumers--have been equipped with these technologies, yet the discourse for how to use them with responsibility is so new that we haven't taken heed of its warnings.

The more interesting aspect of this for me has to do with simulacra and the "real." What sort of "real" effect is illegal acquisition of digital media content having anyhow?? True, some artists complain that illegal downloading and file sharing diminishes from their profits. But other bands, as I'm sure you know, like Radiohead are toying with the "real" value of their music as they enabled consumers to pick the price of their most recent album (http://news.bbc.co.uk/2/hi/entertainment/7021743.stm)

What do you guys think about this notion? Maybe I'm way off! I just saw an interesting link and decided to run with it.

Submitted by 3NT on 15 October 2007 - 8:41am.

I don't think the gap between nuclear warfare and file-sharing is so gargantuan. Structurally speaking, both practices stem from networks of deterritorialized individuals working toward ends that remain fundamentally non-containable according to post-Enlightenment notions of ethics, governmental regulations, responsibility, etc. In any case, I think it's valuable to de-mystify nuclear warfare by situating in the contexts of real-world analogues such as file-sharing; too often, apocalyptic invocations of "nuclear war" ends up foreclosing and de-politicizing discourse in counter-productive ways.

Analogy Continued
Submitted by blankman on 15 October 2007 - 12:50pm.

I also agree that equating file-sharing and nuclear warfare is quite appropriate. As I understand it, Baudrillard basically sees the function of nuclear bombs as deterrents rather than actual weapons. File-sharing/stealing makes it quite simple to get any new CD/movie without having to pay. Yet, the music industry still profits as it bemoans this theft. Some subtle social forces must be inspiring us to pay $20+ for a DVD when it could easily be free. Actually, this is how I read Radiohead's new campaign. They are not the first artists to utilize the internet in discounting their music. Many artists put their songs up free of charge. The fact that Radiohead allows fans to pay whatever they want allows fans to reinforce the notion that music should be bought, not freely distributed.

In this section, B also states, "behind this simulacrum of fighting to the death and of ruthless global stakes, the two adversaries are fundamentally in solidarity against something else, unnamed" (37). Continuing the analogy of file-sharing, I propose that this enemy is the understanding of music as a commodity or the need for a music industry. File-stealing and 'conformist' purchasing of music both continue the cycle of music production as it exists today. Neither side seems to fight for an end to this production chain.
