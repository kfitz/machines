Going through the fantasy

By Guattari Hero - Posted on 12 November 2007 - 2:25am.
Tagged:
  • fantasy
  • zizek

Zizek writes at p.195 that 'going-through the fantasy' involves recognizing any objects of desire as embodiments of a fundamental lack (in the self, in the Other, in the symbolic order), as sociofantastic makeshifts constructed over the lack to conceal the lack. He also writes at 230-1 that

'the final stage of the psychoanalytic process [is] 'subjective destitution.' What is at stake in this 'destitution' is precisely the fact that the subject no longer presupposes himself as subject; by accomplishing this he annuls, so to speak, the effects of the act of formal conversion. In other words, he assumes not the existence but the nonexistence of the big Other; he accepts the Real in its utter, meaningless idiocy; he keeps open the gap between the Real and its symbolization. The price to be paid for this is that by the same act he also annuls himself as subject, because - and this would be Hegel's last lesson - the subject is subject only in so far as he presupposes himself as absolute through the movement of double reflection.'

My question is just how long this self-dissolution is meant to be maintained. Is the ideal analysand the one who suspends herself in such a state indefinitely, never turning her face away from the lack?

Another (related) question: What would constitute the productivity of the psychoanalytic session? An analysand 'goes through the fantasy' and can no longer conceal the lack with a certain sociofantastic embodiment (in this case we'll say that the lack is social antagonism and that the embodiment is the Jew), but doesn't the analysand just leave and find a different 'Jew'? If not the Jews, then immigrants; if not immigrants, then dogmatic theocrats; if not dogmatic theocrats, then sexual deviants; etc. The operation of lack-concealment seems fundamental, an unavoidable fact about how we deal with the trauma of the Real, and if that's the case, then psychoanalytic dissolutions of social fantasies ultimately just result (after a brief, traumatic interlude) in new, possibly improved, social fantasies. Isn't this an arbitrary kind of psychoanalytic 'progress'?

But it seems as though the only alternative to this 'progress' - other than 'informed' or cynical fantasy-construction, which isn't something Zizek seems too enthused about - is that kind of consistent self-dissolution mentioned above. I guess this doesn't seem like that radical of a limit for a theoretical system to propose, but I do wonder - alongside aha - what the social upshot of such a limit would be. Would maintaining such a state require social withdrawal? Could we imagine a social system upfront about and enthralled by its lack? One that embraced the Real in its 'utter, meaningless idiocy' rather than engaging in socio-fantastic evasions of it? And are these questions ultimately misguided? Is the entire discussion of the Real and psychosocial evasive strategies just meant to be a description of brute facts, rather than any kind of ethics?

--Guattari Hero
