Micronarratives and Background Information

Submitted by tophat1 on 22 October 2006 - 3:58pm.
  • Jenkins

In "Game Design as Narrative Architecture," Henry Jenkins talks about narrative presence, in the form of previously encountered narratives as well as micronarratives, in games. He describes certain games that involve previous knowledge. His cites "American McGee's Alice" as an example of a game derived from an older story (Lewis Carroll's "Alice in Wonderland"). The premise of the game is strange: years later Alice is trying to figure out of her experience of Wonderland was real or a hallucination. Jenkins makes the point that the gamer plays against a background of previous encounters with "storybook illustrations and Disney movies." Yet, it would be quite different to draw from the Disney movie or storybook vs. Lewis Carroll's book. The sinister feel of the game would be in much starker contrast to the Disney movie.

Bolter

Submitted by tophat1 on 16 October 2006 - 12:15pm.
  • reading suggestion

I have been reading bits and pieces of Jay David Bolter's book "Writing Space" and I've come across some interesting and helpful articles relating to many of our class discussions and readings. In his book, he writes a section on the gap between the thing represented and the represented sign and our desire to break through this gap. In a later section entitled "Perception and Semiosis," he continues the discussion by talking about the networks of signs we encounter on the computer. He believes many people attribute perception to computers, or are at least trying to create create computers with programs that display perceptual characteristics. Bolter rejects the idea that computers are in the realm of perception and states that computer literacy is semiotic (it involves systems of reading and writing signs).

Janet Murray

Submitted by tophat1 on 15 October 2006 - 3:51pm.
  • reading

At the end of her piece, "From Game-Story to Cyberdrama," Janet Murray calls for us to rethink how we approach the new genre she dubs "cyberdrama." Instead of comparing the different aspects of the cyber-drama to the standards for either games or stories, she believes we ought to think about how we might make a better cyberdrama. It occured to me that, if this is in fact possible, it may also be a good way to get out of the frustration that comes in our attempts to evaluate electronic literature and the recurring tendency to campare it with printed literature. Of course, I think Murray may go a little too far in suggesting that we cannot accept any comparisons of a new genre with other genres it may have as its foundation. In thinking about new forms of story-telling it may be wise to heed her word to evaluate and perpetuate them on their own merits yet it may also be that we can't help but compare them to their predecessors.

an article i came across...

Submitted by tophat1 on 10 October 2006 - 10:04pm.
  • reading

I recently came across an article on Jstor called "Representing and Interpreting Literature by Computer" by David D Miall. In his opening discussion of how online literature may change the reader's experience of literature, he states: "...it is perhaps imprudent to speculate about changes in such a fundamental human activity as reading when we know so little about how we have accomodated to conventional printed materials over the last several hundred years." Miall goes on to discuss how the reading activities that take place with a book are not yet readily available on the computer.
His comments indirectly stimulated a line of thought about the history of the printed book and how this long tradition will hold up as it faces online literature.

Interaction

Submitted by tophat1 on 8 October 2006 - 8:53pm.
  • interaction

As I read through the class blog, I see a lot about interaction including interaction between reader and writer, fellow bloggers, and even people looking for a date online. Also, in our reading there is the perpetual theme of book vs. electronic literature and how our interactions differ from medium to medium. As much as we are able to "interact" with the stories we read in the hypertexts, for example, there is an important physicality that is missing. I feel less connected with literature I read off the computer screen, perhaps because it seems like a barrier.
For those of you who have had the pleasure of taking the GRE recently, you'll know that the reading comprehension sections are now on the computer. After studying for it and taking it this summer, I was glad to be finished with the severe annoyance of reading and trying to fully comprehend something off of a computer screen where you can't underline words or sentences you like or find important. You are even restricted in the angle at which you look at the text (because it's on a computer screen).

images and texts

Submitted by tophat1 on 6 October 2006 - 3:24pm.
  • Hayles

In her book, Hayles introduces the interesting concept of including images in thinking about texts. In speaking about electronic textuality, she says that "we can no longer afford to ignore the material basis of literary production." (p19). She mentions WJT Mitchell who believes that literary studies should expand to include things such as text image. If we think about the history of the idea of image and text, what comes to mind is particularly, for me, poetry and the arrangements of words to create or support meaning. Many poems would not be the same if they were written and read in the form of a novel.

Joyce

Submitted by tophat1 on 24 September 2006 - 9:50pm.
  • more on editing

In "Othemindedness" and elsewhere, I have also been thinking about Google and other search engines that guide our experience on the internet. At least for someone like me, I rely on search engines to find the "best" and "most relevant" information on the web. Yet, as Joyce points out, this both helps us filter through information and "insists upon a hierarchy of information" (54). When you enter a keyword on one of the major search engines, it ranks information for you. To search all of the sites offered would take forever, so you trust in Google (or Yahoo, etc). I think Joyce is right in saying that as such editing tools expand, we will need to create a more "human" community to be sure that the tools remain tools, and do not dictate to us what to read and what not to read.
