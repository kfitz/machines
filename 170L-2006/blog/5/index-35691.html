What Does Armstrong Want?

Submitted by tophat1 on 17 September 2006 - 3:02pm.
  • readings

At the end of her piece, "How Novels Think," Armstrong claims that "the novel of course was not made to think beyond the individual, but neither, on the other hand, was it made to reproduce the status quo." In the previous sections, she outlines the history of the novel and it's tension between individual and "other." In her description of late nineteenth-century novels she refers to a gruesome scene from "She" (which I have not read) in which the hero stumbles across a pit of skeletons piled atop one another and described in painful detail. Armstrong seems to take issue with these descriptions because they portray the destruction of the individual. She believes the pile of bodies represents a sort of collective consuming the individual.

Technology and the Future

Submitted by tophat1 on 12 September 2006 - 9:53pm.
  • blogs

When we split up in class on Monday, my partner and I touched on the subject of technology's history and future. Sometime, I believe, in the 1800's someone claimed that everything had been invented. Of course, in today's world, where new ideas and inventions are constantly emerging, we think this line is ridiculous. I would like to propose the following question: Although we may not reach a point at which all technology ceases to advance, is there a point at which it ceases to improve, or at least improve with respect to our quality of life? This question is relevant in other fields of technology as well.

With the advent of increased technology, are we on the verge of phasing ourselves out of once inherently human activities? In "Remediation," Bolter and Grusin discuss the possibility that , instead of actors, we may watch animations that are so refined that they look like "the real thing." the blogging industry

Submitted by tophat1 on 9 September 2006 - 1:41pm.
  • blogs

I recently opened up a popular women's magazine and found a story about Jessica Coen, a "bored stiff" executive assistant turned blogger for gawker.com. After being accepted to Columbia journalism school, she was offered a job by Nick Denton, the founder of Gawker.com. She accepted the offer and now, at age 26, is the editor of the blog.
Call me old-fashioned, but my initial impression was surprise. She turned down Columbia for a blog?? She said she considered the blog as a "short-cut" to her end goal of being a published writer. I don't know quite what to make of her short-cut. It again brings into question the legitimacy of written work on the net. Additionally, I may question the relative job security of editor at gawker.com vs. graduate of Columbia journalism school. Who knows, maybe she made the right choice...

Amateur Hour

Submitted by tophat1 on 5 September 2006 - 9:16pm.
  • blog ideas

In my first search through the millions of blogs "out there," I realize that the "out there" concept might be an interesting one in itself. As we read in class, blogging is native to the web and so maybe it has some of the seemingly infinite qualities of the Internet. When we log online, we are entering a space in which we can share content and enter into discussions which we would not enter into in real life. Pop psychology suggests that people are more likely to open up to one another in darkness, where there is no eye contact. Perhaps this is the high-tech equivalent?
With this in mind, I started searching for sites that involved offbeat, interesting subjects. I mainly searched through discussions of the paranormal (from crop circles to bigfoot-and some fairly vicious skeptic blogs). One example is thedebrisfield.blogspot.com but there are many others.
