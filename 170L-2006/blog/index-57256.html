talk to the blog

Submitted by thisismycheese on 19 September 2006 - 9:09pm.
  • readings
  • technology

So, in section 3 of the Bush reading, he asks, "...will the author of the future cease writing by hand or typewriter and talk directly to the record?"

When I first read that, I said, "haha," and then remembered that the technology does in fact exist. I could buy some software and voila! I could talk to my computer and it would do the typing for me.

...right. I would never, ever do that. It would hurt my brain.

Do any of you have experience with this software? I know of one blogger who uses it, but it seems like it's uncommon. Obviously it's not perfect software, which is part of the problem (after all, if you're spending time correcting errors, you might as well just type)--but I wonder if there's a deeper issue with the idea of speaking writing instead of writing it. Ong talked about how literacy has shaped the way we think, but at least personally, I can't think unless I write, at least not as well or as articulately (not that I'm articulate here). I'm attached to the physical process of writing, to actually feeling words form, and speaking and then seeing the words appear on the screen doesn't quite cut it.

Class Credit and Blog Identity

Submitted by Natwwal on 18 September 2006 - 3:22pm.
  • blogs
  • identity

I've blogged a little in the past, and I'm finding it a very different experience to blog for class. I think my sense of disorientation comes from three things:

1. This class blog has a very set readership. There is always the possibility that a random person will stumble on our blog, but in all liklihood, people from our class are the only ones who will read a given post. Similarly, people in our class can't just stop reading the blog if they get bored. The set audience makes me far more self-concious about what I say.

2. Everyone who reads this blog has at least some knowledge of me in real life. As a result, it isn't possible for this blog to become my identity to the blog's readership. Readers will certainly filter my posts through what they know of me in real life, and something I post on this blog may influence the way people perceive me or my comments in class.

Causation in Armstrong's Hypothesis

Submitted by Gunslinger1818 on 18 September 2006 - 11:34am.
  • Armstrong

Tophat1, Oz, and Magoo have already had a discussion concerning the nature of Armstrong's thesis and the relationship between the novel and realism. I, however, was much more bothered by Armstrong's notion later on in that paragraph about the modern subject: "Once formulated in fiction, however, this subject proved uniquely capable of reproducing itself not only in authors but also in readers, in other novels, and across British culture in law, medicine, moral and political philosophy..." and then "To produce an individual, novels had to think as if there already were one, that such an individual was not only the narrating subject and source of writing but also the object of narration and referent of writing" (3). What if there already was such thing as an individual? Someone please correct me if I'm missing the point, but I feel that there is a problem with causation here. I read the whole thing and can find nowhere in which Armstrong shows that the concept of individualism was not simply a manifestation of society that the novel has mirrored.

SOS - Please advise

Submitted by magoo on 18 September 2006 - 11:17am.
  • Beowulf
  • Dickinson
  • visual poetry

I'm in early stages of preparing an online edition of Emily Dickinson's Fascicles. (The Fascicles were the little booklets in which Dickinson bound much of her verse).

I wonder how you would prefer to see such a document. Here are the problems.

  • Her nonstandard punctuation changes the meaning of her text.
  • Even her handwriting is critical in these changes
  • Her handwriting is very hard for most people to read.
  • People have tried to transcribe the handwriting in such a way that the visual qualities of the punctuation are preserved. No one has come close.
