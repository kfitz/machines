Another funky book

Submitted by Pimm on 9 October 2006 - 11:05pm.
  • other books

At the end of class today we discussed whether or not the form of Hayles's book somewhat refutes her own claims because it basically forces certain mediums--like the imitations of internet links--into her text. It made me think of a book I read for my African American science fiction (or Afrofuturisms) class that I actually mentioned in my last post. The book, Rhythm Science by musician, turntablist, producer and writer Paul D Miller, aka Dj Spooky that Subliminal Kid, has the image of a record on the front and a hole through the center of the record and the enter book. The hole actually serves as purpose, because it holds a CD that's included at the back of the book. As you might be able to tell from the link, the book has an odd array of images, colors, lines, and words scattered throughout. (Including a repeating image of the playboy bunny; Professor Thomas said she couldn't figure why he included that--) It wasn't a big surprise for me to learn that the book, like Writing Machines, was published by MIT Press. It even has an online version ("WebTake"), and the link to it is right above the link to Writing Machines.

Larsen's navigational elements

Submitted by magoo on 9 October 2006 - 8:21pm.
  • hypertext fiction
  • Larsen
  • website design

I'm interested to read how much success Larsen had with the class despite some complaints about the "writing itself" not being all that one might desire. I wonder whether the clarity and discreet differentiation created by the navigational scheme might not relate in some way to the disappointment some felt with the writing.

One hears that writers like Shakespeare are admirable in part because they don't moralize. In some ways, Larsen's explicit links and navigational elements function like -- well, not quite morals, but certainly labels of a sort. At the same time, they certainly do provide a sense of intentional connection between lexia that seems popular -- I think because it allows readers to consider the lexia together.

Larsen's Electrons

Submitted by Frabby on 9 October 2006 - 6:47pm.
  • hypertext poetry

Whatever you thought of "Disappearing Rain," I have to say that Larsen's poem "Firefly" is cool. I don't know how else to put it. I enjoyed reading this poem. The lines are layered, meaning that the lines of each stanza can change up to six times. Thus the stanza can be read in lots of different ways, yet they all make sense. It's pretty short, so I suggest you check it out.
Like "Rain" it wasn't difficult to understand, but the format was aesthetically pleasing and really clean. The poem's lines could also actually be analyzed and discussed in an interesting way, it leaves more to interpretation.

"Writing Machines", literary criticism, academia

Submitted by Lulu on 9 October 2006 - 3:45pm.
  • literary criticism

When I was reading Hayles's "Writing Machines," I couldn't help but feel skeptical about...well, to be brutally honest, the entire point. I actually agreed with and was sympathetic to a lot of Hayles' points about media ecology, the bathtub theory of literature, and the continual evolution and enhancement of books alongside the progression of electronic literature. In fact, I really loved when she said "as we work toward critical practices and theories appropriate for electronic literature, we may come to renewed appreciation for the specificity of print." (33) However, despite all these great points that I could sympathize with, I was also skeptical about a lot of things, and in particular her analysis of electronic literature like "Lexia to Perplexia." I'm not critical as to the actual content or analysis. Instead, I guess my discomfort stems from a lack of clarity as to who the audience is, and the actual value of literary criticism. So, I guess this entry isn't just a straightforward critique of an assigned reading. It's more a pondering of why I am reading what I'm reading and the usefulness of what I'm learning.

Avatars (Again)

Submitted by thenewblack on 9 October 2006 - 1:47am.
  • Hayles
  • old books

There's been a fair amount of discussion of the idea of "avatars" or created personas, mostly in reference to Hayles' Writing Machines. I think it's interesting in that it makes Hayles' critical process into a narrative, with the critic (and the authors) as characters as opposed to disembodied voices, which I would say is the more typical model. Beyond making the book a lot more readable, I also think that this decision represents literary criticism as a personal process, not a purely rhetorical one.

It is interesting given the personal tone that this decision gives the criticism that Hayles decides to insulate herself from it by working through a persona that she insists is not purely autobiographical. She mentions in her explanation at the beginning that she is repelled somewhat by "self-display," which seems to me to stem somewhat from an idea that the critic should not speak with a personal voice, a convention that I've encountered at Pomona on many occasions. Perhaps Hayles' refusal to engage in overt autobiographical narration is a recognition of the fact that even in such writing there is a degree of mediation owing to the fact that such works are written reflectively, and therefore represent the persona or image of the writer that they have developed in their own memories. An assumption that the image of oneself is not to a degree a created persona, doesn't seem sustainable, so perhaps it is fitting that Hayles doesn't try to maintain that illusion.
