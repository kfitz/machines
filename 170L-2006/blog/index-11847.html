This is what Landow says is possible with a hypertext, and it's hard to disagree with him after trying to read one. But are hypertexts and multiple paths a good thing? Marmalade began a discussion on how hypertexts "decenter" the author, and I think this is a fascinating topic to pursue. As Landow says, hypertexts are "infinitely recenterable" (36) and create "truly active" readers because of their interaction with the text, but in what ways does a hypertext take away the readers' ability to interpret on their own (through providing links to certain explanations, etc.)? And, flipping the coin, hypertexts also seem to give up some of the writers' authority to the reader. It all seems like an odd mixing of losing and gaining control.

Not exactly â€œchoose-your-own-adventureâ€--

Submitted by Pimm on 25 September 2006 - 12:35am.
  • electronic literature
  • Michael Joyce
  • readings

It's amazing how strong--and similar--many of the responses to Joyce's "Afternoon" have been. Like shock and awe and many others from our class have mentioned, I was frustrated with the format of "Afternoon." However, the hypertext was also incredibly intriguing, and it was definitely worth "reading" in order to get first-hand experience with hypertexts.

At first I went through "Afternoon" the same way I used to read the "choose-your-own-adventure" books that I read when I was little (and that Joyce references in his article, as well as Lulu on the blog). I soon realized how different the two are. You can't completely reread, as I used to do with adventure books when I chose a certain path for the heroine but was immediately disappointed with my decision and eager to see option B. I found that with "Afternoon," every time I chose "yes" then clicked back and chose "no", I ended up with the same piece of story on the next screen. I assume that I hadn't reached a part where I had the power to make the decision "yes" or "no" that actually mattered, but it was still interesting to see how active I actually could be as reader and semi-writer (which is, as both Joyce and Landow seem to say, one of the main purposes of the text).

Reflections on Reading

Submitted by silversprung on 24 September 2006 - 10:23pm.
  • electronic literature
  • readings
  • technology

I suppose it is a testament to my underlying control-freak tendencies that as I read "Afternoon," I found myself wanting to explore all the options that were possible from whatever screen I was on-- I didn't want to miss anything. I kept retracing my steps, going backwards in the story in order to go forwards in a different direction. Except that there really is no backwards or forwards in "Afternoon," which laughs loudly in poor little linearity's face and then steals its lunch money.

Eventually, my reading experience became a quest for the new: I began to "surf" the story, looking for new branches I hadn't seen before. Reading "Afternoon" felt kind of addictive in this way, because I didn't want to stop reading the story while I was still discovering new parts of it. I guess this impulse to "finish" the text has a lot to do with the way printed class reading is assigned: one reads the whole selection, and then one has finished the assignment. But with "Afternoon," the challenge is not finishing the reading, but actually unearthing the whole text and making sense of it. In fact, Joyce deliberately denies closure to the reader--even the meta-screens that comment on the reader's unending, evolving experience consciously refuse to end the story. It was engaging, but also somewhat frustrating.

Joyce

Submitted by tophat1 on 24 September 2006 - 9:50pm.
  • more on editing

In "Othemindedness" and elsewhere, I have also been thinking about Google and other search engines that guide our experience on the internet. At least for someone like me, I rely on search engines to find the "best" and "most relevant" information on the web. Yet, as Joyce points out, this both helps us filter through information and "insists upon a hierarchy of information" (54). When you enter a keyword on one of the major search engines, it ranks information for you. To search all of the sites offered would take forever, so you trust in Google (or Yahoo, etc). I think Joyce is right in saying that as such editing tools expand, we will need to create a more "human" community to be sure that the tools remain tools, and do not dictate to us what to read and what not to read.

A small thought

Submitted by thenewblack on 24 September 2006 - 8:33pm.
  • books
  • Classes
  • Michael Joyce

As I read through Joyce's "Othermindedness" or rather the selection thereof, I'm intrigued by the connections that he makes between the filtration systems of the internet (Google, "metasites," etc.) and the filtrations that occur in the "print culture." He writes, "Almost invisibly in the past, for instance, most library patrons read much more of the online or card catalog entries, book spines, or tables of contents than they read from the volumes themselves. People have only so much time. They can't read everything and so they depend on others to link them to what they need or wish to read" (54). It seems then that according to Joyce, mediation has always been a factor in the way that we receive information, but this mediation has never been so overt as in the case of the internet.

The bottom line of any literature, be it hypertext or a papyrus scroll, is: is the writing good? Joyce is a good writer, which is what makes Afternoon impossible to dismiss.

some thoughts on Joyce

Submitted by Lulu on 24 September 2006 - 3:23pm.
  • reading response

Reading Michael Joyce's "Afternoon: A Story" was confusing, to say the least. I was very anxious while reading it, and there was a point at which I kept going in circles. I tried going back and changing my choices, but I'd end up on the same several pages again and again...it took me a while to backtrack enough so that I could end up somewhere new. If there's no coherent plot, does that make this "story" still a story? I understand Joyce's point that we should participate in the creation of a text and give our own meaning to a story, but if that detracts from our comprehension of the story itself, then is the exercise futile? I tend to agree with thisismycheese's point that Joyce seems to be using the technology for the sake of it, rather than because his particular "story" is well-suited for this medium.
