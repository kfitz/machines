A detective story in Larsen's "Disappearing Rain"

Submitted by Lulu on 7 October 2006 - 5:41pm.
  • readings

Like Natwwal, I also really enjoyed Larsen's "Disappearing Rain." I agree with Natwwal that the story is well-suited to the hypertext medium. Just like Amy in the story, I was also searching for the answer. I felt like the process that I was going through to reach an answer mirrored Amy's journey to find her sister. I clicked on different links, hoping that each new one would lead me closer to an answer, and even if there was a disconnect between one link and the next, it was still hopeful because it felt like I was piecing together a puzzle.

At one point Sophie mentions a "modern electronic trap" and the narrator mentions how Amy needed to "systematically go to each URL on the list" as a means of getting closer to finding an answer. As I was clicking on this link and that, I felt that I WAS Amy. As much as she was trying to find her sister, I was also trying to find Anna. This hypertext was the perfect example of how the medium enhanced the content.

Understanding embodiment

Submitted by Pimm on 7 October 2006 - 1:57pm.
  • Hayles
  • readings

Let me begin by saying what a relief it was to read "Writing Machines." I had read a bit of Hayles for an AfroFuturisms class I took with Professor Val Thomas on African American Science Fiction. For that class, I grappled with Hayles's definition of posthumanism, something I think I'll never fully understand because it is too abstract and theorized for my brain to process. "Writing Machines" was much easier to understand. Sure, as silversprung pointed out, there are passages that I didn't comprehend, but I think the way in which the book was written really helped my understanding. Unlike thisismycheese, I enjoyed the design. True, I didn't always understand why the magnified portions were magnified, but they didn't hurt of reading, and I liked the break in monotony. Also, one of my favorite parts was the portions of autobiography. I still don't completely understand if "Kaye's" life and thoughts differ at all from Katherine's. (As Katherine says on page 10, Kaye shares autobiographical elements from her life, but they are not the same person. But Hayles also says in her acknowledgements that "inventing a persona" allowed her to "work through the barriers I had thrown up around the idea of autobiography so I could finally see it" (142), so I assume that Kaye is Kate, just transformed into words on a page). It is through these stories that I came to an understanding, with Kaye, that hypertext is not necessarily on a electronic text, and that "books are more than encoded voices; they are physical artifacts whose material properties offer potent resources for creating meaning" (107). Kaye's stories and other ones that Hayles's inserts, such as Danielewski's relationship and story to his father, made the book much more enjoyable.

Reflections on Larsen

Submitted by Natwwal on 7 October 2006 - 12:08pm.
  • Larsen

Just when I was beginning to fear that I would never understand the appeal of hypertexts, I got to the Larsen reading. In short, I loved it.

I think that Larsen's subject matter lends itself very well to the hypertext medium. The fragmented nature of the narrative is very fitting for a story about a family history. Real-life family histories are rarely told in linear form. The younger generation has to piece together bits of stories and memories from a variety of different sources to gain a sense of what happened. A sense of incompleteness or lack of closure to such stories is the norm.

The first example is the computer itself. Hayles writes: "In their general form, computers are simulation machines producting environments, from objects that sit on desktops to networks spanning the globe. To construct an environment is, of course, to anticipate and structure the user's interaction with it and in this sense to construct the user as well as the interface" (48). This makes a lot of sense to me. Perspectives on the future of the writing

Submitted by Shock and Awe on 6 October 2006 - 5:10pm.
  • hypertext fiction
  • possibilities of writing

Thinking about the future of literature while reading posts on hypertexts, I was reminded of a passage from Helen Dewitt's novel The Last Samurai.

"It seemed to me...that the writers of the future would...approach the level of the other branches of the arts which are so much further developed. Perhaps a writer would think of the monosyllables and lack of grammatical inflection in Chinese, and how this would sound next to lovely long Finnish words all double letters & long vowels in 14 cases or lovely Hungarian all prefixes suffixes" (63-64)

In her view words from languages are each like different colors of paint which could be mixed together and composed to create something more beautiful than anything written before. Clearly, reading/writing such a text would take a great deal of learning and isn't realistic for any kind of mass audience, but it's still a beautiful idea. I suppose I was reminded of it because of how these hypertext authors view literature as such a malleable thing, while I have always thought of it as novel, poetry, drama, etc--the written word, on paper, complete with some sort of narrative.

Hypertexting the classics

Submitted by thenewblack on 6 October 2006 - 4:21pm.
  • hypertext
  • Landow
  • Old Media

A week or so ago, I was searching for an online version of Alexander Pope's "The Rape of the Lock" for my Enlightenment Literature class (death to overpriced anthologies!), when I came across a hypertext version
(http://www-unix.oit.umass.edu/~sconstan) of the poem. While not a very extensively annotated version of the text, it provides in a form sort of like a wiki links to explanations of language in the text and allusions, as well as visual representations of some of the scenes in the poem.

I was reminded of Landow's idea that hypertext has been latent in literature for a long time owing to such things as allusions. Not only does this online edition of Pope's work utilize the capabilities of its medium to make annotation easier, but it also effectively makes Pope's poem a multimedia, visual experience. In a way, it literalizes the associations that the reader draws in the course of reading.

images and texts

Submitted by tophat1 on 6 October 2006 - 3:24pm.
  • Hayles

In her book, Hayles introduces the interesting concept of including images in thinking about texts. In speaking about electronic textuality, she says that "we can no longer afford to ignore the material basis of literary production." (p19). She mentions WJT Mitchell who believes that literary studies should expand to include things such as text image. If we think about the history of the idea of image and text, what comes to mind is particularly, for me, poetry and the arrangements of words to create or support meaning. Many poems would not be the same if they were written and read in the form of a novel.
