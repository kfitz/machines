Virtual presence means being present at virtual locations. In particular, the term virtual presence denotes presence on World Wide Web locations pages and Web sites which are identified by URLs. People who are browsing a Web site are considered to be virtually present at Web locations. Virtual presence is a social software in the sense that people meet on the Web by chance or intentionally. The ubiqitous [sic] (in the Web space) communication transfers behavior patterns from the real world and Virtual worlds to the Web.

Another collaborative authorship project courtesy of wiki software

Submitted by Oz on 13 November 2006 - 8:28pm.
  • collective memory
  • wiki software

I came across a wiki-type site called Memory Archive, the encyclopedia of memories. The stated idea here is that: all the people, like you, who are alive today and who bore witness to the significant events of our age, will pass on. Our collective memory -- yours, mine, all of ours -- will go into the great beyond with us, vanishing forever. Over the coming decade, century, and millenium [sic], your experiences will slowly dissappear [sic]. The past, they say, is a foreign country -- distant, strange, often unknowable. Unless you have a map. Your memoirs, recorded and shared at MemoryArchive, are that map for future generations. Go ahead. Tell the future how it was to be alive now--You've got a story. Make it history. So essentially, this is a wiki software gone memory aggregator: people add "memoirs" about "the significant events of our age," and these memoirs are broken up into categories. For example, I could read all the collected memories about Barack Obama, about the year 1978, about the assassination of JFK, about bra stuffing.

Schemas that talk

Submitted by Oz on 30 October 2006 - 12:29pm.
  • categories
  • Douglas & Hargadon
  • schemas

I am writing a thesis on Henry James. At the beginning of this semester, I was talking with one of my thesis readers, and she recommended that, during this year, I try to be thinking about James with every paper I write. She said this, and I felt myself do the thing -- wide eyes and two or three quick head nods -- that means interest and assent, all the while thinking, "Is she nuts? I wouldn't even know how to try, and anyway, thinking that much about James would be deadly." The weird thing, as it turns out, is that I didn't have to try. Looking back on what I've written so far this semester, everything -- be it on a poem by Lewis Carroll, William Blake, a syllogistic proof, or this blog -- has been processing some Jamesian thematic or another. Or maybe I've just learned to see it that way.

This is partly to do with why I've been thinking, lately, about Douglas and Hargadon's discussion of schema theory. Not only do these schematics help me to think about the way my own mind is working, but they also apply to a whole lot of what we seem to be trying to process in this class and on this blog.
