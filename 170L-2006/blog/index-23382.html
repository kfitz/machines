Language, babble, and Ong

Submitted by Shock and Awe on 11 September 2006 - 11:38am.
  • language
  • Ong

Like Bird, I was struck by a sense of wonder reading Ong. He made me reevaluate writing. I became fascinated by the possibilities of what blogs and the internet will do to language. He traces how symbols and pictures were simplified and turned into written languages. In some cases more than others. In 1716 Chinese had 40.545 characters (86)and "to become significantly learned in the Chinese writing system normally takes some 20 years" (86). Wow! How different this is from English.

So I asked a friend how Chinese computers work. He told me the keyboard keys represent sounds, you type the sound that the word you want begins with and a list of the most common words with that sound pop up onscreen. You choose the word you were looking for. This just seems like such a different experience of interacting with writing and a computer than what we English speakers have. I have trouble even conceptualizing it.

Perfectionists beware

Submitted by silversprung on 11 September 2006 - 10:44am.
  • the nature of the blog

I've decided, as a recovering perfectionist, that blogging is a medium that is ill-suited to people with perfectionist tendencies. I was just reading over my last post, and I found myself wanting to go back and change things about it-- add adjectives, elaborate further on a point, etc. And I have this impulse even though I previewed (and changed) the post 4 or 5 times before I actually posted it. I know that I could still change it, but I think Professor Fitzpatrick can see the changes we make, and it would be embarrassing to have her see that an entire edit consisted of adding one unnecessary phrase to a post. So I restrain myself from additional minor editing. (Except now I've outed myself.) I guess perfection is the price of immediacy, and of the rush of instantaneous publishing.

three thoughts on ong

Submitted by thisismycheese on 11 September 2006 - 8:41am.
  • blogs
  • readings
  • the nature of the blog

1. So, it seems to me that blogs (and online communication in general) sort of find a place between (and share the benefits of) speech and the written word.

Obviously, blogs are written, and therefore they have the benefits of longevity, archival and so on that print has. But the complaints (mostly Greek...those whiners) Ong cites are addressed at least somewhat by the format of blogs. Blogs are much more like a conversation or interpersonal interaction than a book, because they allow for the exchange of ideas, the discourse, that Plato & co. lamented, and because of their sense of (I'm scared to use this word now) immediacy. Entries are written and then posted, and often within a matter of minutes a conversation has started. There isn't some high-and-mighty inaccessible Author--the author is waiting for you to respond. You get the benefits of the written word (it's slower-paced, you can go over an argument before you post it etc.) with a lot fewer drawbacks.

Remarkable

Submitted by a bird on 10 September 2006 - 11:56pm.
  • Ong

"The Nigerian novelist Chinua Achebe describes how in an Ibo village the one man who knew how to read hoarded in his house every bit of printed material that came his way - newspapers, cartons, receipts (Achebe 1961, pp. 120-1). It all seemed too remarkable to throw away."
Ong, p. 92

Did this passage strike anyone else the same way it did me? I read these two lines over and over again, picturing this man who revered the written word as something incredible, and I thought to myself, "Have I been taking this all for granted?"
I'm an English major, like most of you, and of course I love books and I love to write. But have I been missing out on what this man is experiencing in his true appreciation for text? I mean, I know he was just collecting milk cartons and things, but to him they were beautiful, because they immortalized a living word.

Ong and Blogs

Submitted by Pimm on 10 September 2006 - 9:37pm.
  • readings

Continuing a conversation that silversprung began, I think it's interesting and worth pursuing how blogs relate to Ong's article. Ong writes that writing "establishes what has been called 'context-free' language" (77) because it is "detached from the author." It makes me wonder how detached bloggers are from their blogs and from their readers. With many (or most) blogs, of course, this detachment is present to a certain extent; readers often don't know who the blogger is, no matter how personal his or her blogs are. On the other hand, though, there are blogs in which readers do know the blogger, and no matter how artificial or detached writing is as a technology, the writer/reader relationship of blogs makes them more "active" and interactive than the writing that, for instance, Plato criticized. This makes me question what happens when the reader knows the blogger, like in this class, or when a friend keeps a livejournal. After all, the writing in blogs can be critiqued through posting comments, and with the addition of perhaps reading the blog of someone you know, how "context-free" is blog writing?

McLuhan & the (Un)Holy Tube

Submitted by magoo on 10 September 2006 - 9:06pm.
  • McLuhan
  • net ownership
  • readings

Class responses to McLuhan got me reminescing about the infatuation with broadcast media, particularly with TV, in the middle 20th century.

The hype for radio in the early '20's and even TV in the late 40's shows that some people entertained similar hopes that these media would democratize information. I wonder to what extent centralized ownership; profit-driven, MOR, least-common-denominator programming; softcore news and so forth are inherent results of broadcast per se, and to what extent these are results of the largely successful attempt by a few corporations to control production and distribution.
