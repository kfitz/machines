the joys of tabs

Submitted by thisismycheese on 27 September 2006 - 12:44pm.
  • technology

Using Internet Explorer in this class has reminded me of a fairly recent Big Thing in browsing--tabs!

Oh, how I love tabs. I find it hard to work without them, as evidenced by the 589823953 windows I currently have open.

But it got me thinking about what some of the readings for this week talked about--namely, the complaints that if you're on LotsOfLinks.com, you have to keep hitting the "back" button after you've visited each link to get back to the original list of links.

Which tabs fix, really, since you just control-click the link and it opens it in a tab, and you can load tons of tabs at the same time, and it's amazing and great. Clearly Somebody Up There agreed with Joyce that having to go back and forth was really inefficient.

I want my table of contents back!

Submitted by thisismycheese on 24 September 2006 - 11:29am.
  • hypertext fiction

My experience reading "Afternoon" has been incredibly frustrating, and as a result, almost enlightening--as a lot of other people have mentioned, it's made me realize how attached I am to the physical book. I'm also reacting, I think, to how basic (read: limited) the interface is.

The lack of a table of contents that lists every single page, for example, gives me a headache. When I'm reading a book, I earmark pages I want to find again later, or write down page numbers, etc., while in "Afternoon" there isn't really a way to do that (unless there is and I've just missed it). Similarly, I hate having to backtrack through all the pages I've been to in order to get back to where I started. (Maybe we're not supposed to get back to where we started.)

talk to the blog

Submitted by thisismycheese on 19 September 2006 - 9:09pm.
  • readings
  • technology

So, in section 3 of the Bush reading, he asks, "...will the author of the future cease writing by hand or typewriter and talk directly to the record?"

When I first read that, I said, "haha," and then remembered that the technology does in fact exist. I could buy some software and voila! I could talk to my computer and it would do the typing for me.

...right. I would never, ever do that. It would hurt my brain.

Do any of you have experience with this software? I know of one blogger who uses it, but it seems like it's uncommon. Obviously it's not perfect software, which is part of the problem (after all, if you're spending time correcting errors, you might as well just type)--but I wonder if there's a deeper issue with the idea of speaking writing instead of writing it. Ong talked about how literacy has shaped the way we think, but at least personally, I can't think unless I write, at least not as well or as articulately (not that I'm articulate here). I'm attached to the physical process of writing, to actually feeling words form, and speaking and then seeing the words appear on the screen doesn't quite cut it.

the blog as novel

Submitted by thisismycheese on 16 September 2006 - 3:47pm.
  • the nature of the blog

A lot of issues seem to surround the idea of the fictional blog (see Kaycee Nicole)--something that strikes me as really strange, considering that most personal blogging is just storytelling.

I started wondering: What are people's expectations when they read blogs? Is it really voyeurism in the vein of the webcam, where we want to see Exactly What's Happening To John As It Happens?

Well--no. If that's what we were looking for, we would probably just watch a webcame (or buy a telescope...)

Blogs aren't immediate in that way. People write drafts on paper or on Microsoft Word. Everything is time-stamped, sure, but I know I'm not the only blogger who's stewed over an entry for days or even weeks before posting it (or, as pseudoanonymity mentioned in class, plenty of people backdate entries for an array of reasons). Lots of people use pseudonyms and/or fictionalize events, conversations, etc.--there isn't a blog in existence that records life exactly as it happens. Bloggers omit events and anecdotes that aren't relevant to their audience and leave out details that don't contribute to the "plot"--and most blogs, especially the popular ones, do have a clear story, some kind of through-line that keeps us reading.

three thoughts on ong

Submitted by thisismycheese on 11 September 2006 - 8:41am.
  • blogs
  • readings
  • the nature of the blog

1. So, it seems to me that blogs (and online communication in general) sort of find a place between (and share the benefits of) speech and the written word.

Obviously, blogs are written, and therefore they have the benefits of longevity, archival and so on that print has. But the complaints (mostly Greek...those whiners) Ong cites are addressed at least somewhat by the format of blogs. Blogs are much more like a conversation or interpersonal interaction than a book, because they allow for the exchange of ideas, the discourse, that Plato & co. lamented, and because of their sense of (I'm scared to use this word now) immediacy. Entries are written and then posted, and often within a matter of minutes a conversation has started. There isn't some high-and-mighty inaccessible Author--the author is waiting for you to respond. You get the benefits of the written word (it's slower-paced, you can go over an argument before you post it etc.) with a lot fewer drawbacks.
