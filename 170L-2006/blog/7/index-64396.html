Lexia

Submitted by marmalade on 3 October 2006 - 1:52pm.
  • readings

So I started replying to maggo's post about Talan Memmott, and then I began to digress enough that I figured some of this deserved it's own post.

So here goes:

Memmott's style reminded me a bit somehow of Bertolt Brecht--you know, breaking the fourth wall, trying actively to destroy any preconceptions of what a drama (in this case, a story) is, using what Brecht called (pardon my rough English translation) the "alien effect"...

If I remember all the crazy, German-theater theory I read last semester, basically the "alien effect" means alienating the audience, constantly reminding them that they aren't seeing/reading about real characters/events, rather they are actually in a theater, watching actors. One does this by having characters run through the audience, eliminating backdrops, using few props, having characters address the audience, men play women, women play men, etc--all the new-agey theater stuff that we take for granted now and shook the world back in 1945.

Google and publishing

Submitted by marmalade on 30 September 2006 - 9:13am.
  • news
  • newspapers

So, I was being as un-librarian like as possible this morning and reading the news online, and I read this this short article in the London Times. It's basically about publishing companies trying to limit unpaid access to newspaper or journal articles--specifically by affecting the way that Google or other search engines assemble "hits" when they search.

The problem the publishers are facing, if I correctly interpret the article, is that search engines like Google are making money, linking users to online publishing, while publishers aren't making much if any profit from their offerings. They know they need to put their stuff online, but they also need to earn money somehow--and they think that maybe limiting Google searchers' access will somehow take them out of the red.

libraries and librarians

Submitted by marmalade on 28 September 2006 - 9:13am.
  • technology

My mother is a retired librarian. She tried for years to convince the university where she worked to install movable shelves--the old school kind that they have in the basement of Honnold. After she left, the school finally put them in--but the new-fangled sort, that move electronically, beep when they're about to crush you, and malfunction often. They've gotten rid of the card catalog, too. My mother loves that library, but everytime that she comes out, she looks confused and frazzled.

I remember Joyce's criticism that we've forgotten to really read and concentrate, getting caught up in the summary, the list in the card catalog, the table of contents. Maybe that's a flaw--but I don't think it's the real flaw. There's lots of newspaper stories about kids these days being unable to concentrate. And it's not because they've been using the card catalog--psychologists blame it on the computer. My mother's the best reader I know, and she has this ingrained technology aversion. She sits down and reads the New York Times (paper copy) from cover to cover every morning, and then reads the Atlantic, a novel, or something else just to fill up the evening hours.

editors

Submitted by marmalade on 25 September 2006 - 9:23pm.
  • class discussion
  • readings

We've all sort of put up a hue and cry here for some sort of, well, what Derrida might call a "center", or as Lulu and thisismycheese mentioned (both on the blog and in class), at least a table of contents.

We do, after all, get one in our books, on our websites, from blogs like this one. And not having it in a hypertext was (is) a bit unsettling.

And that uneasiness is something that Joyce seems to lash out against. It's something he believes we shouldn't be unsettled by. See his railery against Yahoo and alta vista, or his condescension towards library patrons who read the catalog entries and tables of contents more "than they read from the volumes themselves" (54). In fact, he goes even further: "The notion that editors are 'necessary' to filter out the mass of information insists upon a hierarchy of information and implies one of human beings" (54). It's really terribly radical: he's not calling for a generation of pacifist hippies, he's calling for militant communists. Joyce wants to take away the power position of a publisher, of an "expert", of whoever it is that writes the introductions to critical collections. He wants all of us to go back to reading and discovering on our own, in our own time, on our own, un-influenced terms.

Two thoughts

Submitted by marmalade on 24 September 2006 - 10:52am.
  • readings

Two thoughts--

Do you all recall Ong, and his talk about the hoopla that went on against literacy (in favor orality)? He cited Plato and Socrates as being anti-text, because texts were unquestionable, untestable--they represented the authors opinions in an environment where they could not be responded to or attacked.

Now read this from the Landow introduction: "A full hypertext system, unlike a book and unlike some of the first approziations of heypertext currently available (Hypercard, Guide,), offers the same environment to both reader and writer. Therefore by opening the text processing program, or editor, as it is known, you can take notes, or you can write against my interpretation, against my text. Although you cannot change my text, you can write a response and then link it to my document" (6).

What is it if a story reads like that? Is it a story? Is it a "text" if there is no distinct timeline or story in set form? How do you analyze it? interdependence

Submitted by marmalade on 16 September 2006 - 6:51pm.
  • readings

I have a telephone--technically. It's rarely turned on. My mother has a rule that I should take it with me when I run more than ten miles, but I never have. I dropped it a couple of months ago, so now it takes about ten hours to charge, and I have to remove and reinsert the battery in order to get it to turn on. But I wouldn't give up the thing. I lived for six months last spring in Germany and Austria, and the hardest part was how unreachable I became to my family. I think I talked to my twin twice over the phone in that space--and here at school we talk ritually every Sunday night. I felt like I had lost my family, become divorced from a piece of myself, when I had to rely on Skype, calling cards, and freezing public phonebooths to communicate home.
