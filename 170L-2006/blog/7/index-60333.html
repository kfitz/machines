Flow in stories (not in membranes)

Submitted by marmalade on 22 October 2006 - 12:56pm.
  • readings

I keep trying to make connections between Juul's idea of "flow" and Douglas & Hargadon's idea of the "engaged reader," but I'm not sure if I'm doing a very good job.

Juul is the author who talks about "game time" and has the lovely graph that looks like a slinky. According to him, flow is what occurs when: "--the challenges match the player's abilities and thereby lead to a state of flow (the player loses the sense of objective time -- time will fly)" (139). The player is immersed. But the point is hard to reach, Juul claims. A very exact balance must be struck:
"To reach a state of flow, a game must be neither too hard (which leads to anxiety) or too easy (which leads to boredom). This means the experience of time is tied not only to the play time/event time relation and to the challenged provide by the game, but also to the relation between game difficulty and player ability" (139).

Nobel Prize

Submitted by marmalade on 12 October 2006 - 2:19pm.
  • news

Just a little blurb about today's announcement of the Nobel Prize winner for Literature--it's a fellow whom I know very little about, called Orhan Pamuk. I have a book on my shelf waiting to be read, and maybe this is the impetus to finally crack it open? Anyway, it's a big brouhaha because the fellow has recently been in trouble for insulting "Turkishness", specifically accusing the Turks of massacring the Armenian population of the Ottoman Empire during the WWII. Anyway you can read about it all in the London Times for the political background or in the NYTimes for some discussion of Pamuk himself, his political interests, and what he's written (and whether it's good!).

More McLuhan

Submitted by marmalade on 12 October 2006 - 9:13am.
  • McLuhan

Okay, so I've been reading some of this book by Marshall McLuhan (of "the message is the medium" fame), and (in addition to being an even more trippy read than Understanding Media) it's a pretty amazing document. The book is called The Gutenberg Galaxy and was (obviously?) published sometime back in the sixties when Star Trek fervor was still running strong.

Now there's a million cool (and naturally underdeveloped) ideas running around in this book. One of them has to do with materiality, and McLuhan conjectures as follows:

"As our age translates itself back into the oral and auditory modes because of the electronic pressure of simultaneity, we become sharply aware of the uncritical acceptance of visual metaphors and models by many past centuries ... We become extremely conscious of cultural models and bias when moving from one dominant form of awareness to another, as between Greek and Latin or English and French. So we are no longer amazed that the oriental world has no concept of 'substance' or of 'substantial form,' since they experience no visual pressure to break up experience into such packages [because their alphabet is symbolic rather than syllabic]."

who are these authors?

Submitted by marmalade on 9 October 2006 - 2:02pm.
  • readings

I know the question was brought up in class today about who Deena Larsen really is, and we didn't really get around to answering it.

Here's a link, however, to a bit of a bio about Larsen, some links to her other works, and a brief interview with her. Apparently electronic fiction is her thing--although her day job is as a "technical writer". She's written a lot of stuff over the years, and some of it looks pretty neat (I've only done a quick inspection of some of the links).

Talan Memmott is the guy who I was most interested in, however--specifically whether or not he considered himself an artist or a writer. I did some poking around this weekend and discovered that, unfortunately for my categorization system, he considers himself both! He originally started out with a background in fine arts and then moved into writing. Here's an interview with him on an arty website talking about Lexia, and here's another, somewhat longer interview with him that's more focused on his vision of electronic literature. And, if that's not enough to read, perhaps this will also be helpful--Memmott's own website and some of his newer works... For something in the vein of Lexia, check out The Berth of V-ness (under the Media Work link), but also check out some of his more straight forward (all terms are, of course, relative) literary stuff under the "Writings" link.

Now for a bit of optimism...

Submitted by marmalade on 7 October 2006 - 10:36am.
  • Hayles
  • readings

I almost stood up and cheered when I came across this passage at at the end of Hayles' first chapter:

"As we work toward critical practices and theories appropriate for electronic literature, we may come to renewed appreciation for the specificity of print. In the tangled web of medial ecology, change anywhere in the system stimulates change everywhere in the system. Books are not going the way of the dinosaur but the way of the human, changing as we change, mutating and evolving in ways that will continue, as a book lover said long ago, to teach and delight" (33).

Finally, none of these dire predictions about the end of books!
