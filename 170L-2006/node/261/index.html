Immediacy in text-based games

Submitted by crashingintowalls on 23 October 2006 - 8:56am.
  • agency
  • zork

Well, I have to admit that this was an enjoyable homework assignment. I left my video game habits, modest as they were, in a prior life, so the walk through memory dungeon was quite pleasant. Of course, returning to my old stomping grounds with a critical eye became an experience in itself. I played Zork long ago and far away, and at that point I had painstakingly mapped the terrain and finally bought a strategy guide to get me through a tough section that I cannot remember now, save for vague impressions of fire and ice.

This time around I was more content to wander and observe. I felt far from involved, and I watched my own reactions and the structure of the game much more than the actual play. I wanted to figure the game out, but in a totally different way than I once had. Perhaps this made it difficult to appreciate what the game brought to the table. Before my memory of command truncations and conventions kicked back in (hey, even if you never forget how to ride a bike, you may still wobble a little) I fought with the interface and reached some of the same frustrations that night owl did. The game felt clunky, probably as much because of my desire to pick it apart as my familiarity with more immersive environments.

As I got my rhythm, my reflections came to resemble Pimm's comments on the oddities of the text-based system. I found my share of utterances that looked entirely possible to me that left the game quite confused. I also tried jumping off something for kicks, only to be met with the affirmation, "Suicide is not the answer." Now, while all of this further impressed the difficulty of mapping the human language system, it recalled issues of immersion and agency as well.

The visual format that most recent games appeal to has a number of advantages, including creating a sense of immersion that does not reqire the game to understand the intent of the user. Through visual conventions of clicking on a unit and then selecting from a menu (as in Warcaft), things continue to look real even though your options as a user have already been limited to those which the game can support. The problem lies in those moments in Myst where you really want to go to a certain place in the landscape, only to click on it and find that there is no "there" there. Similarly, the text interface gives the impression that you can do anything, but the actual possibilities are far more restricted. Users like myself who are used to having a human on the other end of AIM who can parse even my own creative deployment of the english language find the limited flexibility of this system a knock on its realism. Likewise, our creativity is foiled by an environment that an only adapt to us in certain ways. However, I would argue that this failure is not so much on the game as a synthesis of the expectations that we bring to the interface. At the end of the day this is a game, and thus a limited world. We can only play by the rules, and if those rules are not satisfying then we can walk away.
