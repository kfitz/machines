"They can force me to shoot; they just can't force me to shoot well."

That's one of Earl's better moments, I think. His formulations sound better as LISG smells worse, though. When 50,000 guys stand in two lines facing each other, let's bless everyone who shoots badly.

"I went into the National Guard," he starts, as he has a couple of times. He figures avoiding the draft gave him some special training for corporate life.

"Any of you like what we're doing? Where there's a job, I'm a nonparticipant."

"You get your batches in on time." Edna's on to him. Branley smiles across at me, perhaps conspiratorially.

"Oh, I acknowledge authority, no question. They can force me to do things."

"Well, alright, but all of us --"

"But I volunteer no extra. I don't want much: a little land -- in Africa, for all I care; I'd prefer somewhere people speak English, but that's not hard -- a house, someone to staff it, enough to live."

"That's all any of us do."

"I figure."

"Is that how you got into the Guard?" -- Branley.

"To avoid the draft? Absolutely. Hey, so I had to play soldier a few weekends."

"Oh, but how far would you have gone? If they had made you fire on people here?"

"People here, people there -- why would I fire on either?"

"But they can force you."

"Yes. Well, yes, they did. I had a gun. I wore a uniform. I saluted sharply: Sir."

"Would you have fired on American students, like at Kent State?"

"Well, what's fired upon'"

"That's what they had to do."

"Had to? How come?"

"They had orders."

"You misunderstand orders. Orders doesn't mean you do what your superior wants. I have a gun, that's easy to check: the gun's at my shoulder; the gun isn't. They can force me to point. They can force me to shoot because they can check the gun later and see that it discharged. But it's very difficult to check where one bullet went out of a line of riflemen."

"You don't have any feeling about letting down anyone, not your country, not your fellow draft resisters, who are really on the same side as you?"

"I help the people I don't shoot. Who's better off if I obey? I think I'm being awfully nice: I humor them. I even wish them well: I'd rather they were sane. But past that, I have to take what I need."

Earl's grey at the temples, looks at me conspiratorially, like a teacher who suspects I'll get the answer first: "Come on, you're a bright boy." But here he is at LISG with another set of orders, and I have no answer to that.
