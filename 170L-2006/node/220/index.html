Materiality in First Person book

Submitted by Lulu on 15 October 2006 - 8:36pm.
  • Materiality
  • readings

I really enjoyed the juxtaposition of essays and commentary in Wardrip-Fruin and Harrigan's First Person. I thought the format of putting responses and commentary directly below their respective essays on the same page gave us a nice contrast of opinions and points. It also reminded me of our class blog, and I thought it was cool that the book was able to achieve this blogging/commenting feature in print.

I don't know how everyone else felt, but the physical experience of reading First Person was also a little strange and bewildering to me at first. Usually when I'm reading for a class, especially if it's a really long book, I really like the feeling of finishing a chapter and moving on. When I get to the end of a huge chapter or a long section, I like the sense of finality. So, with this traditional mindset and experience of reading texts, it definitely was a new sensation for me to have to go back to the beginning of each section after finishing the essays, in order to read the responses. It was a strange sense of re-reading pages and retracing page numbers in a seemingly linear text, and challenged my start-to-finish mentality. In this case, though, I definitely thought this breakdown of linearity was useful in giving us more diverse points of views.

Personally, I think that the

Submitted by night owl on 16 October 2006 - 10:01pm.

I think that the multilinearity would have taken on a different form online. Maybe it's just the current state of the hyperlink, but the tendency seems to be to pepper a text with hyperlinks to content very specifically related to the words or phrases that are blue and underlined. Instead of the relatively autonomous commentary provided in First Person, we would be presented a cleverly integrated list of sentence or paragraph sized annotations. I also enjoyed the structure of First Person... but I don't think it's very similar to online writing.

I think it's interesting how almost all of the printed texts about electronic literature that we've read make some attempt to imitate the medium they address -- this even though the texts themselves are not a product of that medium.

Materiality and multilinearity

Submitted by Oz on 17 October 2006 - 1:41pm.

I'm not sure how the effect of materiality I'm about to describe applies to your questions about naturalness, familiarity, and comfort, but I noted that having the comments/responses set in print made them feel more compulsory. That is, like Lulu, I felt a compulsion to turn back to the beginning and read all the responses that fell under the page assignment. However, assuming these texts had been online and the responses had come in the form of linked pages or otherwise appended material, I would have been much more likely to read/skim/skip over them according to my interest. Perhaps that brings us back to some of our more fundamental questions about medium/materiality, and whether print really does have a more coercive relationship with the reader and her reading practices.
