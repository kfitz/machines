Orality and Literacy in "Lexia to Perplexia"

Submitted by silversprung on 3 October 2006 - 5:33pm.
  • Hayles
  • Memmott
  • Ong

I've been reading N. Katherine Hayles' "Writing Machines" for the past two days, and she has a lot of really thought-provoking insights about various kinds of electronic literature. (She also has many jargony sentences that make my head spin, but that's a different story.) Her analysis of Talan Memmott's "Lexia to Perplexia" enabled me to think about that work with a kind of intellectual depth that I never would have been able to access without her guidance. But I guess that's why we read criticism and theory, huh?

I found Hayles' discussion of how Memmott creates a "creole" of English and programming code particularly fascinating. She says that this creole "forms the medium trought which the origin of subjectivity can be re-described as coextensive with technology. Just as these hybrid articulations do not exist apart from their penetration by code, so the subject does not exist apart from the technology that produces the creole describing/creating the techno-subject (53).

Memmott's creation of this creole is often manifested through punning neologisms such as 'cellâ€¦(f)' or 'cell.f' (to describe a technologically-hybridized 'self') or words like 'remotional,' 'exe.termination,' 'communification,' 'cyborganization'--the list goes on. At first, this made me think that Memmott's hypertext is enacting an even more literary, textual mode of thinking than books do. At least books are usually written in a single language; with Memmott's hypertext, the reader is constantly conscious that a new language is being created out of two distinct, established ones--and this consciousness is a highly literary (as opposed to oral) one. However, when I thought about how central puns and homophones are to Memmott's creole, I amended my judgment. A reader (well, a reader like me) has to say a lot of Memmott's neologisms out loud in order to figure out which component words are contributing their sounds (and, therefore, meanings) to the neologism. So not only is "Lexia to Perplexia" hyper-literate, it also resuscitates orality within its literacy. It's pretty much a hyper-everything text--"nervous" (57), as Hayles puts it.

p.s. I just read marmalade's post, and it looks like Memmott's neologisms fascinated both of us, so I do hope we talk about them some in class.

I just got around to reading

Submitted by marmalade on 4 October 2006 - 5:49pm.

I just got around to reading that section of Hayles this afternoon, and it answered a few of my questions, while raising several more.

You might want to check this out. It's a response to that particular Hayles essay, and it raises some more interesting points about language.

One of my favorites I'll quote here for you:

"This response text was itself written within the framing environment of the "desktop metaphor." Inside this metaphor one asks, where does the metaphor leave off and the text take over? We look at the computer screen closely and we see a set or field of pixels. The pixels are presented through an underlying code functioning within a particular software/hardware environment. This software/hardware environment enables the creation of a particular "language" space. The pixels form patterns that look like words or images. We read these patterns as words or images or some word/image hybrid. Thus, this light configuration is "likened" to a word or image or word/image hybrid and we understand it as such. We compare the environment of the screen to our embodied memory of past relationships, gleaning the shape of words and images, symbols, formulas - patterns. "

Anyway, the essay is worth checking out if you're as interested as I was ;) !
