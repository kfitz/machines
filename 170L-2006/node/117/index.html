some thoughts on Joyce

Submitted by Lulu on 24 September 2006 - 3:23pm.
  • reading response

Reading Michael Joyce's "Afternoon: A Story" was confusing, to say the least. I was very anxious while reading it, and there was a point at which I kept going in circles. I tried going back and changing my choices, but I'd end up on the same several pages again and again...it took me a while to backtrack enough so that I could end up somewhere new. If there's no coherent plot, does that make this "story" still a story? I understand Joyce's point that we should participate in the creation of a text and give our own meaning to a story, but if that detracts from our comprehension of the story itself, then is the exercise futile? I tend to agree with thisismycheese's point that Joyce seems to be using the technology for the sake of it, rather than because his particular "story" is well-suited for this medium.

I read some choose-your-own-adventure stories when I was younger (i think they were goosebumps books), and I enjoyed that particular experience because it was thrilling, and the point at which the reader had to make a choice was usually an exciting fork in the road. For example, the reader would approach a door, and then you could either open it or walk away. Oooh! The suspense! What's behind the door?? Am I brave enough?? For that particular thriller-suspense genre where lives are at stake, I think this medium would be well-suited. In the Afternoon story, however, I think Joyce succeeds in making the reader understand the experience of non-linearity and reading texts unconventionally; however, he seems to fail in the area of allowing the reader to enjoy the experience of reading. Well, I don't know about everyone else in the class, but as for myself, it seems like the enjoyment one should get from reading was lost, to be replaced by anxiety and confusion. For me, the process of reorienting myself seriously got in the way of enjoyment. I was too caught up by the weirdness of the medium to enjoy what was supposed to be happening in the story.

In his article, "Othermindedness," Joyce also talks a little about coherence, and defines it as "the middle voice of consciously making sense for oneself and yet among others." (62) I feel like while his hypertext fiction clearly illustrates the power of this new medium, it's also lost some coherence, which is troubling to me. I'm leaning towards the opinion that non-linearity is the best form for surfing the internet, but when applied to the novel, it suffers. I see that Joyce wants to redefine the meaning of coherence and wants "a rethinking of coherence in terms of the successive attendings to persisting forms." (63) I'm not quite sure what "successive attendings to persisting forms" means (hopefully somebody else can better explain or help out?), but I do get that he's trying to make sense out of what seems to be senselessness. In that process, however, I feel like many "normal" readers are left behind, left on our own to figure out what the point is. My ultimate question is: Who is the audience? The small, elite part of the population that has access to computers and education, and who would thus have the background to start making sense of the senseless?

Also, Joyce wrote this story in 1986, and since then, the non-linear hypertext novel has not taken off to become a huge success. Books are still the way to go. I have doubts as to whether this way of reading fiction will ever replace books. I thought the interactive experience was very interesting--it's something that every reader should try once in their lifetime--but I can't see it as something I'd do over and over again.
