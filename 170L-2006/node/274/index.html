Another kind of right to contract?

Submitted by silversprung on 24 October 2006 - 10:25pm.
  • gaming
  • narratives
  • the nature of the game

I've been thinking about the idea of a contract between reader/player and text/story/game, which several people have made brief reference to in class and on the blog. It's a pretty generally accepted notion that readers enter into contracts with books, and it seems equally plausible that a player enters into a contract with the game she chooses to play. I'm wondering if the contract that a reader makes with a text is substantially different from the contract a player makes with a game.

A reader expects that a story will make some sort of coherent sense (unless we're talking about highly experimental stuff)-- that she'll be able to follow along to a certain extent, that even if she doesn't understand every single paragraph, she'll be able to draw some meaning out of the whole.

A player would seem to expect the same sort of "coherent sense" within the context of the game space-- that performing a certain action will result in a somewhat predictable or reasonable effect, that doing things will actually change what happens in the game, that there is some way to win at the game.

Disclaimer: Judging from this past week's homework, I seem to be very bad at playing computer games. (Card games, too, actually.) And I did not grow up playing complicated adventure-style computer games-- I was raised on a steady diet of Oregon Trail. So my opinion here is particularly up for dispute.

But it seems to me that a lot of games (Zork, I'm looking at you) don't hold up their end of the contract with the player-- they don't seem to tell you what the rules are, or how you're supposed to navigate the game. Now, you could argue that the point of Zork is to figure out how things work, and so telling people some basic commands would ruin it. But if you argue this, then it seems we have to radically redefine/expand my description of the player-game contract. We then permit a great degree of obscurity in games-- even (especially?) in good games. I understand that figuring out the puzzle can be fun, but it seems to me that at a certain point, if you don't have enough hints to go on, the game becomes frustrating, not rewarding. Perhaps I have a greater need for clues than most gamers do. I think that gaming is a way of thinking that requires practice to develop-- and I certainly don't possess it.

I am definately in the same

Submitted by zoey on 25 October 2006 - 1:04pm.

I am definately in the same boat you are...I like having a few clues to direct me, and I get frustrated quite easily if I decide I have no idea how to work the game. Grumpymutt was talking about how as a little kid he would sit down and write out all the moves he had made in Zork, noting where the moves had taken him; so I guess he created a kind of map that would help him figure out the game. I never really had the patience to do things like this...I mean, I figure I would have had the patience if a game really held my interest and I really wanted to figure out, but that never happened. I guess I just give up on things that don't captivate me, like crossword puzzles for example. But I've always been this way, and I'm wondering if I actually started trying to figure games out if I would get into that mode of thinking and start enjoying the process...sometimes I think my experience of gaming has been so completely lacking compared to some people's...

challenges and boredom

Submitted by Natwwal on 25 October 2006 - 1:04pm.

I think that one big difference between literature and most games is that games tend to have a definite "right" answer or solution. Once you've beaten the game, there is often little left to explore. Thus, a game has to be accessible enough that non-gamers don't give up, but with enough challenges that people who are really good at computer games don't blaze through it in a single go. No matter how good you are at literary analysis, it isn't possible to fully analyze a good novel in a single, casual reading. I was frustrated with Zork, too, but I think that the creators of the game were trying to keep it from being too easy.
