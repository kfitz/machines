This is what Landow says is possible with a hypertext, and it's hard to disagree with him after trying to read one. But are hypertexts and multiple paths a good thing? Marmalade began a discussion on how hypertexts "decenter" the author, and I think this is a fascinating topic to pursue. As Landow says, hypertexts are "infinitely recenterable" (36) and create "truly active" readers because of their interaction with the text, but in what ways does a hypertext take away the readers' ability to interpret on their own (through providing links to certain explanations, etc.)? And, flipping the coin, hypertexts also seem to give up some of the writers' authority to the reader. It all seems like an odd mixing of losing and gaining control.

I'm not certain I follow Landow's idea of the hypertext finally making the book "unnatural." As many of us have posted, the most natural form to us is a solid, tangible book. What does it mean to be natural, anyway?

Finally, to put a wiki in this context, does our wiki really the "blur the boundary between reader and writer" (4), as Landow suggests Joyce's text might? I mean, we as a class are definitely active readers and writers, but are other visitors to the site? Aren't they just readers?

hypertext fiction, Landow, readings

Submitted by Anonymous on 27 September 2006 - 5:43pm.

The wiki might blur the boundary between reader and writer in the way Landow talks about if readers were to continue to edit it, say over a number of years. In that way, one might really have "the tale of the tribe" once again.

Meanwhile, I'm not sure the issue is one of relatively empowering reader or auther I disagree with Landow and many others here, but I wonder whether that's not why so many wonder which of the two is happening.

Landow also seems to feel that allowing/forcing readers to make the reading by choosing alternate paths constitutes wreading. I wonder. If the text itself means as the combined product of all or many readings, I'm not sure how the reader is more or less free or more or less authorial. One arcs through the text as the faithful might read The Bible or as one might read a favorite group of poems (I have favorite hypertexts that I occasionally revisit. Or one dislikes it and doesn't re-read, I suppose.)
