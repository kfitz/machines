Moulthrop + Aristotle

Submitted by marmalade on 18 October 2006 - 9:25am.
  • readings

So this post sort of goes along with thisismycheese's post, below, but we both seem to be thinking about different aspects of Moulthrop, so I thought it merited its own space.

What interested me was Moulthrop's emphasis on what seems to be some sort of classical Greek structure. While he admits that "We feel that narrative in certain conventional senses ... no longer animates the work we find most interesting as creators and/or critics" (58), he claims that a fulfilling narrative can be constructed around the old conventional principles, only using games rather than the novel, film, or theater.

Here's an example: "Our ritual release of pity and fear arrives when we fully understand the relationships among characters and the pattern of causes that constitute a plot; or to expand beyond narrative terms, when we grasp the structure of metaphor and memory that informs a lyric, meditation or confession. Our engagement with the text is driven by the desire to apprehend the structure in its entirety" (59) (my emphasis).

Okay--ritual release of pity and fear--now he's talking about catharsis--about Aristotle and the basic meanings of narrative, tragedy, and drama. And Moulthrop claims, like Aristotle if my memory serves, that such a release comes only after fully comprehending the tragedy, the inevitability of which becomes clear by examining the "structure" of the work.

And this is where I get stuck. It starts off being really cool--games are like interactive dramas; we can become the actors; we can see the tragedy both as spectator and as participant; we can thus experience that catharsis from perhaps two different persepctives. Which is really an awesome idea, but unfortunately he doesn't take it farther. He, and I, seem to get hung up on the whole structure thing.

My problem with such a claim is that I wonder how well it can apply back to something like hypertexts (although M is admittedly talking about games here), where the whole point is to hold something back, to leave the readers a bit unsure if they've really read everything and reached the end of the story. But if we're unsure, can we really experience this deep moment that literature is supposed to bring about--can we really comprehend the completeness and inevitability of the narrative if we're not sure that we have all of the facts? If the narrative isn't, perhaps, complete?

Maybe that's the sense of disappointment we keep getting hung up on when we read these hypertexts. And maybe games can address that--every choice in a game leads to an outcome, an outcome that you can examine in detail and pinpoint as causing x event to occur later--just think about Murray's (mildly creepy) dinner-party game. They are narratives that you can look back over and examine, appreciate as having a structure, albeit a structure that you can/could alter by making different choices.

Anyway, I'm not sure if this makes any sense, but it's a couple of ideas that have been rolling around, looking for a way out.
