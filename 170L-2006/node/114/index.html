books and hypertext are both cool

Submitted by Lulu on 24 September 2006 - 1:27am.
  • readings

The discussion in Landow about hypertext and poststructuralism makes me question if deconstructing the linearity of traditional printed material necessitates rendering books obsolete. Several people, including Gunslinger and Frabby, have already expressed uneasiness at the idea of hypertext replacing books. I also have a fond attachment towards books. I don't necessarily agree when critical theorists like Derrida make statements like "changes in media have eroded the power of the linear model and the book as related culturally dominant paradigms." (47) Why is linearity necessarily a bad thing? Does the discovery of a new, more free form of reading texts have to involve antagonizing the previous traditional forms? I feel like many of these poststructuralists truly underestimate the power of human attachment, memory, and emotion--and the historical cultural power and force of the book. Just look at some of the previous entries with people's testimonies, like gunslinger's image of reading a good book at the remote family cabin or frabby's comment about parents reading their children bed-time stories. Many of the critical theorists in Landow's piece are suggesting that the new technological developments are overdetermining the end of the book age. Derrida even declares that "the end of linear writing...is indeed the end of the book." (47) I feel like the effort to decenter the author and institute active reader participation has been flawed in that it privileges these new media developments without considering the powerful, emotional pull that traditional print material has on people. Book culture and the hypertext culture don't necessarily need to be antagonistic forces.

It's so exciting to be creating your own stories in the hypertext world and to travel about in a non-linear manner, but sometimes it's also nice to be mindlessly drawn into the author's world, to immerse yourself in the writer's world and to follow along the linear plot. Then there are also the fusion, choose-your-own-adventure tales where, within an ultimately linear framework, one can still participate and make active choices. Whatever the case, I agree with gunslinger's final note about progressing with the book.

Also, another thing that I wanted to point out is that the Landow article was written in 1997, before the internet was totally widespread. Remember all those TV clips and shows during the 60s and 70s that had all those futuristic and space-like images of life in the 21st century? Well, we're in the 21st century now and we're not living like the Jetsons. Maybe, when Landow was writing in 1997, back when the internet age was just starting up, there was huge anticipation and great expectations for totally revolutionary changes, and their discussions about books becoming obsolete turned out to be just naive predictions--comparable to some of the naive predictions made in the mid-20th century about life in the 21st century.

hypertext fiction, readings, Landow

Submitted by Anonymous on 24 September 2006 - 6:05pm.

A synthesis seems cool, but I wonder how long it can last. I agree that they do a lot of things better, but will we retain them for that?

The standard font for pulp novels is now larger because its readership has crossed their mid-40's.

In the 60's and '70's, I expected to buy books for about an hour's minimum wage. Many were less. Used books were common, and usually less still. I can download thousands of books, store them in a thimble.

A generation or so earlier, Hemingway and Fitzgerald lived kind of like movie stars.

Will the general population use them, or will they continue to be decentered? Illuminated manuscripts were wonderful -- check out the Blake archives! But only a few experts saw them.

Could we have profundity and clarity in cybertext, would we want plot?
