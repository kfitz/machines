I want my table of contents back!

Submitted by thisismycheese on 24 September 2006 - 11:29am.
  • hypertext fiction

My experience reading "Afternoon" has been incredibly frustrating, and as a result, almost enlightening--as a lot of other people have mentioned, it's made me realize how attached I am to the physical book. I'm also reacting, I think, to how basic (read: limited) the interface is.

The lack of a table of contents that lists every single page, for example, gives me a headache. When I'm reading a book, I earmark pages I want to find again later, or write down page numbers, etc., while in "Afternoon" there isn't really a way to do that (unless there is and I've just missed it). Similarly, I hate having to backtrack through all the pages I've been to in order to get back to where I started. (Maybe we're not supposed to get back to where we started.)

I didn't realize how huge the difference is between a book and something like "Afternoon." I don't like the lack of linearity and going in circles not knowing who the narrator is and not knowing where to go next.

Which is part of the reason that I think the wiki works better as a collection of stories that build on one another and link to each other rather than as one story with lengthy (and interconnected) digressions. I find it difficult to work with something like "Afternoon," which appears to be a coherent story presented in a nonlinear format. The format feels artificial to me--"yes" and "no" links seem to be arbitrarily determined (and sometimes do nothing at all), and all of the little digressions seem as if they could have been presented in a book-like format (in flashbacks or footnotes or whatever). Maybe I only feel like that because I'm trying to read this the way I read a book (because that's the only way I know how to read...), but it seems like he's using this technology for the sake of using the technology, rather than because his story is particularly suited to the medium.

Pretty much through its most

Submitted by thisismycheese on 25 September 2006 - 9:39am.

Pretty much through its most basic technological functions--the ability to list all pages, for example, or to list which pages link to the one you're reading. What made me crazy about "Afternoon" was the lack of organizational features.

In terms of the format of the story--I like the idea of electronic literature looking like the project magoo described. The idea of intersecting stories, multimedia, etc.--stories that intersect the way people's lives do. An overarching story, sure, but a broad one. The multivocal narrative--and more explicitly multivocal than "there is a narrator but sometimes he seems to morph into someone else, but damned if I know who"--seems suited to electronic literature better than a univocal (/uni...plotted?) one.

And Pimm, you're probably right. But even in "Afternoon," we don't have any real control. We don't get to decide where a click will take us--Joyce decided that; all we do is, essentially, flip a coin. We're still just playing his game (at least it feels that way to me).

Then again, maybe I just like to keep things simple ;)
