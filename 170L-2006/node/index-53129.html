The future of the printed book

Submitted by Gunslinger1818 on 23 September 2006 - 3:14pm.
  • Landow

Was anyone else a little bothered by some of the discussion in Landow about the possible future irrelevance of books and other printed material as important? Perhaps it's that I love to read by the fire at the remote family cabin, that my mom's a librarian, or that I simply have an attachment to the status quo, but I feel almost morally opposed to reading without a book. I certainly am attached to the "tactility" Landow mentions. I was relieved at Landow's view of escaping the confinement of print: "This common project first requires that one first recognize the enormous power of the book, for only after we have made ourselves conscious of the ways it has formed and informed our lives can we seek to pry ourselves free of some of its limitations" (46-7).

"Why tlk whn u cn txt?"

Submitted by Lulu on 21 September 2006 - 11:36pm.
  • technology

I read this disturbing article about how text- and instant-messaging has replaced actual human contact among many people. It describes how people feel more comfortable with their online alias and conversing in the electronic world than actually talking face-to-face. One teenager even said that "face-to-face you run out of things to talk about." It strikes me as really sad that many people have lost their ability to be social human beings as a result of the internet and other technological gadgets. Some say they feel more in control when they're texting or IMing, and that in face-to-face situations, "you're not presenting the real you, you're just reacting."

Technology and Individuality

Submitted by silversprung on 21 September 2006 - 1:19pm.
  • readings
  • technology

I've been chewing on the relationship between technology and individuality for awhile, partially as a result of a blog post that magoo made several days ago.

I can see how it might seem that technology, as it gradually performs more and more functions that our own bodies once performed by themselves (I'm channelling McLuhan here), would sap us of our individualism. Human beings perform functions like hearing, writing, drawing, etc. in unique ways, but technology tends to standardize them. (Witness the Industrial Revolution and the advent of interchangeable positions on the assembly line and interchangeable mechanical parts, as magoo points out.)

The Daily Show

Submitted by Natwwal on 21 September 2006 - 1:06pm.
  • blogs
  • media
  • news

I've often wondered why it is that people of our generation seem to find Jon Stewart much funnier than people my parents age do. It's not as though the concept of spoofing news is particularly new; SNL has been doing it for years. I'm still trying to sort through the significance of this, but last night, as I was watching the Daily Show, I was struck by the similarities between the show's format and the format of a blog. Instead of "link, commentary," the Daily Show offers us "video clip, commentary." While SNL comics did something similar, they tended to stay in character. Jon Stewart doesn't try very hard to maintain the persona of a newsperson. His own voice and opinions come through very clearly. In this way, he isn't so different from political bloggers.

McLuhan is Everywhere

Submitted by Frabby on 20 September 2006 - 8:41pm.
  • readings

I was trying to enjoy something a little less academic when I came across an article that seemed like a dumbed down version of some of the theory we've been reading. I admit it; I was reading Marie Claire, a fashion magazine that isn't even that good in the scheme of fashion magazines. But in between the Sarah Jessica Parker interview and fall's fashion preview (it's all about tweed) there was an article about the effect of technology on young women's lives. Marie Claire asked two women to participate in an "experiment." One woman stopped using technology (the magazine threw this term around loosely, the woman didn't start living like the Amish, she just didn't use email, instant messenger, and stuff like that) and the other woman had to rely entirely on the Internet (she even had to order food online).
