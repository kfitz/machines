Virtual Ethics (the case of the funeral slaughter)

Submitted by Shock and Awe on 4 October 2006 - 7:36pm.
  • funeral slaughter
  • video games
  • virtual community
  • virtual ethics

Still fascinated by the possibilities of the virtual gaming world, I have been scanning around message boards and whatnot when I came across the link to this Disturbing World of Warcraft Clip. A member of a guild (a unified horde of WOW players) died in real life. Her guild decided to give her a virtual funeral, out in the open, in a kill zone. They invited players from other guilds and stated that it would be a nonviolent event. Well, players from a less scrupulous guild raided the funeral and slaughtered all, and I mean ALL, of the attendees.

Depending on your sense of humor, this is either hilarious or disgusting. Being in the camp that initially found it hilarious, it led me to question what role ethics/morality will have in the virtual world.

Right now the gaming/virtual world seems to be in a kind of ethical limbo. Some within it take their actions very seriously (they hold funerals for dead friends and expect the sanctity of a virtual funeral to be upheld just as the sanctity of a real funeral would be in a functioning society), while others see it all as a silly fantasy world in which they can act as they damn well please (slaughter everyone at a funeral, tape the slaughter, edit it, set it to funny music, and distribute the video.)

I think this issue will become more and more relevant in our lives as more aspects of our relationships/communication become virtual-based. The internet gives everyone (bloggers being the perfect example) the ability to set up a virtual identity for themselves. You can be whoever you want to be on the net. But are you responsible for the actions of that net personality in the same way you're responsible for the actions of your real self? The ramifications are clearly different. Nobody was really killed in the video above, but it was still essentially a morally despicable act. Gamers put a great deal of time and energy into their WOW characters. They reach levels and gain special powers. Apparently you can even buy powers/weapons on ebay. So their deaths do represent a tangible lose.

Should the players who participated in the killing be punished in kind? Perhaps some sort of virtual death penalty. Or is a new, more flexible morality, needed to account for the fact that the virtual world is virtual, and killing a virtual person is quite different from killing a real one? Either way, I think our generation has a unique opportunity to watch this issue develop as the virtual world expands into our real lives.

WoW funeral

Submitted by magoo on 18 October 2006 - 4:48pm.

My resident WoW expert claims that the guild specifically wanted the gesture of holding the funeral

a) in the deceased's favorite battlegrounds and also,
b) in embattled area, specifically.

Apparently part of the gesture was to declare a cessation of hostilities in honor of their dead friend. They declared to other guilds where they would be and also, I understand, that they would be unarmed.

There's another interesting point with these games -- it interests me, anyway. People actually pay for developed RPG characters and for their "gear" -- icons acquired during gameplay that confer capacities during gameplay.
