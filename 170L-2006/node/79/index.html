Fear of technology and its threat to human individuality

Submitted by Pimm on 17 September 2006 - 8:33pm.
  • readings

Bush's article begins with a list of reasons why science and technology is beneficial. It's funny to me yet completely understandable that sometimes we have to remind ourselves why new technology and scientific research is important. I think one of the main reasons we need these reminders is because of the fear of new technology. As Burnett says, "Machines attract and repel humans" (123). One of these fears relates to our McLuhan discussion, and that is the disappearance of technology from our consciousness through immediacy. Burnet asks: "What happens, however, when machines disappear from 'view' into the background and nevertheless retain their importance?" (123). As Burnett says, machines now function with extreme importance in our society, yet they are so important that they pose a danger that the "assumption, if not the reality, that humans no longer control their own destiny" (123). On one hand, we hope that computers can do more than just what people program them to do, yet there is also great danger in their "intelligence."

One of the main points that Burnett seems to make is that the incorporation of new technology in our daily lives has created a whole "new identity for human beings" (125). My previous paragraph probably makes clear that I don't understand technology very well, but on top of that, the readings made me realize that I don't understand humans and their identity outside technology, either. The end of Burnett's article and Armstrong's article raised questions of how our minds work and how we function as individuals in society. Armstrong especially made me realize how little I know of how humans "learn" to sympathize with others and how we act as individuals. What does Armstrong mean, for instance when she says of Richardson's characters, "They were in this sense alone truly exceptional. Each could inscribe him or herself in writing as an abject, or body, separate and apart from the subject that inhabited that body, and put that body through a sequence of moves to enhance its social value" (6). Huh? How do we become self-governizing individuals, as those characters did?

To end, I'm glad to see that others--tophat1 and marmalade--are a bit confused on what conclusions to take from this week's readings, because I, too, felt like I missed pieces of their main arguments, especially with the idea of individualism and, as a result, how technology threatens that individuality.
