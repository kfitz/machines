Loneliness and the Internet

Submitted by zoey on 2 October 2006 - 1:11am.
  • creation of online content
  • the nature of the blog
  • wiki

I've been stuck on this quote: "I am afraid that one of the things that makes the web in its current form so attractive is that so many of us are afraid and lonely and do not know what to think or who will hear us" (from Joyce's "Othermindedness" p52). I've been thinking about the theme of loneliness lately in connection with people who create content and put it on the web where it can be seen by a potentially huge audience. Why do people post intensely personal diaries of their life and why do people want to read them? Why do people want other people they've never met before to know mundane little details of their daily lives--where they went to eat, what they bought at Target, what song they're listening to while they're writing their entry? Maybe because these things all say something about the person who is reporting them; they convey some kind of information the writer wants you to acknowledge. But why do we want to acknowledge this information, to get some brief grasp on how somebody else lives their life, to speculate about what some teenage girl's life is like as with the whole Lonelygirl thing?

I'm still trying to work out how loneliness and the Internet intersect. In the "Orality and Literacy" piece Ong states that "in composing a text, in 'writing' something, the one producing the written utterance is also alone. Writing is a solipistic option" (100). He also says that the writer has to fictionalize the reader, to set a role for the reader to step into, and that in turn the reader fictionalizes the writer. Well...what does this mean for the person who posts content on the internet, who as a writer is alone, yet who, as a writer, also has the possibility of interaction with the reader (responding to posted comments, for example); the writer is...performing a "solipistic operation" so as to find a way not to be alone, to interact with someone else's own recently recorded thoughts. This also all got me thinking about the wiki again. We're creating something that is definitely not a solo effort yet for the most part (I assume) we're all adding content to the wiki by ourselves, without engaging other people (although we are engaging with their recorded thoughts). How are we connecting through the wiki and how are we not? Do y'all ever look up the changes someone made and then find your brain trying to form some kind of judgment about them? Are we trying to fictionalize each other? And how are we fictionalizing the outside reader? Anyway, I haven't completely thought this all through yet...
