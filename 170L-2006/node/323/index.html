Resisting temptation

Submitted by night owl on 31 October 2006 - 8:50pm.
  • bungee jumping
  • technology
  • television

I've been browsing the internet instead of working, so I suppose I best turn the effort into work. A web page that I visit fairly frequently is PopMatters, a site devoted to pop cultural criticism. It's a warmer, fuzzier, more inclusive Pitchfork, the main downside of which being its often unbelievably sappy contributors, who have the tendency to write book reports instead of critical pieces.

Two recent columns (which I happened to read just now) escape this trap. Both draw upon nostalgia, but they go on to make arguments that I'm inclined to agree with, and which have enough relevance to the subject of our course that I was inspired to blog.

One of them is Bill Gibron's The Boob Tube: Elvira, Mistress of the Dark and Movie Macabre. Gibron considers the appeal of the 80's television program Movie Macabre, comprised of schlock-y horror films and frequent interruptions by its bosomy host, Elvira. He spends what may be an inordinate amount of time finding different ways to talk about Elvira's breasts, but he also locates some of the appeal of the show in the way that it made essentially terrible genre films into palatable entertainment. Movie Macabre, Gibron suggests, made space in the culture for retro horror by dialing down the seriousness. Basically: b-movies + boobs + sarcasm = fun.

He then notes the downfall of this kind of entertainment: "Decades later, when home video turned everyone into experts, a concept like Elvira just couldn't work. For every film featured and made fun of, someone would always be nearby, ready to champion its 'challenging' concepts of coherence and continuity. If the truth is told, the ability to experience shocking and shameful examples of stunted cinema in our own homes has practically erased the word 'worthless' from our video vocabulary. Even the biggest celluloid atrocity will find a sponsor in our new self-imposed sense of punditry."

Yes, there it was: the part that made me want to blog this. Radical availability? Endlessly specialized expertise? "Our new self-imposed sense of punditry?" Why, it's the internet! But the best thing about the passage is obviously the part about "'challenging' concepts of coherence and continuity." The scare quotes make me smile every time I read it. I think we all know what he's getting at.

Even more entertaining is Glenn McDonald's column, Kids' TV: A Vast Stygian Wasteland. He recounts a four day period spent sick on the living room couch with his three-year-old son, watching tv. And here's his thesis: "Those four days marooned on the couch with my boy opened my eyes to the bottomless pit of despair that is modern children's television." After a paragraph break, he explains: "The kids shows today are kind, gentle, well-intentioned, and completely unnerving. Not only is there no violence--at all--there's barely any conflict. It's all hugging and sharing and learning." He supports his claim with a number of pretty hilarious examples, eventually concluding that children are rightfully more interested in "headbonking and yelling and chasing" than "hugging and sharing and learning" on TV.

But the point that I took away from it comes early in the article. McDonald writes, of old-school kids' programming: "I loved those cartoons--Bugs Bunny, Tom and Jerry, Woody Woodpecker. The central message appeared to be that the world was a fun, colorful and wacky place, if remarkably violent. A land where dynamite and anvils were cheap and plentiful. Smart-ass rabbits, mischievous ducks, malevolent cats and clearly psychotic coyotes--it was all good."

The world is "a fun, colorful and wacky place," and it is "remarkably violent." Figuring out how to appreciate these different elements, and how to act accordingly, seems to be one of the biggest challenges life presents. We have to learn to love a scary world. And the "wasteland" that is modern kids' TV can't be any help. Nor can the erasure of all quality distinctions, as with post-Elvira expertise.

Our always advancing technology tempts us to try to fix all of our perceived cultural problems -- to patch them up with clean, healthy, democratic and modern principles. We say as much in class: "But what if the electronic medium can encourage people to embrace [whatever concept] in a positive way?" I'd probably regret it if I came out against progressive thinking. . . but I think that these two PopMatters articles suggest something worth considering: there is substance to the things that technology tramples as it advances. For every gain, there's a loss. We can democratize and sanitize the products of our culture all we'd like, but we need to know that we run the risk of leaving them mediocre and ineffectual.
