Super Monsters

Submitted by Frabby on 10 October 2006 - 7:30pm.
  • electronic literature

In class and on the blog, people seem pretty unhappy when we read something really confusing, disarming, or requiring lots of technical back-flips (like running the computer in classic mode or using Internet Explorer). And I would put myself in that camp as well. I certainly see value in reading texts like "Lexia to Perplexia" and "Afternoon" where we are left bewildered and uncertain, but I wouldn't say it was the most enjoyable experience of my life. Again it recalls the question of whether or not the text is worth the work (ANY kind of work). And we've already talked about how sly books like Infinite Jest merit all the work they require (for me at least) while other hypertexts don't.

But after reading an interview with Shelley Jackson, some of her comments make me wonder if I should be more open to confusion. The interview is kind of old (from 2002), I just stumbled upon it while I was looking for something else, but it's still interesting. In it she says,

"A radical text can't just depict monstrosity, but must be itself monstrous. Oddly, I don't think that's too hard. All texts are monstrous, really, always more chaotic and less coherent than they pretend to be, but most writers smooth over the stitches that hold them togetherâ€¦Electronic texts can be as predictable as any others, but the medium can support really radical kinds of indeterminacy--texts that attach themselves parasitically to other texts, that morph over time, that invade non-literary parts of your online reading, send you phony emails, or erase your hard drive. I'm not saying they should, just that they could, and wouldn't that be monstrous?"

Sorry that the quotation is so long, but I found it interesting. On Monday there was definite anger about having to work to get Lexis to work. Imagine if loading "Lexia" did something far more dramatic, like freeze your computer for three days or something really vindictive like destroy all your pictures. I know that's just a tad outside of realistic, that an author would construct such a text and then expect it to be readâ€¦but still. The idea of electronic texts as super-monsters is pretty cool.

I'm really bad at logging in

Submitted by thisismycheese on 14 October 2006 - 10:46am.

That's not just a tad outside of realistic.

Because it's one thing for a book to invade non-literary parts of your life (in the ways they can change the way you think, see things, etc.)...that's very different from actually harming a computer, which is just vandalism.

This entry immediately brought to my mind a parallel between this and graffiti. There is culturally a difference between a vandal and an artist, and this--e-lit that damages someone else's (the reader's) property--seems to be the line for this particular field.
