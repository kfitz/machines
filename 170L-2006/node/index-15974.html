A small thought

Submitted by thenewblack on 24 September 2006 - 8:33pm.
  • books
  • Classes
  • Michael Joyce

As I read through Joyce's "Othermindedness" or rather the selection thereof, I'm intrigued by the connections that he makes between the filtration systems of the internet (Google, "metasites," etc.) and the filtrations that occur in the "print culture." He writes, "Almost invisibly in the past, for instance, most library patrons read much more of the online or card catalog entries, book spines, or tables of contents than they read from the volumes themselves. People have only so much time. They can't read everything and so they depend on others to link them to what they need or wish to read" (54). It seems then that according to Joyce, mediation has always been a factor in the way that we receive information, but this mediation has never been so overt as in the case of the internet.

The bottom line of any literature, be it hypertext or a papyrus scroll, is: is the writing good? Joyce is a good writer, which is what makes Afternoon impossible to dismiss.

some thoughts on Joyce

Submitted by Lulu on 24 September 2006 - 3:23pm.
  • reading response

Reading Michael Joyce's "Afternoon: A Story" was confusing, to say the least. I was very anxious while reading it, and there was a point at which I kept going in circles. I tried going back and changing my choices, but I'd end up on the same several pages again and again...it took me a while to backtrack enough so that I could end up somewhere new. If there's no coherent plot, does that make this "story" still a story? I understand Joyce's point that we should participate in the creation of a text and give our own meaning to a story, but if that detracts from our comprehension of the story itself, then is the exercise futile? I tend to agree with thisismycheese's point that Joyce seems to be using the technology for the sake of it, rather than because his particular "story" is well-suited for this medium.

My experience reading "Afternoon" has been incredibly frustrating, and as a result, almost enlightening--as a lot of other people have mentioned, it's made me realize how attached I am to the physical book. I'm also reacting, I think, to how basic (read: limited) the interface is.

The lack of a table of contents that lists every single page, for example, gives me a headache. When I'm reading a book, I earmark pages I want to find again later, or write down page numbers, etc., while in "Afternoon" there isn't really a way to do that (unless there is and I've just missed it). Similarly, I hate having to backtrack through all the pages I've been to in order to get back to where I started. (Maybe we're not supposed to get back to where we started.)

books and hypertext are both cool

Submitted by Lulu on 24 September 2006 - 1:27am.
  • readings

The discussion in Landow about hypertext and poststructuralism makes me question if deconstructing the linearity of traditional printed material necessitates rendering books obsolete. Several people, including Gunslinger and Frabby, have already expressed uneasiness at the idea of hypertext replacing books. I also have a fond attachment towards books. I don't necessarily agree when critical theorists like Derrida make statements like "changes in media have eroded the power of the linear model and the book as related culturally dominant paradigms." (47) Why is linearity necessarily a bad thing? Does the discovery of a new, more free form of reading texts have to involve antagonizing the previous traditional forms? I feel like many of these poststructuralists truly underestimate the power of human attachment, memory, and emotion--and the historical cultural power and force of the book. Just look at some of the previous entries with people's testimonies, like gunslinger's image of reading a good book at the remote family cabin or frabby's comment about parents reading their children bed-time stories. Many of the critical theorists in Landow's piece are suggesting that the new technological developments are overdetermining the end of the book age. Derrida even declares that "the end of linear writing...is indeed the end of the book." (47) I feel like the effort to decenter the author and institute active reader participation has been flawed in that it privileges these new media developments without considering the powerful, emotional pull that traditional print material has on people. Book culture and the hypertext culture don't necessarily need to be antagonistic forces.

After books

Submitted by Frabby on 23 September 2006 - 6:11pm.
  • readings

I share some of the feelings and concerns that gunslinger and marmalade have voiced. Not only am I very attached to the act of physically reading a book (I can't stand reading things online, I always print them out), but I like answers. And the types of fiction that Joyce discusses provide very few answers, as Marmalade points out. Yet before we can start jumping up and down angrily, he hits us with, "What we see as senseless beauty may be the emergence of as yet unrecognizable new ways of making sense" (63). So we just don't get it right now? I can buy that, I'm sure if I were born in a different era, I would be preaching orality with everyone else (though I'm still impatient for my own understanding of literacy to expand). But it still seems like a bit of a cop-out: it's too amazing, it transcends us, and we can't grasp it.
