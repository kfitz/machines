Standards of transparency

Submitted by crashingintowalls on 24 October 2006 - 4:22pm.
  • art
  • games
  • traditional fiction

A lot of this is riffing off our discussion in class on Monday, particularly my spin on things. If this doesn't appeal to you, stop reading here.

In class, I was trying to put forward the idea that transparency is at the heart of how we determine the quality of a narrative or a game. Taking the angle of Janet Murray's essay, a narrative has more emphasis on plot and while a game prioritizes the actions of the user. This defines a number of continuums with the most paradigmatic games on one side and the most traditional stories on the other. What we started to work with on Monday was what makes for a good story and what makes for a good game, and is there a connection between the two?

I've been rolling it over, and I stick to my guns that a good narrative is good for the transparency of its world. The world may contain gnomes with rocket launchers, but these minute militants will be justified somehow. Ultimately, the degree of realism is not important, but the consistency is. the contract that a reader enters into asks that once the fictional world comes into being, it must behave in predictable ways to maintain a suspension of disbelief.

This jives with the definitions of "narrative" - as different from "game" - that we have been using. A narrative seems to exist in front of a "reader," thus the idea of a consistent world makes a lot of sense. The difference in a game is that the reader is more "inside" a game. With a feeling of agency, the player (participant? gamer? anybody know a good way to talk about someone playing a game?) has a more limited scope: individual events impact require a response that is pertinent to achieving the goal of the game, and overall continuity is not as pragmatically significant. Maxim's comment that the rocket launcher is "sweet" works here, as advantage is primary in a game situation.

Narrative continuity is significant only so much as it impacts the means and ends of a game. Games like tic-tac-toe or even counterstrike do fine with little or no backstory, and even a questionable plot can be put aside so long as the crosshairs stay centered. What is not acceptable is ambiguity in the interface. If games are about agency, then moments of not being able to exert that agency violate the contract of the game. Let us take the example of not being able to pick up an item in Zork. So long as this comes with a justification ("your backpack is full"), but "there is no such thing" represents a failure. If this were the real world, I could think of that thing on the table as "the brick" or "the clay" or "the brown thing" and I could still pick it up. As human beings, we are used to being able to exert agency on the world around us, or at least to know why that agency is frustrated.

Now (just to put more things in play), this standard of transparency seems very out of place when applied to "literary" writing or other things considered "art." Visual or literary inventiveness often challenges expectations. For instance, what do we do with novels that reference themselves or movies in which two hands appear to repair an apparently torn filmstrip? These moments are interesting, mainly as violations of the suspension of disbelief that we take on. Thus far, I cannot think of an equivalent artistic violation in a game (perhaps because of primacy of "rules" here). Well, this lacks any kind of a conclusion. Thoughts?
