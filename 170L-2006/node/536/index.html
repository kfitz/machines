Voices of our Generation

Submitted by tophat1 on 28 November 2006 - 11:08pm.
  • an article i read

My mom saved me an article from the July issue of "Time" magazine about the current young (under 40ish) writers. While I don't usually read all the articles my mom saves up, I decided to procrastinate and read it through. The author is lamenting the loss of a group of young writers who will define the current generation. She begins the article by saying David Foster Wallace, Jonathan Franzen and others are a little old to be defining the current young generation. Of course, we can take issue with that as we like (I absolutely LOVE Franzen and others in his "group" and I can certainly identify with them and their writing) yet the question is: where are the young writers?

The author of the article says there isn't anyone now (under 40 even) that seems to define a generation like, for example, JD Salinger or Fitzgerald seemed to do. She says "Every once in a while a novel comes along that makes everything else feel dated, that feels as current as tomorrow's e-mail, that gives readers the story of their own secret ineffable desperation with such immediacy that it induces spontaneous mass recognition as the Voice. Every once in a while-but not lately."

She suggests that some young writers may be lost in MFA programs or lured away from novels by TV script-writing. It made me wonder whether some fine young meat may be lost in Hollywood but I guess it doesn't really concern me that much. I don't keep up on the young, up-and-coming writers very closely but I'm not worried that there aren't some fantastic ones waiting in the wings. As for the idea that they can speak for our generation, I'm not sure anyone can speak for an entire generation. Rather, it seems that a great writer is situated in a historical context and their work reflects some aspect of this context, making it seem as if they are, in a sense, "speaking" for a generation.

My mom saved this for me

Submitted by marmalade on 29 November 2006 - 9:42pm.

My mom saved this for me too! That is really funny!

Actually, this article is what finally kicked me in the butt and made me read Zadie Smith's On Beauty this summer (good, if in some ways depressing, book by the way)--Smith was one of their recommended voices of our generation, although they offered the caveat that her topics were perhaps too obscure to appeal to the general public.

And that was sort of the most interesting part of the article to me--the idea that there was no longer a cohesive American public to which a single author could appeal or for whom a single character could be representative. The example I remember was that all (reading) Americans in the 1940s were able to connect (in some way) with white, wealthy, royally-screwed-up-and-spoiled Holden Caulfield... but there just isn't that sort of unity (homogeneity?) to America anymore.

Cool, I say.

Writers and reputations

Submitted by magoo on 30 November 2006 - 4:39pm.

One earns a living differently writing than people did in the days when many of the authors we admire wrote. Vonnegut used to talk about how he got by and got a reputation as a writer by contributing to Ladies' Home Journal. Payments offered writers by magazines have actually dropped in absolute terms since the 1970's.

As media and voices multiply, being one voice or being paid exclusively as a voice in that sense would seem to become more difficult, perhaps even less meaningful.

Do you-all still dream of a bestseller or a Nobel Prize?
