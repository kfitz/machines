Another collaborative authorship project courtesy of wiki software

Submitted by Oz on 13 November 2006 - 8:28pm.
  • collective memory
  • wiki software

I came across a wiki-type site called Memory Archive, the encyclopedia of memories. The stated idea here is that: all the people, like you, who are alive today and who bore witness to the significant events of our age, will pass on. Our collective memory -- yours, mine, all of ours -- will go into the great beyond with us, vanishing forever. Over the coming decade, century, and millenium [sic], your experiences will slowly dissappear [sic]. The past, they say, is a foreign country -- distant, strange, often unknowable. Unless you have a map. Your memoirs, recorded and shared at MemoryArchive, are that map for future generations. Go ahead. Tell the future how it was to be alive nowâ€¦You've got a story. Make it history. So essentially, this is a wiki software gone memory aggregator: people add "memoirs" about "the significant events of our age," and these memoirs are broken up into categories. For example, I could read all the collected memories about Barack Obama, about the year 1978, about the assassination of JFK, about bra stuffing.

This memory encyclopedia recalled me to Shock and Awe's post on the "One Day in History" project, where every blogging citizen in the UK was asked to contribute a blog entry about their day which will become part of a "mass blog for the national record." It seems like MemoryArchive, on an infinitely larger scale, is doing the same thing: seeking to construct a historical narrative around individual voices -- as many individual voices as possible -- by gathering as many remembered accounts of the same person, time period, event, etc. as possible. An interesting twist to this particular incarnation of the wiki software is that once you submit a memoir, and it undergoes a formatting and indexing process, it is then locked, i.e., no further edits will be made unless you request them.

In both these cases, the ultimate goal of collaboration seems to be the involvement of the collective networked body, and I wonder if this speaks to some sort of ideal that's held for collaborative authorship or if it has more to do with the nature of historical memory.

But is this collaborative

Submitted by marmalade on 14 November 2006 - 9:43am.

But is this collaborative authorship? I would hesistate to say so. It is definitely intertextual and subject to some element of editing ( formatting as you call it), but I don't know that I would call that collaboration.

For me, collaboration involves allowing someone else (multiple others, perhaps) to affect what the text says. And if the memoirs are locked, then no one is allowed to affect them. They are just as isolated as if they were a print book written by a single author.

You're right, marmalade --

Submitted by Oz on 14 November 2006 - 12:37pm.

You're right, marmalade -- it was a fast and loose application of the term collaborative, which is an easy trap to fall into with a lot of the recently coined genres, definitions, etc., thrown around in this class and in our readings.

I would, however, stand by my use of the term with regard to these historical memory projects, which I believe could even fall under your rubric of "allowing someone else to affect what the text says." Even though the individual entries are closed, discrete units, this vastly polyphonic approach to recording history strikes me as extremely collaborative in contrast to, say, your average history textbook or presidential biography. I mean, if you have 300 versions of the attack on the World Trade Center, or of The Beatles's last concert all collected in one place, isn't it inevitable that they will affect, rewrite, and revise each other? Oh wow...I just thought about what it would be like if this were in standard wiki format, and one person posted a memory of the bombing of Pearl Harbor, and then other people were allowed to edit it. Can you imagine? It would be anarchy...memory is so personal that every single editor would have to revise and recontextualize depending on details as small as where they were standing at the time the event occurred. Would their be any way to collaboratively author individual memories other than this, by grouping them all in the same place and allowing them to be read against each other?

Maybe that just says that

Submitted by marmalade on 14 November 2006 - 3:47pm.

Maybe that just says that collaborative authorship doesn't work for every project or every genre? Memory and memoirs are so personal!

I would say that the historical memory projects are perhaps more "intertextual" than collaborative--they can be lain beside one another and examined in the light of each other's content. They are collaborative as a whole in the sense that an anthology is a collaborative work, but each individual entry, memory, essay, etc., in that anthology isn't really collaborative--it has its own distinct author. But I guess I'm splitting hairs here--this is what my final paper is going to be about, and I'm thinking about this subject way too much these days already!
