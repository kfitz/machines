Feeling Sheepish

Submitted by tophat1 on 23 October 2006 - 8:59pm.
  • video games

I used to be a huge fan of Super Mario Brothers (back when we played on the old grey Nintendo box). Today, I was remembering how, after my sister and I had been playing for an entire afternoon, the competition between Mario and Luigi would get pretty intense. At times, I would find myself leaning to the left and right and jumping up and down as if, by moving my body, I could somehow more effectively move my player (I always had to be Luigi because my sister was older). Sometimes, during her turn, I would press the buttons on my controller and jump up and down still believing that I could control her player. Maybe I was just a little slow or caught up in the passion of Super Mario Brothers, but either way it seemed like I was really doing something at the time.

Then, a couple weeks ago, I read part of Daniel Wegner's piece called "The Illusion of Conscious Will" in which he talks about a very similar, yet more extreme, experience. He was at a store and, while waiting for his family to look around, he walked up to a video game and started "playing" until, quite sheepishly, he realized what he had really been doing was watching the pre-game demo and moving the joy stick.

While Wegner's piece discusses free will in general, I think the relation to video games is very interesting. I think it speaks generally to the semi-mystifying concept of agency that we've been throwing around recently. We generally don't like the games that keeps us too much in the dark. We move a step west and then a step back east but we're not in the same place, for example. Perhaps in many games, we feel like we're pressing buttons and making moves of our own volition but really they were predetermined by the game-makers. It doesn't mean the games aren't fun, but it does mean we sometimes get the illusion of control (hopefully in a more subtle way than Wegner, and I when I was 8 years old)
