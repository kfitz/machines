Ramblings on journalism and the web

Submitted by Pimm on 14 September 2006 - 10:07pm.
  • newspapers

I went on a trip, sponsored by the Career Development Office, to visit the LA Times today. Four other students and I toured their printing presses and main offices as well as spoke to a few journalists who work there. I write about this trip because two of the people who took us on the tour, a Pomona alum and a tour guide for the Times, seemed sensitive and disapproving of online journalism when students brought it up. Their reaction is justified in one sense, because, as they told us, they don't know how to get money from the online editions. Should they charge for any access to their newspaper site? Should they charge for access to back issues of the paper, like the New York Times does? Or should they just let their website open to the public? As they said, even if they restrict access, it's often easy to get old articles by searching certain words or authors through Google.

They mentioned that some previous students had told them that the internet was taking over and that there would be no need for a print edition in the near future. We were a more optimistic group, I suppose, for the continued support and use of a print edition, but I'm still unsure what the future will bring. Two summers ago I worked for a website for a fairly large newspaper near my hometown. The website was devoted to a specific topic that readers find interesting, and my two editors were extremely excited about the project because it allowed virtually unlimited posting of photographs and video to accompany the articles that two other staff members and I wrote. Their enthusiasm spread to me, and I could see why journalists were worried that online journalism was taking over. But here's the thingâ€¦the website (though it won an award) fell through.

I spoke to one of the other students on the trip about this debate, and he said that a lot of the good journalists now have their own blogs. So perhaps my rambling has no point, but I think this discussion ties into our class conversation on where web technology is taking us. I'm still left with so many questions. Do you think that newspapers will soon be completely "mediated" through web? Will they somehow try to make you think you are actually holding a paper, like the new way Macs try to make you feel like you are actually moving things on the desktop or flipping through albums on the new itunes? Or will print prevail? And what are journalists' blogs doing for newspapers? I'd love any feedback!

blogs, News, commodification.

Submitted by magoo on 18 September 2006 - 10:41am.

This is a real problem, and not just for the newspapers. At this point, blogs that deal with news remain dependent on established professional news services -- not just CNN or the NY Times, but API, Reuters, et al.

I doubt I'll miss the cant or conformity enforced by marketing realities. But we may all miss the underwriting of investigative field reporting itself. Because these institutions could charge for services, they could afford to send people and equipment across the globe, maintain offices to process that information, etc. What happens when they cannot do so?

At the same time, some kind of alternative or even pirate news network seems vital. I wonder how this resolves.
