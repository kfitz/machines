highbrow lowbrow

Submitted by Frabby on 5 October 2006 - 2:09pm.
  • hypertext fiction

Reading "Lexia to Perplexia" and "Disappearing Rain" close together was kind of a disconcerting experience because they were SO radically different in style and content. I showed my friend "Disappearing Rain," and he was not impressed. "Rain" definitely takes a much friendlier, easier to swallow form, and I think that one could end up thinking less of it because of its silly (maybe?), soap-opera like characteristics. I wouldn't say that it changed my life, but it was still interesting. It was also actually enjoyable, like reading a fashion magazine, because it was straightforward. I'm not saying it was simplistic, just that it was certainly no "Lexia."

I don't think things have to be convoluted to be worthwhile discussing. Certainly, Larsen's idea about our computer plugged-in generation deciding to leave our families and literally exist in the computer as bouncing electrons is provocative and would be an interesting discussion. Were people also provoked, or did everything just think it was stupid (I sometimes felt that way as well)? On the other hand, it kind of pales in comparison to Memmott's crazy smart presentation. I've noticed that we haven't been discussing "Rain" on the blog. Is that because Memmott left us so confused/intrigued that we want to focus our attention on that? Or because we don't feel "Rain" merits attention?

I agree that there might be

Submitted by marmalade on 6 October 2006 - 8:33am.

I agree that there might be some interesting questions that Larsen is trying to raise, however I just was totally unimpressed with the piece in general.

I was turned off most notably by the "quality" of Larsen's writing--it felt incredibly disjointed (even more so than the other hypertexts we've read). I just wasn't convinced that she knew where she was going with it at any one point. For instance: Was Anna trapped unwillingly in her computer? Was it the scheme of credit card companies? Did she do it willingly as a member of some sort of cult-movement? I felt like all of these were presented as possible explanations, and Larsen really never decided which one she was going to go with. It wasn't even as though she were playing with her readers in a sophisticated way--something that I might credit Michael Joyce or Stuart Moulthrop with doing. It was as if she just couldn't make up her mind and was too lazy to tidy things up as she went along. It's not even as if any of these endings were particularly deep--they're all a bit silly. I guess the best description I have is that I was convinced the whole time that I was reading a collaborative piece with different factions pulling for different endings. Sort of like our wiki, just gone very very wrong...

And then there's the style issue... If I wanted to read pages and pages of corny clichÃ©s, there must be some Babysitter's Club still in the Claremont Public Library...

I just couldn't take "Rain" very seriously. So while there are certainly interesting themes in it, I had a hard time getting past its basic composition.
