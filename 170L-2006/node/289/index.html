Voice

Submitted by marmalade on 28 October 2006 - 11:42am.
  • readings

So I read "Kind of Blue" yesterday, and it got me thinking about voice.

The first installment--Skip's come-on email to Suzanne-- dear Lord, do you know what I mean by 'cringe' reading? I think, personally, the best example (albeit I'm not thinking of the book here) is Bridget Jones' Diary. Where you feel so
embarrassed for the character that you have to close one eye and sort of hold the book a bit further away? I wasn't sure if I could get through the rest of the emails at that rate--my eyes would be too squeezed shut, and the computer screen would be all the way over on the other side of the room. He was so hokey... so clichÃ©d... so totally embarrassing himself... I felt so badly for him!

But it did get better from there. I almost died laughing at Ernesto's "chin up" email... the caps lock? wow. that was pretty great. I'm thinking of such gems as:

"I tried to give Olivia a call and let her know of your predicament but before you know it the BIBLE BEATER was telling me about our DAMNATION, me Darlene and the kids all and I just had to HANG UP before I could even get a word in edgewise so I'm not sure that she'll be much HELP. Jesus fucking Christ, Bert, if you ever get better you and I are going to have to POOL OUR RESOURCES and hire a SPECIALIST team of COMMANDO DEPROGRAMMERS to go down there and RESCUE OUR LITTLE SISTER from that fucking CULT that has taken possession of her BODY AND SOUL in TENNESSEE."

Now that gave me a pretty good laugh. I mean, yes game theory is interesting, but it doesn't make me chuckle. And this was such a totally different voice from Skip's....!

Rettberg manages to do a lot of different voices, pull off a lot of different styles. In addition to those two contrasts, there is the check-in questionnaire for Bert, the police emails back and forth, the poetry. Most of the time I felt genuinely convinced that I was hearing from a new character with a new perspective (and a totally new written voice).

This is really hard to do--think about our wiki--we all have been struggling to come towards some sort of common "voice." Just look at our trouble with the beginning page... or even trying to tie together different strands together that vary in content, person, tense, etc.

The people at the Institute for the Future of the Book had been talking about this issue of voice, too. Here's a post. about it. And there's a comment by our very own KF at the bottom! Basically they claim that online fiction, particularly the collaborative forms exemplified in Wikipedia and other wikis, result in a sort of generic voice. The heart of their argument is as follows:

"Open systems naturally struggle to develop the singular voice of highly authored work. An open system's progress relies on rules to manage the continual process of integrating content written by different contributors. This gives open works a mechanical sensibility, which works best with fact-based writing and a neutral point of view. Wikipedia, as a product, has a high median standard for quality. But that quality is derived at the expense of distinctive voices."

Anyway, I'm not sure if I'm making a coherent argument here for anything in particular, more just observations on voice--how it makes a work enjoyable, how mastering it indicates (at least to me) the skill of the author, how a distinct voice is difficult to attain in an online and collaborative work.
