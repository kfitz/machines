Stalking Rettberg and Privacy

Submitted by Pimm on 31 October 2006 - 12:04pm.
  • Kind of Blue
  • personal blogs

I feel like I'm becoming an internet stalker. When I was looking up information on "Kind of Blue's" author, Scott Rettberg, I saw that he received his PhD from the University of Cincinnati. I'm from Cincinnati, and although I don't love my city, I get oddly excited when I hear others mention it, so I decided to pursue him. I found a link to his blog, and from there the stalking began. There was a link below to a site called "Jill" and, lo and behold, it was Jill Walker's. From looking at the sites, and his intrigued me in particular, you can read how they teach at the same university and about their adventures traveling to, say, a Turkish bath. Or you can scroll through his photos, posted on Flickr. That's right, you can see pictures of his dog, his vacations, his family on Christmas. Or you can look through an album of 10 photos of Jill, which has the heading "This set is mostly for me. Sometimes I want to see my girl." That didn't stop me from looking.

Now if I'm not creepy...well, I don't know what I am. The entire time I read through personal information or looked through his photographs, I felt uneasy yet unable to stop. I think my uneasiness stems in particular from thinking of Rettberg (or should I call him Scott, now that I've crossed a certain privacy line?) as a high-and-mighty author whose text we read for class, and because of this position, I shouldn't be looking into his personal life. But he posts the pictures and his personal blog, so he wants them read or seen, right? I still felt like I was invading his privacy. I saw one photo of Rettberg using his webcam to talk to Jill, with Jill's face on his computer screen. Talk about bizarre.

I'm not sure where I'm going with this, but my thoughts on looking at his blog made me think of Frabby's post on reading personal blogs and the issue of what the blogger shares and the reader's position. Where is the line of privacy, both in sharing information and in reading/looking?

So maybe this comment is

Submitted by silversprung on 18 November 2006 - 9:18pm.

So maybe this comment is just the result of too much time spent staring at Supreme Court cases in my attempt to write my Con Law paper, but here goes, anyway:

In his dissenting opinion in Plessy v. Furgeson (the infamous case that said that 'separate but equal' was indeed equal), Justice Harlan wrote that the Constitution "neither knows nor tolerates classes among citizens."

I think this is true not only of the Constitution, but also of the internet. I mean, in no other class I've ever taken has it been conceivable that the author of a text we've read (and not just read, but discussed and admired and commented on in our blog) will show up and join in the conversation. The lack of hierarchy present in this almost blows my mind. Wait-- scratch that 'almost'-- I think my mind just blew for real.
