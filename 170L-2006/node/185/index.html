Interaction as an Artistic Goal

Submitted by Natwwal on 8 October 2006 - 7:25pm.
  • interaction

We've spent a fair amount of time discussing the interactive aspect of electronic literature, and the way that this manifests itself in blogs and hypertexts. It strikes me as ironic that while some in the literary community are so interested in developing an interactive text, this tends to be more frowned on in film and theater. In film, it is considered cheesy when characters speak either directly to the camera or to a voiceover narrator (a la "Donald Duck in Mathmagicland"). In theater, it is generally frowned upon to "break the fourth wall" by speaking to the audience. I am aware that there are plenty of exceptions to these generalizations, but I find the general patterns interesting.

Interaction

Submitted by Frabby on 8 October 2006 - 7:48pm.

What would interaction as it exists in electronic literature look like in film or theater? The term "break the fourth wall" doesn't seem to apply to the type of interactivity that exists in electronic mediums. That sort of interaction is more of an acknowledgement of a viewer. The narrator in Mathmagicland is not inviting viewers to voice opinions or to change the course of the movie, the narrator is simply not pretending that the viewer doesn't exist.
I'm not sure if interactivity could even exist in a film...I've been to plays where the ending is determined by the audience, but those were cheesy murder dramas rather then "high" art...

hypertext film?

Submitted by Natwwal on 8 October 2006 - 8:42pm.

Good point. It's quite possible that people react negatively to things like movie characters speaking to the camera because they represents a kind of false interactivity. Maybe film and theater would be more open to the idea of interactivity in their mediums if there were more clearly established ways to achieve it. I could imagine a hypertext-like film being made, but I'm unaware of anything like that that has actually been produced. Some video games may resemble what a hypertextish animated film would look like, but that's about as much as I can think of.

I'm betting you've already

Submitted by silversprung on 9 October 2006 - 11:15am.

I'm betting you've already thought of this, Natwwal, but postmodernism (although I hesitate to say all of its manifestations have this characteristic) is often interested in breaking down accepted artistic conventions-- including the fourth wall in theater-- in interesting, (hopefully) non-cheesy ways. Postmodernism is hyper-aware of art's status as art, and its relationship to creator, audience, and society-- and it likes calling our attention to these relationships. (I'm thinking here of-- what else?-- Infinite Jest-- particularly the films of James Incandenza.)

It sometimes seems to me that postmodernism has changed things so that the conventional thing to do is to break with convention, and the unorthodox action is the one that abides by or returns to convention.

hypertext fiction, interaction, 4th wall

Submitted by magoo on 9 October 2006 - 2:19pm.

But many things break the 4th wall:

In theater, look at Bertolt Brecht, (if any can still be found) Jerzy Grotowski, Antonin Artaud, and Augusto Boal.

In fiction, try Kurt Vonnegut, Martin Nakell (Two Fields that Face and Mirror Each Other is a superb text and a pretty friendly read, despite the title and the fact that almost no one has read this book), or If on a Winter's Night a Traveller -- I'm spacing on the author. Oh! Gunter Grass, too. And Rabelais, come to think of it.

Of the lot of them, Brecht and Nakell seem closest to being able to handle severely serious events in this way.
