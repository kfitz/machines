Pynchon <3's Plagiarism

Submitted by night owl on 15 December 2006 - 2:55am.
  • links
  • plagiarism

I haven't really been following the Ian McEwan plagiarism ordeal, but I happened upon (i.e. Google News pointed me toward) this article in the UK's Daily Telegraph. McEwan has been accused of plagiarizing multiple portions of a WWII nurse's memoir for his 2002 book Atonement.

Plagiarism is obviously a rich issue, but the main point I want to raise here is about its relationship to advancing technology. It's easy to imagine a future in which plagiarism (at least of the physical, word-for-word kind) would be impossible to get away with, for any amount of time. Not only will there be more people accessing and commenting upon written works, but, as more and more is digitized, there seems the opportunity for immediate, thorough and computerized cross-referencing. Will we then search more vigorously for writers to ostracize for plagiarism of ideas?

There's also the matter of the link. On the surface, it would seem to make giving due credit that much easier. But the fluidity of the mechanism complicates things. Does a hypertext link, which makes its target readily available, incorporate (perhaps violently) more of the target author's thought or content than would a reference on a written page? We can string together a few links and literally create a web of thought; to what degree is this authorship? Maybe more of a problem: at least for the time being, unless one follows a link, one has no more idea of the target's content or authorship than what the URL affords. How is that an effective mode of citation?

To get back to the article that prompted this post, apparently "a group of the world's best-known authors - including Thomas Pynchon, John Updike, Martin Amis, Thomas Keneally, Zadie Smith and Margaret Atwood" have written to the Daily Telegraph in support of McEwan. They've argued that it's impossible to write historical fiction without drawing substantially from previous works on the subject and that failures in attribution are, if not quite inevitable, then not entirely unexpectable. I'm inclined to think that their response is more a matter of resenting the inquest altogether than being anxious to defend McEwan for his theft, however unintentional it may have been.

Actually, the only thing inclining me toward this point of view is Thomas Pynchon's comically snippy letter (that's right, it seems to have been typewritten and mailed in). Although it's unlikely, I'm desperately hoping that Pynchon's the one who handwrote and underlined "From Thomas Pynchon" at the top of the letter. It's definitely worth a read.

(Incidentally, I've been trying to convince my roommate to agree to reading his shiny new copy of Pynchon's Against the Day in a movie theater running David Lynch's new film, Inland Empire. You know, just to see what that much West Coast pretentiousness might do to a person.)

Slate's Jack Shafer addressed this same subject, but came out conclusively against McEwan and his supporters. And there are obviously arguments to be made both ways, but Shafer's "damning" evidence is that the plagiarized words and phrases are not substantially altered in McEwan's work: "the Booker Prize-winner didn't even improve it."

First off, isn't that what plagiarism... is? Borrowed words and phrases which aren't substantially altered? Second, why is it that Shafer assumes that Ian McEwan, Booker Prize and all, should naturally "improve" the measured phrasing of a woman who actually lived the events? Isn't it likely that McEwan lifted what he did because of how perfect it already was?

But I forgive Shafer. I guess everybody has to write about something.
