Reading and Complicity

Submitted by silversprung on 30 October 2006 - 10:51am.
  • interactive narratives

I deliberately did not read Jill Walker's essay on Caroline (until 5 minutes ago) because I was hoping that I would have enough time to discover the end on my own. But given that getting to the end apparently takes a minimum of 24 days, and that people would probably talk about the end in class today, I decided to bite the bullet and read Walker's essay.

And I was genuinely surprised at what was going to happen to Caroline-- and especially surprised at my own reaction: I felt worried and sad. But then I asked myself why I should feel concern for someone who doesn't exist. I felt sheepish, like I do when I cry in movies-- like, why should my emotions be so easily manipulable? But then I realized that our culture considers it fairly acceptable to be emotionally affected by the fate of a character in a book-- or even a character in a movie or TV show. So why not an online character, too-- especially one with whom I have communicated with far more than I have ever communicated with a character in a book, movie, or TV show? I guess it just takes time for new media to gain legitimacy in our minds, even when what they're doing has ancient parallels.

Being sad at what was going to happen to Caroline-- and Jill Walker's formulation of reading as being "complicit in what happens" (307) and making us "partially responsible for what's happening in this simulated world" (307)-- reminded me of an experience I had as a kid playing Oregon Trail. When we first got the CD-ROM, I refused to give my characters real names. I was afraid that in doing so, I'd somehow be responsible for the deaths of real people if any of them died. So instead, because I hated the Power Rangers, I named them all the different Power Rangers (Blue Ranger, Yellow Ranger, etc.), and then tried to kill everyone as fast as possible. It was as if I couldn't strike the balance that the game wanted ("real" people, and bad things can and do happen to them): I had to either make sure that "real" people survived, or I had to kill "fake" people as fast as possible. I eventually got over this, and played Oregon Trail like a normal kid for several years. But I bring this up because I felt complicit in Oregon Trail in a way that the creators probably didn't intend-- so it's not surprising that I feel complicit now in OnlineCaroline, which more explicitly explores this phenomenon.

And now that I know that my continued "friendship" with Caroline will inevitably result in her disappearance/death, I am in a very uncomfortable position. Do I press the "Dump Me" button and "save" Caroline through ending our friendship? Or do I continue to be her friend, as a real friend would do, and sadistically watch her decline, which I know is in some way my own fault?
