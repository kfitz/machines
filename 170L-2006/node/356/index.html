Reading Implementation and Skin

Submitted by Pimm on 5 November 2006 - 3:07pm.
  • Implementation
  • reading response
  • Skin

Before I realized exactly what I'd be reading, I printed out the "letters"/pages from Implementation as if I'd be reading an article or a novel (which, actually, the authors claim it is). Boy was I mistaken. Some of the story lines were easy to follow, but often the dialogue completely switched topics from one paragraph to the next. I had thought it was a bit ambitious for the authors to say Implementation is a "novel about psychological warfare, American imperialism, sex, terror, identity, and the idea of place, a project." After all, that's a lot of topics for a short novel. But once I started reading, I saw that these sudden changes of topic and interweaving of stories allowed for so much ground to be covered.

Though I was often confused exactly what was going on in the story, I enjoyed Implementation for a number of reasons. The references to current events (war in Iraq and other random topics, such as losing virginity on Mormon church trips) and to my hometown of Cincinnati (as well as the pictures of Implementation posted there!) made me especially happy. I also enjoyed the random, funny bits, like "Frank has been lying since the day he was born. He would cry for his mother's breast, not because he was thirsty for milk but because he simply loved the feel of her nipple between his gums." The way the novel called attention to itself and its own use (like the discussion of the difficulty of removing stickers that black lace mentioned) was also cool.

Finally, I was surprised to see that the name of the novel was a place. Before I began, I thought the title was simply a reference to how people should basically "implement" this story in a physical sense by printing it and sticking it to places. Did anyone have other ideas on why the use of the name?

As for Skin, well...that was a lot harder to read. Because I couldn't read it. It made me much more appreciative of Implementation because, bizarre as its format was, it was readable and I was able to participate, if even as a much less active reader/user than the authors would like. Don't get me wrong, I enjoyed reading about the project and success of Skin, but I felt even more annoyed/mad that I wasn't privileged enough to read the story. It's interesting how both narratives work in a similar way: some people who see a section of Implementation on a telephone post might be dying to know the rest of the story, just as people who see someone with a word tattooed on them might be eager to know the rest of the story. It's just an odd feeling that though I've seen neither Implementation nor Skin anywhere other than my computer, yet I'll never see the full story of Skin. Hmph. I guess I need to get over it or write Shelley Jackson and get a tattoo myself (which would almost be impossible at this point).
