Hypertext and blogging

Submitted by magoo on 28 September 2006 - 4:59pm.
  • blogs
  • hypertext fiction

If reader freedom isn't relevant, & readers get used to any medium in time, the problem must be other.

I assume authors must do what authors and storytellers have done, but in a very different way. So what's that?

People tell stories. Each describes a series of events. So . . . what? The first story-event gives an impression; the next counters, corrects, modifies that impression. Thus, recounted events alter or supplement one's assumptions: one learns, perhaps introspects. A "good" story alters assumptions strongly about something interesting; a dull story alters little or deals with nothing of interest (cf Viktor Schklovsky, Vladimir Propp, Gerard Genette, among many others, BTW).

Interpretation by listeners, readers is active. Readers reframe the narrative at each sentence, each paragraph, each plot-point. Readers echo, recurse, iterate even around texts that do not obviously recurse themselves. In a sense, readers find a center to the text with each phrase; in another sense, they find no center to any text, even after coming to the end: There may always be another text.

However we describe it, readers, listeners, viewers keep creating provisional frames or phrases, interpreting, then creating other frames. You can probably catch yourself doing this visually as you glance around a room: What's in your attention, what's not (onBlur, so to speak)? Look again; that changes.

If the new incident is very like the old, and works from exactly the same assumptions, it can't alter the old assumptions. So the story grows dull. If the author or narrator tells the reader what to think of the story, and the author or narrator can apparently be believed, no change can occur, and the story grows dull. On the other hand, if the new incident bears no relation to the previous incidents, or does not seem necessarily related, the reader cannot apply the new incident to previous incidents, and so no alteration can occur: again, dull. Only to the extent to which the new incident seems inextricably linked to the old and yet the assumptions that encompass it necessarily vary from the old assumptions must readers revise old assumptions. This interests people, whether one limits it to "plot twist" or expands it to "epiphany."

Now, we seem to assume that certain parts of our sensory fields will retain durable characteristics. Speaking very roughly, these parts we call "things" and tend to refer to with nouns. Transitory parts or aspects we call events and tend to refer to with verbs. That's why we're willing to assume that a pronoun must refer to a prior noun, whereas we would assume no such thing about the verbs of succeeding sentences. Likewise, we tend to tell stories about a few things or people involved in various events. If we talk about many people in a single event, we generally break that down, telling about one story thread and then another.

OK, in hypertext fiction, readers seem to take succeeding lexia as random documents, possibly related. One possible reason is that we feel that if two things are equally likely to have happened, they can't be equally true of the same thing at the same time. So we feel it doesn't happen.

So cool things can be done in hypertext (I like them, anyway). But narrative's tough. Hypertext needs to establish sequence, causality, inevitability -- not interactivity. That done, it can and probably should play with indeterminacy as have written and oral narratives. In Wallace's novel, for instance, readers seemed to feel that something happened even if when they felt uncertain what. The same readers seemed to feel that nothing actually happened in Joyce's hypertext, even when they showed clear understanding of the literal events that Joyce described.

I wonder if this doesn't mean that blogdom will produce a workable form of online novel. It has a number of interesting and important characteristics, but most of all, it has sequence: One dates entries.

Indeterminacies and personal agency

Submitted by Oz on 29 September 2006 - 10:33pm.

Your points on active readership and what makes a narrative impactful--regardless of medium--will hopefully help straighten out some of the circles we've been running around in here, but I wonder whether the differing sense of and response to the above mentioned indeterminacies really is medium-dependent. The sense of frustration and self-blame that many of us experienced in chasing after Joyce's narrative does, I think, suggest an experience of "wreadership". It's not that I felt like nothing happened in "Afternoon". As a reading experience, it was more like the maddening feeling of trying to construct an argument for an essay and not quite being able to make your points add up to a conclusion you know is floating just out of your mental grasp, or of looking over the "final" draft of a creative piece you're working on and realizing that, if the narrative is going to work, you're going to have to write in an additional character. Or maybe three. When I'm turning pages, it's easier to accept uncertainties and things withheld because they come from an external source and are frozen in place on the page--it's uncertainty immobilized; when I'm clicking through lexias, I feel like I'm withholding things from myself.

hypertext fiction, agency, indeterminacy

Submitted by magoo on 2 October 2006 - 8:34am.

Frustration is not a necessary aspect of linked text per se, but may be basic to linked narrative. I'd like to think there's a solution, though, that it's as close to mental grasp as a thesis responding to Afternoon.

One can certainly create wreaderly text in print; the printfolk who do so use a rhetoric akin to Joyce-Landow-Bolter et al when they discuss their work. Charles Bernstein, Lynn Hejinian, Ron Silliman, John Cage, McCafferty, Leslie Scalapino, Susan Howe, Johanna Drucker, Haraldo and Augusto de Campos, Ignacio Pignatari, Mallarme, Cortazar, and Borges all play well in print with discontinuities.

How would you institute sequence into linked text? Better, I think, how would you preserve the sequential chronology of event, and its associated implications of irrevocable causitive relationships, and preserve the flexibility of hypertextual retelling?

I wonder whether the relatively inflexible form of blogs -- the dated entries, the chronological sequence of text that can be read out of sequence -- might hold something.
