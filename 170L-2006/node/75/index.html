Ong's technology of the word: insidious determinism and cultural/cognitive bias

Submitted by Oz on 17 September 2006 - 3:50am.
  • Ong
  • rant
  • readings

This post has been festering for a while, and the germ of it came out of Pimm's conclusion to a post on Ong: "I hope these thoughts and questions made as much sense in my writing as they do in my head!" My thoughts usually don't, or at least they don't make nearly the same sort of "sense" as the one they had before I translated them into writing. My thoughts are generally not scripted--rarely do they occur as a linear stream of words, ready to be spoken or written down. Instead, they tend to take shape as a simultaneous barrage of images and sensory impressions that I then have to sift, order, and translate into sound-images that are renderable in speech or text. Apparently, this classifies me as a "visual spatial learner". What this means for my reading of Ong is twofold. On the one hand, I think that he couldn't be more right when he tells his reader that "technologies are not mere exterior aids but also interior tranformations of conscious, and never more than when they affect the word" (81). Words absolutely transform my consciousness, and that's part of why I'm an English major; they have a texture, an artifactual thingness that is fascinating to play with. On the other hand, I find that Ong's presumed internality of language and script bear out in disturbing ways.

For one, the author begins by confining his discussion of orality and literacy primarily to "extant scholarship" (3), i.e., alphabetic, i.e, Western writing systems. This circumscription takes the form of a highly deterministic cultural bias. When Ong does give treatment, "at relevant points, to scripts other than the alphabet and to cultures other than just those of the West", it is largely to assert the ascendancy of literacy as defined by the West. Not only does the author tell us that the Chinese pictographic system is "basically time-consuming and elitist" (86), but he goes on to say that it will, as a matter of course, be replaced by the roman alphabet the instant the population of the People's Republic collectively master Mandarin. Further on, Ong relates Korea's transtion from Sino-Korean pictography to an alphabetic script as a fifteenth century imperial decree that took five centuries to go into effect, with the determining circumstance being "the greater democratization of Korea" (91). Both these instances are presented as deterministic inevitabilities that follow from the dominance of the Western system of thought.

It seems to me that this notion of Western primacy and centricity is associated with a more deep-seated bias toward linear, left-brained cognition. Ong states that the "more nearly total transformation of the word from sound to sight gave ancient Greek culture its intellectual ascendancy over other ancient cultures... that, more than other writing systems, the completely phonetic alphabet favors left-hemisphere activity in the brain, and thus on neuropsychological grounds fosters abstract, analytic thought" (89); that "the exquisitely analytic oral disputations in medieval universities and in later scholastic tradition into the present century were the work of minds honed by writing texts and by reading and commenting on texts, orally and in writing" (104); and that "a modern grapholect" (and it is no coincidence that Ong uses English [105] as his exemplar here), which "bears the marks of the millions of minds which have used it to share their consciousnesses with one another" (106), is, "in a profound sense," dominant over any dialect whose grammar is different from that of the grapholect: "It is bad pedagogy to insist that because there is nothing 'wrong' with other dialects, it makes no difference whether or not speakers of another dialect learn the grapholect, which has resources of a totally different order of magnitude" (107). Although Ong's introduction claims that this book, as a manifestation of the literate consciousness it traces, will work to upset some of our culturally ingrained biases, all I see it doing is affirming the hegemony of the left--the left-hemisphere and the left brain. How can Ong claim that the phonetic alphabet "is a democratizing script, easy for everybody to learn" (90), when pretty much anyone with a learning disability would beg to differ? (I'm having trouble finding definitive statistics, but the number certainly isn't insubstantial. Wikipedia ventures that between five and fifteen percent of the overall population have diagnosable dyslexia, and that's just one of the bunch.) Ong's discourse does not make space for other grammars.

But you know what? Not all thinking--even in the most highly literate of Western cultures--is directed by writing interiorized as print, and neither is the expression of thought. Not "everyone lives each day in a frame of abstract computed time enforced by millions of printed calendars, clocks, and watches" (96). I, for one, can personally attest to the documentedly poor temporal sense of those who tend toward spatial thinking--just try asking me the date. The numerous examples of visual poetry that Magoo cited certainly have a non-linear grammar; and Shock and Awe's response, along with the comments that follow, to the hypervisual and emotional organization (and yes, although We Feel Fine may, in the context of our literate universe, look like free association, those associations are driven by a logic that is complex, holistic, creative, and absolutely gorgeous) of We Feel Fine pretty clearly indicates that we, as readers, are open to a different sort of script, a new form of narrative organization. If the body of the text is shaped by our conception of it, then I'd like to think more about how to re-envision it.
