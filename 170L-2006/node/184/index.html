the body

Submitted by Frabby on 8 October 2006 - 12:02pm.
  • readings

We've already pointed out how Hayles talks lots about the importance of looking at print as a medium just as much as any other medium. That discussion got me thinking about the body in general. In "Material Met" she writes that there was an "emphasis on literary property as an intellectual construction that owed nothing to the medium in which it was embodiedâ€¦With significant exceptions, print literature was widely regarded as not having a body, only a speaking mind" (32).
Now Hayles is making a point about the importance of incorporating the material into the analysis of a text, not a point about the nature of physical bodies. But stillâ€¦it made me think. We've taken our own bodies out of the equation when we post blog entries, run websites, even have IM conversations. I'm not sure what my larger point about this is. I just think it's a pretty strange situation when you have relationships with people and you don't need to involve your body in said relationship. There's a strong desire to want to remove your flawed, unreliable, mortal self, and computers make that possible. Of course any mass-produced text allows the author to remove themselves from the readers' minds. But those authors weren't really engaging in intimate, back and forth relationships with readers. Now we even have online dating situations where people can engage in dating before dealing with the body. That seems a bit ridiculous to me and a wildly bad idea. Isn't the body half the equation?

I've never been able to get over the fact that, with IMing and blogging and other computer-facilitated forms of communication, we've been able to have "relationships" that don't involve our corporal selves.

I think about this a lot in terms of this class blog too. This is an interactive class blog. We read each other's entries. We comment on each other's entries, yet in "real life", not all of us know each other. For me, I don't have a "real-life" relationship with anyone in the class, yet I have "relationships" on this blog. We "talk" to each other. We're talking to each other now. This is real. This is tangible. Yet...it's not? Haha, weird.

Yeah, I'm not sure where I'm going with this, but I just think it's interesting that it's so easy to "talk" to each other anonymously, behind our pseudonyms, and in real-life, not all of us know each other.

I know it's always hard for

Submitted by marmalade on 9 October 2006 - 8:45am.

I know it's always hard for me to reconcile my real-life relationships with what goes on between me and others electronically. My sister on the telephone/my sister in real life/my sister over email are all very different incarnations of the same person. And don't mention romantic relationships (esp long distance ones)! What a nightmare it is to try and connect with people when all you have is this computer-generated dialogue--you can feel close to someone but not with the same amount of spontnaeity or reality, as otherwise. And then all of the misinterpretations that go on...! Plato was right--taking a text and making it autonomous--separating it from its context--these all somehow can interfere with our understanding one another's meaning. And that's what makes this sort of interaction a bit odd. I mean, maybe we're all slightly misinterpreting one another, and it will take several blog posts and a lot of time in between before we figure everything out...

Readings, body

Submitted by magoo on 9 October 2006 - 2:42pm.

I find that a lot of Web writing has more body in the sense that there is both more authorial control over visual appearance -- what I think Hayles means -- and more or at least more varied implied context -- that is, the relationships between reader and author seem more varied.

On the other hand, there's probably less body in that sense than in personal conversation.
