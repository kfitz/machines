Schemas that talk

Submitted by Oz on 30 October 2006 - 12:29pm.
  • categories
  • Douglas & Hargadon
  • schemas

I am writing a thesis on Henry James. At the beginning of this semester, I was talking with one of my thesis readers, and she recommended that, during this year, I try to be thinking about James with every paper I write. She said this, and I felt myself do the thing -- wide eyes and two or three quick head nods -- that means interest and assent, all the while thinking, "Is she nuts? I wouldn't even know how to try, and anyway, thinking that much about James would be deadly." The weird thing, as it turns out, is that I didn't have to try. Looking back on what I've written so far this semester, everything -- be it on a poem by Lewis Carroll, William Blake, a syllogistic proof, or this blog -- has been processing some Jamesian thematic or another. Or maybe I've just learned to see it that way.

This is partly to do with why I've been thinking, lately, about Douglas and Hargadon's discussion of schema theory. Not only do these schematics help me to think about the way my own mind is working, but they also apply to a whole lot of what we seem to be trying to process in this class and on this blog.

Like most of the class, I wasn't overly impressed with Caroline and Jemma. However, it occurs to me that most of our critiques of the sites have centered around the somewhat frivolous content of the stories rather than around the genre/medium itself. McLuhan would be displeased with us. It seems to me that it would be possible to create a very interesting story with this kind of format. There's no reason I can think of why these stories need to center around boys and clothes and alcohol. Hypertext and expectation

Submitted by crashingintowalls on 30 October 2006 - 11:35am.
  • hypertext fiction

So after coming down hard on onlinecaroline
and planet jenna over the weekend, I find myself in the camp with thisismycheese and Shock and Awe who had a much better time with Kind of Blue. I'm trying to put together the difference in my experience, as well as to perhaps begin a frame for some discussion today, and I have two angles on what separated these texts.

What I feel differentiates these hypertexts from those that we read earlier is that they do not readily admit to being hypertexts. We have two hypertexts posing as multimedia online friendships and one posing as a stranger's inbox that we can access. I, who had a good time with the first round of hypertexts, didn't immediately realize that this set of "New Writings" could be considered hypertext until I had been reading for a while. Online writing takes many forms, so it makes sense to me that personal websites and email are as much in bounds for hypertext as epistolary format is for the novel.

Reading and Complicity

Submitted by silversprung on 30 October 2006 - 10:51am.
  • interactive narratives

I deliberately did not read Jill Walker's essay on Caroline (until 5 minutes ago) because I was hoping that I would have enough time to discover the end on my own. But given that getting to the end apparently takes a minimum of 24 days, and that people would probably talk about the end in class today, I decided to bite the bullet and read Walker's essay.

And I was genuinely surprised at what was going to happen to Caroline-- and especially surprised at my own reaction: I felt worried and sad. But then I asked myself why I should feel concern for someone who doesn't exist. I felt sheepish, like I do when I cry in movies-- like, why should my emotions be so easily manipulable? But then I realized that our culture considers it fairly acceptable to be emotionally affected by the fate of a character in a book-- or even a character in a movie or TV show. So why not an online character, too-- especially one with whom I have communicated with far more than I have ever communicated with a character in a book, movie, or TV show? I guess it just takes time for new media to gain legitimacy in our minds, even when what they're doing has ancient parallels.

Kind of Seductive

Submitted by Shock and Awe on 29 October 2006 - 7:38pm.
  • hypertext
  • hypertext fiction

I've just delved into Scott Rettberg's Kind of Blue, and so far I'm impressed. It's the first hypertext we've looked at in which I really feel like the form is elevating the content. Kind of Blue could not function nearly as well in book form. It's the kind of story that needs to be a hypertext, and, as such, it doesn't feel forced--like the author is shaping the story to fit the form or vice-versa.

The device of emails from many senders allows the author to naturally assume many voices, and makes for the kind of story that I at least was seduced into trying to figure out. It's a simple concept: going through someone else's inbox and one common enough that it doesn't feel gimmicky. The multiple perspectives also give Rettberg tremendous freedom in his writing. You can tell he's having fun, throwing in cryptic snippets of poetry, lists, and the butchered spelling of children at different stages of keyboard adeptness.
