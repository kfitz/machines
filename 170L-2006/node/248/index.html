Flow in stories (not in membranes)

Submitted by marmalade on 22 October 2006 - 12:56pm.
  • readings

I keep trying to make connections between Juul's idea of "flow" and Douglas & Hargadon's idea of the "engaged reader," but I'm not sure if I'm doing a very good job.

Juul is the author who talks about "game time" and has the lovely graph that looks like a slinky. According to him, flow is what occurs when: "â€¦the challenges match the player's abilities and thereby lead to a state of flow (the player loses the sense of objective time â€“ time will fly)" (139). The player is immersed. But the point is hard to reach, Juul claims. A very exact balance must be struck:
"To reach a state of flow, a game must be neither too hard (which leads to anxiety) or too easy (which leads to boredom). This means the experience of time is tied not only to the play time/event time relation and to the challenged provide by the game, but also to the relation between game difficulty and player ability" (139).
But the idea still remains--flow means a loss of time, a total absorption in an activity. It's a trick I learned in art class--when you give up thinking with the left, analytical side of your brain and instead just use your right (the creative side, if you're a right hander), time will disappear (crashingintowalls--feel free to tell me if I'm wrong). Creativity doesn't measure minutes.

But this idea of getting lost in something doesn't just happen when I'm painting. It happens on long, long runs--but that's probably just chemicals talking. I'd say it happens most often when I'm reading. But then, there is a specific balance that has to be struck when I'm reading, too--something similar to Juul's balance between difficulty and ability. My biochem book does not count as a pleasurable read. The New Yorker does. I feel every minute crawl when I'm reading about lipids. Reading the newest Joyce Carol Oates short story is a different experience.

Now some of that probably has to do with a narrative--my biochem book doesn't tell me about the lipid family social interactions, what they ate for dinner, the catastrophe that was their daughter's Friday night date. Maybe some people get turned on by membrane fluidity--I find it mildly interesting but certainly not absorbing.

But, to get back to the readings, when we get to the Douglas and Hargadon essay, they bring up their own requirements for becoming absorbed by--i.e. establishing flow in--texts. For them it's schemas--basically patterns or plotlines into which the reader can fit events.

According to Douglas and Hargadon, it's easier to get absorbed--they call it "engaged"--when you know more schemas. We have more schemas when we've read more books--it's a bit of an elitist viewpoint, but most of the people reading D and H in the first place are probably unlikely to complain. But then, this isn't quite the same as what Juul is saying--there are not many challenges if we, "the advanced reader" can already write the plot off as "romantic comedy" or "horror story."

However, the two go on to claim something a bit more interesting: "Readers who enjoy engagement also tend to enjoy confronting situations for which they lack scripts, as these provide opportunities for learning, as opposed to merely performing one of a series of scripts within a conventional framework" (201). This strikes me as fitting into Juul's framework--well-read readers (those with high ability) can still be absorbed by familiarity, but their challenge (their difficulty level) is experimenting in new schemas, making new scripts.

I wonder how well these fit together--am I stretching? Am I doing the unthinkable and making games into narratives, tying together two alien categories?

There is always a bit of discomfort when experimenting in a new form. Think about our discomfort reading Joyce. Maybe we'll get better at it--get used to it (I had a conversation a while ago with Gunslinger about this point) as time goes on. Whether we want to or not, that's a different story. But maybe we can just say we're getting better at the reading game. ;)
