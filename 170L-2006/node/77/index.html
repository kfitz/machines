What Does Armstrong Want?

Submitted by tophat1 on 17 September 2006 - 3:02pm.
  • readings

At the end of her piece, "How Novels Think," Armstrong claims that "the novel of course was not made to think beyond the individual, but neither, on the other hand, was it made to reproduce the status quo." In the previous sections, she outlines the history of the novel and it's tension between individual and "other." In her description of late nineteenth-century novels she refers to a gruesome scene from "She" (which I have not read) in which the hero stumbles across a pit of skeletons piled atop one another and described in painful detail. Armstrong seems to take issue with these descriptions because they portray the destruction of the individual. She believes the pile of bodies represents a sort of collective consuming the individual.
Armstrong believes that this and other "phobic representations" have made it difficult for future writers and readers to create or imagine a society in which each individual has autonomy without encroaching on others. I'm not sure why Armstrong believes this is a necessary vision for the novelist. While it is a noble aim for a society, I don't really understand why it's a necessary aim for the writer. Also, what is the alternative to which she vaguely refers? "One that does not inspire phobia and yet is grounded in the world we now inhabit." After all, when Locke, Hume and Rousseau (all of whom she cites) discuss their philosophies of human nature, though often set up in fictional examples. they are referring to standards of humanity in reality, not in fiction. Why must fiction represent values in reality?

A novel is a mirror walking down the road.

Submitted by Oz on 17 September 2006 - 10:26pm.

I don't think that Armstrong is arguing for a certain representational ethic (namely realism), but rather for an understanding of the symbiotic relationship between the novel and its subject: "This book argues that the history of the novel and the history of the modern subject are, quite literally, one and the same" (3). That is, the novelistic portrayal of the subject we have come to understand as the modern individual is so pervasive that it's been interiorized by its authors, its readers, and the existing social structures. So the distinction between the aim of society and the aim of the writer isn't all that clear. Armstrong's philosophical citations support this contention--philosophic discourse doesn't have any sort of inherent truth-value or privileged access to "standards of humanity in reality". In general, philosophy makes use of and responds to other philosophical literature, or takes something like a "common sense notion" to use as a jumping off point for more philosophical literature; that's why Armstrong's incorporation of Locke, Hume, et al., or, likewise, her analysis of Reynolds's portrait and her passing mentions of the British socio-historical context, prove the capacity of the novel's private, individuated subject to embed itself as the "most basic unit" at all levels of culture. It's not necessarily that the author calls for or describes a break down of the fiction/reality binary, but rather that she is adding her speculations onto a looooong list of literature concerned with how those two realms engage each other.

As pretty much everything we've read so far has argued in some way or another, all of our technologies, all of our media, interact dynamically with culture and society. So when Armstrong says that the novel was not "made to reproduce the status quo" (25), she is saying that she wants a novel that can think with the same level of growth-oriented mobility that it desires for its subject.
