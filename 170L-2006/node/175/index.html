Now for a bit of optimism...

Submitted by marmalade on 7 October 2006 - 10:36am.
  • Hayles
  • readings

I almost stood up and cheered when I came across this passage at at the end of Hayles' first chapter:

"As we work toward critical practices and theories appropriate for electronic literature, we may come to renewed appreciation for the specificity of print. In the tangled web of medial ecology, change anywhere in the system stimulates change everywhere in the system. Books are not going the way of the dinosaur but the way of the human, changing as we change, mutating and evolving in ways that will continue, as a book lover said long ago, to teach and delight" (33).

Finally, none of these dire predictions about the end of books!

But I'm very interested in what this evolved category of books will include. Where is the book/literary criticism going according to Hayles?

In her Embodiment chapter, Hayles tackles this a bit, saying:

"Maybe now is a good time for a double-braided text where the generalities of theory and the particularites of personal experience can both speak, though necessarily in different voices. A text where both voices can be heard, at first very different but then gradually coming closer until finally they are indistinguishable." (108)

and:

"A critical practice that ignores materiality, or that reduces it to a narrow range of engagements, cuts itself off from the exuberant possibiliites of all the unpredictable things that happen when we as embodied creatures interact with the rich physicality of the world. Literature was never only words, never merely immaterial verbal constructions. Liteary texts, like us, have bodies, an actuality necessitating that their materialities and meanings are deeply interwoven into each other" (109)

Okay, so what does this all mean? Are artists' books the answer? Or stuff like House of Leaves? That first quote about interwoven texts screams to me "Hypertexts!"--but then how does one move from the currently distinct voices (like in the newblack's post about hypertext classics) to seamless, indistinguishable ones?

And the materiality issue--somehow, this materiality thing is the hardest for me to grapple with. Hypertexts are somehow terribly immaterial, especially in the sense that they are untouchable, aphysical. There is no way to make them substantial. They fail Hayles' "bathtub test" grandly. So, then, what are the criteria for materiality? Hayles advocates a totally new approach to criticizing electronic texts: "Electronic text had its own specificites, and a deep understanding of them would bring into view by contrast the specificities of print, which could again be seen for what it was, a medium and not a transparent interface" (43). But how does one take a medium and make it material?
