The New York Times is on the ball this weekend...

Submitted by silversprung on 17 September 2006 - 6:12pm.
  • academia
  • famous blogs
  • Lonelygirl15

I came across this article in the online edition of today's New York Times Sunday Magazine. It's written by Michael Berube, who is a professor of English at Penn State and writes one of the blogs that Professor Fitzpatrick has included in our blogroll. I noted that Berube's blog was not mentioned in the author's biography at the bottom of the article.

In this piece, Berube argues that although there are indeed many more liberal than conservative college professors (the ratio is something like 3 to 1), this is not because liberal academics are trying to keep conservative academics out of their departments, as conservatives like David Horowitz maintain. Berube tries to explain this ratio by suggesting that the applicant pool for arts and humanities professorships probably has the same 3 to 1 liberal to conservative ratio (and thereby insinuating that there's something about conservative beliefs that makes one less likely to want to be an academic in the arts or humanities. But Berube never actually says this, so he doesn't have to defend it. Clever.)

Berube ends with the following appeal to freedom of thought and speech: "as students, you should expect to feel uncomfortable about your beliefs as a matter of course -- that is, if your professors are doing their job properly, and keeping the floor open for every reasonable form of debate and disagreement." (For me, emphasis on the reasonable debate.) But I think Berube makes a good-- and not entirely PC-- point here. After all, there are many Pomona students (and to a certain extent, they're correct) who believe that they should not have to feel uncomfortable in their classes. Students shouldn't have to feel like they themselves are being attacked in their classes; but on the other hand, they don't have the right to never have their beliefs challenged-- nor would that, I personally believe, be favorable to the larger goals of education.

__________________________________________________________________

Because my preferred method of procrastination is reading the news, it was only a matter of time before I also came across this article, which is an examination of the Lonelygirl15 phenomenon. Tom Zeller, Jr., the author of the piece, seems interested in a question we hadn't really delved into in our discussion of this phenomenon: whether or not Lonelygirl15 qualifies as art.

The article cites several academics and experts (including E. L. Doctorow) who argue that what the creators of Lonelygirl15 were attempting to create was a "false document"-- the same kind of thing that Orson Welles was going for in his radio adaptation of "War of the Worlds," or that the mockumentary "This is Spinal Tap" was aiming for.

Does Lonelygirl15 qualify as art simply because there's a term to describe it? The experts disagreed on this point, with the ending quotation suggesting that Lonelygirl15 would have to maintain its audience now that the facade has disappeared in order to be considered art. But it seems to me that the facade of the real was an integral part of the Lonelygirl15 phenomenon. What do you all think? Is it art? Can it still be art now that we know it's not real?

I have a theory that most professors are politically liberal for two reasons.

1) Having more money tends to make people conservative.

2) Having more exposure to the world, to communication and ideas -- in short, more education -- seems to make one more liberal. (If you doubt this, look at a map of the red and blue counties in the United States, and compare that with a map that shows major transportation arteries. The blue matches the transportation arteries quite closely).

Of course, I'm one of those people who suspects that there are indeed aspects of conservative ideologies that would make people tend not to go into education, so consider the source.
