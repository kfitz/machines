"Why tlk whn u cn txt?"

Submitted by Lulu on 21 September 2006 - 11:36pm.
  • technology

I read this disturbing article about how text- and instant-messaging has replaced actual human contact among many people. It describes how people feel more comfortable with their online alias and conversing in the electronic world than actually talking face-to-face. One teenager even said that "face-to-face you run out of things to talk about." It strikes me as really sad that many people have lost their ability to be social human beings as a result of the internet and other technological gadgets. Some say they feel more in control when they're texting or IMing, and that in face-to-face situations, "you're not presenting the real you, you're just reacting."

This reminded of McLuhan's argument that technology is an extension of man and also slightly echoed Burnett's fears of technology replacing human processes. In this case, the alias or social life that people create online replaces their actual lives, and the total acceptance of this on the part of many people disturbs me a bit. Is the ability to create a semi-anonymous, faceless persona so appealing that we are willing to abandon the benefits of physical contact? I feel like there is a greater need among parents, institutions, educators, and other influential persons to stress the internet's ability to enhance rather than replace our social lives.

Did you notice the great

Submitted by Anonymous on 22 September 2006 - 10:00am.

Did you notice the great McLuhan moment when the Anthropology professor claimed that email/phones were a "cold medium"?

I guess this didn't disturb me quite as much as it did you--I agree that it's sad if people really have become less adept at basic socializing (just look at us trying to have seminar-style discussions in the IT lab--we're pretty much hamstrung by computers sometimes ourselves!). But the part that struck me most was how chatting and online communication was being used as a tool to help the paralyzingly shy--the last several paragraphs described how people in need of therapy, but who didn't feel really comfortable in a psychiatrist's office, could turn to online-therapy and blossom. Now, I agree, there are some interesting problems with online psychology--I don't know how effective it is going to be, I don't know if a psychiatrist can function without all of the physical cues/verbal overtones that a face-to-face conversation would entail, I don't know if anyone reputable would recommend this or consider doing it. But helping people is helping people, no?

The article also reminded me of Burnett, because it seemed to form such a stark contrast to his thesis--I'm thinking here about his love affair with the telephone, in particular. Remember the way he portrayed us as a telephone-/gadget-dependent society, and romanced on about how some of his most meaningful conversations had come on the phone, as a voice penetrated through his boby, mystically linking him to someone millions of miles away? He definitely didn't think telephones were "cold".

Did you catch the great

Submitted by marmalade on 22 September 2006 - 7:03pm.

Did you catch the great McLuhan reference by the Anthropology professor? He categorizes telephones/email as "cold media". Shows how far the guy has penetrated!

I guess I didn't find this article as disturbing as you--I found it ultimately rather optomistic. Now I agree--it's not a good situation if kids are growing up incapable of socializing. But I'm not sure if that's true (talk about a difficult thing to measure! ). We only see a few extreme examples in the article. And hey, aren't we all a bit tongue-tied by technology? Isn't that why we're having class in Crookshank on Mondays from now on? But, what about the promise that people are finding in the relatively "hot media" of chat and text messaging? I'm thinking in particular about the paralyzingly shy kid who was able to open up to his psychiatrist only online. However I should qualify this by saying that I don't know what sort of psychiatry someone can do over the internet--there must be something lost, what in physical expressions or tone of voice.

I also saw a Burnett connection--but I thought first of Burnett's love affair with the telephone. He seemed to find the phone as an incredible means of connection-- his companion's voice passed almost mystically through the phone into his body. Burnett seemed to find this sort of replacement perfectly acceptable--lovely in fact--particularly if the communication was occurring between people separated by large distances.

Anyway, lots of interesting ideas running around in that article. Thanks for the heads-up!
