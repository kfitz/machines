Joyce and the Search for a Hip Tech-Savvy Story That Works

Submitted by Shock and Awe on 24 September 2006 - 7:36pm.
  • hypertext fiction
  • joyce
  • readings

My experience with Afternoon was similar to Bird's and thisismycheese's, although maybe a bit more extreme. I wanted to take this Joyce fellow by the collar and shake him until a cohesive story that I could read front to back came out. The bottom line of any literature, be it hypertext or a papyrus scroll, is: is the writing good? Joyce is a good writer, which is what makes Afternoon impossible to dismiss.

Reading contemporary fiction I often feel that the writers are so desperate to create something new and original that they allow their story to suffer by trying to fit it into a hip modern frame, rather than working to make the hip modern frame fit into their story--or work as a component that makes it better overall.

A major part of my frustration with Joyce was the techonology itself. First of all, I'm repulsed by the gently-rounded metallic-blue world of Macs, but mostly I was just uncomfortable with the Yes/No, forward backward system of Classic and the way you could click on certain random things. It didn't feel like it worked right. Which made me feel like I didn't work right. I could tell that Joyce was a talented writer and that he had a shape and meaning in mind for his story, but I couldn't find it.

This led me to think about our grand wiki experiment. Technology and user aptitude have improved since 1986 to the point where our story will not need an instruction page (like Afternoon has.) Internet users know how to click through to a link while retaining their place in the story. So I do think it's possible for us, or some writer like Joyce, to do something very cool with hypertexts. The writer/reader of 2006 are much more on the same techonological page than Joyce/most-of-the-rest-of-the-world were twenty years ago.

I have been worried, however, by some comments I've heard in class about how neat it would be if every word were a link in our wiki, and the story some kind of glorious web. I guess what bothers me is that for me to give a crap about the technology or how innnovative and wild the format of the story is, the story itself has to first be good. The writing and story are still the bottom line, and no matter how awesome the technology gets, that's not going to change. When I look back and evaluate our class wiki I'm first going to look at how well the story works, then at how well the technology within the story works.

I agree with you that

Submitted by Lulu on 25 September 2006 - 12:58am.

I agree with you that writing should come first, and then the technology second. As you described, I also feel like we shouldn't get so caught up with the "hip, tech-saavy" aspect that we lose sight of the bigger picture: good writing and a good story.

Also, I echo your concern about the wiki. I think that in the beginning stages, it's perfectly fine to experiment by linking words to other sites. We're getting used to that new wiki-environment and it helps to just play around for a bit first, just so we understand the technical aspect and how to do formatting, linking, etc. Ultimately, though, I think it would be silly to link every word unless that word has relevance to the bigger picture. Wikipedia articles contain lots of links within the articles, but those links to other wiki-articles are only contained if they bear some sort of relevance to the topic at hand. It would be pointless if we linked words like "I" or "happy" or every other word in the sentence; it would visually look like a mess and I think it wouldn't add to the story/topic. In our attempts to be cute or witty with the linking, we shouldn't lose sight of the bigger picture.
