Our desire for interactive media

Submitted by Pimm on 2 October 2006 - 1:53pm.
  • electronic literature
  • technology

After reading some posts, like Lulu's on the Tamagotchi and Shock and Awe's on interactive fiction, and looking at the New York Times website, I'm curious how far this desire for electronic interaction with books, toys, news, etc will go. It seems weird to me that Michael Joyce's "Afternoon" can only be played in Classic on Macs and that, as we talked about in class last week, if Classic were no longer possible to use, his text would become extinct. Of course, from the examples of the Tamagotchi or the interactive fiction, his ideas didn't fade and morphed into different things. When I mentioned earlier that I looked at the New York Times page, I noticed one headline in particular--"Student Killed in Amish Schoolhouse"--that includes a video with the article. I always thought that online news sites provided video for articles that they thought people wouldn't read, but clearly this isn't true anymore (for instance, the article I mentioned is about such a awful, gruesome topic that I feel people will be drawn to the words in order to learn more). So what creates this desire to have electronic interaction? Why do people want more than simply to read words? By the way, I don't mean to imply that I think only hard, non-electronic news, toys, and books are better than interactive material. I love photographs and video that accompany news stories, and I'm a sucker for interactive, electronic toys. I'm just curious where the line will be drawn on this interaction, and, on a somewhat different note, why more hasn't been done to preserve the forerunners of hypertextuality, like the "Afternoon."

Loneliness and the Internet

Submitted by zoey on 2 October 2006 - 1:11am.
  • creation of online content
  • the nature of the blog
  • wiki

I've been stuck on this quote: "I am afraid that one of the things that makes the web in its current form so attractive is that so many of us are afraid and lonely and do not know what to think or who will hear us" (from Joyce's "Othermindedness" p52). I've been thinking about the theme of loneliness lately in connection with people who create content and put it on the web where it can be seen by a potentially huge audience. Why do people post intensely personal diaries of their life and why do people want to read them? Why do people want other people they've never met before to know mundane little details of their daily lives--where they went to eat, what they bought at Target, what song they're listening to while they're writing their entry? Maybe because these things all say something about the person who is reporting them; they convey some kind of information the writer wants you to acknowledge. But why do we want to acknowledge this information, to get some brief grasp on how somebody else lives their life, to speculate about what some teenage girl's life is like as with the whole Lonelygirl thing?

Interactive Fiction Competition and some words on gaming

Submitted by Shock and Awe on 1 October 2006 - 9:06pm.
  • interactive fiction
  • video games
  • virtual community

For those of you interested in further exploring hypertexts/interactive fiction, the twelfth annual Interactive Fiction Competition is going on right now. You can download and judge the entries. Interactive fictions of this sort present the reader with the beginning of the story. Then the reader types in actions for the characters to perform. The story responds by telling what happens as a result of these actions. Your decisions influence the outcome. It very much reminds me of a textual version of a video game like Myst. The reader is presented with an environment and must use their intelligence to navigate through it.

Now for something totally unrelated but still sorta kinda related...

Submitted by Lulu on 30 September 2006 - 1:08pm.

We've been talking a lot lately about the various issues that stem from reading hypertexts. All these discussions about reader participation and power dynamics in the hypertext world reminded me of something very random, but somehow related in a weird sort of way.

Does anyone remember these things? My mom got me one in sixth grade, and I was obsessed for several months afterwards. I would bring it with me to school and feed it, play games with it, clean up its trash--all so that one day it would grow up into the "ideal." Apparently, there's lots of different forms that each "pet" can ultimately grow into, depending on how well you take care of it. If you were a superstar owner, it would turn into a beautiful cat-like thing, but if you neglected it, it often turned into an ugly tadpole-like creature and "die" young. Basically our choices resulted in the final form.

Google and publishing

Submitted by marmalade on 30 September 2006 - 9:13am.
  • news
  • newspapers

So, I was being as un-librarian like as possible this morning and reading the news online, and I read this this short article in the London Times. It's basically about publishing companies trying to limit unpaid access to newspaper or journal articles--specifically by affecting the way that Google or other search engines assemble "hits" when they search.

The problem the publishers are facing, if I correctly interpret the article, is that search engines like Google are making money, linking users to online publishing, while publishers aren't making much if any profit from their offerings. They know they need to put their stuff online, but they also need to earn money somehow--and they think that maybe limiting Google searchers' access will somehow take them out of the red.

libraries and librarians

Submitted by marmalade on 28 September 2006 - 9:13am.
  • technology

My mother is a retired librarian. She tried for years to convince the university where she worked to install movable shelves--the old school kind that they have in the basement of Honnold. After she left, the school finally put them in--but the new-fangled sort, that move electronically, beep when they're about to crush you, and malfunction often. They've gotten rid of the card catalog, too. My mother loves that library, but everytime that she comes out, she looks confused and frazzled.

I remember Joyce's criticism that we've forgotten to really read and concentrate, getting caught up in the summary, the list in the card catalog, the table of contents. Maybe that's a flaw--but I don't think it's the real flaw. There's lots of newspaper stories about kids these days being unable to concentrate. And it's not because they've been using the card catalog--psychologists blame it on the computer. My mother's the best reader I know, and she has this ingrained technology aversion. She sits down and reads the New York Times (paper copy) from cover to cover every morning, and then reads the Atlantic, a novel, or something else just to fill up the evening hours.
