Quality conversation with chat bots

Submitted by crashingintowalls on 21 October 2006 - 8:35pm.
  • artificial intelligence

Reading Pimm's thoughts on the frustrating nature of conversation with Eliza I started thinking a lot about what makes for a quality conversation with a chat bot. As a cognitive science major, I've thought and experimented with this a little. More than satisfying or unsatisfying, conversational prowess gives a window onto intelligence, artificial or not, accurate or not.

Conversation as intelligence is the basis of the Turing Test and the Loebner Prize. The winners of this prize represent some of the best approximations of human intelligence, and are good indicators of where artificial intelligence is going. Notably, no computer has won the prize for being equivalent to a human yet - a "bronze medal" is awarded yearly for the best approximation relative to the competition. A.L.I.C.E won in 2004 (click on "Talk to Alice") and Jaberwacky in 2005. The emphases on emotional intonation, facial expression and speech recognition show that things are quickly moving beyond teletype.

Putting this in the context of "games," it seems that current A.I. is concerned with more than the strict input and output options that are part of the game. The immersive qualities that we associate with human conversation are coming into play, making me wonder if human intelligence is more of a "game" or a "story."

I wondered if you'd ever

Submitted by marmalade on 22 October 2006 - 12:07pm.

I wondered if you'd ever heard of Racter? I couldn't find a free version of it online when I just googled it, but it's a program that generates, well... some might call it poetry. Its creator--a guy called William Chamberlain--assembled some of its ... gems...into a book called The Policeman's Beard is Half-Constructed a couple of years ago. I was reading about this in Aarseth's Cybertext, and the book/the program apparently rather upset people--could a (basically) random-text generator count as a poet? This whole question was further complicated by the fact that Chamberlain was both the designer of the program and did some editing and working with the texts after Racter generated them--leading people to question how much of the work is really Chamberlain's? And, by ascribing it to Chamberlain, did that just provide away for escaping the question of whether Racter counted as an author?

As computer programs get better at this sort of stuff, Aarseth argues that we're going to have to figure out ways to critically analyze what they come up with--a new branch of critical theory.

And while Racter isn't quite independent/up to par yet, it's definitely a contender for the bronze medal in an authorship competition!

Random poetry generators

Submitted by magoo on 23 October 2006 - 10:00am.

Surprisingly interesting things have been done with various degrees of randomness in writing. McCafferty, John Cage, the Oulipo movement might be of interest here, as well as early Dada experiments.

Freud supposedly told Dali that what he found interesting in Dali's work was the conscious, not the unconscious. I wonder whether what's interesting in the random is not its interaction with agency.

On a totally unrelated note, a friend of mine once met Salvador Dali in L.A. on the RTD -- on the bus, that is She thought it might be him when she spotted the signature moustache; a look at the sketchbook where he was scribbling left no doubt.
Dali claimed to always ride the bus whenever he came to Southern California: "You meet the strangest people," he said, eyeing her significantly.
