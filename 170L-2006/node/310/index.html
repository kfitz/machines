Schemas that talk

Submitted by Oz on 30 October 2006 - 12:29pm.
  • categories
  • Douglas & Hargadon
  • schemas

I am writing a thesis on Henry James. At the beginning of this semester, I was talking with one of my thesis readers, and she recommended that, during this year, I try to be thinking about James with every paper I write. She said this, and I felt myself do the thing -- wide eyes and two or three quick head nods -- that means interest and assent, all the while thinking, "Is she nuts? I wouldn't even know how to try, and anyway, thinking that much about James would be deadly." The weird thing, as it turns out, is that I didn't have to try. Looking back on what I've written so far this semester, everything -- be it on a poem by Lewis Carroll, William Blake, a syllogistic proof, or this blog -- has been processing some Jamesian thematic or another. Or maybe I've just learned to see it that way.

This is partly to do with why I've been thinking, lately, about Douglas and Hargadon's discussion of schema theory. Not only do these schematics help me to think about the way my own mind is working, but they also apply to a whole lot of what we seem to be trying to process in this class and on this blog.

First, D & H explain schemas and schema theory thus: schema theory charts how information processing can shape perception and action alike, focusing our expectations and even determining the fine grain of our interaction with objects. Defined simply, schemas are . . . a cognitive framework that determines what we know about the world, the objects it contains, the tasks we perform within it -- even what we see. Schemas enable us to perceive objects and occurrences around us and to make sense of them efficiently by consulting our ready-made store of similar occurrences and understandings, which we gain from reading, writing, personal experience, and even advice we receive from others.

Then, the pair of authors elaborate on how schemas influence our reading practices: the pleasure of engagement with hypertext fiction comes from users' access to a wide repertoire of schemas and scripts, our attempts to discover congruences between the hypertext and an array of often mutually exclusive schemas, and, ultimately, our ability to make sense of the work as a whole.

The authors say "modernist works" could just as well stand in for "hypertext fiction" in the above passage, as could, in the context of our class, games or interactive fiction or cyberdrama or any of the other nascent generic distinctions we've been grappling with. The (mostly practicing) theorists of the texts we've been reading are looking to apply their existing schemas in some way that will deliver a cogent, coherent approach to understanding the work or the genre. As we've encountered more and more of those varied schematic approaches, our general response has been to want to broaden our definitions: one attempt to call for a sorting of e-texts into more clear cut generic distinctions led to a discussion of how unhelpful and potentially misleading those distinctions can actually be; Frabby's thoughts on categories explain how throwing a "wide net" over all these e-text definitions might actually be our best chance at getting at a "deeper understanding" of what they mean and how they work; after staring at the computer screen for long enough, thisismycheese started to feel like all the diverse, polyphonic pieces of schematic and thematic wandering we do on this blog might come to "develop into some sort of cohesive whole." Speaking of polyphony, Marmalade's discussion of voice in "Kind of Blue" hits on (although not in so many words) how the different voices in the email serial novel are compelling, in part, because they all interpret the narrative through a distinct schematic lens (love, drugs, filial drama, Buddhism, postmodern film/lit crit, etc.). On the level of the voices in this blog, we're all engaging with more or less the same texts, but as we begin to focus on our final projects, the blog narrative starts to get fed through different schematic filters, like the text/image, or a networked formation of the individual.

I feel like the above morass of intrablog linking stands as proof that, in a complex and ultimately unmappable way, we are, without even trying, engaged in some sort of cohesive dialogue with each other. And maybe the pleasure of reading this blog, or the e-texts, like "Kind of Blue," that we do enjoy, comes from that really hard to articulate feeling that all the voices or schemas or categories or whatever are in dialogue, and they are cohering. On that note, I'm going to sit and think for a while about how this conception of unconscious dialogue relates to Henry James.
