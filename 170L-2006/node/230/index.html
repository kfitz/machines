who wants to be mediated?

Submitted by thisismycheese on 17 October 2006 - 6:34pm.
  • electronic literature
  • readings
  • video games

So, I was excited to see that First Person talks about gaming, as that topic has spawned a few of the more interesting conversations on this blog. What I wasn't expecting was how closely the articles would tie in with the conversations we've been having about electronic literature--and particularly the complains a lot of us have made about the artificiality/awkwardness of the e-lit we've looked at.

In Bryan Loyall's response to the first article (Janet Murray's) he writes: "One property of Murray's three main examples is that the participant is consciously aware of the story and actively manipulating it. These forms give powerful ways to tell new types of stories, but for me, one of the joys of a story is when I forget about it being a story. I am simply there."

Later on, Moulthrop quotes Murray saying, "Eventually all successful storytelling technologies become transparent: we lose consciousness of the medium and see...only the power of the story itself."

Which is something we've talked about a lot--the desire for immersion, the desire to forget that all experience is mediated. And a lot of the readings we've done and conversations we've had have suggested that this desire is both impossible to achieve and even ignorant or dangerous. But it keeps coming up.

What's interesting to me is the difference between definitions of immersion, which is more-or-less what both of these writers are talking about. Gaming is looking for a sort of immersion in which the world is responsive to your actions, in which you feel not only like you're really there, but like you are an actor in that situation. On the other hand, traditional narrative immerses you in a strictly outside way; there is an emphasis placed on not having efficacy/agency within the piece--you are being told what's happening rather than altering it, and while you're engrossed in what's happening to the characters, you-as-individual isn't there.

Loyall in particular seems to consider immersion-in-narrative as the natural or default sort of immersion, which is weird--after all, the real world is immersive, right? But it's immersion that's nothing like literature. The real world has a lot more in common with video games, really: it's not narrated by some impartial outsider, there's no way to see the end from the beginning, you (usually) can't go back and fix mistakes, and (obviously) your actions create your reality. So why does Loyall insist that a really good computer game must also have "the strong story and characters of traditional linear stories" and allow the player to "experience the powerful dramatic story that the atuhor intended"? How does it help someone "forget about its being a story" when it's a lot more story-like than life/video games?

I don't know. It's interesting. My head is all jumbly.
