movies as books

Submitted by Frabby on 12 November 2006 - 1:53pm.
  • readings

So continuing with Lulu's idea that social software is all over the place, I just scrolled through if:book until I found something that really interested me, which of course ended up being a bit unrelated to the topic at hand.

But, anyways, I found a post called, "the film as book," about the movie Tarnation, which is a super low-budget film about the filmmaker's relationship with his mother. The interesting thing about this film is that it is composed of actual videos, answering machine messages, and pictures from the director's life. The blog post was mostly concerned with how film was merging with books and the way the movie seems to be composed of "writing."

I thought this was a really different way at looking at emerging media forms. Though we've certainly spent some time on the book/narrative/game hybrid, we haven't (at least I haven't) thought about the ways that technology could breed other strange creations. Because Tarnation was made by a single person, the post notes how similar this is to the single authored book. Of course, we've been discussing how the narrative or book is changing ideas about authorship with the creation of collaborative stories. But movies (long the domain of many, many authors/editors/directors/producers) are going the other way (or at least the possibility of going the other way exists now, due to the cheapness of film equipment).

On a side note: This movie is also really cool for the way it questions what a memory is. Of course you can argue forever about whether or not a written memory is true, but when you have a video recording of an event, that complicates the situation. Though it seems that the memory is now preserved perfectly, can it really capture the truth of the event?

This has to do in part with the particularity of bread-baking instructions, but more to do with texture, kneading, the hands-on compositional nature. I actually saw that movie

Submitted by Frabby on 12 November 2006 - 10:14pm.

I actually saw that movie too, it was pretty good. The idea of memory is so contentious, but I think that there may not be a "truth" side to every story. I don't think memories or any events exist outside of somebody's perception of that event. Even a filmed event (even filmed from every possible angle) isn't going to capture what's going on in a meaningful way.
I don't know, I think I may just be confusing myself. Anyways even if the truth of an event could exist, as soon as someone tries to narrate it or describe it there is a chasm between what happened and the story of what happened. I think this takes the idea that when we try to describe ourselves, we cannot (or lie) a bit further.
Memories must be stories, right? so they've been remediated in some way...
