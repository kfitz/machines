images and texts

Submitted by tophat1 on 6 October 2006 - 3:24pm.
  • Hayles

In her book, Hayles introduces the interesting concept of including images in thinking about texts. In speaking about electronic textuality, she says that "we can no longer afford to ignore the material basis of literary production." (p19). She mentions WJT Mitchell who believes that literary studies should expand to include things such as text image. If we think about the history of the idea of image and text, what comes to mind is particularly, for me, poetry and the arrangements of words to create or support meaning. Many poems would not be the same if they were written and read in the form of a novel. One common sentiment with hyper text seems to be that it's arrangement, such as the form of "Afternoon," was frustrating and did not help along any kind of central plot, which may be part of the idea. In novels, we feel like we may have a more central, linear set-up. Yet, consider novels that alternate stories with each chapter so that at the end of each chapter we are, in a sense, left hanging. I realize that hypertext involves much more complicated concepts than this, but is it too out of the realm of possibility that the mediation, and complications in hyper text arise out of a more traditional context such as those I have mentioned?

images and texts, units of emotion

Submitted by Oz on 8 October 2006 - 12:09pm.

I think you're hitting on a number of really interesting ideas here. Your comments on the significance of poetic form reminded me of a Gertrude Stein quote that I think is really great: "Paragraphs are emotional and sentences are not." The idea here is that a sentence, in prose, is divided by line breaks decided not by the writer, but by things like font, type size, and the margin of the page, whereas the paragraphy constitutes a unit, a consciously structured form. The analogue to poetry would be that lines are emotional and words are not.

For something to be emotional in an interesting way, it has to be somehow bound and regulated, and described (not just expressed) in a successive manner--a sentence is balanced within itself, by its own parts of speech (or a word by each of its letters), while the balance of a paragraph, or a line of poetry, is in the way it circumscribes a succession from beginning to middle to end. This is why, as you said, poems would not have at all the same effect if you took away their line breaks, or why a sentence, taken out of context, can be given a wildly different meaning from the one it holds in the body of the paragraph.

And maybe that's part of why a lot of us are so frustrated with hypertexts, or have trouble deriving the same sense of satisfaction reading them as we do with more traditional (i.e., print) forms of literature. That is, because our ingrained conceptions of the successiveness of language and literature, of beginning, middle, and end, are so totally thrown off by hypertext, perhaps it's hard to find (or to know how to find) hypertext writing emotional. Does anyone else have any ideas about what the unit of emotion in the hypertext might be? Is it the lexia? "Lexias are emotional and links are not"? Or is it even possible to parse a hypertext along lines of hierarchical structure?
