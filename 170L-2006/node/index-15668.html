Virtual Classrooms

Submitted by Frabby on 22 October 2006 - 5:49pm.
  • Recommendations

I'm pretty interested in incidences when the theory we read about becomes pertinent to "real life." I'm sure a lot of people saw the article on the front page of the New York Times on Friday about the growing number of high school AP courses that use virtual experiments (like dissections) in lieu of physical labs. The College Board took a pretty hard line against virtual experiments, claiming that it just didn't match up to the real thing. After online schools threw a fit, the Board has softened a little bit and agreed to look into these virtual classrooms. Complicating things further is that many students are drawn to online classes because their own schools lack the resources to offer AP classes. As someone who took AP classes (all non-science) online because my own high school didn't offer them, I sympathize with kids who want these online science classes. However, a picture of a dissection, even if you can manipulate the images, doesn't seem to compare to the real thing. Then again, does it really matter? Certainly the high schoolers taking all these optional AP classes will have a chance to conduct experiments in college.

Micronarratives and Background Information

Submitted by tophat1 on 22 October 2006 - 3:58pm.
  • Jenkins

In "Game Design as Narrative Architecture," Henry Jenkins talks about narrative presence, in the form of previously encountered narratives as well as micronarratives, in games. He describes certain games that involve previous knowledge. His cites "American McGee's Alice" as an example of a game derived from an older story (Lewis Carroll's "Alice in Wonderland"). The premise of the game is strange: years later Alice is trying to figure out of her experience of Wonderland was real or a hallucination. Jenkins makes the point that the gamer plays against a background of previous encounters with "storybook illustrations and Disney movies." Yet, it would be quite different to draw from the Disney movie or storybook vs. Lewis Carroll's book. The sinister feel of the game would be in much starker contrast to the Disney movie.

Flow in stories (not in membranes)

Submitted by marmalade on 22 October 2006 - 12:56pm.
  • readings

I keep trying to make connections between Juul's idea of "flow" and Douglas & Hargadon's idea of the "engaged reader," but I'm not sure if I'm doing a very good job.

Juul is the author who talks about "game time" and has the lovely graph that looks like a slinky. According to him, flow is what occurs when: "--the challenges match the player's abilities and thereby lead to a state of flow (the player loses the sense of objective time -- time will fly)" (139). The player is immersed. But the point is hard to reach, Juul claims. A very exact balance must be struck:
"To reach a state of flow, a game must be neither too hard (which leads to anxiety) or too easy (which leads to boredom). This means the experience of time is tied not only to the play time/event time relation and to the challenged provide by the game, but also to the relation between game difficulty and player ability" (139).

A different kind of RPG

Submitted by thisismycheese on 22 October 2006 - 12:53pm.
  • fan fiction
  • role-playing games

So a lot of people/readings have been talking about text-based games, and how they are kind of lame. (I'm looking at you, Adventure!)

What I would like to talk about is a different kind of (entirely textual) game, and in order to talk about it, I have to make a confession:

I am addicted to reading other people's role-playing games. Particularly this one. Because homework requires a large majority of my free time, I've never played, but I've been following some of the characters for a couple of years.

This particular RPG is called Milliways Bar (anybody else a Douglas Adams fan?), and it's a LiveJournal-based role-playing game, in which people play characters from literally hundreds of different fictional universes, all of whom have mysteriously ended up in this bar/restaurant/motel at the end of the universe. Some characters come and go between the bar and their home universes, while others are permanently stuck in Milliways. You get characters from different points in the timeline (i.e. a Han Solo from the movies, and a Luke Skywalker from fifty years earlier) interacting. And for me, as a media nerd, the most fun part is watching characters from different universes interact--everybody from Buffy to Judas Iscariot (nope, not kidding) to Salad Fingers. Plus there's plot within the bar, so characters are reacting to all kinds of stuff all over the place and it is a whole lot of really dorky fun.

Quality conversation with chat bots

Submitted by crashingintowalls on 21 October 2006 - 8:35pm.
  • artificial intelligence

Reading Pimm's thoughts on the frustrating nature of conversation with Eliza I started thinking a lot about what makes for a quality conversation with a chat bot. As a cognitive science major, I've thought and experimented with this a little. More than satisfying or unsatisfying, conversational prowess gives a window onto intelligence, artificial or not, accurate or not.

Conversation as intelligence is the basis of the Turing Test and the Loebner Prize. The winners of this prize represent some of the best approximations of human intelligence, and are good indicators of where artificial intelligence is going. Notably, no computer has won the prize for being equivalent to a human yet - a "bronze medal" is awarded yearly for the best approximation relative to the competition.

Videogames of the Oppressed

Submitted by Frabby on 21 October 2006 - 8:13pm.
  • readings

What a cool little article from Gonzalo Frasca! After the Moulthrop piece in the last section, I can't say I'm shocked by the socially conscious way that some academics are approaching video games, but I'm still surprised. Claiming that videogames could deal with social issues in a productive way is a bold statement (and maybe a bit ridiculous?...) but pretty interesting. I was particularly provoked by Augusto Boal's work with the "Theater of the Oppressed." I'm pretty sure I wouldn't actually want to attend a performance where the point "is not to produce beautiful or enjoyable performances, but promote critical discussions among the participants" (89). Then again my miniscule attention span prevents me from attending theater in general. Also, don't good plays provoke critical discussion anyways? Perhaps theater-goers should be forced to stay after plays and break into small discussion groups. Or--perhaps not. Still, it sounds like this man's work is intense.

More on the purpose of computer/video games

Submitted by Pimm on 20 October 2006 - 1:56pm.
  • games

I'm still fascinated with the debate over the purpose of video-games and, more importantly, the effects that people attribute to them. Games can "teach" violence, knowledge of another historical period (Oregon Trail), math, etc--but, of course, their main purpose is to allow the user to have fun, or else they wouldn't be called games. I really liked Frabby's post on the computer game conference at UC Irvine and the idea of whether computer games can teach ethics. As I said earlier, I enjoyed playing the Sims because of the fantasy world I could create that still allowed me to "be" myself. I thought that I would try the game KF mentioned in class--Eliza--and see if I liked it for the same reason. Did I? No! I guess it's expected that Eliza can't do much, but I didn't even have fun trying to upset her. Every time I answered "no", even to questions that almost required that answer, she asked me if I was being negative. I think I need to see a real therapist after using her.
