Video games in schools

Submitted by Lulu on 20 October 2006 - 2:24am.
  • education
  • video games

I've really enjoyed reading everybody's posts on videogaming, confessions, and the like. It seems like the topic of video games is broad enough that everyone has their own personal stories and memories of what videogaming means to them, whether it be Sims-style creations or the shoot-and-kill variety like CS and Diablo. What about happy memories of games like Oregon Trail, or Carmen Sandiego in elementary school? Does anyone remember playing video games, or rather, computer games in schools?

This article and this article talks about the various effects of using computer games in school. I feel like the idea of harnessing the popular appeal and power of video games and using them as an educational tool in schools has been around for at least a decade. The author of the first article writes that "scientists call it [video games] the next great discovery, a way to captivate students so much they will spend hours learning on their own." I feel like it's a little late to make a statement such as this, since educational video games have been around for a while now and companies learned long ago that videogames are the "next great discovery." The points that the article makes, though, definitely hold true and I can personally identify with a lot of this from grade school memories.

I remember the rewarding of precious computer time in 5th grade made me work extra hard and fast during the regular lessons so that I would be awarded an extra 15 minutes or so at the computer. Oregon Trail II was my American history lesson. From the POV of a pioneer, I learned what it was like to travel west in a covered wagon. The game was totally replayable, and that was the best part. I could be a doctor. I could be a banker. I could be a farmer. I could choose who I wanted to travel with, and where I wanted to go. I could buy 100s of pounds of meat! I could HUNT! That was the best part, oh my god, that was so fun! I'll admit that, at the time, the appeal wasn't so much that this was an American history lesson, but instead the pure joy of buying food and supplies, journeying, hunting, hunting, fording rivers, hunting. It was an educational game; emphasis on game. It was only later on that I realized the game's historical background.

And I also remember this math game called Treasure Mountain. I vaguely remember catching snowballs and counting gold in a store as a means of, um...getting to the top of a mountain? I think there was some math involved too. Again, this was meant to be one of those "educational" computer games, but the emphasis was more on game rather than education. It was still amazingly fun, though.

I guess the point is, kids like videogames, and if made properly, they can be useful tools in the classroom. There's also all sorts of interesting questions relating to agency, immersion, the game-story continuum. The total immersive quality of such games almost makes the educational aspect a by-product of the game's story. But if the educational aspect involved in a certain game is challenging enough for a certain kid, then I can definitely see the value of using games as a fun, rewarding alternative.

If I can remember correctly, I also feel like the appeal was more that it was a game, and therefore automatically anti-work. Games=not work. If companies can manage to "trick" kids into thinking this and to therefore entice them to play these "game-lessons" then that's pretty darn amazing and somewhat manipulative, although if it leads to educational progress then I'm all for it. The ability for educational videogames to give out material, pixelated rewards for solving a math problem or spelling a word correctly also makes it a hot selling-item among kids, who really like getting rewarded for doing things. Then again, I also think there's value in teaching kids math or reading the old-fashioned way, and sometimes it's scary imagining an entire generation of over-stimulated kids bred only on computer games and the like. Hopefully their parents'll make sure they have balance in their lives.
