Attention Economy

Submitted by pseudoanonymity on 15 December 2006 - 4:51pm.
  • attention span

Two articles necessary for this blog entry (and anyone interested in modern internet theory in general): here and here.

I mentioned in a previous post that not everyone has the privilege of being heard. This differs in a pre-digital society, in which if you had the ability to speak, people had to hear you (barring earplugs or a hurricane). In a digital society, everyone has the ability to speak, but getting heard is a completely different project. For example, in order to speak on Slashdot (the infamous techie hub), one must only login and comment. After a comment has been made, however, that comment is assessed by other users and is assigned an evolving numerical rank from -1 to 5 (-1 being flamebait, 5 being very good). Comments can reach a 5 on different merits â€“ informative, funny, etc.

When looking at a Slashdot post, I don't have the time (or attention, if we're working with the definition of attention given above) to read the hundreds of comments that normally pop up around every post. Instead, I simply set the filter to only display the top 15 or 20 comments â€“ I usually do this by viewing only comments ranked as 5, or those ranked 4 and 5 if it's a small story. This severely limits the information I see, and in my opinion very much improves my surfing experience. I don't have to deal with flamebait or trolls, and most of the comments are highly intelligent and mature (by my subjective standards of course).

Looking back at Pogue's post, I wonder that people still experience an internet that is truly democratic (that is to say, a place where each voice has equal weight, whatever you want to call that). With social networking software taking over the net (myspace, tagging, facebook, technorati, link ranking, etc.), I look at Pogue's post and wonder how long public email accounts will survive.

What do I mean by public email accounts? I mean accounts that anyone can send email to and where every email is read by the singular owner. With the amount of people that have access to web increasing daily, the amount of people that will ignore etiquette will inevitably increase at a higher rate than those who do understand and use it. In this way, these voices will become like spam (it was ok in small amounts, but as it increased to unmanageable proportions, we came up with ways to filter it) and we are currently coming up with ways to filter them.
