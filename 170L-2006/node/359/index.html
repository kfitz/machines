Local skin

Submitted by a bird on 5 November 2006 - 5:07pm.
  • readings

I absolutely hate the idea of "Skin". I thought it was weird when I thought they were temporary tattoos; I thought it was inhumane when I found out they were permanent. How are these people ever going to make a story out of their disparate words when some live in Japan? If they had sentences, it would be easier...but still really really weird.
The map thing was fun, though. Near my boyfriend lives a "rippling". There's a "papery" in Perth, which would make me feel self conscious about my skin as I aged. There's a "junk" in Texas, which could be hilarious if placed correctly on the body. Nobody's doing it around here. The closest we have is an "I" in El Monte.

Oh wow, we had really

Submitted by Frabby on 5 November 2006 - 6:45pm.

Oh wow, we had really different reactions to "Skin." I thought it was really cool and got it in my head that I wanted to be word (that plan was foiled by my laziness, I didn't bother applying). I think (as others have pointed out) that "Skin" isn't really about the story. It's about the project, so it doesn't matter if the words ever meet up. Though, Jackson, as the author, does know the whole story, which seems a bit unfair.

One thing that got me though...Jackson says that as words die, the story changes. How does she manage that? Does the story really change if a "the" dies? Or would it just turn a sentence like, "The paper rustled," into "paper rustled" ?

skin

Submitted by tophat1 on 5 November 2006 - 9:49pm.

I'm not too excited about Skin either. I think that the idea that we can't read the story unless we participate is annoying. Also, even if you do participate you might get a ridiculous tattoo. In addition to measley articles, you may have to get a word and then a punctuation. An exclamation point could be fun but what about a comma or a semi colon! At any rate, I guess you could feel rewarded, even with a bad tattoo, because you're participating in something different and kind of cool. Also, they may all feel a sense of community that's nice. I think ultimately, people like to feel connected even if it's through this strange project.

Skin, comma

Submitted by magoo on 6 November 2006 - 8:39am.

But wouldn't the comma or semicolon be the wonderful thing to have? Something of one would be elsewhere; one becomes a citizen of the world, or this worldlet.

What bothers me (I find the whole concept fascinating, but would never participate) is the permanence of the marks. I'd wake up the next morning and want to say something else, as opposed to being said.
