Blogs as Mediators

Submitted by Shock and Awe on 26 September 2006 - 7:25pm.
  • mediation
  • the dark side of blogging
  • the nature of the blog
  • zakaria

Fareed Zakaria discusses mediation and blogging in a fascinating way in the conclusion of his book The Future of Freedom (W W Norton and Company, 2003).

"The personal web site (blog) was hailed as the killer of the traditional media. In fact it has become something quite different. Far from replacing newspapers and magazines, the best blogs--and the best are very clever--have become guides to them, pointing out unusual sources and commenting on familiar ones. They have become new mediators for the informed public...the creators of blogs think of themselves as radical democrats, the are in fact a new Tocquevillean elite" (254).

By Tocquevillean elite Zakaria is referring to the class of aristocrats the famous French political thinker Alexis de Tocqueville hoped would function as social stabliizers and protect America from the tyrrany of the majority.

This is certainly different from how I look at blogs, but he has a point. The very few blogs that reach a truly mass audience do tend to function as mediators, directing their audience on where to go to find interesting and valuable information and often leading back to traditional news sources--sources that have been filtered for content by the blogger. Bloggers then do become a mediator between the average net surfer and the enormous amount of content available on the internet. A starting point from which users branch out.

This reminded me of our class discussion about the drive for remediation and the current push to give technology more physical limits (icons that drag and collide with one another, etc.) I pictured the future blog as a wise looking old man hovering in 3D dispensing wise links in a wise voice. It also made me consider the possibility that a few of the most influential blogs will eventually become institutions directing cultural traffic. Telling people what they should be interested in, where to find it, and what to think about it, and, as such, holding a great deal of power both commercially and ideologically.

mediating

Submitted by tophat1 on 27 September 2006 - 12:38pm.

Zakaria does have an interesting point and I think it relates back to some of what Joyce was pointing to in his piece. As we move forward with increasingly large amounts of information on the web, it seems that we will inevitably have to turn to someone or something to help sort it out. I think that the concern, which Joyce and you both point out, is that the mediation can establish a hierarchy. In other words, we have to be careful, even when encountering a well-respected blog or other information-sorting site, to keep in mind that the most highly-rated sites aren't necessarily the best.

mediation, the dark side of blogging, the nature of the blog

Submitted by magoo on 28 September 2006 - 2:19pm.

We may wind up with more filtering just because there's more to filter.

Is the objection filtering itself, or who controls the filter? Does more filtering = tighter control?

Might the major problem be that those with other powers -- $$, arms, moguls of other media, governments, corporations -- will grab the controls of the filters? Might this become harder to do in certain structures -- for instance, if the filter works because an individual writes in an interesting way?

It seems the first step to keeping this free is for many people to realize that it is so consequential.
