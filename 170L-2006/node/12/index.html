Xanga

Submitted by Lulu on 5 September 2006 - 4:15pm.
  • blogs

Most of the blogging that I've done these past few years has been on the wonderful but, admittedly, very "high-school-ish" site called Xanga. It's pronounced "zanga." :) Xanga is very much like Livejournal, where the blogger creates an account for herself and then proceeds to musing/journaling/ranting, usually about everyday encounters, and in my case, college life. I would say that about 90% of the "blogging" that I do is on xanga, and what keeps me going back is the large community of "xangans" that I keep connected to in this community. It's basically a process of reading friends' "xangas" and commenting on each other's xangas, keeping each other updated on what we're feeling, thinking, eating, etc. I'm sure xangas (yes, we call these things "xangas" instead of "blogs") are not considered legitimate according to the definition of some of the A-listers we read about in the text, but in this busy college world, these xangas are a good, convenient way of knowing what's up in each other's lives. Xanga also has features called blogrings that allow people with shared interests to join, thus connecting individuals with others who have similar tastes in a particular topic. There is even a Pomona blogring, and that is where I do most of my xanga-ing.

Although most would say that Livejournal has a more professional-looking interface and a variety of better features, I've chosen to stick with Xanga because practically all my friends use xanga, thus making it the more convenient choice. There's also an interesting trend surrounding Xanga that my friend wrote a 15-page term paper about last semester. For some reason, Xanga, although it does not advertise itself as such, has an overwhelming large number of Asian-American bloggers. My friend conducted a random survey of several hundred randomly chosen xangans last year, and she found that the overwhelming percentage of xangans are Asian-American. Even the Pomona College blogring on Xanga is comprised mostly of Asian-American students, which of course doesn't represent the actual racial breakdown of the college. However, this isn't the case with Livejournal communities or other blogging sites that are also open to the masses. This is especially interesting to me, as I am Asian-American, and it does make me wonder a bit about aspects of race, access to technology, community-building and networking along racial lines, youth culture, and other diverse questions about youth identification and socialization. Xanga and the world of online blogging in general seems to be a platform on which we can observe how issues of race and identity manifest themselves...
