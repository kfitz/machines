Books and Materiality

Submitted by Natwwal on 13 October 2006 - 8:15am.
  • books

I've been thinking a lot about Hayles's emphasis on the importance of the materiality of a piece of literature and McLuhan's insistance that "the medium is the message." I wonder how these ideas apply to a "conventional" novel. If materiality is so essential, then editors and illustrators are far more essential in the creation of a piece of literature than we give them credit for. Is it then the case that every piece of "conventional" literature is a collaboration? We could even extend this idea to a sort of collaboration with the reader. I, for one, love to mark up books and write in margins when I read them, particularly for class. Does this change the materiality of the text? I am inclined to say that it does. When I've read a book and marked it up, I find it profoundly difficult to navigate my way around a clean copy or, heaven forbid, a different edition of the same book. Perhaps, then, a paper book is interactive in ways that hypertexts have not yet managed to be.

marking up a hypertext

Submitted by Anonymous on 17 October 2006 - 2:03pm.

It seems that some way of "marking up" hypertexts would be particularly helpful, since they demand so much navigation from the reader. The technology seems to be out there. I'm reminded of the notes you can put up on your desktop. Something along those lines (perhaps in a more savvy format) would be very cool to incorporate into a hypertext, giving the reader the chance to organize their thoughts and come back to them along their hypertext journey.
