Trying Again To Make Books Obsolete

Submitted by silversprung on 11 October 2006 - 8:19pm.
  • electronic literature
  • technology

I just came across an article that is remarkably pertinent to our class. It's from the NY Times Technology section, and it's a review of the Sony Reader, which is a portable 5" x 7" screen that will go on sale this month for $350.

It seems like the Reader has a lot going for it. Apparently it manages to avoid what is our biggest turn-off about reading things on screens: the fact that, well, it looks like a screen, and not a page. I won't describe in detail what the technology is that produces this page-like appearance, but it's actually really cool-sounding-- I recommend you read the article. The Reader can also do things that a traditional book can't-- like play music or photos. I, for one, despite my love of paper's tactile qualities, am curious about how this actually looks. And I'd consider investing in it if I had a job that required me to read a lot on-the-go and it were a little cheaper to buy.

David Pogue, the reviewer, seems realistic about downloadable literature's potential: he doesn't think that it will take over books anytime soon, but he does think it will continue to grow, especially as new gadgets that mimic the look of a paper page come out.

Pogue's last couple of paragraphs are particularly punchy:

"Is that it, then? Is the paper book doomed? Was it only a transitional gadget, a placeholder that came between stone tablets and e-books?

Not any time soon. The Sony Reader is an impressive achievement, and an important step toward a convenient alternative to bound books. It will make certain niche groups very happy: gadget freaks, lawyers with massive document stashes, doctors and pilots who check hefty reference texts, high school students with 35-pound backpacks and anyone who likes to read by the pool for 20 weeks at a time.

The masses, however, may continue to prefer the more established portable-document format. Those older reading machines never run out of power, cost about 2 percent as much and don't break when dropped. You know: p-books."

Reading Machines?! He must know about our class...
