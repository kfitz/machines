The future of the printed book

Submitted by Gunslinger1818 on 23 September 2006 - 3:14pm.
  • Landow

Was anyone else a little bothered by some of the discussion in Landow about the possible future irrelevance of books and other printed material as important? Perhaps it's that I love to read by the fire at the remote family cabin, that my mom's a librarian, or that I simply have an attachment to the status quo, but I feel almost morally opposed to reading without a book. I certainly am attached to the "tactility" Landow mentions. I was relieved at Landow's view of escaping the confinement of print: "This common project first requires that one first recognize the enormous power of the book, for only after we have made ourselves conscious of the ways it has formed and informed our lives can we seek to pry ourselves free of some of its limitations" (46-7).

I suppose that I see some of the limitations of linearity in ordinary text and that there is nothing more "natural" about the book than there is about the internet. It is certainly interesting to consider the possibility of thinking in an entirely different way based on new technology as was the case when the printed matter finally entrenched itself in culture. And I recognize that I am naturally going to cater to thinking in the way I learned to do so.

But that's not enough for me. I've actually been struggling with the concept of reading on the computer since the beginning of this class. I feels unnatural to read on the screen for long periods of time, even if the subject matter is changing rapidly. Reading in we've got blog that people liked to do 2-3 hours of reading a night on the computer sounded strange to me. And maybe it's just something one gets used to. But the concept of the ordinary book in constancy, sturdiness, tactility, portability, and cultural symbolic power are fixed in my head. "Escaping the limits of print culture" conjures for me images of Nazi book burnings. I'm definitely with Landow on this one. If we are to progress in this way, it must be done with the book and with caution.

sundays

Submitted by marmalade on 24 September 2006 - 10:25am.

This whole post made such sense to me; I almost felt like it had come right out of my own mind!

I guess I'm young enough not to want to cast myself as a conservative--but being confronted with this, I guess I really am in someways. Like Lulu, I can't help but feel that there is something to be said for the great comfort that curling up with a nice, normal, physical, linearly-plotted book provides. When you're lonely, when you're sad, when you're comfortable, when you finally have a moment's peace in your life--being able to sit down and just read something like that... to be able to fall asleep in the sunshine with a book on your lap... ok, maybe I'm waxing poetic and this is just my idea of a great Sunday afternoon, but I'm really attached to some of the "limits of print culture." I agree--let's be cautious. Because I love Sundays ;)

Nicely said, marmalade and

Submitted by Lulu on 24 September 2006 - 2:13pm.

Nicely said, marmalade and gunslinger. I couldn't have said it better myself.

I find it nice that a lot of our readings are online in PDF format (partly because that way, I don't have to pay atrocious prices for books), but I ALWAYS print out the readings. I can't stand sitting in front of the screen to read the articles for hours and hours. It hurts my eyes. I like the touch of a page. I don't read the news online either; I need to have a hard copy of the newspaper and I need to get all those print stains on my hand. It just feels...satisfying.

One of things that I would really love to have would be a giant library of my own, with mahogany bookshelves and books and books and books from the floor to the ceiling. Imagine the library in the movie Beauty and the Beast. I'd like one of those. Complete with a moving ladder so I can slide along the bookshelves!
