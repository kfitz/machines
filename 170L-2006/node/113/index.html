After books

Submitted by Frabby on 23 September 2006 - 6:11pm.
  • readings

I share some of the feelings and concerns that gunslinger and marmalade have voiced. Not only am I very attached to the act of physically reading a book (I can't stand reading things online, I always print them out), but I like answers. And the types of fiction that Joyce discusses provide very few answers, as Marmalade points out. Yet before we can start jumping up and down angrily, he hits us with, "What we see as senseless beauty may be the emergence of as yet unrecognizable new ways of making sense" (63). So we just don't get it right now? I can buy that, I'm sure if I were born in a different era, I would be preaching orality with everyone else (though I'm still impatient for my own understanding of literacy to expand). But it still seems like a bit of a cop-out: it's too amazing, it transcends us, and we can't grasp it.
In fact the whole thrust of hypertext as envisioned by Joyce seems like a cop-out. He says, "Emerging meanings gathers in episodes that combine in sessions of reading and rereading and sometimes seem (to the reader) to mean on their own" (147). Yeah, I'm also a little blurry on the rereading concept, but how I understand it right now, it seems that this "emerging meaning" is really just, hey, I get to pick my own meaning. Which I really do not like. EDIT- After reading Afternoon, I think understand rereading now. Rereading is literally an unreading in the sense that you can never go back to how you read it the first time. I guess that was obvious to other people, but after reading that story, and being frustrated, I do feel like I keep on unreading it..
But then again, I love Shelley Jackson's final thoughts, "we protest bad writing. (We are nearly all of us bad or disorderly writers; despite ourselves we are redundant, looped, entangled; our transitions are awkward, our conclusions unsubstantiated.) (1995, 'Lives')" (148). So perhaps the traditional narrative isn't the best way to express human experience. Our lives don't fit into genres and clean endings.

A random thought on reading physical texts: there are some acts associated with reading that would be terrible to lose. I'm thinking about parents reading their children bed-time stories. My gut reaction to replacing parents with computers is negative (as I imagine most other reactions would be). Yet I suppose there could be arguments for that sort of computer education. For parents who can't read, who don't have time to read, or perhaps don't speak the language in which their child is schooled in, a computer bed time story seems like a good idea.

true

Submitted by Frabby on 24 September 2006 - 11:27pm.

I'm not actually too preoccupied with thoughts of robotic parents. But computers are already able to "speak" in voices that sound human, and it seems like it would be a logical, time saving step to push off the bedtime stories to a computer. I know we have books on tape already, and I haven't heard of parents putting headphones on their toddlers while they listen to Good Night Moon. But a computer is able to combine images and sound...Okay, okay, this is a silly tangent. But I'm still trying to think of types of concrete examples of how technology could change how we live and grow up, and I think this is a possibility.

As for fears of computers completely replacing humans, though? No, I haven't jumped on that boat yet.
