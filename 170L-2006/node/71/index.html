the blog as novel

Submitted by thisismycheese on 16 September 2006 - 3:47pm.
  • the nature of the blog

A lot of issues seem to surround the idea of the fictional blog (see Kaycee Nicole)--something that strikes me as really strange, considering that most personal blogging is just storytelling.

I started wondering: What are people's expectations when they read blogs? Is it really voyeurism in the vein of the webcam, where we want to see Exactly What's Happening To John As It Happens?

Well--no. If that's what we were looking for, we would probably just watch a webcame (or buy a telescope...)

Blogs aren't immediate in that way. People write drafts on paper or on Microsoft Word. Everything is time-stamped, sure, but I know I'm not the only blogger who's stewed over an entry for days or even weeks before posting it (or, as pseudoanonymity mentioned in class, plenty of people backdate entries for an array of reasons). Lots of people use pseudonyms and/or fictionalize events, conversations, etc.--there isn't a blog in existence that records life exactly as it happens. Bloggers omit events and anecdotes that aren't relevant to their audience and leave out details that don't contribute to the "plot"--and most blogs, especially the popular ones, do have a clear story, some kind of through-line that keeps us reading.

When it comes down to it, people who read personal, journal-style blogs (other than those of their friends) are reading for entertainment. They like the writing, the characters, or the plot--they're reading blogs as literature, so why are we so attached to knowing that they are "true" at least to a certain extent? Are we only allowing ourselves to get invested in other people's lives because they are real people? Why does our investment change if the person isn't real, especially when we initially thought they were? I can't be the only person who's wanted to have one-on-one conversations with fictional characters...

In some ways, blogs are almost like the new epistolary novel--especially when read in conjunction with one another. If, for example, you read the blogs kept by everyone in my circle of friends, you notice a conversation happening among them--people referencing the same event from different angles, linking back and forth to entries in each others' blogs, replying directly or indirectly to statements made in them. You see the same thing in the blogging elite (blogs like Dooce, not linky blogs) with their constant self-referencing and the real-life relationships formed among them. Through reading all of these different perspectives, some kind of cohesive story starts to take shape, while just reading one person's blog gives you a much more limited perspective.

This made me think of two

Submitted by marmalade on 16 September 2006 - 6:18pm.

This made me think of two things:

(1) As the epistolary novel marked the beginning of a new genre of literature--helped bring the novel as we know it into being--what does that suggest that blogs presage? What sort of new "genre" is the blog helping to define? Or is it even doing this at all?

(2) Perhaps another apt metaphor for this blog fictionalization of reality might be reality television. Yes it's "real"--i.e. real people being followed around by a real camera--but the situations are contrived and then edited to present the viewer with a certain picture, one decided by the producer rather than what might actually be "reality."

cool ideas.

good question, if I do say so myself

Submitted by KF on 18 September 2006 - 10:05am.

(1) As the epistolary novel marked the beginning of a new genre of literature--helped bring the novel as we know it into being--what does that suggest that blogs presage? What sort of new "genre" is the blog helping to define? Or is it even doing this at all?

This is precisely the subject of a talk I'm giving next month at Honnold:

THE PLEASURE OF THE BLOG: THE EARLY NOVEL, THE SERIAL, AND THE NARRATIVE ARCHIVE

Kathleen Fitzpatrick, Associate Professor of English and Media Studies, Pomona College

Wednesday, October 11th, 4:15 PM

Blogs have in the last couple of years loomed large in the western imagination, but the ideas about blogs that have circulated both through the mainstream media and through academia have been extremely limited in scope. In the popular imagination, there is a distinction between "blogs," which are assumed on some level to be doing public work, whether political, technical, academic, or journalistic, and "online diaries," which are primarily personal, if not exactly private. These personal blogs are too often dismissed as the narcissistic rantings of teenage girls and other hysterics, a nonsensical -- and, not incidentally, hyper-feminine -- form of "oversharing." Such a dismissal, however, overlooks the important work that such personal blogs are doing in the construction of an emergent literary form. In fact, western literary history can shed some important light on the current state of personal blogging, with the English novel rooted in the domestic practices and personal writing of middle-class eighteenth-century women. However, new technologies are providing for new forms of self-presentation. Kathleen Fitzpatrick, Associate Professor of English and Media Studies, Pomona College and the author of The Anxiety of Obsolescence: The American Novel in the Age of Television (published this summer by Vanderbilt University Press), will discuss the evolution and unique pleasures of this new literary form, one that merges older traditions of seriality with the newer mode of the database, creating not simply a narrative but rather a first-person narrative archive of the self.
