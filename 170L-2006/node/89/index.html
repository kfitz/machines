SOS - Please advise

Submitted by magoo on 18 September 2006 - 11:17am.
  • Beowulf
  • Dickinson
  • visual poetry

I'm in early stages of preparing an online edition of Emily Dickinson's Fascicles. (The Fascicles were the little booklets in which Dickinson bound much of her verse).

I wonder how you would prefer to see such a document. Here are the problems.

  • Her nonstandard punctuation changes the meaning of her text.
  • Even her handwriting is critical in these changes
  • Her handwriting is very hard for most people to read.
  • People have tried to transcribe the handwriting in such a way that the visual qualities of the punctuation are preserved. No one has come close.
  • The effort of deciphering the immediate visuality of the poetry tends to detract from the resonance of the longer sequence of poems
  • Believe it or not, no print version exists that maintains the poems in print except for The Manuscript Books of Emily Dickinson, which wonderfully and terribly requires readers to decipher Dickinson's handwriting

What I'm thinking at present is that I should provide an HTML rollover icon that stays uniformly onscreen that allows users to select which fascicle and which poem to go to.

I'm thinking that each page could be laid out visually as a scan or digital photo of the original itself (when and if I could actually arrange permission for such a thing). When the user drags the cursor over any given word in ED's precious scrawl, the word would appear as simple typographic text.

The technology would be fairly easy. (XHTML image maps tied to JavaScript rollovers, CSS-generated rollover menus from the icons).

Does this seem like the easiest and/or most productive design?

Someone seems to have done something somewhat similar with The Electronic Beowulf.

my Dickinson fasicle philosophy (yes, I have one)

Submitted by silversprung on 18 September 2006 - 7:56pm.

I took a course on Whitman and Dickinson last year, and one of our assignments was to prepare a 'reader's edition' of around 10 of Dickinson's poems. Our editions had to include an editor's note that explained why we made the choices we did in displaying Dickinson's poems. The text of my editorial note is below.

Before I copy and paste (oh, woe is mine technological ineptitude!) my Dickinson philosophy, I want to suggest that if you do decide to go with the word-by-word rollover idea, magoo, that you make sure that somewhere on your site you include a full typed version of each of Dickinson's poems. As a reader, I would find it very frustrating to only be able to decipher one typed word at a time, and then have that typed word disappear as soon as I moved my cursor to another of Dickinson's illegible manuscript words.

(p.s. On a somewhat sour note, I'm pretty sure that Harvard University owns the rights to all of Dickinson's poems (not sure if this includes her manuscripts, but I'm inclined to think it does) and almost never allows others to use them, because her poems are such huge money-makers for them.)

My editorial philosophy on Dickinson, and apologies to those who have no interest in this:

However, for the curious reader, I have included at the bottom of each poem these places where Dickinson starts a new row of print, but my reprinting does not. Dickinson, hypertext

Submitted by magoo on 28 September 2006 - 5:29pm.

Thanks!

As to including a print version, point well taken. One could click from it to the facsimile page and vice versa.

I agree with some parts of your approach to ED (what a cool assignment!) but disagree with others:

1) I absolutely would NOT assume that she broke the lines because the page ends where the lines are "not metrical." I realize you're in good company, but she so often wrote smaller letters to squeeze words in, then expanded letters on other lines -- even on the same pages where she dropped part of what one might think of as a line.
I take her to be totally intentional in all this. I could be wrong, but it's certainly not obvious, given the furor. Wouldn't the safer path be to follow her breaks, and if it's so clear that they should be different for some reason, the reader to whom that seems so obvious can make that judgment.
(BTW, I don't find them less metrical with the odd breaks , but much the opposite. The breaks seem necessary to preserve rythmn against the profuse, insistent, and irregular punctuation.)

2) I'll nod to Franklin's take on letters, too, although I can't say I'm comfortable about it. He's at least as good as I am, though, probably way better. I find it tough.

3) I have tried to "typographically represent the different lengths, slants, and positions of her dashes." I failed miserably, over and over. I nod to your judgment here, much chagrined and disappointed.

4) Using the later version, even assuming dates to be correct, hardly means ceding to Dickinson's choice, given all the things she did with context. Sometimes this resolves nicely, sometimes not.

Thanks for the advice. I realize copyright's likely to be an impassible point. I'd need permission to re-photograph the originals. So I've for present stopped work at the point where I have the object I'll need to use myself -- a typographical version hypertext that maintains poem sequence and alternative wordings: something that's not otherwise available anywhere that I've found. (The online Dickinson Archive is cool, but priviledges the sequence of correspondence, probably because of Nell Smith's theories about ED's relationship with Susan.)

Anyway, I reckon that taken in sequence -- with alternate readings, line breaks, punctuations, and reader-specific versions in circulation -- the fascicles form something very related to hypertext. But, perhaps ironically, one needs to read the individual booklets in smooth-ish sequence to get the resonances.
