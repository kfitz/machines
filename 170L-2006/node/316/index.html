Voyeurism

Submitted by Pimm on 30 October 2006 - 10:12pm.
  • personal blogs
  • surveillance

Perhaps it's a bit unusual to write a blog with the purpose of asking a question about a definition, but I'm going to do it: What exactly is voyeurism? What's crucial to understanding it? I was confused in class when we talked about it, but I was too embarrassed to ask. However, looking at even recent posts on the blog that use the term make me realize that I am still confused, and I'm not sure if my definition differs from others'.

Wikipedia says voyeurism is "a practice in which an individual derives sexual pleasure from observing other people." Other definitions I've found seem a bit unclear on how sex is involved; is it necessary in voyeurism for the viewer to derive sexual pleasure, or can he/she just watch a sexual act?

For example, I was relieved when I started reading "Kind of Blue" after "reading" Jemma and Caroline because it removed me (even a false, constructed "me") from the picture. I was able to look into the worlds of Skip, Berto, and Susanne without them knowing, and I felt an odd sense of pleasure from reading something I'm not supposed to. But is that just the pleasure of me being an "agent of surveillance"? I mean, I'm certainly not getting sexual pleasure from any of those constructed emails.

I want to make sure that I'm on the same page as everyone. Plus I'm curious, especially since recent blog posts and class discussions have raised the issue of looking at people's personal lives through blogs or videos: How much of reading/watching people's personal lives is voyeurism? Where can we draw the line between being an agent of surveillance and a voyeur?

thoughts on voyeurism

Submitted by Frabby on 31 October 2006 - 12:54am.

I guess this won't help with the defintion...but I'm curious about your reaction to Kind of Blue. Why did you feel that you were reading something you weren't supposed to? I'm not saying you're wrong, actually just the opposite, because it seems like much of the class had the same reaction.

But still, it's a fictional world we see in this hypertext. And the embarassed feeling we get when we see something awkward or painful on tv or in a book isn't really linked to voyeurism, at least for me. Do people feel the same way when they read a memoir? Memoirs are just as personal and revealing, and they're based in reality (usually...)

Also, as for the line between an agent of surveillance and voyeur, I would say in most cases we are voyeurs. Surveillance to me implies an overarching goal (likely an evil one too!), while voyeurism is the enjoyment of watching another. For example, I think facebook is hands-down a tool for voyeurs...unless you're a company checking out a potential hire or something like that.
