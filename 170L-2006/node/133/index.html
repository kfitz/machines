Hypercomics

Submitted by Lulu on 27 September 2006 - 12:36am.
  • hypertext fiction

Has anyone ever seen this hypercomics site? I found it through a google search, and it looks pretty interesting. A lot of the issues that came up when we read Joyce's "Afternoon" also comes up when reading some of the "comics." There is no static linearity, the conventional idea of fixed frames is also deconstructed. This hypercomic looks like it's ultimately linear since you can't jump from one line of the web to another; however, it differs from conventional comics in that one can go forwards AND backwards,up or down, which was an odd sensation for me when I was playing around with it. I don't quite get the point of this "brain slide" hypercomic. The different sets of frames don't seem to have any particular connecting theme.

Playing around on the hypercomic site definitely reminded me of some of the conversations that were going on in class and on this blog. The same sense of anxiety surfaces from not knowing what the point is, or whether there's a conclusion. Like the experience of reading Joyce's hypertext, the point of the exercise seems to be experiencing the medium rather than understanding the plot. Also, I felt like, as far as the question of readerly "freedom" goes, the ball is still in the author's court, and we're still playing his/her game instead of totally taking control.

Nonetheless, I still think it was pretty fun just hopping around in hypercomic world.

cool stuff

Submitted by marmalade on 27 September 2006 - 11:53am.

Cool stuff, Lulu. I guess the lack of linearity didn't really bother me--comics, for me at least, don't really operate under the same set of expectations. It was much easier for me to follow different story lines (wrt the "Afternoon", because each section had a "face" or a visible set of characters that went with it.

Maybe the reason I expect less from comics is that (to me) they're episodes, sort of like television or radio shows, and you get an installment and then have to wait until next week to read more. This just seemed like a comic book collection with an especially short attention span... ;)

The most "paranoia -inducing" part of the whole site was the soundtrack though. God, I just wanted to turn it off!

Wonder what sort of soundtrack (or illustrations, more pertinently) Joyce would endorse for afternoon, or any hypertexts in general. Do music and pictures provide too fixed of an authorial center?

hypertext fiction, comics

Submitted by Anonymous on 27 September 2006 - 4:28pm.

Interesting. I have often wondered whether extremely graphic environments wouldn't be particularly good for hypertext fiction because we don't necessarily assemble a picture in a single sequence (although we do sort of tend to).
You might find some relations when we get to Patchwork Girl and Victory Garden. I do see some relation to the non-framing/re-framing as well, since frame may have to do with sequence of viewing, and some comic artists seem to have depth shots (as do some filmakers) in which several scenes seem to be involved in a single screen. I recall the battle going on in the background of Apocalypse Now while Martin Sheen narrated one or another irrelevancy. A film or arts student could probably give us some other examples.

hypertext fiction, comics

Submitted by magoo on 28 September 2006 - 1:34pm.

In some ways, visual narrative, especially framed sequential narrative -- comics -- resembles hypertext. Visuals aren't altogether sequential, even when the frames are: one glances around. Also, comic artists and filmmakers frame shots where multiple layers of action happen at once, which creates varied types of simultaneity, or can.

There are relations to Patchwork Girl, too, with it's spatialization of the typically temporal business of narrative.
