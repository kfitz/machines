Understanding embodiment

Submitted by Pimm on 7 October 2006 - 1:57pm.
  • Hayles
  • readings

Let me begin by saying what a relief it was to read "Writing Machines." I had read a bit of Hayles for an AfroFuturisms class I took with Professor Val Thomas on African American Science Fiction. For that class, I grappled with Hayles's definition of posthumanism, something I think I'll never fully understand because it is too abstract and theorized for my brain to process. "Writing Machines" was much easier to understand. Sure, as silversprung pointed out, there are passages that I didn't comprehend, but I think the way in which the book was written really helped my understanding. Unlike thisismycheese, I enjoyed the design. True, I didn't always understand why the magnified portions were magnified, but they didn't hurt of reading, and I liked the break in monotony. Also, one of my favorite parts was the portions of autobiography. I still don't completely understand if "Kaye's" life and thoughts differ at all from Katherine's. (As Katherine says on page 10, Kaye shares autobiographical elements from her life, but they are not the same person. But Hayles also says in her acknowledgements that "inventing a persona" allowed her to "work through the barriers I had thrown up around the idea of autobiography so I could finally see it" (142), so I assume that Kaye is Kate, just transformed into words on a page). It is through these stories that I came to an understanding, with Kaye, that hypertext is not necessarily on a electronic text, and that "books are more than encoded voices; they are physical artifacts whose material properties offer potent resources for creating meaning" (107). Kaye's stories and other ones that Hayles's inserts, such as Danielewski's relationship and story to his father, made the book much more enjoyable.

Now for a bit of optimism...

Submitted by marmalade on 7 October 2006 - 10:36am.
  • Hayles
  • readings

I almost stood up and cheered when I came across this passage at at the end of Hayles' first chapter:

"As we work toward critical practices and theories appropriate for electronic literature, we may come to renewed appreciation for the specificity of print. In the tangled web of medial ecology, change anywhere in the system stimulates change everywhere in the system. Books are not going the way of the dinosaur but the way of the human, changing as we change, mutating and evolving in ways that will continue, as a book lover said long ago, to teach and delight" (33).

Finally, none of these dire predictions about the end of books!

We are all created by machines...

Submitted by silversprung on 6 October 2006 - 10:33pm.
  • computers
  • Hayles
  • House of Leaves

It turns out that our discussion of how the novel created the individual (in Nancy Armstrong's book) has several more recent parallels. N. Katherine Hayles mentions two things that struck me as perfect examples of how technology not only affects, but also in a way creates, its users/readers.

The first example is the computer itself. Hayles writes: "In their general form, computers are simulation machines producting environments, from objects that sit on desktops to networks spanning the globe. To construct an environment is, of course, to anticipate and structure the user's interaction with it and in this sense to construct the user as well as the interface" (48). This makes a lot of sense to me. images and texts

Submitted by tophat1 on 6 October 2006 - 3:24pm.
  • Hayles

In her book, Hayles introduces the interesting concept of including images in thinking about texts. In speaking about electronic textuality, she says that "we can no longer afford to ignore the material basis of literary production." (p19). She mentions WJT Mitchell who believes that literary studies should expand to include things such as text image. If we think about the history of the idea of image and text, what comes to mind is particularly, for me, poetry and the arrangements of words to create or support meaning. Many poems would not be the same if they were written and read in the form of a novel.

Orality and Literacy in "Lexia to Perplexia"

Submitted by silversprung on 3 October 2006 - 5:33pm.
  • Hayles
  • Memmott
  • Ong

I've been reading N. Katherine Hayles' "Writing Machines" for the past two days, and she has a lot of really thought-provoking insights about various kinds of electronic literature. (She also has many jargony sentences that make my head spin, but that's a different story.) Her analysis of Talan Memmott's "Lexia to Perplexia" enabled me to think about that work with a kind of intellectual depth that I never would have been able to access without her guidance. But I guess that's why we read criticism and theory, huh?

I found Hayles' discussion of how Memmott creates a "creole" of English and programming code particularly fascinating. She says that this creole "forms the medium trought which the origin of subjectivity can be re-described as coextensive with technology. Just as these hybrid articulations do not exist apart from their penetration by code, so the subject does not exist apart from the technology that produces the creole describing/creating the techno-subject (53).
