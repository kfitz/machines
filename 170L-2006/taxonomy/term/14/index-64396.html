Class Credit and Blog Identity

Submitted by Natwwal on 18 September 2006 - 3:22pm.
  • blogs
  • identity

I've blogged a little in the past, and I'm finding it a very different experience to blog for class. I think my sense of disorientation comes from three things:

1. This class blog has a very set readership. There is always the possibility that a random person will stumble on our blog, but in all liklihood, people from our class are the only ones who will read a given post. Similarly, people in our class can't just stop reading the blog if they get bored. The set audience makes me far more self-concious about what I say.

2. Everyone who reads this blog has at least some knowledge of me in real life. As a result, it isn't possible for this blog to become my identity to the blog's readership. Readers will certainly filter my posts through what they know of me in real life, and something I post on this blog may influence the way people perceive me or my comments in class.

The Mass of the Inconsequential

Submitted by Shock and Awe on 18 September 2006 - 2:18am.
  • blogs
  • bush
  • readings

It was quite a bizarre revelation to find my major trepidation about blogs summed up in an article from 1945. Vandevar Bush worries that "truly significant attainments are being lost in the mass of the inconsequential" in a society in which "publication has been extended far beyond our present ability to make real use of the record." If Einstein came up with his theory of relativity in a forest and no one was around, did he really come up with it? If Johnny Quazar writes the next truly great work of fiction in his blog and the only people who read it are a few kids who link to it from his myspace page, did he really write it?

three thoughts on ong

Submitted by thisismycheese on 11 September 2006 - 8:41am.
  • blogs
  • readings
  • the nature of the blog

1. So, it seems to me that blogs (and online communication in general) sort of find a place between (and share the benefits of) speech and the written word.

Obviously, blogs are written, and therefore they have the benefits of longevity, archival and so on that print has. But the complaints (mostly Greek...those whiners) Ong cites are addressed at least somewhat by the format of blogs. Blogs are much more like a conversation or interpersonal interaction than a book, because they allow for the exchange of ideas, the discourse, that Plato & co. lamented, and because of their sense of (I'm scared to use this word now) immediacy. Entries are written and then posted, and often within a matter of minutes a conversation has started. There isn't some high-and-mighty inaccessible Author--the author is waiting for you to respond. You get the benefits of the written word (it's slower-paced, you can go over an argument before you post it etc.) with a lot fewer drawbacks.

the blogging industry

Submitted by tophat1 on 9 September 2006 - 1:41pm.
  • blogs

I recently opened up a popular women's magazine and found a story about Jessica Coen, a "bored stiff" executive assistant turned blogger for gawker.com. After being accepted to Columbia journalism school, she was offered a job by Nick Denton, the founder of Gawker.com. She accepted the offer and now, at age 26, is the editor of the blog.
Call me old-fashioned, but my initial impression was surprise. She turned down Columbia for a blog?? She said she considered the blog as a "short-cut" to her end goal of being a published writer. I don't know quite what to make of her short-cut. It again brings into question the legitimacy of written work on the net. Additionally, I may question the relative job security of editor at gawker.com vs. graduate of Columbia journalism school. Who knows, maybe she made the right choice...

Who knows

Submitted by zoey on 5 September 2006 - 11:34pm.
  • blogs

So I've never really read that many blogs. I've been looking around and skimming some, but the whole thing is pretty much new territory for me. I kept thinking I needed to post a link to a blog that would say something about what I'm into or what my personality is, like finding the perfect blog and talking about how quirky or interesting it is and how I'm special for appreciating it. Something I've been noticing is that at first glance the writing on a lot of blogs sounds vain and self-absorbed and like the writer while writing is thinking oh man how am I going to get people to notice how cool/smart/talented/funny/sarcastic/whatever I am. But then I realized that maybe it's silly to read blogs thinking like that...like maybe some people are trying to craft a persona and maybe some people are completely real but it's all putting some part of their personality and their ideas out there, and a lot of it is pretty interesting. Anyway, I used to read Belle in the Big Apple because the girl who writes it kind of reminds me of my sister. I hadn't read it in a while but I guess the blog thing is going well for her because she's turning it into a book.
