Why aren't video game reviews more prevalent in the media?

Submitted by Pimm on 23 October 2006 - 7:01pm.
  • video games

I'm still intrigued by our discussion at the end of class today on the lack of video game reviews/coverage in certain newspapers or magazines. During our discussion, I kept thinking the reason why there aren't as many video game reviews, even if the gaming industry is larger than the film industry, is a result of the demographics of who plays. This transcript from a speech given by Doug Lowenstein (with an intro by Hnery Jenkins) at a conference hosted by MIT shows that I was wrong. Granted, this speech was written in 2000, so I definitely need to update my statistics (which I'm having trouble doing), but it showed me that adolescent boys aren't the majority of the users. For example, Lowenstein says of the most frequent users of video games console software, "We found that 65% were male, and 35% were female. 46% of the most frequent users of console games were under 18. 54% were over 18, and an astonishing one fourth was over 36 years old." This made me even more confused why video games aren't more widely covered in the media. Maybe, as someone in class pointed out today, true video game users spend so much time dedicated to play that they wouldn't be the ones writing the reviews. And perhaps the people who control the more "respectable" newspapers/media have the same stereotype of video game users as I do (meaning, adolescent boys play, so those reviews don't need to be in, say, The New Yorker).

Video games in schools

Submitted by Lulu on 20 October 2006 - 2:24am.
  • education
  • video games

I've really enjoyed reading everybody's posts on videogaming, confessions, and the like. It seems like the topic of video games is broad enough that everyone has their own personal stories and memories of what videogaming means to them, whether it be Sims-style creations or the shoot-and-kill variety like CS and Diablo. What about happy memories of games like Oregon Trail, or Carmen Sandiego in elementary school? Does anyone remember playing video games, or rather, computer games in schools?

This article and this article talks about the various effects of using computer games in school. I feel like the idea of harnessing the popular appeal and power of video games and using them as an educational tool in schools has been around for at least a decade. The author of the first article writes that "scientists call it [video games] the next great discovery, a way to captivate students so much they will spend hours learning on their own." I feel like it's a little late to make a statement such as this, since educational video games have been around for a while now and companies learned long ago that videogames are the "next great discovery." The points that the article makes, though, definitely hold true and I can personally identify with a lot of this from grade school memories.

Fun and/or Games

Submitted by magoo on 18 October 2006 - 4:27pm.
  • hypertext fiction
  • video games

I'd be interested in hearing about more examples of very immersive books or games in which form calls attention to itself or that mix game and narrative. I'm not yet familiar with the examples mentioned in class, so I'm having trouble refining the idea.

Here's what I have so far.

1) We find games that involve less aspects of narrative to be more immersive.
2) We find narratives that involve less aspects of gameplay to be more immersive.

Apparently, exceptions exist. An examination of how these exceptions mix the two might show much of how each and both operate.

Meanwhile, I suspect that stories and games become less immersive as they blend because the two forms relate to perceived reality in conflicting ways. Stories represent perceived reality. Games do not. In the sense that I mean this, it's not less true of fanciful stories or photorealistic games. No one asks whether baseball is more or less realistic than football. Chess and even go are often supposed to be representative of war, but having played both for years, I don't see how. Computer games create worlds that involve extensive representation of familiar elements, but I'd argue that insofar as the user plays a game, the representational elements' primary function is to intuitively establish game rules. That is, if one sees a wall, one realizes that an obstacle exists without having to check the manual. In many RPG's, when one sees a bottle or a drumstick, that represents some capacity one can gain for one's avatar. Representational elements contribute to crucial semantics of play in ways that create a different world. The play world is not true or false, but different. Conversely, critics and readers reduce the fluffiest romance or the most unsubstantiated scifi to basic human desires and conflicts, however stylized. And we can talk extensively about particular nuances in the way the story is or is not authentic or truthful.

"Real" video game characters

Submitted by Lulu on 15 October 2006 - 8:14pm.
  • readings
  • video games

In reading Ken Perlin's essay on finding an intermediate form between games and stories, I've started to wonder whether his pursuit of a middle ground is entirely possible and whether consumers would be satisfied with the results.

He contrasts the non-agency of reading books or watching movies with the hyper-agency we experience when we play video games, calling for the creation of a suitable form that can properly combine both. I think during this current stage of videogaming, Wright's response to Perlin's essay is a more relevant and realistic discussion, especially when he says he wants players to "play better stories." That seems most important to me; I feel like that strategy stays true to the goal of games while also respecting player agency.
