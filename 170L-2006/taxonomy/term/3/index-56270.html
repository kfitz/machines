In her comment on thisismycheese's post, Zoey quotes Henry Jenkins' blog, in which he says, "When Espen Aarseth and I sat down together a few years ago at the HumLab, we found that there was relatively little to debate. Ms Dewey and the invasion of the virtual personality

Submitted by Shock and Awe on 17 October 2006 - 3:20pm.
  • technology
  • virtual ethics
  • virtual personality

A few posts ago I mentioned how I imagined the future blog to be a wise-looking old man. Well apparently someone out there sees the future search engine as a sexy young multiracial woman in a lowcut but very classy black suit/dress. Her name is Ms. Dewey. She talks on her cell-phone, reads a magazine, and pesters you to query her with more searches. She grows bored and annoyed when neglected.

And she is damn creepy. Why? Well first of all there are the disturbing racial and gender implications of her submissive+sassy attitude and attire. But, and this is what I'm interested in, it seems also to be an issue of invasion. There's something disturbing about having such a vivid virtual personality present itself on your computer screen. Even when the page is minimized or sub-tabbed you can feel her presence, watching you, waiting for instruction.

New art vs. new books

Submitted by Lulu on 11 October 2006 - 8:42pm.
  • art
  • technology

During a conversation today with magoo and thenewblack, we touched upon the ever-popular theme of materiality in texts. Something magoo said reminded me of the Dada movement. I'm still a little unclear as to what exactly Dada is, but from what I've heard here and there, Dada can be pretty much any form of art that is also "anti-art," manifested through literature, music, art, etc. Back in high school, I had a poetry assignment in one of my English classes in which my teacher asked us to create a poem using random words cut out from magazines and newspaper clippings; he said this was a form of dada-ism. I think the point was to break our conventional idea of what a poem should be, and to be aware that there's more than one, fixed creative process.

Trying Again To Make Books Obsolete

Submitted by silversprung on 11 October 2006 - 8:19pm.
  • electronic literature
  • technology

I just came across an article that is remarkably pertinent to our class. It's from the NY Times Technology section, and it's a review of the Sony Reader, which is a portable 5" x 7" screen that will go on sale this month for $350.

It seems like the Reader has a lot going for it. Apparently it manages to avoid what is our biggest turn-off about reading things on screens: the fact that, well, it looks like a screen, and not a page. I won't describe in detail what the technology is that produces this page-like appearance, but it's actually really cool-sounding-- I recommend you read the article. The Reader can also do things that a traditional book can't-- like play music or photos. I, for one, despite my love of paper's tactile qualities, am curious about how this actually looks. And I'd consider investing in it if I had a job that required me to read a lot on-the-go and it were a little cheaper to buy.

old books smell good

Submitted by thisismycheese on 7 October 2006 - 7:05pm.
  • books
  • hypertext fiction
  • readings
  • technology

1. I was really pleased by this statement (from page 99 of Writing Machines): "Contrary to much hype about electronic hypertext, books like A Humument allow the reader considerably more freedom of movement and access than do many electronic fictions."

...yep. I don't really have anything to say about that, except that I agree completely and so I wanted to point it out. Even if in hypertexty novels (like House of Leaves, which I love) we aren't making "choices" the way we do in electronic literature, we are able to make actual informed choices--like choosing to read the last page--instead of just clicking things at random. Hayles points out that "logical ordering and linear sequencing" are just as important as association (75), and while not all books are chronological, there is at least some sense of order, of beginning and end. And of course we can bookmark, highlight, etc. to our heart's content, which we can't do in hypertexts.

Our desire for interactive media

Submitted by Pimm on 2 October 2006 - 1:53pm.
  • electronic literature
  • technology

After reading some posts, like Lulu's on the Tamagotchi and Shock and Awe's on interactive fiction, and looking at the New York Times website, I'm curious how far this desire for electronic interaction with books, toys, news, etc will go. It seems weird to me that Michael Joyce's "Afternoon" can only be played in Classic on Macs and that, as we talked about in class last week, if Classic were no longer possible to use, his text would become extinct. Of course, from the examples of the Tamagotchi or the interactive fiction, his ideas didn't fade and morphed into different things. When I mentioned earlier that I looked at the New York Times page, I noticed one headline in particular--"Student Killed in Amish Schoolhouse"--that includes a video with the article. I always thought that online news sites provided video for articles that they thought people wouldn't read, but clearly this isn't true anymore (for instance, the article I mentioned is about such a awful, gruesome topic that I feel people will be drawn to the words in order to learn more). So what creates this desire to have electronic interaction? Why do people want more than simply to read words? By the way, I don't mean to imply that I think only hard, non-electronic news, toys, and books are better than interactive material. I love photographs and video that accompany news stories, and I'm a sucker for interactive, electronic toys. I'm just curious where the line will be drawn on this interaction, and, on a somewhat different note, why more hasn't been done to preserve the forerunners of hypertextuality, like the "Afternoon."

libraries and librarians

Submitted by marmalade on 28 September 2006 - 9:13am.
  • technology

My mother is a retired librarian. She tried for years to convince the university where she worked to install movable shelves--the old school kind that they have in the basement of Honnold. After she left, the school finally put them in--but the new-fangled sort, that move electronically, beep when they're about to crush you, and malfunction often. They've gotten rid of the card catalog, too. My mother loves that library, but everytime that she comes out, she looks confused and frazzled.

I remember Joyce's criticism that we've forgotten to really read and concentrate, getting caught up in the summary, the list in the card catalog, the table of contents. Maybe that's a flaw--but I don't think it's the real flaw. There's lots of newspaper stories about kids these days being unable to concentrate. And it's not because they've been using the card catalog--psychologists blame it on the computer. My mother's the best reader I know, and she has this ingrained technology aversion. She sits down and reads the New York Times (paper copy) from cover to cover every morning, and then reads the Atlantic, a novel, or something else just to fill up the evening hours.

the joys of tabs

Submitted by thisismycheese on 27 September 2006 - 12:44pm.
  • technology

Using Internet Explorer in this class has reminded me of a fairly recent Big Thing in browsing--tabs!

Oh, how I love tabs. I find it hard to work without them, as evidenced by the 589823953 windows I currently have open.

But it got me thinking about what some of the readings for this week talked about--namely, the complaints that if you're on LotsOfLinks.com, you have to keep hitting the "back" button after you've visited each link to get back to the original list of links.

Which tabs fix, really, since you just control-click the link and it opens it in a tab, and you can load tons of tabs at the same time, and it's amazing and great. Clearly Somebody Up There agreed with Joyce that having to go back and forth was really inefficient.
