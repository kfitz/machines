Reflections on Reading

Submitted by silversprung on 24 September 2006 - 10:23pm.
  • electronic literature
  • readings
  • technology

I suppose it is a testament to my underlying control-freak tendencies that as I read "Afternoon," I found myself wanting to explore all the options that were possible from whatever screen I was on-- I didn't want to miss anything. I kept retracing my steps, going backwards in the story in order to go forwards in a different direction. Except that there really is no backwards or forwards in "Afternoon," which laughs loudly in poor little linearity's face and then steals its lunch money.

Eventually, my reading experience became a quest for the new: I began to "surf" the story, looking for new branches I hadn't seen before. Reading "Afternoon" felt kind of addictive in this way, because I didn't want to stop reading the story while I was still discovering new parts of it. I guess this impulse to "finish" the text has a lot to do with the way printed class reading is assigned: one reads the whole selection, and then one has finished the assignment. But with "Afternoon," the challenge is not finishing the reading, but actually unearthing the whole text and making sense of it. In fact, Joyce deliberately denies closure to the reader--even the meta-screens that comment on the reader's unending, evolving experience consciously refuse to end the story. It was engaging, but also somewhat frustrating.

"Why tlk whn u cn txt?"

Submitted by Lulu on 21 September 2006 - 11:36pm.
  • technology

I read this disturbing article about how text- and instant-messaging has replaced actual human contact among many people. It describes how people feel more comfortable with their online alias and conversing in the electronic world than actually talking face-to-face. One teenager even said that "face-to-face you run out of things to talk about." It strikes me as really sad that many people have lost their ability to be social human beings as a result of the internet and other technological gadgets. Some say they feel more in control when they're texting or IMing, and that in face-to-face situations, "you're not presenting the real you, you're just reacting."

Technology and Individuality

Submitted by silversprung on 21 September 2006 - 1:19pm.
  • readings
  • technology

I've been chewing on the relationship between technology and individuality for awhile, partially as a result of a blog post that magoo made several days ago.

I can see how it might seem that technology, as it gradually performs more and more functions that our own bodies once performed by themselves (I'm channelling McLuhan here), would sap us of our individualism. Human beings perform functions like hearing, writing, drawing, etc. in unique ways, but technology tends to standardize them. (Witness the Industrial Revolution and the advent of interchangeable positions on the assembly line and interchangeable mechanical parts, as magoo points out.)

talk to the blog

Submitted by thisismycheese on 19 September 2006 - 9:09pm.
  • readings
  • technology

So, in section 3 of the Bush reading, he asks, "...will the author of the future cease writing by hand or typewriter and talk directly to the record?"

When I first read that, I said, "haha," and then remembered that the technology does in fact exist. I could buy some software and voila! I could talk to my computer and it would do the typing for me.

...right. I would never, ever do that. It would hurt my brain.

Do any of you have experience with this software? I know of one blogger who uses it, but it seems like it's uncommon. Obviously it's not perfect software, which is part of the problem (after all, if you're spending time correcting errors, you might as well just type)--but I wonder if there's a deeper issue with the idea of speaking writing instead of writing it. Ong talked about how literacy has shaped the way we think, but at least personally, I can't think unless I write, at least not as well or as articulately (not that I'm articulate here). I'm attached to the physical process of writing, to actually feeling words form, and speaking and then seeing the words appear on the screen doesn't quite cut it.

Computer, Individual, & the Inverted Turing Test of Culture

Submitted by magoo on 18 September 2006 - 9:57am.
  • McLuhan
  • readings
  • technology

I've seen a few entries about machines and individuality or humanity. This is an issue that feels different today than it did in 1965 or 1980.

I strongly suspect that a lot of the assumption that computers would impose inhuman standards on humans comes from the broad dissatisfaction with the culture that came out of manufacturing. Manufacturing enforced uniformity and non-responsiveness to human rythms on many aspects of human life. Trains and corporations had to run on time. Information had to be stored and retrieved by varied people; therefore, filing systems had to be created that used least-common-denominator ways of organizing information -- last name first, alphabetic, for example, or by date.
