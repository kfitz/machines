A Kind of Novel

Submitted by magoo on 30 October 2006 - 3:49pm.
  • books
  • hypertext fiction
  • Kind of Blue

I experienced it almost exactly as I might a novel, and it sounded today as if at least part of you agreed. I'm not sure whether receiving it as emails would have made much difference. I'm willing to entertain the idea that it might have, but I'm suspicious.

It seems novelistic in several ways:

  • Clear unilinear sequence
  • Advancing plot that builds to crisis
  • Defined sense of where one is in the story (just scroll down)
  • Ease in rereading or flipping ahead

I'm not convinced that it engages readers differently than do novels. After all, novels have purported to be lost diaries or manuscripts, sets of letters, all sorts of things, really. One holds the book and knows that it is a book. One views Kind of Blue online and knows it's not an inbox.

Hypertext and expectation

Submitted by crashingintowalls on 30 October 2006 - 11:35am.
  • hypertext fiction

So after coming down hard on onlinecaroline
and planet jenna over the weekend, I find myself in the camp with thisismycheese and Shock and Awe who had a much better time with Kind of Blue. I'm trying to put together the difference in my experience, as well as to perhaps begin a frame for some discussion today, and I have two angles on what separated these texts.

What I feel differentiates these hypertexts from those that we read earlier is that they do not readily admit to being hypertexts. We have two hypertexts posing as multimedia online friendships and one posing as a stranger's inbox that we can access. I, who had a good time with the first round of hypertexts, didn't immediately realize that this set of "New Writings" could be considered hypertext until I had been reading for a while. Online writing takes many forms, so it makes sense to me that personal websites and email are as much in bounds for hypertext as epistolary format is for the novel.

Kind of Seductive

Submitted by Shock and Awe on 29 October 2006 - 7:38pm.
  • hypertext
  • hypertext fiction

I've just delved into Scott Rettberg's Kind of Blue, and so far I'm impressed. It's the first hypertext we've looked at in which I really feel like the form is elevating the content. Kind of Blue could not function nearly as well in book form. It's the kind of story that needs to be a hypertext, and, as such, it doesn't feel forced--like the author is shaping the story to fit the form or vice-versa.

The device of emails from many senders allows the author to naturally assume many voices, and makes for the kind of story that I at least was seduced into trying to figure out. It's a simple concept: going through someone else's inbox and one common enough that it doesn't feel gimmicky. The multiple perspectives also give Rettberg tremendous freedom in his writing. You can tell he's having fun, throwing in cryptic snippets of poetry, lists, and the butchered spelling of children at different stages of keyboard adeptness.

Fun and/or Games

Submitted by magoo on 18 October 2006 - 4:27pm.
  • hypertext fiction
  • video games

I'd be interested in hearing about more examples of very immersive books or games in which form calls attention to itself or that mix game and narrative. I'm not yet familiar with the examples mentioned in class, so I'm having trouble refining the idea.

Here's what I have so far.

1) We find games that involve less aspects of narrative to be more immersive.
2) We find narratives that involve less aspects of gameplay to be more immersive.

Apparently, exceptions exist. An examination of how these exceptions mix the two might show much of how each and both operate.

Meanwhile, I suspect that stories and games become less immersive as they blend because the two forms relate to perceived reality in conflicting ways. Stories represent perceived reality. Games do not. In the sense that I mean this, it's not less true of fanciful stories or photorealistic games. No one asks whether baseball is more or less realistic than football. Chess and even go are often supposed to be representative of war, but having played both for years, I don't see how. Computer games create worlds that involve extensive representation of familiar elements, but I'd argue that insofar as the user plays a game, the representational elements' primary function is to intuitively establish game rules. That is, if one sees a wall, one realizes that an obstacle exists without having to check the manual. In many RPG's, when one sees a bottle or a drumstick, that represents some capacity one can gain for one's avatar. Representational elements contribute to crucial semantics of play in ways that create a different world. The play world is not true or false, but different. Conversely, critics and readers reduce the fluffiest romance or the most unsubstantiated scifi to basic human desires and conflicts, however stylized. And we can talk extensively about particular nuances in the way the story is or is not authentic or truthful.

Daunted by Design

Submitted by Shock and Awe on 11 October 2006 - 9:31pm.
  • hypertext fiction
  • Larsen
  • Memmott
  • squiggly lines
  • website design

I, like magoo, was very interested in the discussion on Monday relating to our frustration with the "bad web design" of Lexia Perplexia and Disappearing Rain. Not being a web-designer myself, I was struck by a vague feeling of dissatisfaction reading the two of them. Upon reflection, I realized it was because of how aesthetically displeasing they were. I'm used to web-sites that look good, with nice color schemes, cool fonts, pictures, layout, etc. I like my websites like I like my magazines: slick.

Certainly part of the problem is that Lexia and Rain are old, and Larsen and Memmott were working with the techonology they had at the time, but I think it raises an interesting question: In regard to hypertexts, is being a good writer not enough? Do you need to be a good programmer/web-designer, too?

Larsen's navigational elements

Submitted by magoo on 9 October 2006 - 8:21pm.
  • hypertext fiction
  • Larsen
  • website design

I'm interested to read how much success Larsen had with the class despite some complaints about the "writing itself" not being all that one might desire. I wonder whether the clarity and discreet differentiation created by the navigational scheme might not relate in some way to the disappointment some felt with the writing.

One hears that writers like Shakespeare are admirable in part because they don't moralize. In some ways, Larsen's explicit links and navigational elements function like -- well, not quite morals, but certainly labels of a sort. At the same time, they certainly do provide a sense of intentional connection between lexia that seems popular -- I think because it allows readers to consider the lexia together.
