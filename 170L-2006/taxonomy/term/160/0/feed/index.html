Writing Machines - morals http://machines.pomona.edu.170L-2006/taxonomy/term/160/0 en Identity Configuration http://machines.pomona.edu.170L-2006/node/259 <p>While this may seem to be something of a regression, I find myself still thinking about certain aspects of last week's reading. In particular I am interested in the idea that Stuart Moulthrop raises that games "appeal because they are configurative, offering the chance to manipulate complex systems within continuous loops of intervention..." Moulthrop's exploration of this topic seems to fall under a more "nuts and bolts" perspective in that he is concerned with the manipulations of the game's rules and the objects in the gameworld (as I understand it).</p> <p>I remember a game I played years ago, back when I was more into games than I am today. It was a role-playing game called Fallout, where you created a character and wandered about in a post-apocalyptic wasteland hanging out with mutants, killing things and tipping radioactive two-headed cows (actually this was a configuration that I never pulled off, though I wanted to). This was before MMORPGs were really big, so creators of these games had to find ways to make the gameworld reactive, which more or less happens as a matter of course in the case of the online games.</p> <p><a href="http://machines.pomona.edu.170L-2006/node/259">read more</a></p> http://machines.pomona.edu.170L-2006/node/259#comments games morals Moulthrop Mon, 23 Oct 2006 10:25:31 +0000 thenewblack 259 at http://machines.pomona.edu.170L-2006
