Eh, why not...I'll confess something too

Submitted by Pimm on 19 October 2006 - 8:45pm.
  • games
  • readings

I have a bit of a confession to make, although perhaps others will say that they did the same thing I'm about to admit. I used to play the Sims a lot, and I got really into it. I created worlds that resembled mine. Actually, they were more fantasy worlds than my own surroundings, and that's where my confession comes in. My friend I used to pick boys we liked and "create" them, and (I bet you can see where this is going) create ourselves, too. It was great: it was like playing a grown-up version of house, complete with cooking dinner, getting a job, and making babies together. Of course, it all seems a bit sophomoric now, but I definitely used it as an escape. For this reason, I loved reading about the different author's analyses to the Sims in First Person. Ken Perlin's article especially resonated with me. I liked the Sims because I became a sort of author, yet that "willing suspense of belief" (17) that the characters were real never came to me. Granted, I did get extremely caught up in the people I "created" in hopes that their actions (which I, of course, controlled) would come true. This ties into thisismycheese's discussion of the different types of immersion. The Sims made me feel mid-way between the two types of immersion thisismychesse describes: I felt like I was in the game, as a character, yet I never truly felt like I was really there. And I certainly never felt the same emotional immersion I feel when I read novels.

So, I was excited to see that First Person talks about gaming, as that topic has spawned a few of the more interesting conversations on this blog. What I wasn't expecting was how closely the articles would tie in with the conversations we've been having about electronic literature--and particularly the complains a lot of us have made about the artificiality/awkwardness of the e-lit we've looked at.

In Bryan Loyall's response to the first article (Janet Murray's) he writes: "One property of Murray's three main examples is that the participant is consciously aware of the story and actively manipulating it. Materiality in First Person book

I really enjoyed the juxtaposition of essays and commentary in Wardrip-Fruin and Harrigan's First Person. I thought the format of putting responses and commentary directly below their respective essays on the same page gave us a nice contrast of opinions and points. It also reminded me of our class blog, and I thought it was cool that the book was able to achieve this blogging/commenting feature in print.

"Real" video game characters

Submitted by Lulu on 15 October 2006 - 8:14pm.
  • readings
  • video games

In reading Ken Perlin's essay on finding an intermediate form between games and stories, I've started to wonder whether his pursuit of a middle ground is entirely possible and whether consumers would be satisfied with the results.

He contrasts the non-agency of reading books or watching movies with the hyper-agency we experience when we play video games, calling for the creation of a suitable form that can properly combine both. I think during this current stage of videogaming, Wright's response to Perlin's essay is a more relevant and realistic discussion, especially when he says he wants players to "play better stories." That seems most important to me; I feel like that strategy stays true to the goal of games while also respecting player agency.

who are these authors?

Submitted by marmalade on 9 October 2006 - 2:02pm.
  • readings

I know the question was brought up in class today about who Deena Larsen really is, and we didn't really get around to answering it.

Here's a link, however, to a bit of a bio about Larsen, some links to her other works, and a brief interview with her. Apparently electronic fiction is her thing--although her day job is as a "technical writer". She's written a lot of stuff over the years, and some of it looks pretty neat (I've only done a quick inspection of some of the links).

Talan Memmott is the guy who I was most interested in, however--specifically whether or not he considered himself an artist or a writer. I did some poking around this weekend and discovered that, unfortunately for my categorization system, he considers himself both! He originally started out with a background in fine arts and then moved into writing. Here's an interview with him on an arty website talking about Lexia, and here's another, somewhat longer interview with him that's more focused on his vision of electronic literature. And, if that's not enough to read, perhaps this will also be helpful--Memmott's own website and some of his newer works... For something in the vein of Lexia, check out The Berth of V-ness (under the Media Work link), but also check out some of his more straight forward (all terms are, of course, relative) literary stuff under the "Writings" link.
