Pinpointer

Submitted by Frabby on 5 November 2006 - 10:09pm.
  • readings

I was pretty surprised to read this from "Distributed Narratives" concerning personal blogs-

"Although these sites are public, and their authors are well-aware that their words are read by many, my pinpointing connections and analyzing relationships by the words in which they are told seems to cross a boundary that should not be crossed"

Walker finds this sort of examination "ethically problematic." Does she mean it would be unethical to map out all the personal relationships and intricacies and then write about it? That may be unethical (maybe) but it also wouldn't be very interesting in an academic arena, so it's kind of a moot point. If Walker means it's unethical to do so out of curiosity, then I think I disagree. If you're invested in someone's blog, particularly the blog of someone you've never met, why wouldn't you want to read their friends' blogs? Why wouldn't you want to read about different perspectives on your blogger? Different stories?

hard-core sticker art

Submitted by Frabby on 5 November 2006 - 12:50pm.
  • readings

I got a lot out of Walker's "Distributed Narratives." She brought up a lot of interesting stuff (though didn't seem to give herself enough space to discuss them all). I particularly liked the sticker art discussion. To me, it seems like the older, pre-Internet project Truisms by Jenny Holzer was more--hard-core. That's not a very good word for it, but in the age of the web, things like Implementation don't have much mystery to them, because the segments are all posted online.

Walker writes, "the implicit links of the distributed narrative are instead invitations that encourage the audience to go and find more" (10). With Implementation, the finding it pretty minimal, it's all right there. There was something more interesting and secret-club-esque about Holzer's pieces scattered all over Manhattan. Of course, the nature of these projects are different in that Holzer was a one-woman show while Implementation incorporated lots of different people who were given freedom to place the stickers where they wanted. But still. If you found a string of stickers out of nowhere, and didn't have the power to Google it, then I think you'd feel pretty cool.

A "mortal" work of art

Submitted by Lulu on 4 November 2006 - 2:38am.
  • Distributed Narrative
  • readings
  • Skin

I was very intrigued by Shelley Jackson's Skin project. The idea that "each participant will be known as a word, and as words die, the story will disintegrate" is fascinating, and I'm also a little disturbed at how destabilizing this can be for a text. (12)

Walker calls this a form of distributed narrative, and it's a perfect example of her so-called "feral hypertext," an undomesticated and wild text. My question is, if this narrative is written in such a way that it cannot be read, is it still a narrative? It fits under Walker's definition of what a distributed narrative should be, but for the unsuspecting reader/viewer, would we approach this as a work of art and not even consider it a narrative? For me, I saw the project's concept as the story which the author is telling, rather than seeing the actual content of "Skin" as the narrative. Does this make sense?

Webcams gone wrong

Submitted by Pimm on 1 November 2006 - 10:52am.
  • readings
  • webcams

I decided to investigate Jennicam, the webcam girl who was mentioned briefly in class on Monday. Wikipedia's information on her was interesting, but the information I stumbled upon that was linked that page was even more fascinating. I clicked on another Wikipedia page entitled " Cam Whores", which, according to Wikipedia, is "a term for people who expose themselves on the Internet with webcam software in exchange for goods, usually via enticing viewers to purchase items on their wishlists or add to their online accounts." To learn more, I clicked on a link on that page to an article in The New York Times from December 2005 on Justin Berry, a boy who at the age of 13 began using webcams in order to get more friends and ended up basically starting a pornography business. For five years, he brought in tons of money from pedophiles. There's a link on the left side to a 7-part interview series from the NY Times on him, and it is incredibly sad, moving, and fascinating. His story, both about how he began his business and why he chose to end it by turning in himself and all his files to the FBI, made me think of our class for a few reasons, some more obvious than others. First of all, I asked earlier about the word voyeurism, and this definitely seems like an example of it. Secondly, when he talked about the pedophiles (who, creepily enough often ended up being people who work with children, such as teachers, pediatricians, and child lawyers), he mentions how he thought of them as "friends," though the friendship was completely constructed and often built on lies and an artificial web relationship. Does this remind anyone else of Jemma? Of course I'm not saying that Jemma played the role of either pedophile or pornographer, but it did ring a bell with me and made her forced friendship feel even more odd (and the promotion of her site to, say, teenagers as wrong). Finally, Justin turned over the conversations he had with his "customers" that were logged on the computer, so, "Kind of Blue"-style, the FBI could (and is currently, I think) investigate them in a huge lawsuit.
