Writing Machines - Douglas &amp; Hargadon http://machines.pomona.edu.170L-2006/taxonomy/term/184/0 en Schemas that talk http://machines.pomona.edu.170L-2006/node/310 <p>I am writing a thesis on Henry James. At the beginning of this semester, I was talking with one of my thesis readers, and she recommended that, during this year, I try to be thinking about James with every paper I write. She said this, and I felt myself do the thing -- wide eyes and two or three quick head nods -- that means interest and assent, all the while thinking, "Is she nuts? I wouldn't even know how to try, and anyway, thinking that much about James would be deadly." The weird thing, as it turns out, is that I didn't have to try. Looking back on what I've written so far this semester, everything -- be it on a poem by Lewis Carroll, William Blake, a syllogistic proof, or this blog -- has been processing some Jamesian thematic or another. Or maybe I've just learned to see it that way.</p> <p>This is partly to do with why I've been thinking, lately, about Douglas and Hargadon's discussion of schema theory. Not only do these schematics help me to think about the way my own mind is working, but they also apply to a whole lot of what we seem to be trying to process in this class and on this blog.</p> <p><a href="http://machines.pomona.edu.170L-2006/node/310">read more</a></p> http://machines.pomona.edu.170L-2006/node/310#comments categories Douglas &amp; Hargadon schemas Mon, 30 Oct 2006 20:29:20 +0000 Oz 310 at http://machines.pomona.edu.170L-2006
