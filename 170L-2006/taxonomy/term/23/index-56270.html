Arianna Huffington as "the human blog"

Submitted by zoey on 15 October 2006 - 7:30pm.
  • blog and human interaction
  • the nature of the blog

I came across this article "The Human Blog" about Arianna Huffington. It's a lot of promotional stuff and blah blah but it did make me think about the nature of the blog and the nature of the person who is participating in most of the technologies (and the communities that arise because of them) that we have today. The article states, "Everywhere she goes turns into a series of links, links that lead to other links. She's a human blog." At first this just seems like one of those tagline throwaway sentences, but then I started wondering how it might have some relevance. Further down in the article the author claims, "her ability to shift swiftly from topic to topic, her swashbuckling political rhetoric, her penchant for attention-getting--are what the online world is all about." It links the online world to the idea of popularity and "contagious ideas." All this interconnectedness and sharing of information, and sharing information quickly and being blatantly honest and persuasive, well, it's working for her, right? Influential people use her blog as a place to put out their opinions, and she gets to orchestrate the whole thing. The article seems to be suggesting that a certain kind of person will excel in the online world...does this mean that some people are better suited for the world of print? I guess where I might go with that is, well, if Huffington is more successful in the world of blogs, and there are obviously other people who find the most success in the online world, how is their influence different from the influence of those who have success in print? Does someone like Huffington gain influence because her work is constantly on the computer screen and constantly changing, because her work can shift continuously?

Loneliness and the Internet

Submitted by zoey on 2 October 2006 - 1:11am.
  • creation of online content
  • the nature of the blog
  • wiki

I've been stuck on this quote: "I am afraid that one of the things that makes the web in its current form so attractive is that so many of us are afraid and lonely and do not know what to think or who will hear us" (from Joyce's "Othermindedness" p52). I've been thinking about the theme of loneliness lately in connection with people who create content and put it on the web where it can be seen by a potentially huge audience. Why do people post intensely personal diaries of their life and why do people want to read them? Why do people want other people they've never met before to know mundane little details of their daily lives--where they went to eat, what they bought at Target, what song they're listening to while they're writing their entry? Maybe because these things all say something about the person who is reporting them; they convey some kind of information the writer wants you to acknowledge. But why do we want to acknowledge this information, to get some brief grasp on how somebody else lives their life, to speculate about what some teenage girl's life is like as with the whole Lonelygirl thing?

Fareed Zakaria discusses mediation and blogging in a fascinating way in the conclusion of his book The Future of Freedom (W W Norton and Company, 2003).

"The personal web site (blog) was hailed as the killer of the traditional media. In fact it has become something quite different. Far from replacing newspapers and magazines, the best blogs--and the best are very clever--have become guides to them, pointing out unusual sources and commenting on familiar ones. They have become new mediators for the informed public...the creators of blogs think of themselves as radical democrats, the are in fact a new Tocquevillean elite" (254).

the blog as novel

Submitted by thisismycheese on 16 September 2006 - 3:47pm.
  • the nature of the blog

A lot of issues seem to surround the idea of the fictional blog (see Kaycee Nicole)--something that strikes me as really strange, considering that most personal blogging is just storytelling.

I started wondering: What are people's expectations when they read blogs? Is it really voyeurism in the vein of the webcam, where we want to see Exactly What's Happening To John As It Happens?

Well--no. If that's what we were looking for, we would probably just watch a webcame (or buy a telescope...)

Blogs aren't immediate in that way. People write drafts on paper or on Microsoft Word. Everything is time-stamped, sure, but I know I'm not the only blogger who's stewed over an entry for days or even weeks before posting it (or, as pseudoanonymity mentioned in class, plenty of people backdate entries for an array of reasons). Lots of people use pseudonyms and/or fictionalize events, conversations, etc.--there isn't a blog in existence that records life exactly as it happens. Bloggers omit events and anecdotes that aren't relevant to their audience and leave out details that don't contribute to the "plot"--and most blogs, especially the popular ones, do have a clear story, some kind of through-line that keeps us reading.

Symbiosis between computers and humans

Submitted by silversprung on 12 September 2006 - 9:44am.
  • Bolter and Grusin
  • McLuhan
  • readings
  • the nature of the blog

Something I've been thinking about (the relationship between people and media):

Bolter and Grusin write: "Computer programs may ultimately be human products, in the sense that they embody algorithms devised by human programmers, but once the program is written and loaded, the machine can operate without human intervention. Programming, then, employes erasure or effacement" (27).

Two observations on this quotation:

1. The blog seems to be one big exception to B&G's assertion that all computer programs can function without human intervention. You can't have a blog unless you have a human interacting with the computer software-- and frequently. The same goes for 'online community' sites like MySpace, Friendster, and Facebook. Because the purpose of these sites is to extend the human processes of talking to other people and making friends into a technological world, they do not function unless people interact with them and input information into them. Even computer-programmed chatbots rely on actual human beings talking to them in order to learn the patterns of human speech. They can't be programmed with everything they need to know and then left alone.
