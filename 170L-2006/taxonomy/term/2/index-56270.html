Trying Again To Make Books Obsolete

Submitted by silversprung on 11 October 2006 - 8:19pm.
  • electronic literature
  • technology

I just came across an article that is remarkably pertinent to our class. It's from the NY Times Technology section, and it's a review of the Sony Reader, which is a portable 5" x 7" screen that will go on sale this month for $350.

It seems like the Reader has a lot going for it. Apparently it manages to avoid what is our biggest turn-off about reading things on screens: the fact that, well, it looks like a screen, and not a page. I won't describe in detail what the technology is that produces this page-like appearance, but it's actually really cool-sounding-- I recommend you read the article. The Reader can also do things that a traditional book can't-- like play music or photos. I, for one, despite my love of paper's tactile qualities, am curious about how this actually looks. And I'd consider investing in it if I had a job that required me to read a lot on-the-go and it were a little cheaper to buy.

Every time I start trying to write down my messy thoughts about this material, I find that what I'm writing quickly turns negative in tone. Maybe this is because the thoughts are messy; I wouldn't exactly be the first person to not really like what I don't really understand. On the other hand, maybe there's a reason for my inherent, if mild, antipathy toward this stuff, and maybe that reason is informative.

In class and on the blog, we have definitely identified the resistance that the electronic literature we've encountered seems to have toward conventional readability. The theorists seem to regard this resistance as a virtue, while we have been considerably more suspicious. And justifiably so, I think. Here we've spent a few years learning to be increasingly serious about literature, and suddenly we're reading stuff that seems... hokey. Seems to rely on preciousness, fireworks and formal conceit.

Our desire for interactive media

Submitted by Pimm on 2 October 2006 - 1:53pm.
  • electronic literature
  • technology

After reading some posts, like Lulu's on the Tamagotchi and Shock and Awe's on interactive fiction, and looking at the New York Times website, I'm curious how far this desire for electronic interaction with books, toys, news, etc will go. It seems weird to me that Michael Joyce's "Afternoon" can only be played in Classic on Macs and that, as we talked about in class last week, if Classic were no longer possible to use, his text would become extinct. Of course, from the examples of the Tamagotchi or the interactive fiction, his ideas didn't fade and morphed into different things. When I mentioned earlier that I looked at the New York Times page, I noticed one headline in particular--"Student Killed in Amish Schoolhouse"--that includes a video with the article. I always thought that online news sites provided video for articles that they thought people wouldn't read, but clearly this isn't true anymore (for instance, the article I mentioned is about such a awful, gruesome topic that I feel people will be drawn to the words in order to learn more). So what creates this desire to have electronic interaction? Why do people want more than simply to read words? By the way, I don't mean to imply that I think only hard, non-electronic news, toys, and books are better than interactive material. I love photographs and video that accompany news stories, and I'm a sucker for interactive, electronic toys. I'm just curious where the line will be drawn on this interaction, and, on a somewhat different note, why more hasn't been done to preserve the forerunners of hypertextuality, like the "Afternoon."

Submitted by Pimm on 25 September 2006 - 12:35am.
  • electronic literature
  • Michael Joyce
  • readings

It's amazing how strong--and similar--many of the responses to Joyce's "Afternoon" have been. Like shock and awe and many others from our class have mentioned, I was frustrated with the format of "Afternoon." However, the hypertext was also incredibly intriguing, and it was definitely worth "reading" in order to get first-hand experience with hypertexts.

At first I went through "Afternoon" the same way I used to read the "choose-your-own-adventure" books that I read when I was little (and that Joyce references in his article, as well as Lulu on the blog). I soon realized how different the two are. You can't completely reread, as I used to do with adventure books when I chose a certain path for the heroine but was immediately disappointed with my decision and eager to see option B. I found that with "Afternoon," every time I chose "yes" then clicked back and chose "no", I ended up with the same piece of story on the next screen. I assume that I hadn't reached a part where I had the power to make the decision "yes" or "no" that actually mattered, but it was still interesting to see how active I actually could be as reader and semi-writer (which is, as both Joyce and Landow seem to say, one of the main purposes of the text).

Reflections on Reading

Submitted by silversprung on 24 September 2006 - 10:23pm.
  • electronic literature
  • readings
  • technology

I suppose it is a testament to my underlying control-freak tendencies that as I read "Afternoon," I found myself wanting to explore all the options that were possible from whatever screen I was on-- I didn't want to miss anything. I kept retracing my steps, going backwards in the story in order to go forwards in a different direction. Except that there really is no backwards or forwards in "Afternoon," which laughs loudly in poor little linearity's face and then steals its lunch money.

Eventually, my reading experience became a quest for the new: I began to "surf" the story, looking for new branches I hadn't seen before. Reading "Afternoon" felt kind of addictive in this way, because I didn't want to stop reading the story while I was still discovering new parts of it. I guess this impulse to "finish" the text has a lot to do with the way printed class reading is assigned: one reads the whole selection, and then one has finished the assignment. But with "Afternoon," the challenge is not finishing the reading, but actually unearthing the whole text and making sense of it. In fact, Joyce deliberately denies closure to the reader--even the meta-screens that comment on the reader's unending, evolving experience consciously refuse to end the story. It was engaging, but also somewhat frustrating.
