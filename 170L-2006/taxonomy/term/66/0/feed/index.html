I was reminded of films like "I, Robot" (a movie which I've never actually seen but apparently involves robots taking over the world) and the "Star Wars" series. Hollywood seems to enjoy portraying robots as dangerous creatures with the power to destroy their own human creators.</p> <p>Burnett also brings up the power struggle between humans and machines. He says there's a "'natural' and negative 'gut' reaction to the idea that machines may have more power than humans do." (123) I wonder, though, if this fear of technology and machines' "power" is a bit over-exaggerated and silly. Yes, computers are powerful, but they're only powerful because humans gave them that power. Yes, a computer beat Garry Kasparov during a game of chess, but it was humans who programmed the computers to calculate in the first place. I am also unclear--and pretty skeptical--as to the usefulness of the debate about machines threatening human individuality. Perhaps I'm simplifying the situation too much, but to be perfectly frank, I often have a hard time figuring out why people like Burnett are just so fixed on anthropomorphizing machines. I see machines as machines. When he asks questions like "Can a machine feel pain?"(126) I am not sure at all how to answer his question. I even wonder, "Is he seriously asking this question?!" WHY is he asking this? Perhaps I am too practical a person and cannot use my imagination. Then again, my other cynical side tells me that humans are just egotistical and obsessed with themselves and therefore must attribute human qualities to everything. An annoyingly frustrating endeavor, in my opinion...</p> <p><a href="http://machines.pomona.edu.170L-2006/node/81">read more</a></p> http://machines.pomona.edu.170L-2006/node/81#comments reading response Mon, 18 Sep 2006 06:57:18 +0000 Lulu 81 at http://machines.pomona.edu.170L-2006
