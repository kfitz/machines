Lor d of the Flies, Pigs, and Zits

By sarawrs - Posted on 7 February 2007 - 12:34am.
Tagged:
  • lord of the flies
  • Pigs

When I read the section where Slothrop dresses up like a pig, I immediately thought of Golding's Lord of the Flies. I don't know if Pynchon meant to allude to that book, but it's just something that I happened to take notice of. Slothrop, I felt, represented that little fat kid, Piggy, from Lord of the Flies. The children "prod his stomach," declare him the "fattest man in the world," and ultimately ask him to wear a pig costume (578). I was half expecting them to hunt him down and stick his head on a pole or something!

This section also made me wonder, "Why a PIG?" I don't have a companion, so I don't know if this is already something that is explained, but it just struck me as odd that Pynchon would go pig-crazy. The talking female pig that grows more and more attractive in Slothrop's eyes was entertaining in a weird beastiality kind of way...

On a totally random note, I loved Slothrop's "Partial List of Wishes on Evening Stars for This Period." It's good to know that our protagonist is worried about the really important things in life, such as that "fucking zit" on his back and his constipation. (562)

Pigs are generally regarded
Submitted by kettledrum on 7 February 2007 - 1:33am.

Pigs are generally regarded as the dirtiest, smelliest, greediest animals around. Plus, they're often used to comment on humans or the human condition, so it makes sense that Pynchon would use them to symbolize colonialism, males, humans' animal instincts, etc. Pigs are often seen as comparable to humans."Dogs look up to us. Cats look down on us. Pigs treat us as equals." -- Winston Churchill. (Yeah, Wikipedia)

Intelligence
Submitted by KKT on 7 February 2007 - 9:38am.

I read into pigs a little bit too (yes Wikipedia!) and read that one of the reasons that they are often seen as comparable to humans is because they're so intelligent ("one of the more intelligent animals on the planet," as the article says). It's interesting to wonder if Pynchon knew this (most likely)and how it contrasts with the animalistic instincts that Slothrop (and many characters in the novel) often revert to.

Pigs as companions

Remember though that William Slothrop, an ancestral Slothrop, loved to take the journey from the Berkshires to Boston with all his pigs (Page 564). "They were good company. Despite the folklore and the injunctions in his own Bible, William came to love their nobility and personal freedom, their gift for finding comfor in the mud on a hot day--pigs out on the road, in company together, were everything Boston wasn't..." I think that Tyrone Slothrop's travel with Pokler's pig is supposed to be sort of symmetrical to his ancestor's pig travels. There was a lot in the first sections about Slothrop's ancestors and how they began in the Berkshires, but I'm having trouble finding that.
