The Great American Novel

By two bird - Posted on 28 February 2007 - 2:12am.
Tagged:
  • Underworld

A friend found out we're reading Underworld for class, and asked if it's worth reading; if it's a "page turner." I replied that it isn't in the traditional sense of the word, but more of a sweeping American epic. "Like the Great American Novel?" she joked, having read far too many of those in her time here.

I've read that Underworld was, in fact, Don DeLillo's attempt at the Great American Novel. But what exactly is THE Great American Novel? Wikipedia says it "is the concept of a novel that perfectly represents the spirit of life in the United States at the time of its publication. It is presumed to be written by an American author who is knowledgeable about the state, culture, and perspective of the common American citizen."

Okay. But then doesn't that make Gravity's Rainbow a sort of Great American Novel about the Cold War as well, even though it's not set here or then?

And what is it about both of the books we've read that makes me shy away from putting them in the same category as To Kill A Mockingbird, Huckleberry Finn, The Catcher in the Rye, The Sound and the Fury, The Great Gatsby, or The Grapes of Wrath? Their disaffected post-modern stance, maybe. Or is it just that neither has the amount of history behind it as these do? Maybe it's only that we weren't made to read these in high school. All of them, these two included, deal with deep-seated American issues, whether acted out on a large scale or on the personal stage. Who decides what is a Great American Novel, anyway?

anti-great American novel?
Submitted by body without organs on 28 February 2007 - 9:46am.

Definitely a good final question. If my high school curriculum was any indication, I think it's safe to say that the wrong people are deciding what comprises a Great American Novel. The common thread in my mental reprsentation of such a novel, as well as your two prototypic examples, is not just in setting or theme but, I would say most importantly, in narrative. Rises and falls, many lessons learned, more or less obvious ethical weight of characters, pervade in all six of those (at least on my reading). Even Catcher in the Rye, which might be slightly more iconoclastic than the others, still follows these trends in important ways. According to a set of criteria like this, GR (and to be it seems like Underworld too, though I'll withhold my verdict until we finish) might be the foremost anti-Great American Novel - no finger-wagging, no dialectical climax and denoument, ethical ambivalence everywhere.
