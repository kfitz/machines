Apologies

By Running Silent - Posted on 3 April 2007 - 9:19pm.
Tagged:
  • Infinite Jest

I just wanted to explain my take on why Infinite Jest, a video of mother saying "i'm sorry" over and over again is as entertaining as it is. My reasoning may seem childish if not outright silly but I think that part of the reason this video is as addicting as it is because the idea of a motherly apology is so remote. Call me crazy and playing on exaggerations and jokes, but I don't think anyone can deny that at some point in their life before adulthood, they were "wronged" in some way and felt they deserved an apology they never received. What's more, you probably were forced to admit that you were at fault even when you truly believed otherwise; in a sense, a false forced confession. This double whammy makes people loose faith in any idea of justice and fairness. Moreover, because parents are the authority and monopolizer of power within a family unit, we as children view such an act as an abuse of power.
Therefore, the apology rectifies all such internal psychological injuries. It first is the apology we never recieved, and to us acts as a correction of a great injustice. Secondly, it levels the hierarchy of power because this person who is normally above us has stooped below to apologize. Its important to note that I am explaining this from a rather abstract psychological perspective and should in no way be taken in literal terms; i mean lets face it, who would honestly get suicidally captivated watching their mother apologize over and over again?

Mother life/death cycle, and fathers
Submitted by littlerock on 4 April 2007 - 12:44am.

What I didn't get was why this book followed a story of mothers of the next life killing their children and then giving birth to them again, only so that the mother of their next life can kill them and complete the cycle once again.... sure, mothers give birth and create life, but how do they take it away? And killing a person from a previous life only to bring them to their womb and give them life? Is this reincarnation? Mothers as simultanious murderers and life-givers? Huh?

Also, in terms of the psychology of a child desiring an apology from the people who dominated and shaped their lives (their parents), I can buy that but.... why don't people also desire an apology from the father? When Hal was in that Inner Infant group, members were complaining about not getting enough love as a child, and just wanting to be held. Perhaps that unfulfilled desire for attention warrants an apology. But why not from both parents? All I know is that I grew up with a single father, so hearing a mother figure apologize to me doesn't seem particularly satisfying or entertaining :-/

That was definately
Submitted by Running Silent on 4 April 2007 - 8:37am.

That was definately something I was struggling to understand, why it wasn't the father. It could be that the apology for unfairness is also coupled with the apology for bringing the child out of the womb. I know for you that the idea of a mother apologizing is not something that you really connect with, but for me at least, even if both parents have wronged me, there is something more powerful and moving when my mother apologizes. Perhaps it is because our culture paints mothers as the traditional head of child rearing that we associate them with the highest positions of power (and thus the greatest satisfaction from their apology). No doubt, the impact of a mother's apology depends on many, many circumstances, that are different to each family.

Meta-narratives
Submitted by body without organs on 4 April 2007 - 10:53am.

I read this whole thing as a critique of the meta-narratives that people generally employ to understand their own lives, as well as history. Questions like, "why not an appology from the father?" to my mind gets to the crux of the matter: why have some questions, some impulses (e.g. oedipal or castration narratives), some desires, been privileged over others? I think there are actually profound reasons to object to seeing the womb as Eden-like, the harmonious totality before the fall into language that we must constantly seek to re-create. Indeed, I read the fact that this form of entertainment is so captivating as a devastating critique of whatever cultural horizon Wallace is critiquing (American? North American?): its precisely the kind of paradise-fall-alienation-reconciliation narrative that leads people to continually seek their fix, to be unable to deal with plateaus.
