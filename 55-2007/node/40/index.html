in response to the recent discussion of the masculine

By two bird - Posted on 28 January 2007 - 5:26pm.
Tagged:
  • too big for a comment

This was going to be a comment....but it's not.

A fellow senior English major here at Pomona is actually writing his thesis on the feminine in Gravity's Rainbow (and one other book). I was very surprised to find that out, as I thought the masculine would be the obvious choice. We've got how many characters at this point, and something like two of them are women? Anyway, I realized, while contemplating this guy's choice, that their role in the novel is actually a lot more powerful than we are giving them credit for. I'm pretty sure Katje is a major power player, or at least was in her past. It remains to be seen whether or not she'll reemerge as one, but I get the feeling she might. And Jessica is completely changing the way Roger thinks and feels about the most important character in the book, the war. And the war is characterized as feminine - Roger's mother. So who knows? Maybe this guy chose wisely.

the war is also characterized as masculine in other parts: "Damned Beaver/Jeremy is the war, he is every assertion the fucking War has ever made" (is there a way to do italics on this blog? the is in Jeremy is the war should be italicized) I think that the females in this book are only portrayed as how they relate to men, which I guess follows from sex being one of the major themes. While I haven't finished the book and therefore can't give a definitive opinion, I think it would be hard to write an entire thesis on the feminine in GR, but very easy to write on how the feminine roles define the male characters. Did the thesis-writer give any hints as to what specifically he's writing about? I think it would be really interesting to hear some of his points...

Excellent Choice!
Submitted by Surgiomcsurgexc on 28 January 2007 - 10:35pm.

I think this English major made a good choice. I think this example is analgous to when one is allowed to choose their side in a debate and one chooses the side in which they are less familiar. I think on the surface GR says a lot about men, but there is much to be found about the females once the reader delves inside. True, we haven't met very many females yet, and I don't expect that we will meet that many more, but I think you can find more enlightening information about the females from the given about the males with whom they ecounter. For instance, Roger's constant worrying and obsequiousness when presented with Jessica shows her influence on him. Maybe the quote, "Behind each (successful) man is a strong woman" (or the converse of it) will come to play with this book.

Females, Males, the War, Control
Submitted by Stumpy on 29 January 2007 - 11:32am.

I've been thinking a lot about male and female roles in the book. Some of us seem to believe that the novel's lack of women means that it's glorifying males, while others think that the males are portayed as bumbling idiots and the women are the real power players. I think that regardless of sex, humans in this story seem to think that they have control, when they really don't at all. The doctors and pschiatrists and Pavlovians are all interested in their experiments and in a sense this gives them a feeling of control because they are in charge of the fate of their patients and subjects. However, the rockets are a symbol of chance and randomness and the doctors can't escape this. The war too is destroying people at random. It seems to me that regardless of sex, the book maintains that humans have a false illusion that what they do is important and that their actions affect their outcomes , when in reality, anyone can be a victim of the war.

Social Construction / Determinism
Submitted by body without organs on 29 January 2007 - 6:39pm.

I think your emphasis is right on here. Actions in the novel seem to range from totally externally determined to ostensibly-but-really-not-quite-volitional, and this cuts accross gender lines. Every is indeed a victim of the war, and allegorically a "victim" of postmodernity. I think a lot of the discussion we've been having misses the true "point" behind Pynchon's treatment of gender, namely, how relationships between people (of which sex is one common example) are always mediated, constructed, manipulated.
