Humor

By KKT - Posted on 4 February 2007 - 5:13pm.
Tagged:
  • Gravity's Rainbow

"It is difficult to perceive just what the fuck is happening here" (512). I laughed when I read this--it seems to sum up my thoughts about the novel in general pretty well.

I thought the differences in tone between the two scenes when Slothrop falls into the water were really interesting. The first time, Pynchon writes that "Slothrop dithers, goes to follow her--at the last moment some joker pulls the ladder up and the boat moves away, Slothrop screams, loses his balance and falls into the river"(467). This scene (although I'm sure not amusing for Slothrop) struck me as really humorous for some reason. Maybe it's Pynchon's use of the word "joker," or maybe it's the manner in which he fell, but I saw a decisive tone of amusement throughout that passage.

On the other hand, Slothrop's second journey into the water is far from amusing. " . . . and be flipped that easy over the side and it's adios to the Anubis and all its screaming Fascist cargo, already no more ship, not even black sky as the rain drives down his falling eyes now in quick needlestrokes, and he hits, without a call for help, just a meek tearful oh fuck" (500). Whereas with the first passage it is stated outright that Slothrop fell into the river, the second passage is far more subtle (in fact, I had to read it twice to get that he actually fell overboard). This scene seemed to have an air of desperation and helplessness to it (Slothrop doesn't even try to call for help). Pynchon's use of the word "needlestrokes" seems to give it a sort of a dark tone, as does the image of the endlessly pounding rain.

I thought it was pretty cool how Pynchon took essentially the same event--Slothrop falling into water--and made it into two vastly different scenes.

black/white
Submitted by littlerock on 6 February 2007 - 7:50pm.

Yeah, I noticed the contrast between those two falls as well.

The first time that Slotrop fell in, the moment he got out, he was pushed back in the water by a bunch of drunk aristocratic women in tiaras who decide "Let's cut it and see him fall in again!" (467). Slothrop gets out of the water fairly quickly and complains about being wet, but seems fine. It all seemed very harmless and amusing. In that paragraph it also describes the name of the ship as "lettered in black" and it is around this time that we run into mysterious black lady.

The second fall was definitely much more tumultuous and unclear, and it felt like the entire ship was caught in a large maelstrom. And while it seemed like a very dark scene, the image of white was constantly evoked in that paragraph: "the white ship"..."sudden whiteness exploding over the bow"..."tears that will add nothing to the whipped white desolation"(500). These contrasting black/white images, and with seemingly inconsistent moments, adds to some of the reversals/dualities in this novel.
