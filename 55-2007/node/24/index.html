Hopefully it's not just me.

By potted plant - Posted on 23 January 2007 - 3:42pm.
Tagged:
  • icky

I try to keep an open mind. But is anyone else put off by the fact that human excrement is actually a motif in this book?

I also find the book....not really sexist....but overwhelmingly masculine. I think that makes it hard for me to really *like* the book (as a female). It just feels uncomfortable, but not just because some of the subjects are uncomfortable. It's something in the book's personality, or in the author's voice. Even when he is focused on a female, it is clearly a male writer. Maybe it's the overabundance of penises that's doing it. I think it's more than that, though.

Does anyone else feel this way?

I agree but also disagree
Submitted by U 235 on 23 January 2007 - 9:12pm.

Somebody's blog entry title reads T.M.I.; it's a sentiment that I get sometimes reading Pynchon's work. I think you are all right to say that the novel includes a fair amount of gratuitous detail especially with descriptions of women. However, I think that the so-called "masculine vibe" is an important part of the reader's immersion into the world of Gravity's Rainbow. The setting of the characters in an intelligence bureau and the armed forces during the war would have been more or less a male dominated environment. As a result, the work, to a certain extent, justifiably reflects the period.

Honestly, I don't think any
Submitted by littlerock on 24 January 2007 - 3:28pm.

Honestly, I don't think any of the "disturbing" things Pynchon puts in this novel are gratuitous. Quite the contrary, I feel he is making a point, shocking though it may be to some readers.

And on that note, I agree with you: it is refreshing. The only other prominent novel I've read that actually deals directly with human excrement is "Gulliver's Travels" (when Gulliver is a giant and the little people must set up a whole system for carting away his shit). I seemed to be the only person in the class happy to see a book dealing with something so ubiquitous and universal that, for many cultural reasons, we like to pretend does not exist.

Not to mention the symbolic importance of shit in this novel, as Professor Fitzpatrick mentioned, regarding shit being a human product without order or value, now suddenly having meaning. I forsee it having some potential importance in our later reading...
