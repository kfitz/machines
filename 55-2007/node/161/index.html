Better Things for Better Living Through Chemistry

By U 235 - Posted on 28 February 2007 - 12:07am.
Tagged:
  • Lenny Bruce
  • Nick Shay
  • Underworld

DeLillo ends the section of the alternating bits of storyline with Nick failing to call Marian because loneliness is "a thing [he] tried never to admit to and knew how to step outside of, but sometimes even this was not means enough. He ultimately says, "I didn't call her because I would not give in, watching the night come down" (637). I think that although the story threads have great differences between them, for the most part, they involve characters who knowingly practice and in a certain sense have an addiction to brinksmanship. Lenny Bruce's ideas are dead on and by bringing it to an audience he has the potential to effect real change, yet his addiction to heroin and his paranoid need to please the crowd weakens his message. In spite of the overwhelming evidence of their inevitable decline, Edgar and Clyde continue to insist on shaping the world in their own way. In Clyde's case, he ultimately mortgages his individual identity and potential in exchange for the safety and security of Edgar's patronage. Worst of all, Nick fails to make the simple gesture of calling Marian out of stubborn opposition to feeling vulnerable even as he can see a window of opportunity closing before him.

As I mentioned in a previous comment, I think the scattered small bits of narrative serve to recreate the random critical moments of decision that have a great impact on our future lives which live on in our patchwork of memory.

the importance of individual moments
Submitted by body without organs on 28 February 2007 - 9:28am.

I think that character diagnosis is exactly right accroess the board. DeLillo's engagement with the theme of constructed history is very interesting. Moments like Nick's refusal to call Marian certainly are both random and critical (another one that comes to mind is his post hoc revision of the circumstances of his father's death), but I think DeLillo is actually trying to destabilize, in some way, our tendency to understand history as defined by these nodal climaxes. I keep coming back in my thinking about Nick as a character to that moment early on where he wishes he had brought up Brian when Marian was watching TV, as opposed to in the bedroom, so he "could have been the one who walks out of the room" (117). We're used to defining ourselves through "big-deal" moments - deaths of loved ones, sociopolitical events, all of our firsts - but it seems that logic is what has led Nick to become a caricature of himself (especially with all the performativity). I get the feeling that DeLillo's ideal subject would be someone whose presence in the world consisted in more moment-to-moment immediacy.
