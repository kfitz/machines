wanting to live history

By two bird - Posted on 15 February 2007 - 5:59pm.
Tagged:
  • Underworld

I was thinking again about the idea of people wanting to be a part of a historical event, and again, I came back to the cover's uncanny prophecy. I know the idea of throwing paper to touch the event might seem silly, but just think about 9/11 - when it happened, who didn't say to someone, "Oh, man, my boss's brother-in-law's sister died in the towers"? Even though it was a tragedy, it was an event of huge national importance, and shapes this century so far. People want to have some sort of link to it. I know, living in CT, people who drove over to the beach to see the smoke coming from the western horizon. And then, those people can say, "In a way, I was there".
There's a part of the self that wants to own a historical event, too. Coming out to California, the first anniversary of 9/11 I spent here, merely 2 years afterwards, I instinctively sought out other New York area kids to spend the day with. Those who weren't in the area, didn't know people who worked there, just "didn't understand." Yet in a way, the event belongs to the entirety of the nation. Or does it not belong to anyone, but history? It's a weird phenomenon, trying to connect with a history-making event.

Owning History--Literally
Submitted by KKT on 17 February 2007 - 12:13pm.

What you say about wanting to own a historical event really struck me, because there's a section in the novel that seems to say exactly that in literal terms.

"'People who save these bats and balls and preserve the old stories through the spoken word and know the nicknames of a thousand players, we're here in our basements with tremendous history on our walls . . .there's men in the coming years they'll pay fortunes for these objects. They'll pay unbelievable. Because this is desperation speaking'"(182).

This sort of desperation that Marvin is talking about seems to be kind of what you were discussing--people who are desperate to have a link to history, even if it's a purchased one. And, as Marvin says, some of these people are so desperate for that link that they'll spend anything to be connected. One example I can think of is the Titanic. I've read about some auctions where people pay millions for a plate that was retrieved from the sunken ship--is it for that link to a historical event? And, as evidenced by this novel, the same type of thing happens in baseball.
