victorian/american ideals

By sarawrs - Posted on 24 March 2007 - 12:44am.
Tagged:
  • marathe and steeply
  • victorians

So this is just a random thing I noticed. In my Victorian/Gothic Lit class, a lot of what my prof explains about the time period and the development of the Gothic novel really seems to have been carried through to the post-Modernist period and many of the themes, such as personal history and an obsession with the past, are still prevalent in encyclopedic narratives.

I had to read about the Victorian frame of mind on the subject of love, sex, and women, and this quote from Infinite Jest really reminded me of what I had read. "We want choice. A sense of efficaciousness and choice. To be loved by someone. To freely love who you happen to love" (423). Some Victorians believed that the institute of marriage was a negative influence on society because marriages that were the product of socio-economical incentives only increased the number of extramarital affairs. This is why many were beginning to support marriages of love. Marathe's take on the American ideal of "Pride in your work and family, and home" is also a very Victorian trait (423).

SURPRISE! IT'S A BABY!

By sarawrs - Posted on 21 March 2007 - 1:08am.
Tagged:
  • brotherly love
  • mario

When I read about Mario's birth, I was a little disturbed. "He had to be more or less scraped out, Mario, like the meat of an oyster from a womb to whose sides he'd been found spiderishly clinging...(313)" The description of scraping him out of a womb sounds like an abortion, and it makes him seem like some unwanted parasite. It's strange that he survived such a traumatic birth, and in some ways, maybe it can be interpreted that part of him did die? His eyes have an odd dead gray-green color to them, and his scalp is the same color. I envisioned a semi-decaying body...(is that morbid?) Anyway a question I had, is Mario James' son or Charles'? His hair is likened to that of the "athletic director and Academy Headmaster," (314) and it flaps up in the same way Charles' hair does.

Tagged:

Going with the issue of numbers and paranoia (which I commented on): this need to find a logical explanation for situations, or to predict events, that don't necessarily have one is relieved through the use of numbers and math/statistics. I think this is ironic, because if people look into some of these details too much, they'll just be paranoid about everything!

I noticed a lot of parts that indicate how history shapes the present and the future; there are "channeled paths through time," "lines of intersection," (157) that join the past to the present, thereby shaping future events. On 374, Klara is experiencing the view from her loft and she wonders if the "intersecting...lines might tell her something."

Just thought this was interesting: Video Cameras

By sarawrs - Posted on 19 February 2007 - 10:45am.
Tagged:
  • Gravity's Rainbow
  • realism
  • video cameras

I loved the first part of Part 2: Elegy for Left Hand Alone. I thought it was both funny and poignant.

"You know how children with cameras learn to work the exposed moments that define the family cluster. They break every trust...It is not a joke. They will shoot you sitting on teh pot if they can manage a suitable vantage." (155-156)

I think DeLillo's description of film contrasts well with the movies in Gravity's Rainbow. In Underworld, DeLillo writes about the "home video," that possesses a "crude power." It captures candid scenes with brutal honesty. There is no airbrushing and fantasy is not the aim. It seems more real, even "superreal," more true to life than anything else. The author seems to play with the concept of something that is predestined, "The world is lurking in the camera, already framed." Film is only the material manifestation, or the proof, that these moments exist. In GR, as we've discussed in class, movies and films do not portray reality, but do seem to CREATE it.
