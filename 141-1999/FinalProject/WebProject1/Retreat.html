--José Arcadio Segundo Retreats--

Úrsula fails to end her great-grandson's solitude. José Arcadio Segundo's fear prevents any hope of ending the solitude. His anxiety stems from the railroad which represents Macondo's interaction with outsiders, technology and capitalism. José Arcadio Segundo's refusal to leave his room gives insight into Márquez's ideas about Latin American and its isolation. Latin American nations, like José Arcadio Segundo, resent Western imperialism's exploitation of their land. Rather than construct a successful Latin American version of Western capitalism, utilizing available technology, nations remain isolated. José Arcadio Segundo is content to isolate himself from the imperialists and their methods, in a sense denying their existence. Úrsula leaves her great-grandson's room defeated, realizing the solitude is "impenetrable". The cycle of poverty in Latin American will remain "impenetrable" until countries acknowledge Western capitalism. Perhaps Márquez does not necessarily advocate the adoption of Western style capitalism in Latin American. Nevertheless undeveloped nations can never hope to compete with imperialist countries until they confront the success of such powers.
