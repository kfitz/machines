--Fernanda and Úrsula Try to Restore the Buendia House--

Acknowledging her failure to interupt the solitude, Úrsula, along with her great-granddaughter-in-law, try another method to end the cycle of despair. The Buendía women's interest in further restoration of the house arises when Fernanda's son, José Arcadio announces he is returning from Europe. Literary critic Brian Conniff contends that for Macondo's residents, "the important things have always happened some where else -and their future will be determined by somebody else."* Fernanda's "bustle" results from her understanding that her son, educated "somewhere else", is important to the future of the Buendías. While Úrsula's attempt to break José Arcadio Segundo's isolation was an active attempt by a Buendía to change her town's fate, Fernanda's preparation for José Arcadio demonstrates reliance upon outsiders to effect Macondo's future.
Fernanda and Úrsula's attempt to restore the household is an obvious metaphor for Latin American's futile efforts at prosperity. Rather than acknowledge the success of Western imperialists and attempt to compete, Latin American nations survive by succumbing to exploitation. Fernanda sells off the treasures of the Buendía family. This act compares to Spain and Portugal's plunder of the ancient Aztec and Inca empires. Úrsula imagines further exploitation in the name of "driving off ruin." Úrsula's vision of carousing outsiders, partying at the Buendía's expense, parallels many modern day cities in Latin American, such as Tijuana. These bastions of excess and vice succumb to the desires of Westerner's, hoping to benefit from foreign wealth. Úrsula proposes the ugly scheme while remembering the success of her "candy animal" business. Ironically, while lucrative, Úrsula's candy animal endeavor eventually causes the insomnia plague: "Children and adults sucked with delight on the delicious little green roosters of insomnia, the exquisite pink fish of insomnia, and the tender yellow ponies of insomnia, so that dawn on Monday found the whole town awake" (49). Thus, Fernanda and Úrsula's attempt to restore prosperity to the Buendías is another endeavor doomed to culminate in devestation. Once Úrsula fails to end José Arcadio Segundo's isolation she must resort to the same ill-fated survival strategies which Macondo and Latin America continually rely upon.
