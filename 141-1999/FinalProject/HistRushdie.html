History in Midnight's Children

In Midnight's Children, Rushdie exhibits a similar stance on the recording of history to García Márquez's. One has the definite sense that as much as Saleem would lead the reader to believe otherwise, he is responsible for the events of the novel, rather than they merely occurring. Additionally, Rushdie expands on a commentary García Márquez presents on the nature of recorded history and how it can be manipulated by the historian. Similar to the forgetting and remembering in One Hundred Years of Solitude, Saleem merely tells what he wants how he feels like telling it; he even admits on occasion that what he has just related was false. As for García Márquez, the repetitiveness of history and learning from that is significant for Rushdie.
