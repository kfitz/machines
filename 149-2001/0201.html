Today:

In small groups:

  • Take two to three minutes to make some notes for yourself about the major ideas that you have taken from Gramsci
  • Everyone shares those ideas with the group
  • As a group, select one concept or idea that you feel interacts in an interesting way with either the same or a similar concept from the Marx/Engels essays
  • Find a specific passage in Gramsci that deals specifically with that concept or idea
  • Prepare a comparison between Gramsci's treatment of this concept, with specific reference to this passage, and Marx's treatment of it
  • Each group will present their concept/passage/comparison in a relatively formal way, so be prepared

Group 1:

  • Caesarism -- conflict between two groups (A&B)
  • Marx sees class conflict as way revolution occurs
  • P. 219: three possibilities (A wins; B wins; neither wins, but third force [C] intervenes, and A and B dissolve into one another)
  • Marx sees this in general sense: sooner or later there will be synthesis from conflict; Gramsci sees more specifically: conflict may be revolutionary or reactionary
  • Gramsci: one group defeats another as random process; for Marx, more of a definite pattern of progress

Group 2:

  • G: before intellectuals were part of bureaucracy; now breaking away; M: didn't really discuss intellectuals; saw as part of bourgeoisie
  • P. 244: people have been condensed into science -- sarcasm; how we have intellectualized social science as a study of a surrounding society and not necessarily the people in it

Group 3:

  • How the ruling classes use the middle classes' aspirations against them
  • P. 210: reabsorption of control by ruling class
  • Expands on 18th Brumaire; both abstracts and makes more specific by talking about the methodology of how the ruling class rules, keeping bureaucracy in mind
  • P. 225: ruling class as bureaucracy; ruling class as body -- even harder to extricate from

Group 4:

  • P. 244-5: people condensed into science
  • Metaphor of science for development of human history
  • Is it arbitrary or rational?
  • M: science as one of tools of ruling class; G: is that necessarily a bad thing?
  • G suggests that science transcends Marx's capitalism; might be more to it

Group 5:

  • P. 276: rift between popular masses and ruling ideologies
  • Fusion of classes needs to take place instead of just one group taking over; needs to be consensus, cohesion of classes
  • Intellectuals breaking away: M gives no attention to middle; G calling for coherent social group to steer attention away from bourgeoisie, not just proletariat
  • P. 219: reciprocal destruction -- opposition between two groups so strong that there can be no synthesis resulting from contradiction
  • Seems more cautious; need to have ideological goals before jumping into revolution.

Group 6:

  • Caesarism: p. 219 connected to 18th Brumaire; struggle between petty bourgeois and proletariat reflected in Caesarism
  • See Group 1
  • Gramsci's view of reactionary is reflected in 18th Brumaire

Group 7:

  • Caesarism, A, B, C
  • P. 222: no passage from one type of state to another; evolution
  • Departure from M's revolution
  • Revolution may not be effective; may be futile or detrimental
  • Fable of the beaver: self-evolution by avoiding conflict by radical self-alterations; means of keeping political dignity; group works to change itself from within
  • Hard to tell whether G. advocates the evolution or sees it as necessarily compromise (castration v. death)
  • Revolution is very much a political idea; but political equality cannot exist without economic equality -- sees revolution as seeking equality rather than political upheaval

Group 8:

  • Fable of the beaver
  • P. 258: the ethical state; ruling classes will only help lower classes as much as they'll help themselves
  • Ethical state will bring people to a certain level, but done in order to increase the forces of development, which technically favors the ruling class interest; connects to M's "ruling ideas of time are ideas of ruling class"
  • Fake moralism; superficial helping out without betraying their own interests
  • Distinction between ethical and political state -- sees in eternal contradiction; perhaps true ethical state can't really exist
  • What does the "political state" mean?

Group 9:

  • Fable of beaver: very horrifying! Beaver = lower class; to survive, must self-mutilate; beaver is nothing to hunters but chemicals; must eliminate its manhood in order to survive
  • Analogy to commodification/reification of proletariat labor; workers become only their labor instead of fully human
  • G goes on to explore why there way no defense; relates to M. in thinking about how exploited classes will go along with exploitation
