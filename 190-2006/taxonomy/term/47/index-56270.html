Here, when you visit, you can find not only a list of what songs you've been listening to lately, but also a ridiculous amount of statistical data, providing you with your top albums, tracks and artists on a weekly, monthly, yearly, or all-time basis. A great event this Thursday: about Music, History, Politics, Diaspora, and Vietnam!

Submitted by neurotica on 30 October 2006 - 1:49am.
  • and Vietnam!
  • Diaspora
  • history
  • music
  • politics

The Music of the Republic of Vietnam - Before and After the Diaspora
- Lecture by Jason Gibbs

Thursday, November 2 at 4:15 - 5:30 p.m. (followed by reception)
Hahn 108 (420 Harvard Ave.)

Ever since the end of World War II, civil conflict has divided Vietnamese society and, for a period, Vietnam itself. During the Cold War, an actual boundary at the 17th parallel blocked contact between the Democratic Republic of Vietnam in the North and the American-supported Republic of Vietnam in the South. While the communist North used centralized control to insure that its music served the interests of socialism and total mobilization for war, the South developed an eclectic, market driven popular music culture. Following the reunification of Vietnam in 1975, the victorious communists banned and worked to confiscate nearly all cultural products of the South. The music of the Republic of Vietnam, however, has survived and developed among the waves of refugees and immigrants from Vietnam to the U.S. and other countries in the West. Despite government suppression, it has also continuously thrived in Vietnam's black markets. Over the past decade the cultural division between Vietnam and diasporic Vietnamese moderated considerably with singers and songwriters from both sides regularly performing both in Vietnam and the West. This talk will discuss the music of the Republic of Vietnam and consider its genesis and continued popularity in Vietnam and overseas.

What if I changed my name?

Submitted by BuildingsAndFood on 11 October 2006 - 12:45am.
  • authorship
  • awesomeness
  • music
  • naming

So, not entirely related to anything, but something interesting and hopefully preceeding more relevant material...
As some of you may or may not know, the band I'm in recently changed our name. Someone asked me at Symposium one night if I thought the new name made any difference. I immediately said it did. I felt freed as an author. The old name had come to symbolize a certain set of musical requirements, and the new one to me represents creative freedom. It symbolizes a willingness, almost a necessity, to step outside my personal boundaries of familiarity, and still somehow manages to encapsulate a more cohesive end product. My creative process has been restructured at every level by simply assigning a new name to the collective author of the final product. I'm not sure why this is, how it relates to being one author in a group of four - or seeing so many changes undergo the creative product from start to finish - or whether the old name had become an author-function representing a body of work I had outgrown...

I Like Puppies, I Swear

Submitted by BuildingsAndFood on 17 September 2006 - 8:00pm.
  • authorship and film
  • awesomeness
  • music

Okay, so first I wanted to use the title to clear up a popular misconception. Now that that's out of the way, I just got done reading the article in the book about the Irving Berlin/Darryl Zanuck film, "I Hear Music and...". Knowing me, I liked it quite a bit.

I thought the writing style was fantastic - much more accessible than some of the other essays in the collection. But the meat of the essay, discussing the authorship of music (in the context of the film) was a fascinating story. I like that it wasn't about necessarily the one song that Berlin wrote, but more about the entire genre of ragtime music and how to locate Berlin's place within that. Musical geneology is difficult enough to trace, but the fact that so many other people (Berlin and White America) had so much at stake in the decision of authorship. Things like saying that American popular music begins with that Berlin song help to define America as White America, and Berlin's own insecurities about his immigrant status make it imperative to him to claim that song and genre as American.
