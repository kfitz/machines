Anyone?</p> <p>So I've been thinking about cookbooks, the recipes in them, and what makes them unique or different from predecessors, and what makes traditional recipes worth revising. Obviously there are a lot of questions we can raise here: What constitutes enough change for a recipe to be made new? Is the substitution of cream cheese for animal lard enough? Adding an extra clove of garlic? Setting the oven at a lower temperature and cooking a dish for 10 extra minutes? Or is it the intent of a revision that makes it different, such as making a pesto using low fat parmesan (does this even exist?) for inclusion in a low-fat cookbook? Who exactly authored pesto, anyway? We all know pesto as an established foodthing, but what constitutes it, and what constitutes a revision?</p> <p><a href="http://machines.pomona.edu.190-2006/node/146">read more</a></p> http://machines.pomona.edu.190-2006/node/146#comments authorship food mario batali Wed, 27 Sep 2006 08:17:11 +0000 oneoutofseven 146 at http://machines.pomona.edu.190-2006
