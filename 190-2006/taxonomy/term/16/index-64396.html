Thesis: A New Reader?

Submitted by WildCherry15 on 2 October 2006 - 6:11pm.
  • thesis
  • thesis reader
  • thesis topic

Okay, so as mentioned at the end of last entry, I don't think I will be able to take advantage of the inspirational professor I met with for two hours two weeks ago. This is because he deals more with simple, practical classroom usages of media, whereas I need to do a theoretical paper. I now have appointments with two other professors, who will hopefully be my readers. Profesora Donapetry, a Spanish teacher specializing in women's studies, might be very helpful because I am will be continuing the work I started over the summer, the creation of an online biography of my grandmother (a Mexican woman.) Profesora Donapetry would be able to help me in the content of the website, perhaps not so much the format. However, the content and its cultural implications might help shape the structure. For example, the website's audience might be older (closer in age to my grandmother.) And many viewers may be from Mexico, where digital technology is less available. Both older and technologically inexperienced people would need a very simple interface, perhaps one that starts with an introductory video, which is very attention-grabbing. I could then comment on the importance of the image in an increasingly globalized, international world, and I could expound on the differing experiences of the internet for people of various ages. Yes, the content does inform the structure.

Since thesis seems to be vouge at the moment...

Submitted by PureJaqassary on 1 October 2006 - 1:35pm.
  • please respond
  • thesis

(Questions to the reader at the bottom, please read more and give me feedback, if you have the time)

As I mentioned in our opening class I'm planning to write my thesis on and in the attempt to re-theorize public art. As I have also mentioned, Banksy was part of my inspiration to do this. Spring before last, when he put his paintings up in NYC museums I was grabbed by his fanciful problematization of the museum system (and his concurrent anti-war messages). I decided to write my final paper for Media Theory on the political efficacy of public art, with a particular emphasis on what I dubbed "ephemeral public art" (i.e. Banksy, Guerilla Girls, ACT Up, Think Again, etc.) I very quickly discovered that while there was no end to critical debate on the relative virtues of Richard Serra's "Tilted Arc" and the questions that it brought to light (primarily what should public art be? does it have to be aesthetically accessible to everyone? should it be art for art's sake or something more? and so forth), there was very little, if any, critical attention given to the very forms of public art that I was most interested in. With a limited time line and a 20 page limit, I did the best I could to discuss ephemeral public art with the resources available; however, it left me wholly unsatisfied.

International Hip Hop

  • music industry
  • racism
  • thesis

So... as we all begin to turmoil over the horrible 't' word, I just thought I'd lay out my general idea and see if anyone had feedback/ideas/criticisms, etc.

Basically, I took this Hip Hop Culture class while I was abroad in New Zealand. Seeing as hip hop culture has its roots in the United States, it was interesting to study it outside of the US (although my prof was American) and the class actually focused on tying in the abundant use of rap music in the Maori community in New Zealand and other marginalized groups across the globe. This inspired me. So I want to write about international hip hop on the small scale, i.e. how individuals outside the US use hip hop as a form of voice to speak out against oppression in their individual communities. I'm debating whether I tie this in with the early stages of US hip hop, before it was a mainstream commodity and most rappers used hip hop to communicate within their communities and speak out about their daily experiences and oppressions.
