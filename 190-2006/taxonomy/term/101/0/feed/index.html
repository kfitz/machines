It gives the reading a good rhythm. Not to mention that it was a lot easier to comprehend as a united whole. Anyhow...</p> <p>I particularly liked the secion about authorship as signature. I had never really thought about an auteur's films as being a signature, because in fact, it does make sense to think about the way the audience gets to know a director is consists mostly "by repetition among various texts [that are] 'signed.'" The author's identity as far as we know him/her is thus a compilation and accumulation of various signs found within his/her texts. These signs form a message that we come to know as the author and his signature. Hmmmm. I like that-knowing that you immortalize "yourself" within your work. Rather than thinking of directors' sets of films as separate entities (at least for the purpose of authorship as signature), thinking of them as "the product of a single intelligence" (Staiger 44) helps for a more cohesive understanding of the author and behind the finished work. And as a result, also provides a way of bringing together individual films as a full set of films that share common ideas.</p> <p><a href="http://machines.pomona.edu.190-2006/node/110">read more</a></p> http://machines.pomona.edu.190-2006/node/110#comments author authorship as signature Wed, 20 Sep 2006 10:20:07 +0000 reliablesrc07 110 at http://machines.pomona.edu.190-2006
