A Million Little Lies?

Submitted by msblogger1220 on 17 September 2006 - 10:20pm.
  • random thoughts

I recently realized that although I have read the articles and discussed the controversy about James Frey's book "A Million Little Pieces" with friends and relatives, I have not discussed it in an academic setting. I feel as though the issue of authorship that we are focusing on actually connects really well with the controversy.
First of all, I have read the book, but I read it after the realizations that some parts of it were false. I believe this knowledge skewed my perception of the novel while I was reading it. However, I feel it's really interesting that our society was so connected with this particular authorial situation. Why was it so important that the novel line up perfectly with the events in Frey's real life? Was it simply because he lied and said that the book was non-fiction?? or were people disappointed because their connection with the author's story was shattered?

Don't you just love...

Submitted by racinian on 11 September 2006 - 8:00pm.
  • Barthes
  • random thoughts

...when your courses overlap? Today in my 19th Century U.S. Women Writers course we spent a chunk of time discussing the "role of the author" in a text. This was actually kind of shocking because, I'll admit, I found myself questioning the importance of last week's discussion (is that bad?). The class consensus seemed to be that the author could not be separated from the text, but likewise that the reader should not rely solely on the author's background/purpose to understand a text- one of those happy, indecisive "middle-ground" things. Nothing new here, right?

The conversation became more interesting when we accepted that the author and the text were, to a degree, inseparable, and focused on the implications of this connection, such as the effect the gender of the author has on a text.

Remixing

Submitted by ghostwriter on 10 September 2006 - 1:52pm.
  • random thoughts

Reading msblogger's post got me thinking again about the Barthes essay, especially the part about ideas/texts not really being unique because everything is so socially constructed. Last semester for another class I read Paul D. Miller/AKA DJ Spooky's book "Rhythm Science" and it deals with a lot of similar themes in a new way. For Miller, everyone has to the potential to be a DJ of sorts, a role he links to the griot (a highly revered storyteller) in West African societies. You take aspects from culture, from the social constructions around us, and you sample and remix them to make them into something new that's relevant for you.
