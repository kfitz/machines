a movie i saw the other day

Submitted by TheGoodConstable on 31 October 2006 - 10:47pm.
  • movies

On page 197 of First Person, Henry Jenkins' online response says, "Contemporary film style is more elliptical and rapid-fire than the classical continuity system precisely because a generation that spends many hours per day consuming media demands cognitive challenges and perceptual puzzles rather than straightforward exposition."

I saw the Prestige last weekend, which may be a textbook example of this. in this film, the beginning is pretty much the same scene as the end. In between, the film shifts back and forth between periods of time, and references made in the beginning are not made clear until the end, just as things that are told in the beginning change meanings throughout the film. This film could have been told as a straightforward linear narrative, but it wouldn't have been as interesting, for just the reason that Jenkins says. We are constantly consuming media everyday, and if the story is not going to be extremely original (which very few are) then the way in which it is told needs to be.

gimmick vs. thought provoking

Submitted by neurotica on 31 October 2006 - 5:03pm.
  • first person
  • memento
  • movies
  • time code

After I posted, I thought of another good example of how the relationship between form and content could either seem interesting or just gimicky.
I am reminded of the difference between the films, "Time Code" by Mike Figgis and "Memento" by Christopher Nolan. (Did I make a concsious choice in saying who the author of the film was? Did I preface the director over the screenwriter or vice versa? Well in both films, the director was the screenwriter, so no choice between the two had to be made!) Granted, I saw Time Code once when I was 14, so maybe if I watch it again my opinion would be different. In Time Code, the screen is divided into four segments, and in each segment the viewer simultaneously. The story deals with love, sex, infidelity, with people working within the entertainment industry. The structure in a way did make the story, in the sense, that in one frame we see a woman witnessing her girlfriend cheating on her (she hid a mic on her girlfriend somehow, and was listening to the sounds in her car) while in the other frame, we see the situation of the cheating. you can see different angles of the same stories, it's layered, etc. Howver, I felt that this story didn't complement the structure well enough. It was just kind of a soap opera-y tale of people working in Hollywood. While the content was enhanced by the structure, in didn't take full advantage of the unique structure.

gender in "The Black Dahlia"

Submitted by ghostwriter on 24 September 2006 - 10:46pm.
  • movies

I guess I'll follow the trend and blog a bit about the movie that I saw this weekend. I went to the drive-in in Pomona as a sort of field trip for an organization that I'm a part of. My group/car decided to see The Black Dahlia-- let's just say I wish I had seen Barnyard or something instead. Although overall I was not a fan of the film, it did have a lot of interesting stuff going on concerning gender and sexuality.

*Mild Spoiler Alert*

The story is based on the murder case of a small-time actress in the late-40s. Two of the major female characters (including the murder victim) are bisexual. Although there's an awesome scene with kd lang singing in a lesbian nightclub, overall I felt the portrayal of bisexual women in the film was pretty lacking. One of them is murdered at the beginning of the film and only shown through screen tests and flashbacks, denying her any real in-depth character development. The other one ends up being a crazy psycho killer. It seems like there have already been enough films with bisexual women as crazy, deceitful, and conniving. Watching the film reminded me a bit of our discussion about the article on John Waters last week. We talked about the difference between films that are really engaged with queering mainstream Hollywood, and films that just have LGBTQ characters in them. In my opinion, The Black Dahlia was definitely one of the latter.
