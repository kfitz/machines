Mixed Feelings about Man with a Movie Camera

Submitted by lrob on 30 September 2006 - 2:10am.
  • response

Let me just say that on Wednesday, I really enjoyed Man with a Movie Camera. I thought it was incredibly visually engaging and groundbreaking (especially considering it was made in 1929). I left class that night impressed and inspired...

BUT the following night I watched Berlin: Symphony of a City in another class, and it totally changed my opinion of Man with a Movie Camera. Remember all that stuff at the beginning about it being a huge, unprecidented experiment? Well... Berlin: Symphony of a City was made in 1927, two years before, and it's actually strikingly similar. No "actors," no "sets," just the city... and film AS film... not trying to reference the theater, literature, etc... along with tons of montage. Although I preferred the visuals in Man with a Movie Camera, I lost some respect for the film after seeing that it wasn't based on a unique concept, which I'd originally been led to believe.

Yuri Tsivian's Talk on Tuesday

Submitted by lrob on 30 September 2006 - 1:53am.
  • reflection

So I went to Yuri Tsivian's talk on Tuesday, and it ended up being very different from what I expected. The title "Chaplin and the Soviets" was pretty broad, so I guess I didn't know exactly what to expect... maybe something about the fan base of moviegoers in the U.S.S.R. who were into Chaplin's films. But as it turns out, it wasn't really about admiration of Chaplin's acting; it was about people's awe of his "perfect movement." Apparently the Soviets, who were obsessed with efficient ways of moving the body for the sake of efficient labor, thought that Chaplin was a role model because of the way he moved his body in his films. He had an entire school of followers who saw him as an academic and intellectual based on his movements. They used his movies in studies of human locomotion, hoping that this would increase labor output in their communist society. They even asked him for an annotated bibliography of sources he had used to study motion. Needless to say, Chaplin was baffled. He saw himself as a comedian, an actor--not an intellectual--and he had no idea how to deal with this sort of fame.

Meetro?

Submitted by oneoutofseven on 27 September 2006 - 3:41pm.
  • internet
  • meetro
  • my privacy just got violated
  • stalking

As inspired by reliablesrc07's post on Wallop, I thought I'd tell you guys about another creepy networking/message service called Meetro, which is completely different and far more stalker-y than anything I've seen before.

Meetro works like AIM--you sign up, get a screen name... And then you put in your street address. In Meetro, instead of creating and having your own buddy list, when you sign on you get a list of the people all around you, down to the 1/4 mile. Creepy enough for you?!?! The idea I think is that maybe when you have a question about say where to get your hair cut in Claremont, you can have it answered easily. People have profiles (I think there's some sort of link with MySpace), and so basically, you can know everything about a person down to their SPECIFIC PHYSICAL LOCATION from one instant messaging service.
