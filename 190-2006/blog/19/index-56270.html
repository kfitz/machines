  • Metallica
  • music

The Constable mentioned in his blog that he would like to hear the view of Lars Ulrich, the leader of METALLICA, regarding Napster and copyright infringement. What follows is Ulrich's statement, read to the U.S. Senate in 2000. I've obnoxiously copy-and-pasted the whole thing into this blog, but if you want to read it from the site, go HERE. Ulrich's statement is honest and heartfelt, but just a tad annoying because 1) Lessig and Vaidy both mentioned that there is no PROOF that downloading ever hurts the artists or even the industry and 2) If anything, downloading hurts the actual musician the LEAST out of everyone else (the distribution company, manager, etc.). They get a tiny fraction of the overall yield of their record sales; they make much more money off personal appearances and concerts. In a sense, the record companies are the ones that rip off a band's intellectual property.

Masturbating in the Marketplace

Submitted by DaLynziiChic on 27 November 2006 - 12:15am.
  • cynicism
  • Diogenes

In chapter 3, Vaidhyanathan (I should probably learn how to pronounce that) references two different types of cynicism. The first pertains to Diogenes (ancient Greek who would whack it in the marketplace) and is referred to as "Diogenic cynicism". According to Vaidy: "Diogenic cynicism values discipline, self-sufficiency, and rejecting the influence of social convention or cultural power" (26). The other type of cynicism is "Costanzan", referencing George Costanza of SIENFELD. Costanzan cynicism is "disengaged" and "selfish" (26). The overall impression is that Diogenic cynicism is socially responsible, while Constanzan cynicism is egotistical.

PopBlogging

Submitted by DaLynziiChic on 13 November 2006 - 12:45am.
  • Turducken

In the chapter "Piracy", Lessig states:

"If a particular blogger writes a particularly interesting story, more and more people link to that story. And as the number of links to a particular story increases, it rises in the ranks of stories. People read what is popular; what is popular has been selected by a very democratic process of peer-generated rankings." (43)

I found this statement to be pretty interesting. It is true that what is popular is democratically selected...some of the time. The popularity system can be easily thwarted by a group of radicals or even a monetary boost (as with Google and those affiliated).
