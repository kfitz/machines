Madame Butterfly, meet the Big Screen

Submitted by racinian on 27 September 2006 - 10:45am.
  • random thoughts

This past Monday the Metropolitan Opera in New York City decided to simulcast the opening night of their production of Puccini's Madama Butterfly in Times Square. The streets were closed down, 650 cushioned seats were assembled, and the event was open to the public. There was a second simulcast in Lincoln Center Plaza where tickets were required, but handed out for free at the box office before the event.

Peter Gelb, the Met's general manager, said he wanted to end the "isolation" of the art form, "It's essential for the broader public to know what's going on inside. I want to demonstrate that the Met is interested in being part of mainstream society and contemporary culture" (http://www.playbillarts.com/news/article/5228.html).

Submitted by racinian on 19 September 2006 - 11:08pm.
  • thesis

Let me apologize ahead of time. This post has almost nothing to do with authorship. It does, however, slightly relate to my thesis idea[s] so I thought I'd throw it up here and get a few opinions.

Moving on.

This year the organizers of Madrid's Fashion Week have decided that the average runway model is--brace yourselves--too thin. The average runway model is currently 5'9" and 110 pounds. For you BMI-buffs out there, that's a body mass index of 16.2, a good 2.3 points below normal (read: healthy) BMI (http://www.nhlbisupport.com/bmi/). Now, the same 5'9" model must weight at LEAST 123 pounds to strut the catwalk.

Don't you just love...

Submitted by racinian on 11 September 2006 - 8:00pm.
  • Barthes
  • random thoughts

...when your courses overlap? Today in my 19th Century U.S. Women Writers course we spent a chunk of time discussing the "role of the author" in a text. This was actually kind of shocking because, I'll admit, I found myself questioning the importance of last week's discussion (is that bad?). The class consensus seemed to be that the author could not be separated from the text, but likewise that the reader should not rely solely on the author's background/purpose to understand a text- one of those happy, indecisive "middle-ground" things. Nothing new here, right?

The conversation became more interesting when we accepted that the author and the text were, to a degree, inseparable, and focused on the implications of this connection, such as the effect the gender of the author has on a text.

Oh, Barthes--

Submitted by racinian on 6 September 2006 - 2:33pm.
  • Barthes

I found Barthes' essay rather extreme. Perhaps this is because I'm very attached to the concept of "The Author." I usually tell people "I like Sylvia Plath," before "I like The Bell Jar." I believe this is primarily influenced by my opinion of an author as a whole, rather than just the work produced. When I find an author whose work I truly enjoy, I try to learn more about who she/he is and how she/he lives, not only to give me a new understanding of the text, but to give me a new appreciation for the work. Would I enjoy The Bell Jar as much if I didn't have a sense of who the author was? (Okay, okay, maybe this is an extreme example--) Of course, I can't answer this question, because almost every text I have encountered is supplemented with information about the author/s.

Blog = Heart Palpitations

Submitted by racinian on 6 September 2006 - 1:50pm.
  • blogging

Because the posts have been going in a more personal direction, I'm just going to be honest. I'm a bit apprehensive about this blog.

Okay--I'm very apprehensive about this blog.

I'm not a blogger and I haven't had any desire to become one. Class presentations, sharing my work in class, even having friends read my work is a highly stressful experience. Academic work aside, posting my personal thoughts/opinions/beliefs doesn't make me feel liberated, it makes me feel exposed. Last semester I even dropped a course because it required each student to read their response papers out loud at the start of every class (pathetic, I know). But, I suppose I can't run from this one. In any case, I guess it will be a "learning experience."
