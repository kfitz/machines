If I did it, or I did it?

So I know that someone already posted about OJ Simpson's new/cancelled book "If I did it," but now that it has become a media uproar and book and interview have both been pulled, I think it warrants another post. Doujinshi

Submitted by TheGoodConstable on 20 November 2006 - 10:31pm.
  • free culture

While reading Free Culture, I was super interested in doujinshi, mentioned in the first chapter. These are comics that are essentially copies of other comics that are altered to make it the authors' own.
I think I can see why an art form like this is great, and why it is negative. Of course, from a consumer standpoint, it is great. Allowing artists to be more free with 'borrowing' or whatever we call it will probably mean more content is produced. And if there is good ideas, it benefits the consumer to have many artists (possibly) copy them and expand on them.
However, from the artists' standpoint, I can see how you wouldn't want other artist to use your ideas to make money that you probably could have made using your own intellectual properties.

Virtual Earth

Submitted by TheGoodConstable on 20 November 2006 - 1:10am.
  • computers

In the same Newsweek I just posted about, there was another article about the internet in the 3rd dimension.

The article was specifically about Microsoft's 'Virtual Earth 3-D.' This is a site that is being developed currently, and is available but not complete (nor will it be for quite some time). Essentially, the site is similar to Google Earth, but seems more concerned with creating a video-game style landscape. This virtual world only consists of 15 cities at the moment, but they hope to add 100 cities by summer 2007.

There is no specific purpose for Virtual Earth 3-D other than it's really cool. Some people in class said that they liked the game Myst because they could just explore the beauty of the world. This technology offers that same possibility, except the world is OUR world, and the exploration can be for a better purpose than beating a game. In the magazine, they offer examples such as, if you can't remember the name of the bar you love in Baltimore, you can just go there, and read the sign on the front of the place. This also has the possibility of working as a Mapquest on steroids. Not only can it tell you where to turn, but you could actually SEE the turns as well as any landmarks that could help you. This means that the only excuses for getting lost ever again would be tehnophobia or stupidity. Im sure it will be many years before this technology encompasses the whole US, let alone the world. And it will be longer still until it is made to be user-friendly in practical ways, but still, i think this is pretty exciting.
