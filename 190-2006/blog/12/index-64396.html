Comics at the ATH

Submitted by neurotica on 18 October 2006 - 6:32pm.
  • athanaeum
  • cool speakers
  • upcoming events

On November 2nd, at the Athenaeum, there's going to be an "Evening with the Author" featuring Marjane Satrapi who is the amazing graphic novelist/comic book artist. Her work includes Persepolis I and II, where she talks about growing up during the Islamic Revolution in Iran, her adolescence abroad and then her return to Iran. It would be really interesting in light of our discussions on "Understanding Comics". On one hand, her work challenges assumptions made about comic books. She addresses very serious, historically issues, yet also delves into her own personal, human experience. Her style is very unique, it what it is and what it expresses. Her drawings are pretty minimalist, almost childlike. The worlds she creates are solely in black and white, no grays, no shading or intermediate hues.

thoughts

Submitted by neurotica on 18 October 2006 - 1:06pm.
  • understanding comics

i really liked "understanding comics". it touched upon several major media studies topics that we've gone over during our academic careers, but in a very readable and enjoyable format. from the use of icons to represent ideas, to the high art vs. low art debates of what does get to constitute art, to the more recent house of leaves inspired discussions on form's relationship with content.

one aspect of the book that really fascinated me was the comparisons between western comics and japanese comics. Even though the same format is used, and there is a similar relationship between icon and representation, what those icons, and what modes of visual storytelling that are considered natural, are are completely different. It amazed me how unique they were from each other. This made me wonder how would this class be like if we were in another culture where the notions of narrative, and possibley even the notions of the role of author are completely different?

why blogs make me sad....or AOL USER 23187425

Submitted by neurotica on 25 September 2006 - 2:39am.
  • aol
  • books
  • search engines

I totally posted a blog this afternoon and pressed submit and everything and lo and behold there is no post!! Similar stuff (though my fault), I posted some stuff last week but had forgotten to log in, so now I'm guessing they will be lost in cyberspace forever!

Anyways--my blog was about something that came up in my Digital Art class. One of the teachers (though not the official professor), Jason Brown talked about one of his projects. It all started with this guy who published the search inquiries of the users of an AOL search engine. You don't see the names of the users, instead users are assigned a number. So with this list you could see that at this time and day user 111 searched for this and later on he searched for that, etc. Here's a link with more info: http://www.lot49.com/2006/08/the_search_engine_diary_of_aol.shtml One user, over the course of May, searched over 8,000 entries. However, these entries were mostly bits of phrases that garnered no results, and instead created a strange poem or narrative. Jason Brown compiled the search inquiries in a book, which can be seen at this link http://superbunker.com/projects/i-feel-better/ Is this AOL user an author? What does it mean when something is published when it wasn't created with that intention? What does it mean when the medium itself isn't meant for authorship in how we normally think of it. In general I am fascinated by this, why this person typed so much so often, and what does it mean? Are they typing it for themselves or they are purposely transmitting their words into a global space?

the crazy world of blogging

Submitted by neurotica on 6 September 2006 - 2:36am.

hopefully next week i'll be more on the uppity up side of blogging and commenting on others. setting this up and trying to figure out sakai has taken a major chunk of my life. but voila (in the mother tongue of foucault)! I am here!

while reading nesbit's piece and how she talked about the impact of technology on notions and laws pertaining to authorship, i was wondering how would sampling fit into that? when describing the process of photographers struggling to be considered authors, she quotes Taine who said "photographic work is property; it belogs to the producer under the same title that gives the engraving to the engraver, and in the two cases, the property should be protected" (237). when a dj uses different samples in hir songs, whose property is that? there is a human process in that creation, and the samples are (at least theoretically) being used in a different context and being experienced differently. however, what makes it tricky, particularly with music, is that the samples are being experienced in the same medium though it is not the same work. the music industry in general is an interesting place to explore the realm of authorship.
