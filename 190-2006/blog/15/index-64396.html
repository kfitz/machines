The missing director

Submitted by bloggityblog07 on 20 September 2006 - 12:53am.
  • authorship and film

It has come to my attention while reading Authorship and Film that I too am in the habbit of ignoring authorship in film. While I would never read a book without knowing the author, I consistently watch movies without having any idea who directed the film. While I think this phenomenon can be attributed to the fact that we more often than not associate a film with its actors because of their star power, it is interesting to consider the many other factors that facilitate the routine dismissal of cinematic authorship.

I think a large part of the lack of directorial recognition has to do with the fact that the mainstream population has very little knowledge of the production process for example what exactly goes into lighting, staging, and set design.

Cinema vs. Literature

Submitted by bloggityblog07 on 19 September 2006 - 12:51am.
  • authorship and film

I took a class while studying abroad about cinema and literature and we devoted a large part of our time to the analysis of cinematic adaptations of literary works. Since then I have been very interested in the comparison of the two mediums and this dichotomy between the written and the visual modes of story telling. While reading one of the essays in Authorship and Film, I was reminded of that class and this discussion of cinema vs. literature. Janet Staiger in her essay "Authorship Approaches," quotes Astruc as saying "cinema is becoming a language, which allows it to break free from the tyranny of what is visual, from the image for its own sake...to become a means of writing just as flexible and subtle as written language."

The Powers That Be...

Submitted by bloggityblog07 on 13 September 2006 - 1:30am.
  • bell hooks
  • random

I have heard the phrase "the powers that be" used frequently in black literature and I always had a curiosity as to its origin. So in the spirit of sharing random and often useless information as is so common the mission of blogging, I decided to visit our friend Wikipedia. For those of you who don't know, it turns out that the phrase "The powers that be," also known as TPTB, comes from the King James Version of the Bible. "Let every soul be subject unto the higher powers. For there is no power but of God: The powers that be are ordained of God" (Romans 13:1). The current use of the phrase has evolved to refer to any group that holds power over a certain entity: politicians, television and film writers, CEOs, or in bell hooks' article, the continual force of society in the alienation, and marginalization of African Americans.

Various thoughts

Submitted by bloggityblog07 on 6 September 2006 - 4:33pm.
  • blogging
  • Foucault

I am just going to go ahead and make some general observations on the readings. I have no idea whether or not these observations have already been made because like most of you, this week has not been the best week for me to begin the ever-so daunting task of blogging. Thus I shall make my first true blog only three hours before class with hopes that next week I will be blogging with a little more punctuality. Anyway, my thoughts on the readings were varied. I found the articles to be somewhat scattered at times but there were some very valid and interesting points made throughout.
One point that I found particularly interesting to me in the Foucault article was when he interrogated the term "work." He writes, "What in short, is the strange unit designated by the term, work? What is necessary to its composition, if work is not something written by a person called an "author?" He continues to propose the question, "If an individual is not an author, what are we to make of those things he has written or said--?" This really got me thinking. Knowing next to nothing about art, a similar question has often crossed my mind. Once someone is considered to be an artist, is everything he or she creates from then on considered art as well? Where are we to draw the line? Just as Foucault says, "Assuming that we are dealing with an author, is everything he wrote and said, everything he left behind, to be included in his work?" It is a very interesting question and one that I feel is deeply intertwined with the question of authorship and what exactly it means to be an author.
