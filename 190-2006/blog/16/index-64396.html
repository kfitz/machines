Stigwood

Submitted by Leesoid on 20 September 2006 - 12:59am.
  • post

I quite enjoyed reading the chapter by Michael deAngelis about Robert Stigwood. This was for two reasons. The first was, I kept singing "Jesus Christ Super Star" as I read and the second, because I found it interesting to think about authorship with a black and white consumer driven mentality. The Stigwood approach on authorship isn't anywhere near that gray area, ambiguous, dead, dying, terminally ill media studies analysis of author. This authorship is the cold, rational economy driven author who sacrifices high art for high pay check. And really, it is these "authors" these money makers, that are controlling the media. They have the power because they have the money. They have the money because they aren't trying to write what is most revolutionary and innovative but rather what has the most audience appeal.

Anonymous

Submitted by Leesoid on 9 September 2006 - 12:38am.
  • categorical

The beginning of Marchessault's article alludes to the question, if Shakespeare had a brilliant sister would she have been marginalized, would she have been placed so far out of the context of expression, that from her own hand no similar art would have arose? Yet, when one looks at the language of Shakespeare's time it seems, in terms of communicating to the heart of human emotion and spirit we have not moved all that far. This makes one realize the severity of the battle female authors have fought in such a short period of time to find a space in the literary world, when their expression has been viewed as the other. Is language then read, judged and conceptualized in a phallic space as the reading suggests because it was the norm. A norm in which female authors had to enter into? If this is true then the fact that a piece of work was written by a female is an essential part of the piece. However, Barthes would say that this is part of biography and isn't essential to the author as subject. The suggestion of female authors being tied to a feminist, autobiographic and reflective tone, seems to makes this context very important for the reader to receive. While the reader is still taking their own decoding out of the material that they are reading, the whole concept of female authorship seems to change. If the female author is dead, or non-present , why is there a reason to discuss her as the marginalized other?

The word document turned into a blog

Submitted by Leesoid on 5 September 2006 - 11:35am.

I applaud the blogging pioneers on here. Your authorship has provided me with some guiding light to this quite foreign concept to me of academic blogging. This is how I have always thought of blogging:

Current mood :) Current music: Beach Boys

As the title alludes to, I wrote this entry in word first. Here are my thoughts on why that is. As soon as you display something you write in front of peers, or simply an audience larger than just your professor, authorship takes on a whole new meaning. For me, I am uncomfortable and unconfident in this medium. There is an uncertainty that comes with the exposure of my thoughts to other people. Yet this is what authorship is. The creations of our minds should be meant for open examination by others. I however, haven't gotten used to sharing yet, other than of course in live discussion type forums.
