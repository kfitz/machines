  • personal space

So I think I really don't do this much in the blogspace, but I feel like ranting today. Maybe it's this meeting that i have today to try and convince Dean Clark to let me graduate a semester early (something I think should be a no brainer as long as all my credits/requirement/thesis are done). Maybe it's finals time or my trip to Vegas. Whatever, this little blurb of an editorial about a fairly trivial thing just set me off.

They're putting TVs in the LA transportation buses. Sounds fun, huh? like JetBlue TVs on transcontinental flights so you can watch crappy movies you wouldn't have payed to see otherwise but will secretly enjoy. But these ones will be on the entire time over speakers that will probably emit sound between volume levels of loud and ear-splitting. Plus, commercials.. lots of them. And targeted at the specific "bus riding demographic" (i.e. poor people without cars). I don't know about anyone else, but I actually love riding buses - it's a time to zen out on the slowly moving cityscape, or delve into a book, or people watch - but for the most part it is a silent activity (other than eavesdropping on close conversations and the occasional alcoholic/drug induced outburst). It's kind of like swimming or something - tune out if you want to. But now books and conversations and even outbursts will have to contend with bad credit advertisements and worthlessly summarized news broadcasts.

Models are people too, just not when they're on TV

Submitted by achillesheel on 19 November 2006 - 10:59pm.
  • Tyra Banks is obnoxious

So in lieu of doing anything productive on a Sunday night (they're sacred, right?), I watched America's Next Top Model. I obviously wasn't expecting anything substantial, culturally aware, or even entertaining - just something to kill time. But it's deliberate avoidance of any of these ideas was incredible.

In just one episode, a girl admitted to having attempted suicide, and a Spanish guy told one of the models that he "didn't like black girls." Meanwhile we are supposed to be focused on the trials of the competition: "Nobody thinks I want this bad enough," "I don't feel pretty enough," "No one thinks models feel." The suicide attempt confession was blurted out during one of the "challenges" and after the shocked reaction shots, no one bothered ot mention it again. Except for saying that she was really brave and strong, it was a non-issue or even plotline in the show. She won the challenge for being so strong and brave.. and cut to her on One Tree Hill as a guest star. Whoo - suicide attempts make you a strong person with more emotion to tap into when you're acting.

selling out can be sneaky

Submitted by achillesheel on 16 November 2006 - 11:52pm.
  • all good
  • Big Macs
  • bling and money

Simultaneously one of the funniest and more important articles that I have read in a while: Business week's "Hip Hop two-step over product placement." First off, it includes a photo of one the whiter shade of pale kind of middle-aged man who freely admits to knowing little to nothing about hip hop music, saying he's more of a "Van Morrison/Eric Clapton kind of guy." But the article includes snipets of an interview with Russell Simmons, hip hop impressario extraordinaire, who is talking about a deal with McDonalds to pay hip hop and rap artists for including things like "Big Mac" in their lyrics. but he says the deal might not work unless listeners are unaware of said agreement. In other words, Micky D's is uninterested in publicity that is not sneaky and underhanded. Simmons had the idea to instead produce songs in which rappers would rap about how mcdonald's wants to throw money at them to reposition their brand and thus create a criticism of the exploitation of hip hop culture and also corruption in adversiting. But then wasn't it simmons that brokered the deal for P. Diddy to pen "Pass the Courvosier" to reposition that brand? The whole thing seems pretty tricky to me. I may just be naive and optimistic, but I usually figure even commercial artist aren't always just plugging a product. I'm not saying creative advertising has no place, I just think it would be too bad if art was reduced to a series of commercials. As David Kiley characterizes hip hop, I just want it to return to "a lot of lyrics about bling and money."

Bujalskian - is that even a word?

Submitted by achillesheel on 20 October 2006 - 9:24am.
  • anthropological artwork

When we have discussed filmmakers and directors as authors, we've mostly talked about the role of cinematographers or writers or producers, but somehow avoided discussing the role of actors in the authorship of a film. True, they are mostly pretty, vapid, blank slates for the director to carve into his vision. Paul Walker- definitely not an author. But in some cases, they have a huge impact on the text. I was reading an old article I clipped out of the LA Times about Andrew Bujalski's new film "Mutual Appreciation." I had pulled it because of one quote about his characters, where he said that they "get reconsidered retroactively through the prism of whoever I asked to play them." We forget that actors often rewrite their characters, and certainly they at least "write" the character with their own specific mannersims and what they bring to the role.
