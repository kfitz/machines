Return of the author

Submitted by achillesheel on 17 September 2006 - 10:26pm.
  • presenting
  • reading

so in the effort to avoid a pointless and scattered presentation/discussion on wednesday, I'm trying to work through "authorship and film" with coherence in mind... and the only thing i know for sure so far is that the author is definitely back from the dead in a big way. All the essays (so far) seem to be asserting auterism (or something very much like it). All the readings of films (especially those about a body of work) use the author and his relationship to the text to create meaning. Though they all recognize postmodernist critiques of authorship (getting quite sick of references to Barthes and Foucault and the same quoted passages), none of the essayists consider the author to be dead - all the films are deeply contextualized in relation to the author.

A Million Little Lies?

Submitted by msblogger1220 on 17 September 2006 - 10:20pm.
  • random thoughts

I recently realized that although I have read the articles and discussed the controversy about James Frey's book "A Million Little Pieces" with friends and relatives, I have not discussed it in an academic setting. I feel as though the issue of authorship that we are focusing on actually connects really well with the controversy.
First of all, I have read the book, but I read it after the realizations that some parts of it were false. I believe this knowledge skewed my perception of the novel while I was reading it. However, I feel it's really interesting that our society was so connected with this particular authorial situation. Why was it so important that the novel line up perfectly with the events in Frey's real life? Was it simply because he lied and said that the book was non-fiction?? or were people disappointed because their connection with the author's story was shattered?

I Like Puppies, I Swear

Submitted by BuildingsAndFood on 17 September 2006 - 8:00pm.
  • authorship and film
  • awesomeness
  • music

Okay, so first I wanted to use the title to clear up a popular misconception. Now that that's out of the way, I just got done reading the article in the book about the Irving Berlin/Darryl Zanuck film, "I Hear Music and...". Knowing me, I liked it quite a bit.

I thought the writing style was fantastic - much more accessible than some of the other essays in the collection. But the meat of the essay, discussing the authorship of music (in the context of the film) was a fascinating story. I like that it wasn't about necessarily the one song that Berlin wrote, but more about the entire genre of ragtime music and how to locate Berlin's place within that. Musical geneology is difficult enough to trace, but the fact that so many other people (Berlin and White America) had so much at stake in the decision of authorship. Things like saying that American popular music begins with that Berlin song help to define America as White America, and Berlin's own insecurities about his immigrant status make it imperative to him to claim that song and genre as American.
