The Passion

Submitted by oneoutofseven on 27 September 2006 - 3:34pm.
  • fiction

I talked in class the other day (maybe it was two weeks ago?) about Jeanette Winterson during a discussion on gender & literature. To paraphrase the discussion, we were basically wondering whether its posisble for a male writer to write a successful female narrator, and vice versa; I mentioned that Jeanette Winterson comes close to doing this in one book, "Written on the Body", where she writes as a gender-ambiguous voice throughout the entire book. I forgot to mention Winterson's "The Passion", where she uses a male narrator throughout the book.

This book, I think, is her most acclaimed. The book, as the title suggests, is about passion, and it manifests in various incarnations throughout the book including Henri's (Winterson's male narrator) own passion for a woman named Villanelle.

International Hip Hop

Submitted by msblogger1220 on 27 September 2006 - 1:51pm.
  • music industry
  • racism
  • thesis

So... as we all begin to turmoil over the horrible 't' word, I just thought I'd lay out my general idea and see if anyone had feedback/ideas/criticisms, etc.

Basically, I took this Hip Hop Culture class while I was abroad in New Zealand. Seeing as hip hop culture has its roots in the United States, it was interesting to study it outside of the US (although my prof was American) and the class actually focused on tying in the abundant use of rap music in the Maori community in New Zealand and other marginalized groups across the globe. This inspired me. So I want to write about international hip hop on the small scale, i.e. how individuals outside the US use hip hop as a form of voice to speak out against oppression in their individual communities. I'm debating whether I tie this in with the early stages of US hip hop, before it was a mainstream commodity and most rappers used hip hop to communicate within their communities and speak out about their daily experiences and oppressions.

The internet made you go broke

Submitted by reliablesrc07 on 27 September 2006 - 12:29pm.
  • internet privacy

More about the internet and personal identity...

How much can the internet affect you?
Consider this, what if in a few years you are applying for a job and your future employer goes online and conducts a search foryou and finds incriminating information, or worse, pictures of you. And then you do not get the job. And what if you never even knew that such things were online.

As much as I enjoy the internet and the way it facilitates many everyday things, I', definitely aware of the potential danger it can pose to you and the way you are presented to the public. Living in a time when most people have the internet and have the ability to post things about you on a whim... how do you protect yourself? How exactly do you take control of your identity and prevent someone else from controlling you. For now, I suggest you be careful about who takes pictures of you in certain situation, because once its online, always on.

Madame Butterfly, meet the Big Screen

Submitted by racinian on 27 September 2006 - 10:45am.
  • random thoughts

This past Monday the Metropolitan Opera in New York City decided to simulcast the opening night of their production of Puccini's Madama Butterfly in Times Square. The streets were closed down, 650 cushioned seats were assembled, and the event was open to the public. There was a second simulcast in Lincoln Center Plaza where tickets were required, but handed out for free at the box office before the event.

Peter Gelb, the Met's general manager, said he wanted to end the "isolation" of the art form, "It's essential for the broader public to know what's going on inside. I want to demonstrate that the Met is interested in being part of mainstream society and contemporary culture" (http://www.playbillarts.com/news/article/5228.html).

Potential Thesis Topics

Submitted by reliablesrc07 on 27 September 2006 - 10:41am.
  • facebook-myspace-overexposure of information-personal identity

So I have been mulling over thesis for a months now and have now come up with a new idea. Initially I began to toy with it when Facebook brought us the News Feed a few weeks ago but when it opened up to the public a few days ago, I pretty much decided this is somewhere I wanted to go with thesis. SO anyway, previously I had wanted to write about the publics increasing desire to participate in the authorship of media (ex Snakes on a Plane) and how this could potentially affect media in the future. But now, I'd like to maybe write about the overexposure of personal information through networks sch as myspace and facebook and how that affects identity. I would definitely have to take into account the publics reaction on new facebook features, such as the news feed and public access. I'm beginning to wonder why facebook is trying harder and harder to become some sort of myspace when what I used to like about it is that it was different enough for me to want to join it. I guess we'll have to see how recent decisions regarding its layout will change who joins it and who doesnt, and maybe even who leaves it.
