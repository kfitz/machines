A Million Little Lies?

Submitted by msblogger1220 on 17 September 2006 - 10:20pm.
  • random thoughts

I recently realized that although I have read the articles and discussed the controversy about James Frey's book "A Million Little Pieces" with friends and relatives, I have not discussed it in an academic setting. I feel as though the issue of authorship that we are focusing on actually connects really well with the controversy.
First of all, I have read the book, but I read it after the realizations that some parts of it were false. I believe this knowledge skewed my perception of the novel while I was reading it. However, I feel it's really interesting that our society was so connected with this particular authorial situation. Why was it so important that the novel line up perfectly with the events in Frey's real life? Was it simply because he lied and said that the book was non-fiction?? or were people disappointed because their connection with the author's story was shattered?

Who is she actually writing this for??

Submitted by msblogger1220 on 13 September 2006 - 2:53pm.
  • bell hooks

hooks' reading really made me question who her intended readers were. While she makes it quite clear that she wants black women (and men) to read and understand the oppression society's racial constructions, it is evident throughout her essay that she also wants the white, intellectual male population to read and interpret her work. In one part of her essay she states that she worried about her arguments lacking conviction. Well, it is possible that if these arguments were more common or better known (especially among the dominant white male intellectuals), they would have the conviction necessary to make an impact. While reading her essay, it was clear to me that she knew that many potential readers were going to be these white intellectual males she is discussing. She clearly knows this, as she says she is cautious when discussing such topics. However, the essay is also potentially aimed at blacks to raise their awareness of the societal situations they are placed in everyday. So clearly she can have more than one target audience, but I find myself torn as to discover which one she wanted to reach or influence more...

Scattered thoughts

Submitted by msblogger1220 on 6 September 2006 - 3:21pm.
  • Barthes

I would have to agree with others that Barthes' essay was a little extreme for me. The essay was not the most coherent reading I have ever done and the messages were thoughtful, but a little too intense. While I do agree that text should be interpreted through the eyes of the reader and deconstructed for what it actually is, I do not think the author needs to be completely dismissed. Learning about an author after interpreting one of their texts can introduce a new criticism or shed new light upon the text that the reader would not have seen without that knowledge. I do believe it is important to acknowledge that there is never a single author of a work (society, surroundings, family, etc. all influence an author). Foucault talks about how literary works are a result of cultural settings, not a single individual. While I agree with the idea that society influences all authors and their ideas are not necessarily unique because they are constantly receiving ideas from their surroundings, I think it is important to consider the writer's background to take into account any spin they may have placed in the writing. So while I agree that literary works are not the result of one individual, but the result of a cultural experience, it is important to realize that one person did take the time to put their experience or opinions into a work and although they are influenced by the world they live in, they are still the person who expressed this experience.
