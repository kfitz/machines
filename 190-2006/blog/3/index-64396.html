Meetro?

  • meetro

As inspired by reliablesrc07's post on Wallop, I thought I'd tell you guys about another creepy networking/message service called Meetro, which is completely different and far more stalker-y than anything I've seen before.

Meetro works like AIM--you sign up, get a screen name... And then you put in your street address. In Meetro, instead of creating and having your own buddy list, when you sign on you get a list of the people all around you, down to the 1/4 mile. Creepy enough for you?!?! The idea I think is that maybe when you have a question about say where to get your hair cut in Claremont, you can have it answered easily. People have profiles (I think there's some sort of link with MySpace), and so basically, you can know everything about a person down to their SPECIFIC PHYSICAL LOCATION from one instant messaging service.

The Passion

Submitted by oneoutofseven on 27 September 2006 - 3:34pm.
  • fiction

I talked in class the other day (maybe it was two weeks ago?) about Jeanette Winterson during a discussion on gender & literature. To paraphrase the discussion, we were basically wondering whether its posisble for a male writer to write a successful female narrator, and vice versa; I mentioned that Jeanette Winterson comes close to doing this in one book, "Written on the Body", where she writes as a gender-ambiguous voice throughout the entire book. I forgot to mention Winterson's "The Passion", where she uses a male narrator throughout the book.

This book, I think, is her most acclaimed. The book, as the title suggests, is about passion, and it manifests in various incarnations throughout the book including Henri's (Winterson's male narrator) own passion for a woman named Villanelle.

Why Mario Batali is more Psychologically Stable than Foucault / Why Mario Batali is Kind of a Scoundrel

Submitted by oneoutofseven on 27 September 2006 - 1:17am.
  • authorship
  • food
  • mario batali

It's 1 AM and I'm thinking this is a great time for us all to break break to discuss the topic of authorship and food!

...Bueller? .....Bueller? Anyone?

So I've been thinking about cookbooks, the recipes in them, and what makes them unique or different from predecessors, and what makes traditional recipes worth revising. Obviously there are a lot of questions we can raise here: What constitutes enough change for a recipe to be made new? Is the substitution of cream cheese for animal lard enough? Adding an extra clove of garlic? Setting the oven at a lower temperature and cooking a dish for 10 extra minutes? Or is it the intent of a revision that makes it different, such as making a pesto using low fat parmesan (does this even exist?) for inclusion in a low-fat cookbook? Who exactly authored pesto, anyway? We all know pesto as an established foodthing, but what constitutes it, and what constitutes a revision?

The Postmodern Condiiton

Submitted by oneoutofseven on 13 September 2006 - 5:02pm.
  • bell hooks
  • postmodernism

Something that interested me in the hooks essay (ahh, the sweet ease of not having to hit the shift button to type author's names) was her description of the postmodern "condition". hooks describes this condition as "many other groups now shar[ing] with black folks a sense of deep alienation, despair, uncertainty, loss of grounding, even if it is not informed by shared circumstances." Wow. Though a somewhat common description of the plight of "black folk", it's kind of intense to see hooks extend it to all of contemporary culture.

This gets me thinking about postmodernism, its emergence in critical theory, and its roots outside of academia.

Narrative, Database & Literature

Submitted by oneoutofseven on 11 September 2006 - 9:17pm.
  • manovich
  • reading response
  • the language of new media
  • thesis

I haven't made it through all of the Lev Manovich reading yet, but some parts of what I've read are pretty exciting (mostly because, I admit, they are relevant to things I'm thinking about for my thesis). I'm really interested in what he's got to say about the narrative v. database argument, and the position new media is in to play on both teams. And the projects he describes on page 226 sound incredible...

Manovich (so far) has described the narrative & database sythesis available to new media developers as also being available to cinema and video games. He may touch on other subjects further on in the article, but I'm guessing because of some of his previous statements that he's planning on leaving literature out for good. I'm not sure, though, I think differently: what about Italo Calvino's Invisible Cities? In fact, I think that text is a great example of a synthesis between narrative and database-- Marco Polo presents descriptions of cities in a loose order to Kublai Kahn, and every 5-15 cities or so the descriptions are interrupted by short conversations between Marco Polo & Kublai Kahn which again relate only loosely & sometimes not at all to the descriptive database of cities. In addition, each city is categorized as cities & memory, cities & signs, cities & desire, thin cities, cities & eyes, etc.

Barthes & Me

Submitted by oneoutofseven on 5 September 2006 - 6:58pm.

So part of me really identifies with what Barthes is saying: how many times, in an English class, has some gracefully beautiful and nebulous passage or work been deduced down to its small awkward parts, or explained away by something like, "This was written when Anne Sexton was having her 5th breakdown and she really liked cats then, so obviously this poem is about a cat."

I hate that.

But as someone who writes things, part of me still wants that kind of authorship recognition that Barthes suggests doing away with. What's the point of writing if there's no one who's going to read it? I think this is the problem, though.
