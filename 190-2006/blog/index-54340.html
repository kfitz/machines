Submitted by Leesoid on 3 November 2006 - 9:51pm.

In light of the Borat movie release this weekend, I would like to discuss Ali G (Baron Cohen) and his other fun filled characters. Wine, I like. I read this article where the author was trying to sound self-important because she had experienced a moment in which she was able to speak with Baron Cohen himself, (out of character). Apparently this is rare. The article talked about how he keeps up his character face in any interview even in light of the political issues that have been brought up surrounding the Borat movie. He still responds as Borat.
Another reporter in the article was making the argument that when reporters speak to him in character they are not fully doing their job, that of seeking information. What they are doing is further promoting his act. They are simply being another venue in which Borat is gaining popularity instead of getting at the real issues of who Baron Cohen is and why he is doing this. We don't really ever get to know what he thinks about the fact that his Borat My Space page says he lives "three mile north of fence to jewtown". I think this is of course fitting because he is in the genre of comedy, the moment he makes commentary, it becomes a little to serious. But it is true that they are never going to get a real answer out of him, from the point of view of what the artist thinks of his creation if he is always talking as the man from Kzakhstan. Perhaps Barbara Walters could get a compelling interview out of him. Make him squeeze out a few tears?! It could happen. Or Opera? She might be able to do it. BULLY

Submitted by racinian on 2 November 2006 - 6:28pm.
  • first person

Last night, while watching South Park, I saw a preview for a new video game (for PlayStation 2) called "Bully." It looks like Harry Potter without the magic, and I just could not figure out for the life of me what the point of the game was. The preview centered on "the new kid" as he met people at Bullworth Academy (the jocks, the nerds, the bullies, the hot girls, etc.) and I'm assuming he takes up the "stop bulling the nerds" cause. In any case, I found this interesting primarily because of the article in First Person titled "Videogames of the Oppressed" which advocated for a computer/video game based on the techniques used in "Theater of the Oppressed." Basically, the game/performance involves a lot of audience interaction and actors work "undercover" in the "Forum Theater." Frasca writes, "Forums are created around a short play (5-10 minutes long), usually scripted on-site, and based on the suggestions of the participants. The scene always enacts an oppressive situation, where the protagonist has to deal with powerful characters that do not let her achieve her goals...The scene is enacted without showing a solution to the problem. After one representation, anybody in the audience can take over the role of the protagonist and suggest, through her acting, a solution that she thinks would break the oppression" (88- whew!).

ADS ADS ADS

Submitted by bloggityblog07 on 2 November 2006 - 11:52am.
  • advertising
  • fun
  • procrastination

I just got my take home mid-term for my other media studies class and I had to share it with you. The first half of the mid-term is pretty typical. Upon selecting a scene from a current TV show, you have to write a 2-3 page essay imagining what it would be like if you were seeing that scene 40 years ago and what would be controversial or groundbreaking about it.

The second half of the mid-term is a little more interesting. We have to watch a solid hour of TV and write down every ad that we see during the commercial breaks while taking brief notes about what the ad is selling, who is represented in the ad etc. Then we have to organize this list of ads into groups based on the product i.e. beer, cars etc. and then we have to organize the list into subgroups based on what is being represented in the ad and who is the target audience.
