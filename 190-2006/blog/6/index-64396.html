Understanding Comics through Ancient Art

Submitted by lrob on 17 October 2006 - 10:29pm.
  • reading response

I found Scott McCloud's discussion of the history of comics extremely interesting, especially because I'm taking a class on Ancient Art. Since I'm learning about the visual iconography of ancient cultures every week, it's great to see McCloud compare these same concepts to a more contemporary art form. In some ways, it might even be possible to extend his analysis back to some of the cave paintings of the Upper Paleolithic era, which (debatably) show animals and people in sequence. From this perspective, "comics" as McCloud defines them ("juxtaposed pictorial and other images in deliberate sequence, intended to convey information and/or to produce an aesthetic response in the viewer") are much more than 3,000 years old (which he claims on page 107).

A bit of my thesis proposal

Submitted by lrob on 11 October 2006 - 1:30pm.
  • thesis

Key Question: How have photographs been distorted for political purposes throughout history?

Readers: Prof. Howe and Prof. Fitzpatrick

"A photograph is often perceived to be an unmediated copy of the real world, a trace of reality skimmed off the very surface of life." -- Sturken and Cartwright's Practices of Looking: An Introduction to Visual Culture, 2001

In my thesis, I would like to explore and examine the ways that photographs have been used to deceive the public, specifically for political purposes. This idea grew out of my interest in the myth of photographic truth. This myth has persisted since the time of the medium's invention in the nineteenth century. Louis-Jacques-MandÃ© Daguerre and William Henry Fox Talbot, both credited with discovering photographic means of creating images, portrayed photography as a factual, automatic, and completely objective process, stressing that it enabled nature to "draw herself." Daguerre and Talbot made the photograph sound like a scientific window onto the world when, in reality, it always had the potential to deceive. Images that defy the myth of photographic truth date back to 1839 and can be found within the work of Daguerre himself. Nonetheless, this myth continues to persist, perhaps now more than ever. In the digital age, computer programs like Photoshop invite and even encourage editing, manipulation, and fabrication. People today know how easy it is to alter images. But somehow, in spite of this awareness, the public still buys into instances of photographic fakery. Because of this, photographs have been a powerful political tool for more than a century. Photographic images are often read as factual--especially when presented by the government or in newsprint--which makes photography the perfect medium for convincingly deceiving the public.

Authorship in House of Leaves

Submitted by lrob on 3 October 2006 - 5:44pm.
  • reading response

I'm exactly 91 pages into House of Leaves. In relation to this course, I'm especially interested in how the book contains several layers of authors--Zampano, the author of the manuscript on The Navidson Record, Johnny Truant as he comments on Zampano's text and adds details about his own life, and Navidson, the videographer (that is, if he ever actually existed). It's also interesting that there is an appendix in the back that gives even further insight into these fictitious authors' lives (i.e. bits and pieces from Zampano's other writings, letters from Johnny Truant's mother, etc). The book is very complex and somewhat hard to keep up with at times, since Johnny's own stories often diverge from the plot of Zampano's manuscript. Still, it's interesting, and it's nice to be reading a novel, since the material for all my other classes is pretty much just history and theory.

Mixed Feelings about Man with a Movie Camera

Submitted by lrob on 30 September 2006 - 2:10am.
  • response

Let me just say that on Wednesday, I really enjoyed Man with a Movie Camera. I thought it was incredibly visually engaging and groundbreaking (especially considering it was made in 1929). I left class that night impressed and inspired...

BUT the following night I watched Berlin: Symphony of a City in another class, and it totally changed my opinion of Man with a Movie Camera. Remember all that stuff at the beginning about it being a huge, unprecidented experiment? Well... Berlin: Symphony of a City was made in 1927, two years before, and it's actually strikingly similar. No "actors," no "sets," just the city... and film AS film... not trying to reference the theater, literature, etc... along with tons of montage. Although I preferred the visuals in Man with a Movie Camera, I lost some respect for the film after seeing that it wasn't based on a unique concept, which I'd originally been led to believe.
