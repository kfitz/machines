Mixed Feelings about Man with a Movie Camera

Submitted by lrob on 30 September 2006 - 2:10am.
  • response

Let me just say that on Wednesday, I really enjoyed Man with a Movie Camera. I thought it was incredibly visually engaging and groundbreaking (especially considering it was made in 1929). I left class that night impressed and inspired...

BUT the following night I watched Berlin: Symphony of a City in another class, and it totally changed my opinion of Man with a Movie Camera. Remember all that stuff at the beginning about it being a huge, unprecidented experiment? Well... Berlin: Symphony of a City was made in 1927, two years before, and it's actually strikingly similar. No "actors," no "sets," just the city... and film AS film... not trying to reference the theater, literature, etc... along with tons of montage. Although I preferred the visuals in Man with a Movie Camera, I lost some respect for the film after seeing that it wasn't based on a unique concept, which I'd originally been led to believe.

This seems like a question of authorship, since the creators of Berlin: Symphony of a City invented the genre of the "city-film," yet the creators of Man with a Movie Camera claimed this idea as their own. Sketchy, sketchy.

i don't know if this is in defense of not...

Submitted by PureJaqassary on 30 September 2006 - 3:15pm.

...but the concept of film as film was not unique to Vertov nor do I think he thought people would see it as such. I may be a little hazy on the dates and chronology of all of this but, there was a lot of talk about film as film when the medium first became practical (i.e. when you could actually move the camera around instead of having to move things in front of a fixed shot). With the lighter, movable models, like the one we saw in the movie, a whole lot of debate sprang up in the '20s about what film *should* be. There was a lot of pressure to create a film asthetic that was somehow essentially filmic. It was part of a larger school of artistic critism that was way too essentialist for my tastes. Basically a piece of art that embodied the best possible use of its medium and the least interference from other mediums was judged surperior, while a sculpture that seemed too painterly, for example, was deemed inferior. Given that, I think the paragraph at the beginning was meant more to inform those who didn't know about the nature of this movement, and to cue those who did know into the fact that film was to be an experiment in those ideas. Maybe I'm wrong, maybe he did think he was being truely revolutionary, but it's a thought.
