reality tv ethics

Submitted by Leesoid on 2 October 2006 - 7:17pm.
  • hummis

I was talking to my friend today and she told me about a reality TV show she had heard of in England that was set up like the Bachelorette. The twist was the girl these guys were dating used to be a man (unknown to the contestants). I googled it and couldn't find this show, so I am not sure if this premise is entirely true.

This however made me think about the ethics of reality television. If psychologists have to submit experimental designs to ethics committees to ensure minimal physical or psychological damage there is no reason reality TV shouldn't face these same restrictions. Psychology is in the name of science but an experiment in which heavy amounts of psychological stress was induced by deception, would never fly. In reality television I feel they push the boundaries, trying to find more ways to push there contestants in the name of entertainment. In other countries for example Big Brother is a huge success, I have heard stories of people giving birth in the house, people going nearly crazy in the house, people being manipulated by the producers and forced (slightly) to do things they clearly don't want to do, est.. Yes, these people agree before hand to do whatever the producers throw at them, but there is a lot of pressure involved.

Thesis: A New Reader?

Submitted by WildCherry15 on 2 October 2006 - 6:11pm.
  • thesis
  • thesis reader
  • thesis topic

Okay, so as mentioned at the end of last entry, I don't think I will be able to take advantage of the inspirational professor I met with for two hours two weeks ago. This is because he deals more with simple, practical classroom usages of media, whereas I need to do a theoretical paper. I now have appointments with two other professors, who will hopefully be my readers. Profesora Donapetry, a Spanish teacher specializing in women's studies, might be very helpful because I am will be continuing the work I started over the summer, the creation of an online biography of my grandmother (a Mexican woman.) Profesora Donapetry would be able to help me in the content of the website, perhaps not so much the format. However, the content and its cultural implications might help shape the structure. For example, the website's audience might be older (closer in age to my grandmother.) And many viewers may be from Mexico, where digital technology is less available. Both older and technologically inexperienced people would need a very simple interface, perhaps one that starts with an introductory video, which is very attention-grabbing. I could then comment on the importance of the image in an increasingly globalized, international world, and I could expound on the differing experiences of the internet for people of various ages. Yes, the content does inform the structure.

weird art and authorship

Submitted by TheGoodConstable on 1 October 2006 - 11:39pm.
  • art

Speaking of off the topic, let me waste 30 seconds of your time.

In my sculpture class last week, we were looking at some slides of some famous "art" pieces. We were looking at the work of some guy who's name i can't remember who chiseled these incredibly detailed marble sculptures. We were comparing this to the work of some other guy I can't remember whose sculptures were essentially just things he found that he modified slightly. For example, one of his pieces of "art" was a urinal that he painted some very small letters on, and set on its back.
By taking an object that he did not create, and altering it ever so slightly, yet calling it his work, he is raising many questions of authorship. Is he still the "author" of this work, even though he didn't really create it? Does his style of "art" make him any less of an "artist" than the first guy who does something that people consider more legitimate.

"HIV is a gay disease"?

Submitted by ghostwriter on 1 October 2006 - 6:52pm.
  • ad campaign
  • lgbtq

So I was reading this article today online about the LA Gay and Lesbian Center's new ad campaign to fight the spread of HIV. Basically, the ads say "HIV is a gay disease. Own it. End it." The goal is to target apathetic gay men, which I think is valid, but the way they're going about seems seriously flawed to me. "HIV/AIDS is a gay disease" is a stereotype that the queer community has been fighting against since the disease was discovered. It has been used as a justification for homophobia. I don't think that using language that comes out of a tradition of homophobic rhetoric is the wrong way to go. It also glosses over the ever increasing numbers of black women with AIDS today. There has to be a better way to raise awareness about HIV, for gay men and for everyone.
