Barthes & Me

Submitted by oneoutofseven on 5 September 2006 - 6:58pm.

So part of me really identifies with what Barthes is saying: how many times, in an English class, has some gracefully beautiful and nebulous passage or work been deduced down to its small awkward parts, or explained away by something like, "This was written when Anne Sexton was having her 5th breakdown and she really liked cats then, so obviously this poem is about a cat."

I hate that.

But as someone who writes things, part of me still wants that kind of authorship recognition that Barthes suggests doing away with. What's the point of writing if there's no one who's going to read it? I think this is the problem, though. When you delve down deeper, my desire for authorship is a part of that author/critic game: I write something down, you look at it for twenty minutes, and then you come back to me and tell me what I'm all about. Which is interesting, you know, therapeutic almost in that a) someone is paying attention to what I am saying, aka to me, b) better because I don't need to pay them, and c) the critic (or fellow writing workshop participant, I mean let's be honest) gets to break people and their work down into tiny, defenseless pieces thus solving the riddle created for them by the author. It's a mutually beneficial process, really, but certainly a game nonetheless.

Submitted by DaLynziiChic on 6 September 2006 - 12:06pm.

Yes, I definately understand you here. There is a "game" that comes along with writing. Your post makes me think about authors who hide certain texts away under their floorboards to be discovered 10 years later by post-mortem tenants. Usually the public embraces the writings and wonders why the author stowed them away...it's certainly because s/he is afraid of what the texts reveal; what people will think, not just of the words and how they interplay but why the author wrote it. Is it about a cat or a lover? Insanity or understanding? What was s/he thinking? As readers we can't help but wonder, and as authors we can't help but fear.
