Why?

Submitted by Ikura on 13 September 2006 - 1:57pm.
  • readings

I just finished reading Marchessault's: Is the Dead Author a Woman?, and after sifting through, what most of the time was next to impossible to comprehend, I have come up with a question so powerful, that it shall end all other questions about Deaths of Authors...Why? Why is it important that the dead author be male or female, as either will only serve to confuse the reader, who, according to Barthes, shouldn't care about the author in the first place. Sure, there are people (readers) who come to the table with pre-reservations on gender, ethnicity and class when experiencing a work, but at least personally, the background of the person is more fun to find out after the work is finished and thought through, almost as a self perscribed afterword.

Anyway, this whole essay made me think of books that are written in the first person and are about a certain gender but are authored by a person of the opposite gender. Actually, I haven't read a book like this, but I have heard of them and they always make me wonder how the author comes to embody the personality in the book. What does it take to become another gender and how much meaning is there in doing so? I should probably read a book like this so I can have a good example to back this paragraph up, but for some reason, my Mom was just talking about this and it rang in my head as I was reading (but not during the Freud part...).

book suggestion and why important

Submitted by WildCherry15 on 13 September 2006 - 5:07pm.

Real quick, a good book for your thoughts is Orlando by Virgina Woolf. Orlando morphs between sexes and across different centuries as well, so you get perspectives that vary both by gendered outlook, as well as temporal context.

Second thought: I think it is pretty important that the dead author is a woman. Here is what I consider the key passage from Marchessault's article:
"The equation of the avant-garde and experimental forms with the 'feminine' foregrounds a disturbing problematic: if, in the post-structuralist scheme, an author is man in his purest expression, then the dead author is indeed a woman-a 'fluid' phantom, 'unfixed' and 'multiply' existing always in the elsewhere" (85).

If traditional author is man, woman is anonymous experimental author. Experimental is a reaction to traditional patriarchal forms. Feminism is a reaction to traditional patriarchal forms. So feminism=experimental=anonymous=dead author. It's exaggerated, but it kind of makes sense in a way. Women authors have not been able to contribute very much to the canon of traditional literature, and now that traditional literature is phasing out in favor of more experimental, anonymous literature, women will not have time to make a significant contribution that is recognized as such.
