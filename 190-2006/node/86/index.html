Stepping into the Ring for Bob Dylan

Submitted by oneoutofseven on 18 September 2006 - 12:37am.
  • bob dylan
  • new york times
  • plagiarism

There've been a couple articles in the news lately regarding Bob Dylan's latest release, "Modern Times", and some of its lyrical or poetic similarities to the Civil War poet Henry Timrod. One of the articles, Motoko Rich's "Who's This Guy Dylan Who's Borrowing Lines from Henry Timrod?", is especially critical of Dylan's apparent usurping, citing such striking similarities as these:

Henry T. : "A round of precious flowers / Oh! here, where in the precious noon I basked, / And strove, with logic frailer than flowers; "
Bobby D : "More frailer than the flowers, these precious hours." [do you think this grammar fudge is like when we tried to cheat on a math test in 4th grade and messed up a few problems to throw the teacher off our trail?]

Henry T. : "There is a wisdom that grows up in strife."
Bobby D. : "Where wisdom grows up in stife." [okay, it's possible they have a point here, but still--]

That sort of resonance is a long-time part of folk music tradition, and it appears that Bob Dylan's Timrod borrowing isn't any more egregious than that of his predecessors if it even exists on an intentional level. But lately America hasn't been easy on those writers showing the faintest whiff of plagiarism; does anyone remember the Kaavya Viswanathan "How Opal Mehta Got Kissed, Got Wild and Got a Life" fiasco? Okay, maybe that one was warranted, but I feel like we're quick to jump on everything we find these days. We want our content raw, our language made new, and we want you to do it all over again tomorrow. Only tomorrow you need to do it in a bubble with sound-proof earphones and a concussion which has robbed you of every piece of literature you've ever read.

But really, I mean think about it: even if Dylan did hack a few lines from Timrod's work, can you imagine how happy Timrod would be to see his work appear in the New York Times in 2006? What's the real point here? I feel like Motoko Rich was maybe that kid in middle school that told on you for passing notes during a lecture on mitosis.

So what gives, Motoko? Cut my main man Dylan a break. I don't care if he has to borrow from the Dead Sea Scrolls, he's Bob Dylan, and I trust that the result will be fantastic.
