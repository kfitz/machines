Don't you just love...

Submitted by racinian on 11 September 2006 - 8:00pm.
  • Barthes
  • random thoughts

...when your courses overlap? Today in my 19th Century U.S. Women Writers course we spent a chunk of time discussing the "role of the author" in a text. This was actually kind of shocking because, I'll admit, I found myself questioning the importance of last week's discussion (is that bad?). The class consensus seemed to be that the author could not be separated from the text, but likewise that the reader should not rely solely on the author's background/purpose to understand a text- one of those happy, indecisive "middle-ground" things. Nothing new here, right?

The conversation became more interesting when we accepted that the author and the text were, to a degree, inseparable, and focused on the implications of this connection, such as the effect the gender of the author has on a text.

On a different note, my class recently decided to meet in a lounge in Dennison Library as opposed to a classroom in Crookshank. It's interesting to observe the effect a change in environment has on people's participation and attitude during a discussion. Comfortable couches bring out the best in us, I suppose.

Don't want to start no beef, mind you.

Submitted by roundmidnight on 11 September 2006 - 11:51pm.

i know the feeling of happy coincidence when classes overlap, its great. But, I think you went a bit far in implying that our discussion was unimportant. Different classes have different goals: a woman writers course is going to talk about the effect of gender; a more general class on authorship will talk about the evolution of the "author." I do think comfortable couches do stimulate discussion though... don't get me wrong.

On a different note, after reading Marchessault's Open letter I realized that my knowledge of feminist literature is slim. I have not taken a Woman's Studies class (i thought i was at a liberal arts school) in my carrer as a college student. Why is that? Or, to look at it another way, is it wrong of me to be upset for not being forced, the same way we are forced to take a science/language/PE/yougetmypoint course? Is it important to seperate male writing from female writing?

I think so.

I don't mean to say they can't be catagorized equally in importance - that would be idiotic. I am only saying that I have not heard enough feminist voices in my years getting my edumacation - at a prestigious school, nonetheless.
Marchessault's quote of Miller in the conclusion of her essay makes me all the more sure of this: "only those who have power can play at not having it" (89). Im interested in hearing from the authors who are not playing.
