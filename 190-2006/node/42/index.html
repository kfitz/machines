Genderswapping zombies...

Submitted by roundmidnight on 12 September 2006 - 12:37am.
  • The Death of the Author

Being a media studies-psychology dual major is difficult. In one major i am forced to learn the intricacies of freudean theory; in my other major, i am forced to laugh at practically anything he ever said. Anyways...

After reading Marchessault's thoughts regarding the gender (not sex, gender is cultural, or?) of the dead author The Acadamy has been presented with, i must say i am intrigued. Her detective work in the bathroom stall was logical. A man would not sneak into a woman's stall simply to theorize on the gender of The Dead Author. But, is she saying that the dead author is a woman, or that the dead author is a genderswapping zombie?

I can't figure it out. Marchessault argues with the "utopian chauvinism" (84) of Foucault and Derrida; she says they give the dead author the gender of woman. They do so because the new author is "simply feminine," due to his/her fluidity, unfixedness, and multiplicity (84).

My question is this: how can you be dead if you've never been alive in the first place? While the author was alive he was male, therefore, the living author was never a female. Now, that the author has been murdered, he turns into a she? It does not make any sense to me.

Marchessault argues with the notion of a gendered analysis of texts when she mentions the difficulty it is to create a unifying polemic surrounding feminism (88). Although my knowledge of feminism is embarrasing, her examples from the film Calling The Shots (1988) provided me with sufficient evidence to agree with her on this point. It is easy to say that writing styles fall into feministic/phallocentric binary, but, that's the problem, its too simplistic.

Or, do does anyone agree with that binary?

I gotta say your title's a killer!

Submitted by PureJaqassary on 12 September 2006 - 5:48pm.

I most certainly do not agree with that binary (or pretty much any binary, as far as that goes).
While I do not completely agree with all of Marchessault's arguements, I am eternally fond of playing devil's advocate, so here it goes...
I don't think she means anything so literal as to say that the author was alive, and he was male, then he was murdered and his corpse became a woman. I think she means that when the author could be said to be alive, that is that the author had power, in the world and over the interpretation of the work, he was envisioned as male. The Father of Knowldege, the Devine Creator. With the death of the author (if we are to accept that death as given) a great deal of the real world and textual power were wrested from the author's grasp. The author was no longer the-God-who-created-all and continue to control it, but rather the vessel through which the text was brought to life, and who, upon bringing it into the world, proceeds to give up increasing amounts of control over it as it builds it's own life and moment as a text independent of it's creator. Though, as I said, I disagree with pretty much all binaries (and specifically those related to gender), I think it is easy to see how one might project onto this change in the authority of the author that of the change from All Powerful Devine Creator (that is THE Father) to the vessel of it's birth (that is the Mother). Thus it is the position of the author, if we are to follow Marchessault, that morphs from one of masculine authority to feminine passivity.
But then again, I'd like to reassert that I'm just playing devil's advocate here.

Also on the matter of sex and gender, a broadly accepted definition is that sex is biological (and therefore inherent) and that gender is social (and therefore learned). I, however, argue that sex is just as socially constructed as gender and that biology is as much a social science as psychology. Anyone who wants to take me up on that point, by all means, please do. I welcome the debate.
