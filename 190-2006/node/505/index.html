Sony BMG merger

Submitted by gwen on 24 November 2006 - 12:33pm.
  • hybrid
  • media conglomerates
  • sony bmg merger
  • thanksgiving

At thanksgiving dinner I was struggling to make conversation with my dad's friend's son's wife, who is an antitrust lawyer. Digging into the (maybe 3) facts I've picked up from Students for Democratic Media, I said something along the lines of "Soâ€¦80 major media companies in the 80s consolidated into only 5 these daysâ€¦bad news for the consumer, eh?"

Cindy (the lawyer) responded that her main job is to facilitate mergers between major companies, and that she had worked on the 2004 Sony-BMG merger and had no opinion on its effect on the consumer. This is where our conversation dwindled and I turned to my dad's godson's grandmother, who had a job with a clear purpose I could relate to (selling children candy. That didn't exactly come out like I intended, the point is not that I am a sicko but that this grandmother cared about children, so she made a children's store. Unlike the lawyer who doesn't bother to form an opinion about a multi million dollar merger she's working on).

The thing is, Cindy may have no idea about the effect of her merger on the general public, but really neither do I. I go around trumpeting (oh yes, I trumpet) my three facts about media consolidation but I have nowhere near the depth of knowledge on the issue than Cindy, and even Cindy is unsure about the morality of the merger. I looked up a little about it online and found that, although the US approved the merger, the European Union annulled the European Commission's approval. So Sony BMG is approved in the US and not Europe, and it seems like no one is sure what will happen next. In the meantime Sony BMG was fined 10 million dollars because it used payola to market several artists including Franz Ferdinand and Celine Dion (haha. Hahahahaa.). You know the contests disc jockeys do to give away tickets to shows and whatnot? Apparently Sony BMG worked it out so that the disc jockey would pretend they were giving away the prizes, but they actually kept them for themselves.

But payola is nothing new with the Sony BMG merger, and I can't begin to understand the US/European Commission rift in half an hour of research. Now I kind of wish I was doing my thesis on the sales of Big Four music companies verses small independent labels, and the amount of diversity and innovation in their products (Cindy actually did say that independent labels are the most innovative). At this point in this blog I was planning to go into the autopilot "who is the author?" speech about the merger, but let's be honest, that's ridiculous. So I'm done with you!
