Talent Agency Is Aiming to Find Web Video Stars

Submitted by bloggityblog07 on 26 October 2006 - 9:40am.
  • awesomeness
  • internet fame

While reading the NYTimes on-line today I found an article that I reminded me of some previous discussions both on the blog and within class. The gist of the article is that one of the top five talent agencies in Hollywood, United Talent Agency has recently launched a new online unit which is devoted to finding up and coming creators of internet content.

Brent Weinstein who is heading up the new division was quoted in the article as saying, "The barrier to entry is so low, everybody is now a potential artist. So there's this great unwashed of talent out there, 99.999 percent of which is probably not good enough to have a traditional film and television career. But on the
Internet, a lot of different types of things go."

According to the article, the new agency has already signed six-figure deals with major media portals and signed a number of clients whose web-based serials have attracted a lot of attention within the internet community.

For example Weinstein said that two weeks ago, one of his new agents showed him a Web video that had been up for less than an hour called "Paxilback," a parody of the JT video "sexyback," and by the time they arranged a meeting five days later with its creators, the video had already been seen 600,000 times.

I think it is A) kinda crazy how quickly information is spread in our present obsession for all that is "on-line" and B) very interesting how, like the article says, the easy access to the internet is making everyone a potential artist.

Here's the link to the article if you want to read it all: http://www.nytimes.com/2006/10/25/technology/

Will lower barriers to entry result in increased quality?

Submitted by ofcabbagesandkings on 31 October 2006 - 10:49pm.

I think the agency is smart to look for web video stars. It would seem logical that if anybody is a potential artist those with more sheer raw talent (who didn't just happen to gain fame through any number of non-ability-related "qualifications") would rise to the top without such high barriers to entry.
