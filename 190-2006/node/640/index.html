media in education

Submitted by WildCherry15 on 12 December 2006 - 10:02pm.
  • education
  • internship
  • media

as i was watching the "what is art" claymation piece from racinian's recent blog entry, i started thinking about how i miss making creative pieces about theoretical concepts. we did that in high school kind of a lot, but instead of stop-motion we did collages, videos, and some other stuff.

for a literature class, we filmed a version of the show, "the dating game," where the three possible choices to date were portia (from julius caesar), scout (to kill a mockingbird), and veruca salt (willy wonka and the chocolate factory). it was hilarious, but really analytical as well with the comments we threw in. my friends and i had so much fun writing the script and doing the acting. this class was AP and very well-taught, yet still fun.

thinking about how media really augmented the concepts in that class makes me not feel so guilty being a media studies major and calling this (catching up with blogs) work. (my friend was sorta laughing at me as i was watching those youtube stop-motion clips.)

although i do not currently want to enter a career in education, i am very fascinated by the effects of media in the learning process, especially in generating motivation and increasing the ability to remember concepts. i did an internship for course credit over the summer working for UCSD on the groundwork for creating a new multimedia civics curriculum. one of my supplemental readings was the book "teaching youth media: a critical guide to literacy, video production, and social change." it was very cool in asserting the importance of media literacy in today's society, especially by those poorly served by the media, such as urban minority youth who are portrayed as either trendsetters or degenerates, neither stereotype giving them much humanitym according to author steven goodman.

ANYWAYS i did some other reading on recent teacher comments on the actual application of technology in the classroom and its effectiveness. it really seems that, although technology is wonderful, nothing can replace a teacher who really know what he or she is talking about. that's kind of a given, but people forget this sometimes and spend a disproportionately long amount of time and budget money on technology, which is really only supplemental. granted, it is a wonderful supplement. interestingly, i think the act of creating multimedia interpretions of work is much more beneficial for students, than looking at already made multimedia pieces. that's how it is for me anyway.

this is a really interesting topic for me (multimedia and education), so if anyone would like to discuss it further, i'm so up for that. bye!

really, though, i am interested in the use of media to supplement education, and i think both parodies and the use of alternative forms (like clay-mation) really do a good job of being entertaining, getting the point across, and encouraging people (especially the creators) to think out of the box. i could go on about my thoughts on education and media forever. i think i'll write another blog on that. later!
