L' Ultimo Bacio, One Last Kiss

Submitted by oneoutofseven on 19 September 2006 - 1:15pm.
  • italian cinema
  • l'ultimo bacio
  • one last kiss

While I was studying abroad in Florence, I took an Italian film class which focused on Italian films whose directors were from the south, or that featured portrayals of the south. One of the last films we watched was L'Ultimo Bacio, a recently released film that had nothing to do with any of the cinematic themes we'd spent the semester examining. The film featured an attractive but bland 30-something young couple, engaged to be married. The fiance, Carlo, freaks out about becoming a grown up, and may or may not have an affair with an 18 year old girl. And that was it-- no style, no rich symbolism, no nothing.

Ultimately, our professor was showing us this film as a warning: all of the characteristics of Italian cinema we'd spent the semester studying, all the self-referentialism was completely missing from this film; had the characters not been speaking Italian, there was no way to know where the film had been made. The film was Western, to be sure, but the subject matter was divorced of all other aspects of identity.

As if to further prove her point, just yesterday I saw an advertisement for an American film called "One Last Kiss"-- surprise! It's identical. How strange that a film can be so devoid of real character that it can be translated almost verbatim from one culture to another.

You can find the trailer for the American version here. Everything but the voice over is identical to the Italian version.

Seriously, she was

Submitted by oneoutofseven on 20 September 2006 - 3:51pm.

Seriously, she was ridiculous.

I know it's a remake, but the fact that the film is so easily translatable is the part that irks me. To see that film in the light of all these other films with various orientations in terms of place and history, where the filmmakers had a reason for making a film beyond commercial.... that's the difficult part. You wouldn't be able to translate Rossellini's Paesa'n, for instance, or Roma Citta Aperta, because they're both so steeped in history and, well, Italy.
