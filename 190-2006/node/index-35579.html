Happily Wedded?

Submitted by WildCherry15 on 6 September 2006 - 12:59am.

In her article "What Was an Author?" Molly Nesbit spends considerable time discussing the mixing of culture and industry. I personally find this combination to be perplexing. Is it ultimately beneficial for traditional art or not? I think the main point of art is expression, and as long as that is maintained, industry-infused art does not undermine traditional art, but extends it. In Lyotard's Xerox exhibition, visitors made photocopies, but each copy was unique due to the machine's inexactitude (254). To me, this is art when viewed as such; the personal expression of the art is in the discourse surrounding it. By early French law, cultural work was defined by "the material used and the imprint of the author's personality which would follow from working in this material" (234). As I just said, the Xerox example does show personality via individual interpretation. I think the tricky part comes with pinpointing the material. Is it the Xerox copy or perhaps the verbalized ideas inspired by the copy? I would argue for the latter. Without the discourse, the industrial machine's product does not reflect any human personality. Can industry and culture be happily wedded? Yes, as long as industrial technology does not force culture to deny a fundamental part of itself, personal expression.

I hate that.

But as someone who writes things, part of me still wants that kind of authorship recognition that Barthes suggests doing away with. What's the point of writing if there's no one who's going to read it? I think this is the problem, though.

Authorship & TS Eliot

Submitted by oneoutofseven on 5 September 2006 - 6:31pm.

For an English class last week, we read Eliot's Tradition and the Individual Talent, a sort of treatise on how Eliot views the role of the poet. In his essay, Eliot urges the poet to consciously place himself in the context of all English literary history-- applied universally, such as Eliot requests, when a poet is writing slam poetry, he needs also to be thinking about Chaucer & the implications of his work on Hawthorne and what all of this means for new slam poem.

Obviously I am taking this somewhat out of context (if Eliot could have predicted the emergence of slam poetry he might have given up before ever having written this essay), but I think Eliot's point is obsurd. Sure, some poetry (such as Eliot's) makes references to every literary work in the history of mankind and there exists a sort of person who gets a good deal of pleasure from knowing the nature of those allusions. I don't know, though, if that can be said to be the only poetry of value, in fact it seems absurd to even be arguing the point. Isn't a new literary work the manifestation of everything which has ever been written before it simply by existing in the first place anyway? Hasn't all published literary work changed, in some part, what "literature" is, what the word means, how it is perceived?

The Author Lives (sort of)

Submitted by ghostwriter on 3 September 2006 - 11:52pm.

Overall, I had a mixed reaction to the Barthes reading. In terms of the tendency to exalt "the Author" and seek out a direct correlation between the life and experiences of the author and the text, I agree with him in that this is problematic. As he states on page 50, "explanation of the work is still sought in the person of its producer, as if, through the more or less transparent allegory of fiction, it was always, ultimately, the voice of one and the same person, the author, which was transmitting his 'confidences'." I don't write fiction, but I do write music and I have definitely experienced people reading way too much into my songs, trying to analyze me probably even more than my work. The link between a text and its creator is much more complex than this. And here's where Barthes and I start to diverge, because I think that this link - although it's not the be all and end all in literary analysis - exists. And that it matters.
