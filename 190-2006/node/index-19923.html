Case of the Fake People

Submitted by ghostwriter on 23 October 2006 - 11:33am.
  • computer games
  • hamlet on the holodeck
  • narrative
  • The Sims!

In Chapter 2 of Hamlet on the Holodeck, Murray discusses how new narrative formats are being developed along with new electronic technology. Beginning on page 51, she explores the narrative potential of video games. Although games have "more detailed visual environments and faster response time-- the narrative content of these games is thin, and is often imported from other media or supplied by sketchy and stereotypical characters" (Murray 51). While it's clear that we still have our share of games with thin narratives, or narratives based on stereotypical characters ("Grand Theft Auto San Andreas" comes to mind), I think that narratives in games have changed a lot since Murray wrote this in 1997.

going back to mainstream v. underground

Submitted by gwen on 23 October 2006 - 1:30am.

  • cash money
  • creativity

Building off my last blog, I'm starting to think that in every form of for-profit art or entertainment, the forces of creativity and fiscal marketing work directly against each other in today's society. This might fit with our definition of art as "beyond the necessary," since we all work to bring home the bacon. It's not always the case that a brilliant movie, novel, or musical composition will flop against competition created for the sole purpose of moneymaking, but it seems to me that companies are unwilling to take the risk of backing a new or unique project when they have already broiled the process of creating lucrative entertainment down to a simple equation.

make a choice

Submitted by neurotica on 22 October 2006 - 11:32pm.
  • hamlet on the holodeck

One of Murray's point that really struck me was in the first chapter, "Harbingers of the Holodeck". She talks about multiform narratives, giving examples such as "It's a Wonderful Life", where altnernative worlds are possible. With the advent of computers, expressing what she calls constantly bifurcating plotlines are becoming more possible. One quote I enjoyed was "The commitment to any particular story is a painful diminiution of the intoxicating possibilities of the blank page." I have noticed this sentiment in both my personal life and in how I enjoy narratives. In my personal life, i have crippling indecisiveness (which prolonged my thesis-topic finding process) is based on the idea that in making a choice, i am simultaneously shutting the door to a lot of possibilities. I have watched most of the TV series, "Queer as Folk". My favorite episode has always been the pilot, not necessarily because it was a better written episode (though it may have been), but because of all the possibilities it had, the potential relationships, the potential plots, character arcs, story points. What draws me to that episode is not necessarily how good it was but of all the potential of goodness it showed. From then on, of course, the show had to pick directions, letting some possible story ideas that were hinted at lead to no fruition. For example, in the pilot, it was hinted that one friend had a crush that borderlined on obsession for another friend. this could've been a very complicated interesting relationship dynamic, but is never really returned to in the series. Though it very well may be because "Queer as Folk" is a trashy show, but I feel like the actual show was never as good as what I thought it could be when I had watched the first episode.

Submitted by bloggityblog07 on 22 October 2006 - 10:11pm.
  • TV

So I was going to include this in my last blog but if you guys are anything like me, I know the more lengthy the blog, the less likely it will get read all the wa through. So... here is one more of my thoughts on television culure.

Despite the more recent uproar that violence in the media has caused in Congress and legislation, sex has always been the more debated issue when it comes to censorship and regulations. In a day and age when murder and mayhem rule the silver screen, dominate prime-time television, and grace the front page of every major news source, why is the majority of our attention always on sex? In th article I read for class, the author, George Gerbner says in his article, "formula driven violence in entertainment and news is not an expression of freedom, viewer preference, or even crime statistics-- but rather the product of a complex manufacturing machine" In other words, violence and blood sells. He continues to say that "Sex is second but, ironically, it runs into more inhibitions and restrictions."
