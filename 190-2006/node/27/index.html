Thoughts on Authorship

Submitted by lrob on 6 September 2006 - 2:25pm.
  • reading response

While reading Foucault's "What Is an Author?", I found myself disagreeing and questioning some of his points and assumptions. For instance, on p. 118, he wonders about what Sade's writings were "before he was consecrated as an author." I believe that becoming an author does not require being "consecrated," especially in the age of the Internet, since we are all authors as we post information or contribute to discussions on the World Wide Web. Also, he implies that everything Nietzsche contributed to the world, from references and reminder notes and laundry bills, might be included in his life's works as an author. References and reminder notes might be uniquely Nietzsche's, but a laundry bill does not strike me as an original work, especially since someone else probably wrote it out and Nietzsche probably only signed it. I agree more with a definition of authorship presented in Molly Nesbit's essay, found on p. 238, describing authorship as creation of "work of the mind, whatever the kind, the form of expression, the merit or the destination," ranging from literary writings to speeches and artistic creations.

However, towards the end of "What Is an Author?", Foucault makes some good points. I found his second paragraph on p. 138 especially interesting, where he writes: "The author--or what I have called the 'author-function'--is undoubtedly only one of the possible specifications of the subject and, considering past historical transformations, it appears that the form, the complexity, and even the existence of this function are far from immutable. We can easily imagine a culture where discourse would circulate without any need for an author. Discourses, whatever their status, form, or value, and regardless of our manner of handling them, would unfold in a pervasive anonymity." I feel that in this statement, Foucault predicted in 1977 what would happen decades later with the advent of the Internet. We blog with anonymity, unconcerned about our own status as authors or our rights to our work, and in doing so, we become practitioners of the redefined "author-function" of this digital era.
