the crazy world of blogging

Submitted by neurotica on 6 September 2006 - 2:36am.

hopefully next week i'll be more on the uppity up side of blogging and commenting on others. setting this up and trying to figure out sakai has taken a major chunk of my life. but voila (in the mother tongue of foucault)! I am here!

while reading nesbit's piece and how she talked about the impact of technology on notions and laws pertaining to authorship, i was wondering how would sampling fit into that? when describing the process of photographers struggling to be considered authors, she quotes Taine who said "photographic work is property; it belogs to the producer under the same title that gives the engraving to the engraver, and in the two cases, the property should be protected" (237). when a dj uses different samples in hir songs, whose property is that? there is a human process in that creation, and the samples are (at least theoretically) being used in a different context and being experienced differently. however, what makes it tricky, particularly with music, is that the samples are being experienced in the same medium though it is not the same work. the music industry in general is an interesting place to explore the realm of authorship.

reading this, pepped up on red bull as i am, i thought it was interesting the relationship between authorship, legal issues and industrialism. nesbit touches on the idea that with the increase of industrialization, the notion of authorship is declining, with an example of how with objects and goods are less and lese "not officially allowed to emobdy the personality of their makers" (235). while that may be true, as foucault talks about, being an author is associated with property rights (though he says that it's becoming less like that, with an attempt to restore writing to something at the margins). with the issue of sampling and copyright, one can sue someone else for stealing a three note hook or something like that. Can someone really be able to claim that they're the exclusive author of that? if they could, can a few notes embody your mark as an author?
