A Million Little Lies?

Submitted by msblogger1220 on 17 September 2006 - 10:20pm.
  • random thoughts

I recently realized that although I have read the articles and discussed the controversy about James Frey's book "A Million Little Pieces" with friends and relatives, I have not discussed it in an academic setting. I feel as though the issue of authorship that we are focusing on actually connects really well with the controversy.
First of all, I have read the book, but I read it after the realizations that some parts of it were false. I believe this knowledge skewed my perception of the novel while I was reading it. However, I feel it's really interesting that our society was so connected with this particular authorial situation. Why was it so important that the novel line up perfectly with the events in Frey's real life? Was it simply because he lied and said that the book was non-fiction?? or were people disappointed because their connection with the author's story was shattered?

I just thought it would be an interesting topic to discuss since the connection of the author's life to his work was so pertinent in this particular situation. Would the piece of work be different if the author in fact was dead? Just thought maybe some of you knew more/felt passionately about this particular authorial situation.

Fact or fiction...its all words to me

Submitted by bloggityblog07 on 18 September 2006 - 8:04pm.

I have had three seperate discussions about the James Frey controversy in the last week. Two in class and one outside of class. This book seems to be haunting me. While I have little to add to the discussion of this book since I have yet to read it, I am amazed by how offended people were by the controversy. Everyone who had read the book seemed to be taking the issue so personally. While I understand a general feeling of deception after having read the book as non-fiction and then realizing that it was in fact largely a lie, but does it really change the quality of the book? Last time I checked most people read for entertainment. The fact that some of his stories never actually happened doens't change the fact that the stories are nonetheless interesting to read. I agree completely that the credibilty of the author is destroyed but does it change anything fundamental in the text?
