the bloody mess we call "authorship"

Submitted by gwen on 6 September 2006 - 6:27pm.
  • Barthes
  • The Death of the Author

I agree completely with msblogger, who says, "I do believe it is important to acknowledge that there is never a single author of a work (society, surroundings, family, etc. all influence an author)." If Barthes is saying that an author pens nothing that has not been taught to him by his external surroundings, I could not agree more. Still, his thesis can't accurately be labeled "the death of the author," since it seems the author never really existed with this train of thought. Also, if no person exists outside her influences, why distinguish between the author and the scriptor (if we are equal to the sum our influences, and so is our writing)? I don't see why Barthes should mix his philosophy and the everyday functionality of conventions, acting as if we should rebel against the ever-evil "Author's empire": "The author still reigns in manuals of literary history, in biographies of writers, magazine interviews, and in the very consciousness of litterateurs eager to uniteâ€¦their person and their work; the image of literature to be found in contemporary culture is tyrannically centered on the authorâ€¦" (50).

There's a lot I don't understand about this essay. In addition to recognizing roughly 2% of Barthes' references, I'm not sure I understand the significance of even those I do know. For example, he writes, "by urging the hand to write as fast as possible what the head was unaware ofâ€¦Surrealism helped desacralize the image of the Author" (51). This makes me feel like I've complete missed Barthes' point â€“ cool, an author is not represented by his works or vice versa and countless other unnamed factors come into play, but why attempt to separate your conscious thoughts (self-conscious thoughts) from your writing? Does this really help "desacralize the image of the Author," or does it serve other, unrelated purposes? Barthes seems like the trendy fashion queen of literary terms, pushing to be the first to declare a new trend (eg "Last, outside literature itself (in fact, such distinctions are becoming quite dated)â€¦" (51) etc). If these distinctions are so 1967, why, Barthes, are you writing an essay distinguishing an author from her work? I can't see any practical reason to make the simple idea that 'a work does not equal an author' so dramatic. Maybe I am so blinded by the deity of the Author-God that I'm missing Barthes' point.

In an effort not to be outrageously presumptuous, a disclaimer: I (we) started on page 49 of this reading, and it seems that Barthes has already moved passed his argument that the author has died, and is now presuming we all agree with him in this essay (assuming the book isn't a collection of unrelated essays). So it's possible that the first 50 pages gave a knockout argument that I'm just failing to understand in my criticism. FAT CHANCE! Just kidding. Ah, blogging is weirdâ€¦

On another tangent, I would personally say authorship emerged when humans entered the level of consciousness where we developed the capacity to lie. I feel like the emergence of "the lie" marks when people actually began adding something original to history they recount, something is created rather than just relayed by an author. Sorry, scriptor. My bad.
