Why do the sites where we get our online readings change every semester?

Submitted by lrob on 10 September 2006 - 5:26pm.

I guess this has to do with Media Studies, since it deals with the posting and sharing of information online. I'm just wondering why the sites where we get our online readings for class change every semester. First everyone used eRes and WebCT... then last year it was Moodle... and now it's Sakai. Why hasn't Pomona just made one site for online readings and stuck with it? I guess the advantage of Sakai over Moodle is that students from all the Claremont Colleges can access it... but Moodle could have just been updated to allow for this. an infuriating bit of business

Submitted by KF on 10 September 2006 - 8:54pm.

This is a major thorn in the side of the faculty, I assure you; every year, we have to learn a new system. But the reasons make a perverse kind of sense: the WebCT project was run by CMC, through a big grant from the Mellon foundation. The grant ran out, and WebCT is quite expensive (and frankly, I hate it), so CMC told the other colleges that they could only support their own faculty, and not the rest of the Claremont folks. Okay.

Moodle was Pomona's replacement; Moodle is open-source (which WebCT is definitely NOT), and is in some ways at least less clunky and less overwhelming. But only Pomona was using Moodle, and last year the council of academic deans made the decision that all five colleges (and the grad school) should use the same system. That system turns out to be Sakai, which has better integration with the portal, and has something else going for it that I can't quite remember, in addition to also being open-source.

So Sakai it is, for the foreseeable future. (Which they said about Moodle last year, but go figure.)

The ERes system is entirely run by the library, as a replacement for the old-style reserves where you actually had to GO to the library and ASK for the reading. (Imagine!) The only thing that can be delivered through ERes is thus readings and other such library-type materials, and the other software packages (like Sakai) provide for more kinds of interactivity. Also, the downside of ERes from my perspective is that the faculty member can't really control what goes on the site and when; we're pretty much at the library's mercy.

There you are: way more information than you could possibly ever have wanted.
