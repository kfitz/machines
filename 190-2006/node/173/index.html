weird art and authorship

Submitted by TheGoodConstable on 1 October 2006 - 11:39pm.
  • art

Speaking of off the topic, let me waste 30 seconds of your time.

In my sculpture class last week, we were looking at some slides of some famous "art" pieces. We were looking at the work of some guy who's name i can't remember who chiseled these incredibly detailed marble sculptures. We were comparing this to the work of some other guy I can't remember whose sculptures were essentially just things he found that he modified slightly. For example, one of his pieces of "art" was a urinal that he painted some very small letters on, and set on its back.
By taking an object that he did not create, and altering it ever so slightly, yet calling it his work, he is raising many questions of authorship. Is he still the "author" of this work, even though he didn't really create it? Does his style of "art" make him any less of an "artist" than the first guy who does something that people consider more legitimate.

"Art" is obviously not balck and white, but I think the guy who takes a urinal and lays it flat and calls it his, is really not the author of that work at all. Some factory is the author of that thing. And it was meant to be peed on, which further questions its validity as art. any thoughts?

You're talking about Duchamp's Fountain. I understand why you say that you don't think Duchamp "created" the piece, but I don't think it's fair to say that he wasn't the author of that work. Wasn't he the author of the idea? (And a pretty clever idea it was, may I add...) Sure, the factory â€œauthoredâ€ the actual urinal, in the sense that they gathered the materials and assembled them, but did they think to paint a name on it and put it on a pedestal? Nope. What I think you're really analyzing is artistic ability- and since when has that safely served as a measure of who is, and who is not, an author?

To make this a bit more complicated, this was not Duchamp's first piece, nor his first appearance on the art scene. That said, what impact did his title as an "artist" have on this piece? Did it validate the piece? Would the same urinal, altered by an unknown person, be art?

also on the subject of "Fountain"

Submitted by PureJaqassary on 3 October 2006 - 6:03pm.

Did you take into account that Duchamp and "Fountain" are largely credited with birth of the Dada movement, one of who's main purposes were to call into question prevailing artistic canons and institutions of the day? Duchamps idea was far more original and creative than any contemporary of his who was simply making beautiful, finely wrought sculptures in the already accepted style with already accepted subject matter. While one is certainly justified in calling the sculptor a master craftsman, one has far more grounds for arguing that Duchamp was an artistic genius. Furthermore his work entered into discourse with the art institution of his place and time and he changed the way that the Western world viewed art in general, while your master sculptor probably did nothing more than create very pretty, and ultimately very boring, pieces of sculpture. Both are valid uses of art, I feel it is ill-guided to try to priviledge one over the other based solely on your own aesthetic preferences.
