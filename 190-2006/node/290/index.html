PRT: pt. 2: the Korean Wave!

Submitted by neurotica on 24 October 2006 - 12:35am.
  • asia
  • korean pop culture
  • major thesis depression

2) In East Asian countries, like China, and Vietnam, Korean popular culture is ridiculouslyâ€¦.erâ€¦.popular. Television dramas, such as Winter Sonata, to singers such as Rain have become phenomenons across Asia. In fact, in 2006, Time Magazine named Rain (who is akin to Usher and JT) the second most influential entertainer in the world. Korean actors are some of the most highest paid outside of Hollywood. This first piqued my interest because a lot of people in my family, including my mom and cousins are really into Korean dramas. Also, when I went to Vietnam, Korean culture was the cool trend, from films to food.

What interests me is how this pop cultural influence doesn't follow the normal legacies of other types of imperialism, which made/makes Western popular culture so widespread. For instance, Korean wave has impacted both Japan and China, which both have legacies of conquest with Korea. There has been several reasons attributed to why the Korean Wave exists, that the shows seemed to have high production values, yet are cheap to purchase by other Asian companies. Also, there is a closer cultural resemblances between Korean shows and other Asian nations than Western shows, such as issues of filial piety, belief systems, etc. However, there's been some controversy because Korean cultural products are becoming more popular than the products of the particular country.

i am interested in what are the implications at hand. Is the popularity of Korean Wave a way to create a resistance against Western pop cultural hegemony? People in Asian countries can buy representations of people who somewhat look like them, as opposed to someone like me in America, where the American and European pop culture I consume will probably have little resembling at all. Also, since with the spread of pop culture, the spread of values often occurs concurrently, is it a way to resist further Westernization? However, if the Korean Wave offered these empowering possibilities, why can't other Asian countries find these things within their own popular culture? Does it simply have to do with economics, since South Korea has the 10th largest economy in the world. Developing nations, such as Vietnam, may not have the resources yet to produce stuff with as a high a production value. However, that doesn't explain why the Korean Wave occurs in Japan, which has a similarly high economic status in the world. Finally, the popularity of Korean cultural exports rooted in the fact that, as one website points out, "A hearty diet and two years of forced military duty, industry leaders and fans insist, have also made young South Korean men among the buffest in Asia."

In general, I'm really interested when the pop cultures of non-western countries become popular in other cultures. When I went abroad to Cameroon, one of the most popular programs was this drama from the Philippines that was dubbed in English. When asking my host parents why they liked it, it was just that they related more easily with the culture, where family is the priority, dealing with one's own culture in the face of westernized modernity, etc. also, in the Northern part of Cameroon, which is predominantly Muslim, Bollywood is pretty popular. My host mom there said because the representations were more in line with her beliefs, and also these films resembled Hausa movies, Hausa being an ethnic group of the area.
