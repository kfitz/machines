Popazow

Submitted by DaLynziiChic on 20 September 2006 - 1:30am.
  • beached whales
  • fingerpuppets
  • Kevin Federline
  • yogurt

I feel like every subject I could tackle right now has already been addressed. Seeing as there have been about 10 blog posts in the last hour, I assume everyone else have the same anxiety I do and just really want to say something before their topic is run down by the merciless Blog Truck.

Originally, the whole idea of the "auteur" as the director was to circumvene the fact that a film is created by way more than one person. According to les frenchs, the director of the film has the most flexible power over all areas of production, from cinematography to lighting to costumes. I agree with this, although it's a shame when other people don't get the credit they deserve. However, if you've gotta put a name on something, it might as well be the director. A "good" auteur, therefore, was a director who could consistantly implement the most of his personality or style into the final product, even working through several other people. Examples of such auteurs would be Hitchcock and (gag) Jean-Luc Godard, who systematically used the same cinematic language within their films. If the director was "killed" in the many hands of production, s/he is certainly resurrected if successful as an auteur.

Anyhow, I guess the whole auteur theory brought on a whole lot of crap from auteur wannabes; the K-Feds of 1950's cinema, the "Popazows". I found that amusing; these french guys think they're so cool by making up these exclusive "rules" about what makes you an auteur, then the random K-Feds of the world make awful movies and think that, because they consistantly feature a bloody beached whale or a lephrechaun prone to seizures, they are "auteurs". Wrong! Popazow! (Or ARE THEY auteurs? Let's open this up to debate like Britney Spears opened up her life to Kevin Federline.)

However, I DO think that the auteur theory is valid in some senses. When I think of a modern-day auteur, I think of Tarantino or Baz Leurhman. These directors have films that are very distinct and have very original styles. But that makes me wonder--would a director like James Cameron, who (I feel) is devoid of any original filmatic personality be considered an auteur? Is that fair?

Oscars

Submitted by BuildingsAndFood on 20 September 2006 - 11:33am.

I think something interesting to note in this discussion is that when a movie wins Best Picture at the Oscars, it's the producers who win the award. Everyone else has awards for their parts in the film - screenwriting, directing, acting, etc. - but when it comes down to taking credit for the picture it's the producer who wins out. I think it's easy to write this off as being reflective of the industry part of moviemaking and I think it is reflective of that. But in my opinion, what it means is that there is no auteur. The producer can't possibly take credit for the final product. All they get is credit for assembling the pieces that put together a great product. And without any of those pieces, the product wouldn't have been made. I'm sure someone else is saying something along these lines, but I just figured I'd throw in this food for thought.
