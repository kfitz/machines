going back to mainstream v. underground

Submitted by gwen on 23 October 2006 - 1:30am.
  • adaptation
  • cash money
  • creativity

Building off my last blog, I'm starting to think that in every form of for-profit art or entertainment, the forces of creativity and fiscal marketing work directly against each other in today's society. This might fit with our definition of art as "beyond the necessary," since we all work to bring home the bacon. It's not always the case that a brilliant movie, novel, or musical composition will flop against competition created for the sole purpose of moneymaking, but it seems to me that companies are unwilling to take the risk of backing a new or unique project when they have already broiled the process of creating lucrative entertainment down to a simple equation.

During my presentation, we talked briefly about the differences between underground and mainstream hip hop. I feel like my position is best described by Theodor Adorno's essay, "On Popular Music." Adorno accuses the mainstream music industry of manipulation of its consumers - check it out at

http://www2.hu-berlin.de/fpm//texte/adorno.html

Or just read my summary:

Adorno identifies three specific faults of popular music. First, he writes that popular music leads to the standardization and homogenization of music. "Once a musical and/or lyrical pattern has proved successful, it is exploited to commercial exhaustion, culminating in 'the crystallization of standards'" (eg, sampling). Successful musical details are shifted from song to song, causing an audience to unconsciously feel as if they've already heard a new song in the very first listen. This leads to Adorno's second point: "popular music promotes passive listening." The repetitive nature of popular music, caused by "musicians" more concerned with profit than with musical authenticity, allows audiences to sit back and enjoy the barrage of hit songs on the radio without ever digesting a new tone or style of music. Like a cheap and hollow high, audiences "'crave a stimulant:' popular music satisfies the craving." Adorno's third claim is that popular music acts as 'social cement,' causing in listeners a 'psychical adjustment to the mechanisms of present-day life.' Adorno writes that listeners give in to an imperfect reality reflected in the repetition and lack of originality/vitality of the marketing techniques mentioned above. I personally found this claim a little excessive, because musical geniuses have not found the secret to living a happy life, nor do all people value music as highly as Adorno feels that they should. Still, I think he's getting at a good point (that I thought was also awesomely reflected by Scott McCloud's first 24 hour comic).

I think it's blaringly evident from any popular radio station that there is some truth in Adorno's first two claims. A professional jazz bassist once told me that nearly every hit song on the radio is written by five Berkley graduates sitting together for three hours, mapping the trends in recent music, regurgitating the chord progressions and failing to come close to actually composing a song in the traditional sense. The whole "art" element kinda flies out the window.
