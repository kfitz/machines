Intro to Computer Science

Submitted by BuildingsAndFood on 24 October 2006 - 1:47am.
  • awesomeness
  • computers
  • media studies classes

Okay, so I'm still in the earlier parts of the book, but I've had some thoughts already regarding much of what Murray has been saying. Some background first:

So I'm taking Intro to Comp Sci mostly to fulfill an Area Requirement, but also because it counts toward Media Studies major requirements. Initially, I thought, "Really? That doesn't make any sense!" except for the fact that computers are a form of media, but we had never really discussed a theory of computers (that I recall) in any of my previous theory classes. As I explain to people who are as incredulous as I that I'm taking this class, I'm often met with similar reactions and frequently have to justify it. Sometimes it's hard. Because of the department's association with the Math department, it's prettyer similar to a math class. There's a lot of analytical thinking required and much of programming language (with which I am not at all well acquainted) is fairly obtuse. Additionally I had a real crisis of faith today after receiving a pretty terrible grade on my midterm exam. Why am I doing this, I asked.

Well, looking at the first couple of chapters of this book, I've been able to think abstractly of computer programming as storytelling. I know it seems farfetched (and I'll testify that it's not very applicable yet with the program staring me in the face), but computer programs have a beginning (almost all programs start with a "begin" method), and a series of descriptive words that build up to a logical conclusion (the functioning program on the screen). You could read a program from start to finish and your imagine will be able to figure out what's going on. There are different characters (aspects of the program) that have to interact with each other before getting to the conclusion. And if a step in the storytelling method is left out the conclusion doesn't make any sense. It's kind of like an outline for a short story. But it's even required in my class to write descriptive comments about each action, and it's hard for me to avoid filling it up with flowery literary adjectives (though I do, because I'm dealing with the Math department essentially). If any of you have any experience with programming language, hopefully you have some idea what I'm talking about, thus validating it.

I don't think this approach will resuscitate my grade, but it will resuscitate my interest in the class and hopefully get me through the rest of my semester in an entirely unmathematical way. But I really identified with the cross-interest on Murray's part of computers and literature. Perhaps it's something I'll be able to expand upon as I either read more of the book or spend more time in the CompSci class.
