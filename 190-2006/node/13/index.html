The word document turned into a blog

Submitted by Leesoid on 5 September 2006 - 11:35am.

I applaud the blogging pioneers on here. Your authorship has provided me with some guiding light to this quite foreign concept to me of academic blogging. This is how I have always thought of blogging:

Current mood :) Current music: Beach Boys

As the title alludes to, I wrote this entry in word first. Here are my thoughts on why that is. As soon as you display something you write in front of peers, or simply an audience larger than just your professor, authorship takes on a whole new meaning. For me, I am uncomfortable and unconfident in this medium. There is an uncertainty that comes with the exposure of my thoughts to other people. Yet this is what authorship is. The creations of our minds should be meant for open examination by others. I however, haven't gotten used to sharing yet, other than of course in live discussion type forums.

Here is why I think blogging makes me uncomfortable. Blogging often seems to require coming up with original discussion generating ideas, rather than being in direct response to something. I think that there is a pressure here, especially when you know that your classmates will be reading your comments. Also, I feel more judged when blogging then commenting in class. It is easier for me to articulate my thoughts verbally and feel confidence in discourse, then simply writing down my thoughts and leaving them open for interpretation, without being present to explain or defend.

With this said, I am thinking that posting on this blog three times a week will change some of my thoughts on the above. So, I suppose it is really part of what I should expect from an education, to be pushed beyond my comfort zone. Ahh, the self reflective blog, I think I just had to get this one out of the way. I hope some people share my sentiments.

On the matter of the blog format and commenting etiquette

Submitted by PureJaqassary on 5 September 2006 - 8:40pm.

I gather that you are a fellow LJ-er

I completely understand the concerns you voice here. Like yourself, I feel far more confident about expressing myself verbally in a realtime discussion in which I can make my meaning known through inflection and non-verbal cues and in which I will have the opportunity to immediatly face my detractors and defend myself without what, to me, seems a shameful hiatus in which the disagreeing party might be thinking less of me due to misunderstanding.

In that vein, I suggest that we make a concerted effort (I assure you, I direct this very firmly at myself, as well as our classmates) to avoid comments which are merely contentious in favor of constructive criticism and discussion furthering perspectives.
