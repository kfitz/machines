Brangelina loves Banksy...

Submitted by achillesheel on 26 September 2006 - 6:21pm.
  • banksy
  • beautiful people
  • money
  • pachyderms
  • Paris Hilton

and so do a lot of other celebs who attended the "Barely Legal" show (Keanu, Jude Law, McCauley Culkin, Xtina).
Unfortunately I didn't make it to the show so I can't comment directly, but evidently and according to LA weekly writer Linda Immediato, the audience took an ironic turn for the worse. In addition to the high star power, there were a bunch of "well-dressed sorts in expensive clothing... a handful of miscreant graffiti kids, a few tattoed rabble rousers, but by and large the crowd was college-educated gorgeous. Everywhere I turned, my ears were flooded with people praising the show: 'brilliant,' 'amazing,' 'genius,' blah blah blah, 'important.'" She thought she was in the wrong place. The scene she depicts makes my stomach turn even in print. And I know it's lame to let art be affected by the other people who like it, but I (and Linda, evidently) am affected. the simple fact that some idiot likes the same thing I might enjoy makes me have disdain for it. anyway, back to banksy. what's interesting to me is that (assuming Linda wasn't the only one with this response) is that the composition of the audience affected the actual art... It seems that Banksy's intent (whatever revolutionary social change it might be) was overshadowed or even subverted by the overwhelming prescence and acceptance of those "college-educated gorgeous." In the end, Immediato makes the genius comparison between the exhibition's reception and "the Banksy sketch of people lined up to buy T-shirts that read 'destroy capitalism.'" So maybe this is the kind of hypocritical scene Banksy craves - mainstream richies hobnobbing and talking about how "brilliant" and "important" doctoring up Paris Hilton CDs was, and all the while gawking at Christina Aguilera. Gotta love the irony.

Further discussion of the Drink Cocaine campaign

Submitted by PureJaqassary on 26 September 2006 - 6:20pm.
  • consumer capitalism
  • Drink Cocaine
  • hegemony

I want to preface this by saying that I think that our country's stance on the matter of illegal substances is terribly misguided, I think that DARE does a lot more damage than good, and that the FDA and DEA are clearly more interested in regulating private citizen's personal morality on the basis of a highly conservative, fundamentalist Christian-oriented, and over-all misinformed perception of the dissemination, use, and effects of most so-called "narcotics" and controlled substances than in the protection of the citizenry. The FDA is a powerhouse of hegemonic regulation of "controlled substances", not only does it directly control what substances can and can not be legally sold and how and in what quantities, etc. but it also controls what sort of research can be done on controlled substances. You can't research their effects without obtaining the substance and you can not legally and ethically obtain it for a viable research experiment without going through the FDA, which categorically refuses to give clearance or supplies to any proposed research with the intent or potential to disprove the governmental and departmental stance on controlled substances. They not only control the substances but the knowledge that can be generated in regards to them.

Response to "Where's the line?"

Submitted by ofcabbagesandkings on 25 September 2006 - 7:11pm.
  • cool factor
  • Drink Cocaine

What a bizarre idea. On the one hand, the company is appropriating the name of a very hard core drug to capitalize on what it represents -- it is illicit, hardcore, rebellious and taboo to be a cocaine user. On the other hand, however, the drink claims to be more pure than most energy drinks and all of the charities the company supports are ones that deal with drug and alcohol addiction. If they are really so concerned with helping these causes, wouldn't it seem more productive to resist tapping into the "cool factor" of hardcore drugs strictly for the company's own monetary purposes?

why blogs make me sad....or AOL USER 23187425

Submitted by neurotica on 25 September 2006 - 2:39am.
  • aol
  • books
  • search engines

I totally posted a blog this afternoon and pressed submit and everything and lo and behold there is no post!! Similar stuff (though my fault), I posted some stuff last week but had forgotten to log in, so now I'm guessing they will be lost in cyberspace forever!

Anyways--my blog was about something that came up in my Digital Art class. One of the teachers (though not the official professor), Jason Brown talked about one of his projects. It all started with this guy who published the search inquiries of the users of an AOL search engine. You don't see the names of the users, instead users are assigned a number. So with this list you could see that at this time and day user 111 searched for this and later on he searched for that, etc. Here's a link with more info: http://www.lot49.com/2006/08/the_search_engine_diary_of_aol.shtml One user, over the course of May, searched over 8,000 entries. However, these entries were mostly bits of phrases that garnered no results, and instead created a strange poem or narrative. Jason Brown compiled the search inquiries in a book, which can be seen at this link http://superbunker.com/projects/i-feel-better/ Is this AOL user an author? What does it mean when something is published when it wasn't created with that intention? What does it mean when the medium itself isn't meant for authorship in how we normally think of it. In general I am fascinated by this, why this person typed so much so often, and what does it mean? Are they typing it for themselves or they are purposely transmitting their words into a global space?
