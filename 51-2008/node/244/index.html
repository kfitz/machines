rape in cyberspace? hmm...

By arianaib714 - Posted on 30 March 2008 - 8:02pm.
Tagged:
  • Reading Response 9

In this week's readings, we discussed the concept of the immersion our society finds itself in when using online games such as MUDs for entertainment. Where is the line drawn between reality and cyberspace, between a subject as horrifying as rape and a violent computer game character? Is there even a distinction between what happens in virtual reality as opposed to the real world?
In the essay by Julian Dibbell, "A Rape in Cyberspace ; or How an Evil Clown, a Haitian Trickster Spirit, Two Wizards, and a Cast of Dozens Turned a Database into a Society," Dibbell discusses an event that took place on a MUD website LambdaMOO, a "rape" of another player through one players power to silence a typed response and then proceed to type imaginary profanities to the other player, who's only option was to sit by and take it in. This incident is referred to as the "bungle affair." Dibbell brings up an interesting point about how this incident should be looked at in today's society.
" I mention it only as a warning that my own perspective is perhaps to steeped in the surreality and magic of the place to serve as an entirely appropriate guide. For the Bungle Affair raises questions that - here on the brink of a future in which human life may find itself as tightly enveloped in a digital environment as it is today in the architectural kind - demand a clear eyes, sober, and unmystified consideration. It asks us to shut our ears momentarily to the techno-utopian ecstasies of the West-Coast cyber hippies and look without illusion upon the present possibilites for building, in the online spaces of this world, societes more decent and free than those mapped onto dirt and concrete and capital.... and most forthrightly it asks us to wrap our late-modern ontologies, epistemologies, sexual ethics, and common sense around the curious notion of rape by voodoo dolll- and to try not to warp them beyond recognition in the process ( Dibbell 200). "
This excerpt from the essay brings up the issue of how our society acts and reacts with itself online. It seems as though the appeal of these online interactive, social, and completely virtual games is the engulfment in a completely different world where anyhting is possible and anyone can present themselves however they choose. This idea has drawn in children and adults alike, people from all genders, races, and religoins, into an imaginary world known as cyberspace, and allowed them to immerse themselves in a game that seems to be freeing from the constaints of regular society. In this virtual world, the players feel free, free from not only their bodily constraints, but also the constraints society places on them, the norms they must abide by in everyday life. This idea, of a virtual world, as Dibbell describes, is the future of our human lives, that this idea of a "digital environment" is only going to continually surround us until it BECOMES the future itself, and the end of the human experience as we know it today.
This desire for a utopian-esque world that exists online, a world free from all the problems in real life, is a desire that is common among the players, yet unachievable, as displayed through the Bungle Affair. The Bungle Affair is a perfect example of why, no matter whether our surrounding environment is virutal, digital, or physical, we cannot escape from the realities of humanity, and our societal problems resulting. The concept of rape is about power and violation. Although a "rape" online is shocking in an environment based upon people seeking to escape such an atrocious concept, the people who want to commit these crimes just leak through to our online societies as well. We cannot escape them, and whether an online "rape" may not be quite as violating as a physical one, the sexual harrassment that does affect users on the other end reflects the fact that a human's issue with power or hurtful words exists in every world we could possibly create, and until as a society we can live with and address the outsiders who commit these crimes, running from them will not save us for long.

there is no end
Submitted by miscellaneous on 30 March 2008 - 9:38pm.

I agree. The fact that anyone can access these MUD games makes it possible for offenders to leak through. Everyone has their own version of the perfect world. These views will clash with one another. One might want to live in a happy, safe society, while another has a darker vision of an utopia. It's difficult to regulate what crimes are committed online unless evidence is saved on the computer. As human beings, we will be affected when harassed with hurtful words directed toward our virtual selves or physical selves. Indeed, running away from universal issues will not solve anything. Our greatest obstacle to finding a solution is the infinite nature of cyberspace.
