I am very interested with the concept of the online self from monday's readings. In order to further dive into the subject, I propose a critical study using one of the currently more popular chat room websites as a case study. This website, called Habbo, launched in 2000 and has recieved numerous awards in several different countries. As a possible focus, I will look into legal cases that Habbo has been involved in regarding the virtual space and how the Habbo organization deals with hackers, theft, etc. According to a BBC article, a 17 year old boy was arrested in Britain for stealing around 4000 euros worth of virtual furniture in the online webspace. Habbo is known for having rampart scamming, stealing and general ruining of personalized spaces, including making the furniture in rooms impossible to get, basically destroying virtual furniture paid for by real money. Another interesting thing about habbo is that several bands, including the Guerillas, have paid the company to advertise and have events in the virtual world. A final interesting thing that I have heard is that Habbo was a medium for a protest by Anonymous, and an organized protest in the virtual world was used to close one of the larger areas and eventually the entire Habbo website went down for maintanance. As a critical paper, I would explore how Habbo is used in the extraordinary ways, focusing on how the virtual world is translated into the real world, as in cases like the theft charge in Britain and the virtual protest by Anonymous.

From my research (actually setting up a Habbo account and using it) my question has shifted into exploring what makes Habbo so popular. Specifically, I have found that the most popular parts of Habbo are still grounded in taking the real world and moving trends online. An example of this that I have found as a very popular, deep concept in Habbo is one of the group systems.
The special interest groups within habbo can really be seen as online gangs. Titles range from Saints Row Mafia to US Army. However, the concept is the same. Form a group and a room with a specific theme and structural system, similar to a gang, and gain as much popularity and as many members as possible. There is, however, a system of gang war within habbo.
Before I explain this system, I must explain the system of "rights" within private rooms. With rights, a player may move around the furniture within a room or manipulate certain pieces of furniture, like gates. A player with rights also may kick people from the private room. A room owner may give out rights at their discretion. The danger in giving out rights is getting a room destroyed. Since the furniture in habbo costs real money, losing furniture is a big deal. Furniture can be stacked to a point that it is impossible to retrieve. A room owner can return to their room at a time only to find that most of their furniture has been lost. A player with rights can also clear out a room by rapidly kicking the habbos present.
Since popularity is very important, and popularity is often judged on the number of habbos in a given room, clearing a room or messing up the furniture set up will stop new recruits from coming, thus putting the particular gang at a distinct disadvantage. Members of one gang will often try to pose as new recruits and will try to climb the ladder of command until the owner of a given room trusts them and gives them rights. A single such person can lead to the doom of an entire gang.
I think it is very interesting that the intended purpose the special interest groups has been extended to include very real life aspects. The creaters of habbo couldn't have envisioned that people would be able to start such conflicts, but concepts anchored in real life have transitioned over to the virtual world.
