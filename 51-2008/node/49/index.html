privacy on the internet?

By arianaib714 - Posted on 3 February 2008 - 10:04pm.
Tagged:
  • reading response 2

In the essays by Boyd and Langue, the online social networking craze and all that comes along with it is discussed. The concept of the existance of privacy in the use of these websites is discussed, and how truly safe any kind of information we place on the internet can stay. For example, as we discussed in class, your information is still stays on facebook, even if you choose to delete your profile.
First of all, I believe that the reason these social networking websites are so popular in the first place is because they are egocentric, and people are pretty into themselves. Sure, the websites are convenient for staying in touch with old friends, looking at pictures of new things happening in their lives, keeping them updated on yours etc. These websites are also used for meeting new people or maintaing relationships. However, they are also very egocentric, and I think people like that. People WANT to put their information on the web, they want to present themselves to the world however they choose. I think that should be kept in mind when discussing issues of privacy on the web because putting anything about yourself on the web is a risk, its a risk that millions of people across the country are perfectly willing to take to achieve the weird pleasure that comes with websites like Myspace and Facebook. The problems of hackers and illegal advertisers come up all the time with websites like this, and although there is new software created to help create more of a sense of security on the web, the actuality is that new hacking software is created at the same time, so really the "privacy" we seem to think we have on the web is actually a false sense of security.
In Boyd's article about "Facebook's 'Privacy Trainwreck,'" Boyd reports that the "News Feeds" on the website "...upset many Facebook members who responded with outrage. Groups emerged out of protest. Students Against Facebook News Feeds is the largest with over 700,000 members. " The "News Feed" aspect of Facebook seems to have initially been used as a way to update the members of the actions of their friends on the website, for example, posting new pictures, updating their profile etc. In fact, this aspect of the website seems to be very helpful and logical. However, with continuing problems on the web with privacy and the security of user informaion, as well as certain information readers do not want to be shared, it seems that this aspect of Facebook might disappear, deemed a flaw in the birth of the website.
The central issue of this article, and of the issue of privacy on the web in general, is how safe are we really? With these websites who are very popular among young teens who are also pretty naieve of the concequences of their actions (especially on something as seemingly mystical as the world wide web), how much information is too much? Is it a smart idea to really trust anything we put on the internet, any information given about us? The internet is the media of the future, but it also holds danger when we place ourselves in it, an issue we never had with any other form of media in the past.
