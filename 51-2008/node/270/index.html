assumptions in race and gender

By miscellaneous - Posted on 6 April 2008 - 4:32pm.
Tagged:
  • reading response 10

Cyberspace is a shared space. Anyone can enter it with a new identity. Unlike real space, cyberspace allows people to explore race and gender roles without having to return to reality with these experimental identities. Laura Miller's essay on "Women and Children First" argues that women can take care of themselves online, despite the generalized "idea that women's minds are weak, fragile, and unsuited to the rough and tumble of public discourse" (RDC 220). The stereotype of women as docile creatures has long been ingrained in our society. Mainstream media encourages this generalization by commenting on how women need protection online against aggressive males. Although the media appear to support the rights of women, they actually strip them away by constantly treating females as victims or vulnerable children. Women are not children, and thus should not be treated as such. The assumption that women's minds are unfit for public debate needs to change.

Online, people can protect their gender identity because nobody knows who they are. Miller brings up an interesting point that "there may not actually be a "masculine" or "feminine" mind or outlook, but simply a conventional way of interpreting individuals that recognizes behavior seen as in accordance with their biological gender and ignores behavior that isn't" (219). Society defines what is considered "masculine" or "feminine." It evolves with time; thus, there is no set of characteristics that make an individual one or the other. We associate behavior typically "masculine" with males and vice versa for females. What happens to individuals who do not identify with either gender? The Internet allows the blurring of gender differences because we cannot see the physical body and determine one's gender identity as easily. An online user could come off as "feminine" but in actuality be male. From this reasoning, we should not attribute certain traits to males or females because they seem more "masculine" or "feminine."

We are forced to release our common conceptions of gender in cyberspace. Is it possible to do this with race? Do racial groups possess characteristics that apply solely to their race? In "Race In/For Cyberspace," Lisa Nakamura argues that racial identity is "a vacation from fixed identities and locales" (230). White, middle-aged men can pretend to be young Asian females for recreational purposes without the obligation of bringing their online role-playing back to reality. Playing a character of a different race does not reflect a player's attitude toward his/her own race. It is based on curiosity. Nakamura provides another example of the samurai. Online gamers may play the samurai because they like his fighting ability, but it does not mean that they want to be Japanese warriors. They choose characters based on their skills. I wonder if the programmers who create the game are encouraging racial stereotypes by attributing certain characteristics to characters of different races. If game players choose characters because they assume Asians are better street fighters than Caucasians, then they propagate society's racial stereotypes as well. Society has taught the game creators and game players which racial groups are better fighters, and they use these assumptions in their selection of game characters.

Race is not necessary to forming an opinion about an individual but it is helpful in understanding one's background and cultural values. People online are not obligated to disclose their race. However, the concept of race cannot be completely dispelled from cyberspace because our society has racialized groups to the extent that we subconsciously consider race in our judgment of others.
