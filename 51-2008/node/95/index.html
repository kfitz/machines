Why don't we use Nelson's "hypertext"?

By shimla2901 - Posted on 17 February 2008 - 5:57pm.
Tagged:
  • hypertext
  • Nelson
  • reading response 4

With all of the computer software we have today, in addition to the internet, Nelson probably wonders why we don't currently use his model of hypertext. Although the internet may seem similar to his hypertext on the surface, he writes that hypertext would be "a body of written or pictorial material interconnected in such a complex way that it could not be presented on paper." Most informative websites on the internet seem to me to be a way of linking separate pages; they more like the table of contents in a book than a way of representing information in a radically new way. So why don't we have hypertext in the way Nelson envisioned? I would argue that it is not because we lack the technology, but because his model wouldn't give us what we want out of media.
One of the key reasons we read or watch a piece of media is that it can be something of a shared bonding experience. I might watch the football game on Sunday because I like watching football, but also because I like discussing the highlights with my friends. Similarly, I might read a book because it was recommended by a friend. Reading or watching this "old" media gives us something in common with other people. If everyone finds something different in the media, however, then we might no longer look to media as a way of connecting with people.
Furthermore, there is something disconcerting in the notion that I will look at a piece of media, and there will be something that is there that I can't see. If I buy a book, I want to physically see and know what I have, even if I only end up reading a couple of chapters. If I buy it in the form of Nelson's hypertext that allows me to read something different based on the mood I'm in, I am a little creeped out. I have to know it is there but I can't see it. The same idea applies when I write something. I want to be able to see it, and all of it, potentially at the same time. I could not write with Nelson's outlining program. Maybe this distress is simply because I am used to being able to see all that is there when I buy or write something.
You might say that I would feel weird whenever I go to a website, but I would disagree. When a website posts an article or a story, it posts the entire piece. It is not interconnected in a way that it could not be on paper. To me it is not what Nelson wrote about.
In class, professor Fitzpatrick told us that Nelson called the internet a "watered down" version of what he envisioned. I think his hypertext model just wasn't as popular has he expected. Similarly, I just read the segment on Oulipo, and it seems like some of their story methods where everyone has their own ending are not as popular as they might have imagined. I think this unpopularity is mostly because they doesn't give us the shared experience we want in media, and it is disconcerting to know that there is much there that we can't currently see.

On another note, I realize that this blog post is basically an essay, I guess that is naturally what I write because that is what I'm used to writing.
