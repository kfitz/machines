The Long Tail

By knavery - Posted on 27 January 2008 - 9:45pm.
Tagged:
  • reading response 1

So I just finished reading Anderson's The Long Tail. I found it very interesting. He delved deep into things that I had only thought breifly of before, and I agreed for the most part with his general message. However, there were some passages that I believed were either just plain wrong or poorly supported.

For instance, Anderson says, "more than half of Amazon's book sales come from outside its top 130,000 titles. Consider the implication:If the Amazon statistics are any guide, the market for books that are not even sold in the average bookstore is larger than the market for those that are" (page 3). The "if" at the beginning of that statement is a REALLY big one. Amazon statistics are a really bad comparison between books that are sold online and those that are sold in stores. The reason for this is that when you think about buying a book from a store, you probably have a good number of options as to which store you turn to. You could go to your neighborhood store, Borders, Barnes and Noble, or any others that you happen to know of. Anderson is correct in that these stores only sell "hit" books, so if you are looking for a rarer book, you would probably turn to the internet. The difference is that when most people consider buying a book online, they turn to Amazon. There are not many other well-known options that can offer the same services that Amazon does. This causes all of the sales of those "rare" books to be consolidated into Amazon's sales. That being said, it is not realistic to assume that because Amazon sells more than half of their books outside of the top 130,000 the market for "rarer" books is greater than the one for "hit" books. The online and physical situations here are just not comparable.

I also have issues with the folllowing statement by Anderson: "What matters is not where customers are, or even how many of them are seeking a particular title, but only that some number of them exist, anywhere. As a result, almost anything is worth offering on the off chance it will find a buyer" (page 3). I see what he is trying to say here, but Anderson completly disregards the cost of aquiring the rights to distribute these forms of media. If a business decides that they want to purchase the rights to a "miss" song so that they can distribute it online and only a couple people in the world purchase the song, the business is going to lose money. The situation is not as simple as Anderson makes it out to be. A better way to make his point would have been to look at say, Wal-Mart, which sells items both online and in physical stores. Here it actually is applicable to make more items available online, because they may not sell well in individual stores, but they could possible be worthwhile to sell online.

On another note, the idea of basing the cost of songs/movies/other media on their relative popularity is an interesting one. I am very curious as to how that would turn out. On the one hand it could increase the sales of "less popular" songs because they are cheaper (which I guess would in turn make them more expensive), or it could also backfire and have the cheap songs automatically labeled as "bad songs" and no one would buy them. I feel like it could go either way, or maybe some mixture of the two.

Anyway, I think I've rambled on long enough. I know I may have sounded very critical of this reading but I don't really feel that way. The critical aspects were just the parts I had the most to talk about. Feel free to ask me anything if you have any questions or comments. I don't profess expertise on any of this and I am open to the very-real possibility that I am completely wrong about everything I say. Hopefully not, but it's possible.
