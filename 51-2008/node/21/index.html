The Long Tail

By arianaib714 - Posted on 27 January 2008 - 4:21pm.
Tagged:
  • reading response 1

In the article "The Long Tail" by Chris Anderson, Anderson discusses the way the internet has changed the way we look at entertainment, the way the popularity of certain parts of entertainment reflect change in our society and how we consume it, and how "the future of entertainment lies in millions of niche markets" as opposed to chart topping radio hits, because nowadays we have the ability to access so quickly and easily what appeals most to us. There were a few main points I decided to reflect on from the article.
First of all, our society has reflected a mass change in the amount of music we download as oppose to purchase on actual cds, bringing record sales to an all time minimum and putting many huge record companies out of buisness. However, as well as music being downloaded legally through Itunes or other websites that have a monthly charge, there is also a huge audience (mainly a younger audience) who enjoys downloading their music for free off of peer to peer sharing software such as limewire. It is interesting, that this form of accessing music is pretty much socially acceptable now, and except for being occassionally sued by artists or record companies, illegally downloading music is quite a popular, normal thing, and barely looked at as stealing someone's art, even though it essentially is.
But if you look at illegal downloading of music in another light, perhaps it is just the evolution of our instant gratification society. Perhaps it is just change, it is the future, and artists should let go of trying to hold on to making money every time their song plays, because free music still builds a huge fan culture who buys concert tickets and promotes the artists they like in other ways. In the future of America, will all music just be free? Although the illegal downloading of music does reflect a selfish group of listeners, who simply don't seem to think the music they listen to is wourth paying for.
Another interesting point from the reading is that Itunes has played a huge role in helping underground bands become mainstream, therefore as opposed to the radios deciding what is popular, the mass audience has more control to make something initially unknown a huge success. Anderson explains, "Many of our assumptions about popular taste are actually artifacts of poor supply-and-demand matching - a market response to inefficient distribution. (Anderson 1). " This is also reflected in Netflix, because the convenience and popularity of Netflix has the power to reflect what movies people are really attracted to as opposed to box office numbers because instead of going to see a movie in theatres, people wait to watch it on Netflicks to save time and money. On Demand on Comcast/Time Warner cable reflects this very same ideal, the ideal of instant gratification and the need for ease when doing anything that is leisurely itself, Everything in the digital age reflects our countrys need of convenience. We want it as easily as we can possibly get it. Instant Gratification.
